# Bhañña

## English source

Bhañña. The word occurs in the compound <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkalāvassabhaññā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Okkalā vassabhaññā</span>) (SN.iii.73; MN.iii.78; AN.ii.31), a group described as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ahetuvādā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">akiriyavādā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">natthikavāda</span>, but who, nevertheless, agreed that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">khandhas</span> might be divided into past, present and future and who accepted the teaching of the Great Forty (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Cattārīsakasutta</span>). They also accepted non-covetousness, non-malice, right mindfulness and right concentration as worthy.

The Commentaries (AN­a.ii.497; MN­a.ii.894; SN­a.ii.201 says, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dve pi te mūladiṭṭhigatikā</span>) explain that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Vassa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Bhañña</span> were two people (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dvejanā</span>) (? tribes).
