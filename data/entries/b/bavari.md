# Bāvarī

## English source

Bāvarī. A Brahmin ascetic who went from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇāpatha</span> and lived on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godhāvarī</span> in a hermitage which lay half in the territory of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaka</span> and half in that of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aḷaka</span>.

He received the revenue of a village near by and held a great sacrifice, spending all he possessed. Then to him came a Brahmin of terrible mien, demanding five hundred pieces. He was a Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dunniviṭṭha</span>. His wife was a descendant of the family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jūjaka</span> and was constantly nagging at him. It was she who sent him to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span>, AN­a.i.183.

When Bāvari told him of his poverty, the Brahmin cursed him saying that his head would split in seven pieces. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span> was greatly distressed, but a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Devatā</span> (his mother in a previous birth, AN­a.i.183), seeing his trouble, reassured him by saying that the Brahmin knew neither the meaning of “head” nor of “the splitting of it.” “Who then knows it?” asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span>, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Devatā</span> told him of the appearance in the world of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>. Forthwith he sent his sixteen pupils – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajita</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissametteyya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mettagū</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhotaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upasīva</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Todeyya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kappa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jatukaṇṇī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhadrāvudha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udaya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Posāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mogharāja</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṅgiya</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> to see the Buddha and to find out if his claims to Buddha-hood were justified. The pupils went northward, through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aḷaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Patiṭṭhāna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māhissati</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gonaddha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vedisā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vanasavhya</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tumbava</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t18">v.l.</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vanasāvatthi</span>), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>; then, finding that the Buddha had gone to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, they followed him there to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāsāṇakacetiya</span>, passing through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setavyā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhoganagara</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>.

When they arrived before the Buddha, they greeted him in the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span>, and being satisfied that he bore the characteristic signs of a Great Being, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajita</span> asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span>’s question of the Buddha, and when that had been answered, each of the pupils asked him a question in turn, to which the Buddha replied. For a problem arising out of the manner in which some of the marks were seen, see Mil.168f.; DN­a.i.275f. This account is given in Snp.vs.976-1148.

According to the Commentary, all <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span>’s disciples and their sixteen thousand followers whom they had gathered on their way, became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span> at the conclusion of the Buddha’s sermon, save only <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṅgiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span>’s nephew, who became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmī</span>, because he had been thinking of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span> when the Buddha preached (Snp­a.603f). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṅgiya</span> took leave of the Buddha and returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span>, to whom he recounted all these events. At the end of his recital, the Buddha appeared before them in a ray of glory and preached to them. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṅgiya</span> thereupon became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span> an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmī</span>.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span> was King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭṭhavāhana</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). Hearing of the Buddha from his friend, the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, he sent messengers, including his nephew, to find out about the Buddha and to report to him. But the nephew returned with the news of the Buddha’s death, which had taken place before their arrival at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. Thereupon, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭṭhavāhana</span>, having accepted the Buddha’s teaching, engaged in various good deeds and was reborn after death in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāmāvacara</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span>-world. From there he was born in the family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>’s chaplain and was the teacher of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>’s boyhood. Unwilling to remain longer in the court, he took leave of the king and lived in the royal park as an ascetic. Then, wishing for greater peace, he retired to an island (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t18">antaradīpa</span>) in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godhāvarī</span> where the two kings <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aḷaka</span> gave him a tract of land, five leagues in extent, the residence of the sages of old. It was from there that he sent his disciples to the Buddha (Snp­a.575ff.; AN­a.i.182ff). At that time he was one hundred and twenty years old. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span> was the name of his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gotta</span>. He bore on his body three of the marks of a Great Being. Snp.vs.1019.
