# Bhaddāli Thera

## English source

Bhaddāli Thera. When the Buddha, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, laid down the rule that monks should eat one meal a day and that in the morning, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddāli</span> protested and refused to keep this rule because he said that, in so eating, he would be a prey to scruples and misgivings. For three months he avoided the Buddha, until, just before the Buddha was starting on a journey, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddāli</span>, acting on the advice of his fellow monks, confessed his fault to the Buddha and begged for forgiveness (MN.i.437ff). The Buddha praised this action and preached to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddālisutta</span> (*q.v.*).

It is said (MN­a.ii.648) that, in a previous birth, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddāli</span> had been a crow, therefore in this life he was always hungry and was known among his fellows as the Great Eater (*Mahā Chātaka*).

Thirty *kappas* ago he met <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumedha Buddha</span> in the forest, wrapt in meditation, and, having tidied the place where the Buddha was sitting, he erected over him a pavilion. Ap.ii.365f.
