# Beluvapaṇḍuvīnā

## English source

Beluvapaṇḍuvīnā. The lute carried by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>); it belonged originally to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>, after wasting seven years trying in vain to discover some shortcoming in the Buddha – six years before the Awakening and one year after it – left the Buddha in disgust and weariness, the lute which he carried slung on his shoulder slipped and fell. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> picked it up and gave it to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span>. It was so powerful that when plucked with the fingers the lovely music produced echoed on for four months (Snp­a.ii.393f).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vīnā</span> was three <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gāvutas</span> in length (Bv­a.239) and had fifty trestles (AN­a.i.72). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumaṅgalavilāsinī</span> (DN­a.iii.699) describes it at length. It was pale yellow, like a ripe <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">beluva</span> fruit. Its base (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pokkhara</span>) was of gold, its stem of sapphire its strings of silver, and its knots (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">veṭhikā</span>) of coral.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vīnā</span> was probably so-called partly because its base was made of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">bilva</span>-fruit, instead of the usual gourd, and partly because of its colour.
