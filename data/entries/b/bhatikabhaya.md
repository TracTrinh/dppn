# Bhātikābhaya

## English source

Bhātikābhaya, also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhātika</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhātiya</span>. Son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṭakaṇṇatissa</span> and king of Ceylon for twenty-eight years (38-66 CE.). He was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhātika</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhātiya</span> because he was the elder brother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Dāṭhika Mahā Nāga</span>. He was very pious, and once had the whole of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> covered with sandalwood paste in which were embedded sweet-smelling flowers. On another occasion he covered the whole *Thūpa* with flowers and sprinkled them with water drawn by machines from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayavāpi</span>. He made a plaster covering for the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> into which were mixed many cartloads of pearls. A net of coral was made and thrown over the *cetiya*, and in its meshes were fastened lotus flowers of gold, as large as wagon wheels. One day the king heard the sound of the chanting of *Arahants* in the relic chamber of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span>, and he lay down resolving not to rise until he had seen them. The Theras made a door by which he could enter, and, having seen the glories of the chamber, he described them for the benefit of the people, making figures in illustration of his descriptions. Bhātikābhaya did many other works of merit, held <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesākha</span> festivals, organized offerings for the Bodhi-tree, and showed great hospitality to the monks at various places. He was succeeded by his brother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Dāṭhika Mahā Nāga</span> (Mhv.xxxiv.38ff.; Mhv­ṭ.553f).

Bhātikābhaya once heard of a skilful judgment being given by *Ābhidhammika* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godha Thera</span> and laid down a rule that all disputes should be taken to the Elder for settlement (Vin-a.ii.307). On another occasion he appointed a Brahmin minister, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha Kārāyaṇa</span>, to settle a controversy between the monks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> and those of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> (Vin-a.iii.583). He had a queen called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmadevī</span> who was the daughter of a cattle butcher. A large number of cattle butchers were once brought before the king, but as they were unable to pay the fine demanded, he appointed them as scavengers in the palace. One of them had a beautiful daughter, and the king fell in love with her and married her. Owing to her, her kinsmen, too, lived in happiness (Vibh­a.440).

Bhātikābhaya once heard a *Sutta* (see AN.v.21f) in which the Buddha had declared that, of all perfumes, that of jasmine was the strongest. In order to test this the king filled a room with the four kinds of perfume and then placed in it handfuls of various flowers, including jasmine. He then left the room and shut the door. After a while he entered again, and the first scent which greeted him was that of jasmine. Convinced of the truth of the Buddha’s statement, he fell prostrate and worshipped him (AN­a.ii.819).

It is said (SN­a.ii.180) that the king once asked a reciter to tell him of an auspicious stanza (*jayamaṅgala*) connected with all the Three Jewels. After thinking for a while, he recited the stanza beginning *divā tapati ādicco, ratti ābhāti candimā* (SN.ii.284). At the end of the first *pāda*, the reciter saluted the setting sun, at the end of the second the rising moon, at the end of the third the Saṅgha, and at the end of the stanza he stretched his hands upwards in salutation of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span>. The king asked him to hold his hands there and placed in them one thousand pieces.
