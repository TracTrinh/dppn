# Buddhadāsa 01

## English source

Buddhadāsa 01. King of Ceylon for twenty-nine years (between 362 and 409 CE.) He was the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jeṭṭhatissa</span> and led a very pious life. He was renowned as a great physician, and various miraculous cures are attributed to him, even snakes seeking his assistance. A jewel, which he received from a snake in gratitude for a cure, he placed in the stone image in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagirivihāra</span>.

A medical work, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāraṭṭhasaṅgaha</span>, in Sanskrit, is ascribed to him (but see Cv.Trs. 13, n.7). It is said that he appointed a royal physician for every ten villages, and established hospitals for the crippled and for the dumb and also for animals. He appointed preachers to look after the people’s spiritual welfare.

Among religious buildings erected by him was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Morapariveṇa</span>. He extended his patronage to a holy monk, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Dhammakathī</span>, who translated the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span> into Sinhalese.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhadāsa</span> had eighty sons, named after the Buddha’s eminent disciples, the eldest being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upatissa II</span>. who succeeded him. Cv.xxxvii.105ff.
