# Buddhaghosa 01

## English source

Buddhaghosa 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa Thera</span>. The greatest of Commentators on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Tipiṭaka</span>. He was a Brahmin. The Sās. (p. 29) says his father was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Purohita</span>, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kesa</span>, his mother being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kesī</span>. Gv.66 says his father was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Purohita</span> of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅgāma</span>. He was born in a village near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhagayā</span> and became proficient in the Vedas and allied branches of knowledge. One day he met a monk, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span>, and on being defeated by him in controversy, entered the Saṅgha to learn the Buddha’s teachings. Because his speech was profound, like that of the Buddha, and because his words spread throughout the world (like those of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>), he came to be called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>.

While dwelling with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span>, he wrote the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ñāṇodaya</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atthasālinī</span>, and also began to write a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parittaṭṭhakathā</span> (a concise commentary) on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Tipiṭaka</span>. In order to complete his task, he came over to Ceylon at the suggestion of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span> and studied the Sinhalese Commentaries at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>, under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghapāla</span> (Sās.p.29, says he was sent to Ceylon as punishment for thinking himself wiser than his teachers). When his studies were ended he wrote the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visuddhimagga</span>, and having thereby won the approval of the Elders of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>, he rendered the Sinhalese Commentaries into Pāli. During this period he lived in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganthākaravihāra</span>, and on the accomplishment of his task he returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>. (Burmese tradition says he obtained his copy of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Tipiṭaka</span> and the Commentaries from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ālokavihāra</span>, but see PLC.83, n.1.4).

Besides the above mentioned works of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>, we have also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samantapāsādikā</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṅkhāvitaraṇī</span> on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vinayapiṭaka</span>; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumaṅgalavilāsinī</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Papañcasūdanī</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāraṭṭhappakāsinī</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manorathapūraṇī</span> on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttapiṭaka</span>. He is also said to have compiled Commentaries on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khuddakapāṭha</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span> (called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paramatthajotikā</span>) and on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada</span>. He also wrote a series of Commentaries on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhammapiṭaka</span> (the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atthasālinī</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sammohavinodanī</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcappakaraṇaṭṭhakathā</span>). Some ascribe to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka Commentary</span>. For further particulars relating to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>, see Law’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Life and Work of Buddhaghosa</span> and PLC.79 ff. The account of his life given here is taken from Cv.xxxvii.215ff. For a list of works ascribed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> see Gv., pp.59 and 68.
