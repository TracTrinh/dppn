# Bhūripañhajātaka

## English source

Bhūripañhajātaka (Ja 452). The name given to a section of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Ummaggajātaka</span>, which describes how <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span>, having lost the king’s favour, lived with a potter. The deity of the king’s parasol put several questions to the king, but his wise men (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Senaka</span> and others) were unable to answer them. The king then sent messengers with gifts to look for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span>; they found him in the potter’s hut and brought him back. The king expressed surprise that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> should have borne him no resentment. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> pointed out to him that wise men were incapable of ingratitude or meanness. Ja.vi.372-6.
