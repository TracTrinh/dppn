# Brahmāyusutta

## English source

Brahmāyusutta. Records the story of the conversion of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmāyu</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> contains a description of the thirty-two marks of the Mahā Purisa (Cp. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lakkhaṇasutta</span>) and also particulars of the Buddha’s conduct in various circumstances – such as walking, eating, meditating, preaching, etc. That is an example of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> in which the word “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dhammacakkhu</span>” means the three Paths leading to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">anāgāmiphala</span>. MN­a.ii.617.
