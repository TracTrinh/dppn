# Bahuvedanīyasutta

## English source

Bahuvedanīyasutta. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcakaṅga</span> asks <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span> (Paṇḍita <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span>, says MN­a.ii.629) how many kinds of feelings the Buddha mentions. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span> answers that there are three: pleasant, unpleasant and indifferent. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcakaṅga</span>, however, insists that there are but two: pleasant and unpleasant. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, overhearing the conversation, reports it to the Buddha, who says that both <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcakaṅga</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span> are correct because he himself classified feelings in various ways; sensual pleasures might be pleasant, but are not the highest pleasures; far better and more excellent are the pleasures enjoyed by a monk who develops the four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jhānas</span>, the plane of infinity of consciousness and the plane of nought. MN.i.396ff.; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> is repeated at SN.iv.223ff., under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcakaṅgasutta</span>.
