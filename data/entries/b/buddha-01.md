# Buddha 01

## English source

Buddha 01. A generic name, an appellative – but not a proper name – given to one who has attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> (*na mātarā kataṁ, na pitarā kataṁ – vimokkhantikaṁ etaṁ Buddhānaṁ Bhagavantānaṁ bodhiyā mūle... paññatti*, not by mother or father (is he named, but) liberation is at the root of the Awakening of the Buddhas the Bhagavās MNid.458; Paṭis.i.174). He is a man superior to all other beings, human and divine, by his knowledge of the Truth (Dhamma).

The texts mention two kinds of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>: viz.,

- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Pacceka Buddhas</span> – i.e., Buddhas who also attain to complete <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t30" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t30">Awakening</span> but do not preach the way of deliverance to the world; and
- Sammāsambuddhas, who are omniscient and are teachers of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Nibbāna</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Satthāro</span>).

The Commentaries, however (e.g., SN­a.i.20; AN­a.i.65) make mention of four classes of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>:

- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Sabbaññu Buddhā</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Pacceka Buddhā</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Catusacca Buddhā</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Suta Buddhā</span>

All *Arahants* (*khīṇāsavā*) are called *Catusacca Buddhā* and all learned men *Bahussuta Buddhā*. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> practises the ten perfections (*pāramitā*) for two *asaṅkheyya*s and one hundred thousand *kappas*, a *Sabbaññu Buddha* practises it for one hundred thousand *kappas* and four or eight or sixteen *asaṅkheyya*s, as the case may be (see below).

Seven *Sabbaññu Buddhas* are mentioned in the earlier books; these are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vīpassī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sikhī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessabhū</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakusandha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇāgamana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> e.g., DN.ii.5f.; SN.ii.5f.; cp. Thag.491; Ja.ii.147; they are also mentioned at Vin.ii.110, in an old formula against snake bites. Beal (*Catena*, p. 159) says these are given in the Chinese *Pātimokkha*. They are also found in the Sayambhū Purāṇa (Mitra, Skt. Buddhist Lit. of Nepal, p. 249).

This number is increased in the later books. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhavaṁsa</span> contains detailed particulars of twenty-five Buddhas, including the last, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>, the first twenty-four being those who prophesied <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>’s appearance in the world. They are the predecessors of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī</span>, etc., and are the following:

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīpaṅkara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇḍañña</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṅgala</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sobhita</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anomadassī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paduma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumedha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujāta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piyadassī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atthadassī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammadassī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siddhattha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phussa</span>.

The same poem, in its twenty-seventh chapter, mentions three other Buddhas – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Taṇhaṅkara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Medhaṅkara</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saraṇaṅkara</span> – who appeared in the world before <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīpaṅkara</span>.

The *Lalitavistara* has a list of fifty-four Buddhas and the *Mahā Vastu* of more than a hundred. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavattisīhanādasutta</span> (DN.iii.75ff) gives particulars of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Metteyya Buddha</span> who will be born in the world during the present *kappa*. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāgatavaṁsa</span> gives a detailed account of him. Some MSS. of that poem (JPTS 1886, p. 37) mention the names of ten future Buddhas, all of whom met <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> who prophesied about them. These are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Metteyya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttama</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi Kosala</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhibhū</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghasoṇī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkacca</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Todeyya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālāgiripalaleyya</span> (*sic*).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāpadānasutta</span> (DN.ii.5f) which mentions the seven Buddhas gives particulars of each under eleven heads (*paricchedā*) – the *kappa* in which he is born, his social rank (*jāti*), his family (*gotta*), length of life at that epoch (*āyu*), the tree under which he attains <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> (*bodhi*), the names of his two chief disciples (*sāvakayuga*), the numbers present at the assemblies of *Arahants* held by him (*sāvakasannipāta*), the name of his personal attendant (*upaṭṭhākabhikkhu*), the names of his father and mother and of his birthplace. The Commentary (DN­a.ii.422ff) adds to these other particulars – the names of his son and his wife before his Renunciation, the conveyance (*yāna*) in which he leaves the world, the monastery in which his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhakuṭi</span> was placed, the amount of money paid for its purchase, the site of the monastery, and the name of his chief lay patron. In the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>, the further fact is stated that on the day of his birth there appeared also in the world <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulamātā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kanthaka</span>, Nidhikumbhi (Treasure Trove), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Bodhi</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷudāyī</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> was conceived under the asterism (*nakkhatta*) of *Uttarāsāḷha*, under which asterism he also made his Renunciation (Da.ii425), preached his first sermon and performed the Twin Miracle. Under the asterism of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākha</span> he was born, attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> and died; under that of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgha</span> he held his first assembly of *Arahants* and decided to die; under *Assayuja* he descended from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhavaṁsa Commentary</span> says (Bv­a.2f) that in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhavaṁsa</span> particulars of each Buddha are given under twenty-two heads, the additional heads being the details of the first sermon, the numbers of those attaining realization of truth (*abhisamaya*) at each assembly, the names of the two chief women disciples, the aura of the Buddha’s body (*raṁsi*), the height of his body, the name of the Bodhisatta (who was to become <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>), the prophecy concerning him, his exertions (*padhāna*) and the details of each Buddha’s death. The Commentary also says that mention must be made of the time each Buddha lived as a householder, the names of the palaces he occupied, the number of his dancing women, the names of his chief wife, and his son, his conveyance, his renunciation, his practice of austerities, his patrons and his monastery.

There are eight particulars in which the Buddhas differ from each other (*aṭṭhavemattāni*). These are length of life in the epoch in which each is born, the height of his body, his social rank (some are born as *Khattiyas*, others as Brahmins), the length of his austerities, the aura of his body (thus, in the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṅgala</span>, his aura spread throughout the ten thousand world systems, while that of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> extended only one fathom (but when he wishes, a Buddha can spread his aura at will, Bv­a.106); the conveyance in which he makes his renunciation, the tree under which he attains <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>, and the size of the seat (*pallaṅka*) under the Bodhi-tree.

Only the first five are mentioned in DN­a.ii.424; also at Bv­a.105; all eight are given at Bv­a.246f., which also gives details under each of the eight heads, regarding all the twenty-five Buddhas.

In the case of all Buddhas, there are four fixed spots (*avijahitaṭṭhānāni*). These are: the site of the seat under the Bodhi-tree (*bodhipallaṅka*), the Deer Park at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> where the first sermon is preached, the spot where the Buddha first steps on the ground at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span> on his descent from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>?), the spots marked by the four posts of the bed in the Buddha’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhakuṭi</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>.

The monastery may vary in size; the site of the city in which it stands may also vary, but not the site of the bed. Sometimes it is to the east of the *vihāra*, sometimes to the north (DN­a.ii.424; Bv­a.247).

Thirty facts are mentioned as being true of all Buddhas (*samatiṁsavidhā dhammatā*).

In his last life every Bodhisatta is conscious at the moment of his conception;

in his mother’s womb he remains cross-legged with his face turned outwards;

his mother gives birth to him in a standing posture;

the birth takes place in a forest grove (*araññe*);

immediately after birth he takes seven steps to the north and roars the “lion’s roar”;

he makes his renunciation after seeing the four omens and after a son is born to him;

he has to practise austerities for at least seven days after donning the yellow robe;

he has a meal of milk-rice on the day of his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>;

he attains to omniscience seated on a carpet of grass;

he practises concentration in breathing;

he defeats <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>’s forces;

he attains to supreme perfection in all knowledge and virtue at the foot of the Bodhi-tree;

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span> requests him to preach the Dhamma;

he preaches his first sermon in the Deer Park at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>;

he recites the *Pātimokkha* to the fourfold assembly on the full moon day of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgha</span>;

he resides chiefly in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, he performs the Twin Miracle in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>;

he preaches the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>;

he descends from there at the gate of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span>;

he constantly lives in the bliss of *phalasamāpatti*;

he investigates the possibility of converting others during two *jhānas*;

he lays down the precepts only when occasion arises for them;

he relates Jātakas when suitable occasions occur;

he recites the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhavaṁsa</span> in the assembly of his kinsmen;

he always greets courteously monks who visit him;

he never leaves the place where he has spent the rainy season without bidding farewell to his hosts;

each day he has prescribed duties before and after his meal and during the three watches of the night;

he eats a meal containing flesh (*maṁsarajabhojana*) immediately before his death;

and just before his death he enters into the twenty-four crores and one hundred thousand *samāpattī*.

There are also mentioned four dangers from which all Buddhas are immune: no misfortune can befall the four requisites intended for a Buddha; no one can encompass his death; no injury can befall any of his thirty-two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Purisalakkhaṇā</span> or eighty *anubyañjanā*; nothing can obstruct his aura (Bv­a.248).

A Buddha is born only in this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavāḷa</span> out of the ten thousand <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavāḷas</span> which constitute the *jātikkhetta* (AN­a.i.251; DN­a.iii.897). There can appear only one Buddha in the world at a time (DN.ii.225; DN.iii.114; the reasons for this are given in detail in Mil. 236, and quoted in DN­a.iii.900f). No Buddha can arise until the *Sāsana* of the previous Buddha has completely disappeared from the world. This happens only with the *dhātu-Parinibbāna* (see below). When a Bodhisatta takes conception in his mother’s womb in his last life, after leaving <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span>, there is manifested throughout the world a wonderful radiance, and the ten thousand world systems tremble.

Similar earthquakes appear when he is born, when he attains <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>, when he preaches the first sermon, when he decides to die, when he finally does so (DN.ii.108f.; cp. DN­a.iii.897).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Padānasutta</span> (DN.ii.12-15) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Acchariyabbhutadhammasutta</span> (MN.iii.119-124) contain accounts of other miracles, which attend the conception and birth of a Buddha. Later books (e.g., Ja.i.) have greatly enlarged these accounts. They describe how the Bodhisatta, having practised the thirty *Pāramī*, and made the five great gifts (*pañcamahāpariccāgā*), and thus reached the pinnacle of the threefold *cariyā* – *ñātatthacariyā*, *lokatthacariyā* and *buddhicariyā* – gives the seven *mahādānā*, as in the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span>, making the earth tremble seven times, and is born after death in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span>. The Bodhisatta, who later became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span>, remained in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> during the whole permissible period – fifty-seven crores and sixty-seven thousand years. But most Bodhisattas leave <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> before completing the full span of life there. Five signs appear to warn the *Devaputta* that his end is near (see *Deva*); the gods of the ten thousand worlds gather round him, beseeching him to be born on earth that he may become the Buddha. The Bodhisatta thereupon makes the five investigations (*pañcamahāvilokanāni*).

Sometimes only one Buddha is born in a *kappa*, such a *kappa* being called *Sārakappa*; sometimes two, *Maṇḍakappa*; sometimes three, *Varakappa*; sometimes four, *Sāramaṇḍakappa*; rarely five, *Bhaddakappa* (Bv­a.158f). No Buddha is born in the early period of a *kappa*, when men live longer than one hundred thousand years and are thus not able to recognize the nature of old age and death, and therefore not able to benefit by his preaching. When the life of man is too short, there is no time for exhortation and men are full of *kilesa*. The suitable age for a Buddha is, therefore, when men live not less than one hundred years and not more than ten thousand. The Bodhisatta must first consider the continent and the country of birth. Buddhas are born only in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>, and there, too, only in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhimadesa</span>. He must then consider the family; Buddhas are born only in Brahmin or *Khattiya* families, whichever is more esteemed during that particular age. Then he must think of the mother: she must be wise and virtuous; and her life must be destined to end seven days after the Buddha’s birth.

Having made these decisions, the Bodhisatta goes to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandanavana</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span>, and while wandering about there “falls away” from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> and takes conception. He is aware of his death but unaware of his *cuticitta* or dying thought. The Commentators seem to have differed as to whether there is awareness of conception. When the Bodhisatta is conceived, his mother has no further wish for indulgence in sexual pleasure. For seven days previously she observes the *uposatha* vows, but there is no mention of a virgin birth; the birth might be called parthenogenetic (see Mil.123).

On the day of the actual conception, the mother, having bathed in scented water after the celebration of the *Āsāḷha* festival, and having eaten choice food, takes upon herself the *uposatha* vows and retires to the adorned state bedchamber. As she sleeps, she dreams that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cātummahārājika</span> raise her with her bed, and, having taken her to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span>, bathe her in Lake <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anotatta</span>, robe her in divine clothes, anoint her with perfumes and deck her with heavenly flowers (according to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nidānakathā</span>, Ja.i.50, it is their queens who do these things, *re* the Bodhisatta assuming the form of an elephant, see Dial.ii.116n). Not far away is a silver mountain and on it a golden mansion. There they lay her with her head to the east. The Bodhisatta, assuming the form of a white elephant, enters her room, and after circling right wise three times round her bed, smites her right side with his trunk and enters her womb. She awakes and tells her husband of her dream. Soothsayers are consulted, and they prophesy the birth of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span> or of a Buddha.

The two *Suttas* mentioned above speak of the circumstances obtaining during the time spent by the child in his mother’s womb. It is said (DN­a.ii.437) that the Bodhisatta is born when his mother is in the last third of her middle age. This is in order that the birth may be easy for both mother and child. Various miracles attend the birth of the Bodhisatta. The Commentaries expound, at great length, the accounts of these miracles given in the *Suttas*. Immediately after birth the Bodhisatta stands firmly on his feet, and having taken seven strides to the north, while a white canopy, is held over his head, looks round and utters in fearless voice the lion’s roar: “*Aggo ‘ham-asmi lokassa, jeṭṭho ‘ham-asmi lokassa, seṭṭho ‘ham asmi lokassa, ayam-antimā jāti, natthi dāni punabbhavo*, I am the chief of the world, I am supreme in the world; this is my last birth; henceforth there will be no rebirth for me (DN.ii.15).

To the later Buddhists, not only these acts of the Bodhisatta, but every item of the miracles accompanying his birth, have their symbolical meaning. See, e.g., DN­a.ii.439; thus, standing on the earth means the attaining of the four *iddhipādas*; facing north implies the spiritual conquest of multitudes; the seven strides are the seven *bojjhaṅgas*; the canopy is the umbrella of emancipation; looking round means unveiled knowledge; fearlessness denotes the irrevocable turning of the Wheel of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="119" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span>; the mention of the last birth, the *Arahant*-ship he will attain in this life, etc.

There seems to have been a difference of opinion among the Elders of the Saṅgha as to what happened when the Bodhisatta took his seven strides northwards. Did he walk on the earth or travel through the air? Did people see him go? Was he clothed? Did he look an infant or an adult? *Tipiṭaka* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="120" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cullābhaya</span>, preaching on the first floor of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="121" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span>, settled the question by suggesting a compromise: the Bodhisatta walked on earth, but the onlookers felt he was traveling through the air; he was naked, but the onlookers felt he was gaily adorned; he was an infant, but looked sixteen years old; and after his roar he reverted to infancy (DN­a.ii.442)!

After birth, the Bodhisatta is presented to the soothsayers for their prognostications and they reassert that two courses alone are open to him either to be a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="122" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span> or a Buddha. They also discover on his body the thirty-two marks of the Great Man (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="123" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Purisa</span>) (These are given at DN.ii.17-19; also MN.ii.136f). The Bodhisatta has also the eighty-secondary signs (*asīti anubyañjanā*) such as copper-coloured nails glossy and prominent, sinews which are hidden and without knots, etc. (The list is found in *Lal*.121 106). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="124" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmāyusutta</span> (for details see MN.ii.137f) gives other particulars about <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="125" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>, which are evidently characteristic of all Buddhas. Thus, in walking he always starts with the right foot, his steps are neither too long nor too short, only his lower limbs move; when he gazes on anything, he turns right round to do so (*Nāgavilokana*). When entering a house he never bends his body (cp. Dhp­a.ii.136); when sitting down, accepting water to wash his bowl, eating, washing his hands after eating, or returning thanks, he sits with the greatest propriety, dignity and thoroughness. When preaching, he neither flatters nor denounces his hearers but merely instructs them, rousing, enlightening and heartening them (MN.ii.139). His voice possesses eight qualities: it is frank, clear, melodious, pleasant, full, carrying, deep and resonant; it does not travel beyond his audience (for details concerning his voice see DN­a.ii.452f.; and MN­a.ii.771f). A passage in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="126" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttara</span> (AN.iv.308) says that a Buddha preaches in the eight assemblies – of nobles, Brahmins, householders, recluses, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="127" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="128" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cātummahārājika</span> world, and of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="129" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, of *Māras* and of *Brahmas*. In these assemblies he becomes one of them and their language becomes his.

The typical career of a Buddha is illustrated in the life of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="130" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>. He renounces the world only after the birth of a son. This, the Commentary explains (DN­a.ii.422), is to prevent him from being taken for other than a human being. He sees the four omens before his Renunciation: an old man, a sick man, a dead man, and a recluse. Some Buddhas see all four on the same day, others, like <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="131" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī</span>, at long intervals (DN­a.ii.457). On the night before the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="132" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>, the Bodhisatta dreams five dreams (AN.iii.240). After the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="133" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> the Buddha does not preach till asked to do so by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="134" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span>. This is in order that the world may pay greater attention to the Buddha and his teaching (DN­a.ii.467). A Buddha generally travels from the Bodhi-tree to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="135" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> for his first sermon, through the air, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="136" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> went on foot because he wished to meet <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="137" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upaka</span> on the way (DN­a.ii.471).

The Buddha’s day is divided into periods, each of which has its distinct duties (DN­a.i.45f; Snp­a.i.131f, etc.). He rises early, and having attended to his bodily functions, sits in solitude till the time arrives for the alms round. He then puts on his outer robe and goes for alms, sometimes alone, sometimes with a large following of monks. When he wishes to go alone he keeps the door of his cell shut, which sign is understood by the monks (Ibid., 271). Occasionally he goes long distances for alms, travelling through the air, and then only *khīṇāsavā* are allowed to accompany him (Thag­a.i.65). Sometimes he goes in the ordinary way (*pakatiyā*), sometimes accompanied by many miracles. After the meal he returns to his cell; this is the pure *bhattakicca*.

Having washed his feet, he would emerge from his cell, talk to the monks and admonish them. To those who ask for subjects of meditation, he would give them according to their temperament. He would then retire to his cell and, if he so desire, sleep for a while. After that, he looks around the world with his divine eye, seeking whom he may serve, and would then preach to those who come to him for instruction. In the evening he would bathe, and then, during the first watch, attend to monks seeking his advice. The middle watch is spent with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="138" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> and others who visit him to question him. The last watch is divided into three parts: the first part is spent in walking about for exercise and meditation; the second is devoted to sleep; and the third to contemplation, during which those who are capable of benefiting by the Buddha’s teaching, through good deeds done by them in the past, come into his vision. Only beings that are *veneyyā* (capable of benefiting by instruction) and who possess *upanissaya* (potential), appear before the Buddha’s divine eye (DN­a.ii.470).

The Buddha gives his visitors permission to ask what they will. This is called *Sabbaññupavāraṇa*, and only a Buddha is capable of holding to this promise to answer any question (Snp­a.i.229). Except during the rains, the Buddha spends his time in wandering from place to place, gladdening men and inciting them to lead the good life. This wandering is called *cārikā* and is of two kinds – *turita* and *aturita*. The first is used for a long journey accomplished by him in a very short time, for the benefit of some particular person. Thus <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="139" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> travelled three *gāvutas* to meet <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="140" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span>, thirty *yojanas* to see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="141" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="142" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgulimāla</span>, forty-five *yojanas* to see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="143" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span>, etc. In the case of *aturitacārikā* progress is slow. The range of a Buddha’s *cārikā* varies from year to year. Sometimes he would tour the *Mahā Maṇḍala* of nine hundred *yojanas*, sometimes the *Majjhimamaṇḍala* of six hundred *yojanas*, sometimes only the *Antomaṇḍala* of three hundred *yojanas*. A tour of the *Mahā Maṇḍala* occupies nine months, that of the *Majjhimamaṇḍala* eight, and that of the *Antomaṇḍala* from one to four months. Details of the *cārikā* and the reasons for them are given at length in DN­a.i.240-3. When the Buddha cannot go on a journey himself, he sends his chief disciples (Snp­a.ii.474). The Buddha announces his intention of undertaking a journey two weeks before he starts, so that the monks may get ready (Dhp­a.ii.167).

The Buddha is omniscient, not in the sense that he knows everything, but that he could know anything should he so desire (see MNid.178,179; see also MNid-a.223; Snp­a.i.18.). His *ñāṇa* is one of the four illimitables (neither can the Buddha’s body be measured for purposes of comparison with other bodies, MN­a.ii.790). He converts people in one of three ways: by exhibition of miraculous powers (*iddhipāṭihāriya*), by reading their thoughts (*ādesanāpāṭihāriya*), or teaching them what is beneficial to them according to their character and temperament (*anusāsanīpāṭihāriya*). It is the last method, which the Buddha most often uses (Bv­a.81).

The Buddha’s rivals say that he possesses the power of fascination (*āvattanīmāyā*); but this is untrue, as sometimes (e.g., in the case of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="144" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambi</span> monks) he cannot make even his own disciples obey him. Some beings, however, can be converted only by a Buddha. They are called *Buddhaveneyyā* (Snp­a.i.331). Some are pleased by the Buddha’s looks, others by his voice and words, yet others by his austerities, such as the wearing of simple robes, etc.; and finally, those whose standard of judgment is goodness, reflect that he is without a peer (Dhp­a.iii.113f.).

Though the Buddha’s teaching is never really lost on the listener, he sometimes preaches knowing that it will be of no immediate benefit (see, e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="145" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udumbarikasīhanādasutta</span>, DN.iii.57). It is said that wherever a monk dwells during the Buddha’s time, in the vicinity of the Buddha, he would always have ready a special seat for the Buddha because it is possible that the Buddha would pay him a special visit (DN­a.i.48). Sometimes the Buddha will send a ray of light from his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="146" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhakuṭi</span> to encourage a monk engaged in meditation and, appearing before him in this ray of light, preach to him. Stanzas so preached are called *obhāsagāthā* (Snp­a.i.16, 265).

Every Buddha founds a Saṅgha; the first *pātimokkhuddesagāthā* of every Buddha is the same (DN­a.ii.479). The attainment of *Arahant*-ship is always the aim of the Buddha’s instruction (DN­a.iii.732). Beings can obtain the four *abhiññā* only during the lifetime of a Buddha (AN­a.i.204). A Buddha has ten powers (*balāni*) which consist of his perfect comprehension in ten fields of knowledge, and physical strength equal to that of one hundred thousand crores of elephants (Bv­a.37) A.v.32f.; MN.i.69, etc. At SN.ii.27f., ten similar powers are given as consisting of his knowledge of the Paṭiccasamuppāda. The powers of a disciple are distinct from those of a Buddha (Kv.228); they are seven (see, e.g., DN.iii.283).

He alone can digest the food of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="147" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> or food which contains the *ojā* put into it by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="148" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>. No one else can eat with impunity the food which has been set apart for the Buddha (Snp­a.i.154). Besides these excellences, a Buddha possesses the four assurances (*vesārajjāni*, given at MN.i.71f), the eighteen *āveṇikadhammā* (described at *Lalitavistara* 183, 343, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="149" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> also gives (at DN­a.iii.994) a list of eighteen *Buddhadhammā*, but they are all concerned with the absence of *duccarita* in the case of the Buddha), and the sixteen *anuttariyas* (given by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="150" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="151" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sampasādāniyasutta</span> (DN.iii.102ff)).

The remembrance of former births a Buddha shares with six classes of purified beings, only in a higher degree. This faculty is possessed in ascending scale by *titthiyā*, *pakatisāvakā*, *mahāsāvakā*, *aggasāvakā*, *Pacceka Buddhā* and *Buddhā* (e.g.,Vsm.411).

Every Buddha holds a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="152" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Samaya</span>, and only a Buddha is capable of preaching a series of *Suttas* to suit the different temperaments of the mighty assembly gathered there (DN.ii.255; DN­a.ii.682f).

A Buddha is not completely immune from disease (e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="153" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>). Every Buddha has the power of living for one whole *kappa*, but no Buddha does so, his term of life being shortened by reason of climate and the food he takes (DN­a.ii.413).

The Commentary explains (DN­a.ii.554f.) that *kappa* here means *āyukappa*, the full span of a man’s life during that particular age. Some, like <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="154" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sīva</span> Thera*,* maintained that if the Buddha could live for ten months, overcoming the pains of death, he could as well continue to live to the end of this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="155" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddakappa</span>. But a Buddha does not do so because he wishes to die before his body is overcome by the infirmities of old age.

No Buddha, however, dies till the *Sāsana* is firmly established (DN.iii.122). There are three *parinibbānā* in the case of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="156" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>: *kilesa-Parinibbāna*, *khandha-Parinibbāna* and *dhātu-Parinibbāna*. The first takes place under the Bodhi-tree, the second at the moment of the Buddha’s death, the third long after (DN­a.iii.899f.; for the history of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="157" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>’s relics see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="158" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>).

Some Buddhas live longer than others; those that are *dīghāyuka* have only *sammukhasāvakā* (disciples who hear the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="159" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span> from the Buddha himself), and at their death their relics are not scattered, only a single *Thūpa* being erected over them (Snp­a. 194, 195).

Short lived Buddhas hold the *uposatha* once a fortnight; others (e.g. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="160" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>) may have it once in six months; yet others (e.g. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="161" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span>) only once in six years (Thag­a.i.62).

After the Buddha’s death, his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="162" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span> is gradually forgotten. The first *Piṭaka* to be lost is the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="163" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span>, beginning with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="164" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭṭhāna</span> and ending with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="165" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammasaṅgaṇī</span>. Then, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="166" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="167" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttapiṭaka</span>, from the eleventh to the first *nipāta*; next the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="168" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> from the *Cakkapeyyāla* to the *Oghataraṇa*; then the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="169" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhima</span>, from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="170" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indriyabhāvanāsutta</span> to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="171" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mūlapariyāyasutta</span>, and then the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="172" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha</span>, from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="173" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dasuttara</span> to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="174" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmajāla</span>. Scattered *gāthā* like the *Sabhiyapucchā*, and the *Āḷavakapucchā*, last much longer, but they cannot maintain the *Sāsana*. The last *Piṭaka* to disappear is the Vinaya, the last portion being the *mātikā* of the *Ubhatovibhaṅga* (Vibh­a.432).

When a Buddha dies, his body receives the honours due to a monarch (these are detailed at DN.ii.141f). It is said that on the night on which a Buddha attains <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="175" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>, and on the night during which he dies, the colour of his skin becomes exceedingly bright (DN.ii.134). Here we have the beginning of a legend which later grew into an account of an actual “transfiguration” of the Buddha. At all times, where a Buddha is present, no other light can shine (Snp­a.ii.525).

No Buddha is born during the *saṁvattamānakappa*, but only during the *vivaṭṭamānakappa* (Snp­a.i.51). A Bodhisatta who excels in *paññā* can attain Buddhahood in four *asaṅkheyya*s; one who exels in *saddhā*, in eight, and one whose *viriya* is the chief factor, in sixteen (Snp­a.i.47f). When once a being has become a Bodhisatta there are eighteen conditions from which he is immune (Snp­a.i.50). The Buddha is referred to under various epithets. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="176" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> gives one such list. There he is called *Samaṇa*, *Brāhmaṇa*, *Vedagū*, *Bhisaka*, *Nimmala*, *Vimala*, *Ñāṇī* and *Vimutta*. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="177" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> gives seven others: *Cakkkumā*, *Sabbabhūtānukampī*, *Vihātaka*, *Mārasenappamaddī*, *Vusitavā*, *Vimutto* and *Aṅgīrasa* (DN­a.iii.962f).

The Buddha generally speaks of himself as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="178" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tathāgata</span>. This term is explained at great length in the Commentaries – e.g., DN­a.i.59f. His followers usually address him as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="179" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhagavā</span>, while others call him by his name (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="180" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>). In the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="181" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>, we find him also addressed as Sakka (Snp. vs. 345; perhaps the equivalent of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="182" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākya</span>), *Brahma* (Snp. p.91; Snp­a.ii.418), *Mahā Muni* (Bv­a.38) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="183" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> (MN.i.386; also KS.i.262). Countless other epithets occur in the books, especially in the later ones. One very famous formula, used by Buddhists in their ritual, contains nine epithets, the formula being: *Bhagavā arahaṁ sammāsambuddho, vijjācaraṇasampanno, sugato, lokavidū, anuttaro, purisadammasārathi, satthā devamanussānaṁ, Buddho, Bhagavā* (these words are analysed and discussed in Vsm. 198 ff). It is maintained (e.g., DN­a.i.288) that the Buddha’s praises are limitless (*aparimāṇa*). One of his most striking characteristics, mentioned over and over again, is his love of quiet, e.g., DN.i.178f.; he is also fond of solitude (*paṭissallāna*) (DN.ii.70; AN.iv.438f.; SN.v.320f., etc.). When he is in retirement it is usually *akāla* for visiting him (DN.ii.270). There are also certain accusations, which are brought against a Buddha by his rivals, for this very love of solitude. “It is said that his insight is ruined by this habit of seclusion. By intercourse with whom does he attain lucidity in wisdom? He is not at his ease in conducting an assembly, not ready in conversation, he is occupied only with the fringe of things. He is like a one-eyed cow, walking in a circle” (DN.iii.38).

In this his disciples followed his example (DN.iii.37). The dwelling place of a Buddha is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="184" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhakuṭi</span>. His footprint is called *Padacetiya*, and this can be seen only when he so desires it. When once he wishes it to be visible, no one can erase it. He can also so will that only one particular person shall see it (Dhp­a.iii.194). It is also said that his power of love is so great that no evil action can show its results in his presence (Snp­a.ii.475). A Buddha never asks for praise, but if his praises are uttered in his presence he takes no offence (Thag­a.ii.42). When the Buddha is seated in some spot, none has the power of going through the air above him (Snp­a.i.222). He prefers to accept the invitations of poor men to a meal (Dhp­a.ii.135).
