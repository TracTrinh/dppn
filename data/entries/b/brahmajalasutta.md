# Brahmajālasutta

## English source

Brahmajālasutta. The first <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghanikāya</span>. It was preached to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">paribbājaka</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppiya</span> and his disciple <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span>. It first explains the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sīlā</span>, or moral precepts, in three successive sections <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cūḷa</span> (concise), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">majjhima</span> (medium), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">mahā</span> (elaborate) and then proceeds to set out in sixty-two divisions various speculations and theories regarding the “soul” (DN.i.46). Other names for it are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atthajāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammajāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Diṭṭhijāla</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅgāmavijaya</span>. At the end of the discourse the ten world systems trembled (DN.i.46). It is said that once when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍapātiya</span> Thera recited this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalyāṇiya</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span>, his mind concentrated on the Buddha, the earth trembled; the same phenomenon occurred when the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghabhāṇaka</span> Theras recited it at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambalaṭṭhikā</span>, to the east of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span> (DN­a.i.131).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmajāla</span> was the first <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> preached in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suvaṇṇabhūmi</span>, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span> visited it as missionaries (Mhv.xii.51).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> is often quoted, sometimes even in the Canon, e.g., SN.iv.286, 287.
