# Bhadra Hatthakasutta

## English source

Bhadra Hatthakasutta, or ? <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhagandha Hatthakasutta</span>. The headman <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhadragaka</span> visits the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelakappa</span> and asks for a teaching about <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dukkha</span>. The Buddha says he will talk neither of the future nor of the past, but only of the present. By means of questioning <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhadragaka</span>, the Buddha makes him realize that sorrow and suffering come only through desire. For example, he would grieve if anything happened to his friends in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelakappa</span>, or to his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ciravāsi</span>, or to his wife; but he would not worry about those who were unknown to him and therefore unloved by him (SN.iv.327f).

The Commentary says (SN­a.iii.103) that in this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta vaṭṭadukkha</span> (? the sorrow of transmigration) is described.
