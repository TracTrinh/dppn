# Brahmaloka

## English source

Brahmaloka. The highest of the celestial worlds, the abode of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahmas</span>. It consists of twenty heavens: the nine ordinary <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>-worlds, the five <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhāvāsā</span>, the four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arūpa</span> worlds, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asaññasatta</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vehapphala</span> (e.g., Vibh­a.521).

All except the four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arūpa</span> worlds are classed among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Rūpa</span> worlds (the inhabitants of which are corporeal). The inhabitants of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> worlds are free from sensual desires (but see the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅgajātaka</span>, Ja.iv.378, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Diṭṭhamaṅgalikā</span> is spoken of as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">bhariyā</span>, showing that some, at least, considered that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahmas</span> had wives).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> world is the only world devoid of women (Dhp­a.i.270); women who develop the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jhānas</span> in this world can be born among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmapārisajjā</span> (see below), but not among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahmas</span> (Vibh­a.437f). Rebirth in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> world is the result of great virtue accompanied by meditation (Vsm.415). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahmas</span>, like the other celestials, are not necessarily <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span> or on the way to complete knowledge (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sambodhiparāyaṇā</span>); their attainments depend on the degree of their faith in the Buddha, the Dhamma and the Saṅgha. See, e.g., AN.iv.76f.; it is not necessary to be a follower of the Buddha for one to be born in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> world; the names of six teachers are given whose followers were born in that world as a result of listening to their teaching (AN.iii.371ff.; iv.135ff.).

The Jātakas contain numerous accounts of ascetics who practised meditation, being born after death in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> world (e.g., Ja.ii.43, 69, 90; v.98, etc.). Some of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahmas</span> – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Baka</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) – held false views regarding their world, which, like all other worlds, is subject to change and destruction (MN.i.327). When the rest of the world is destroyed at the end of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappa</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> world is saved (Vsm.415; Khp-a.121), and the first beings to be born on earth come from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ābhassara</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> world (Vsm.417). Buddhas and their more eminent disciples often visit the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> worlds and preach to the inhabitants, e.g., MN.i.326 f.; Thag­a.ii.184ff.; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sikhī Buddha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhibhū</span> are also said to have visited the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> world (AN.i.227f.). The Buddha could visit it both in his mind-made body and his physical body (SN.v.282f.).

If a rock as big as the gable of a house were to be dropped from the lowest <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>-world it would take four months to reach the earth travelling one hundred thousand leagues a day. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahmas</span> subsist on jhāna, abounding in joy (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sappītikajjhāna</span>), this being their sole food. SN­a.i.161; food and drinks are offered to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Brahmā</span>, and he is invited to partake of these, but not of sacrifices (SN­a.i.158 f.). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmīs</span>, who die before attaining <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship, are reborn in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhāvāsā</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>-worlds and there pass away entirely (see, e.g., SN.i.35, 60, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Comp.</span>v.10). The beings born in the lowest <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> world are called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmapārisajjā</span>; their life term is one third of an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t18">asaṅkheyya kappa</span>; next to them come the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmapurohitā</span>, who live for half an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t18">asaṅkheyya kappa</span>; and beyond these are the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahmas</span> who live for a whole <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t18">asaṅkheyya kappa</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Comp.</span>v.6; but see Vibh­a.519f., where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahmas</span> are defined).

The term <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmakāyikā Devā</span> seems to be used as a class-name for all the inhabitants of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>-worlds (AN.i.210; v.76f).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Niddesa</span> Commentary (p.109) says that the word includes all the five (?) kinds of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sabbe pi pañca vokāra Brahmāno gahitā</span>).

The Bv­a.p.10 thus defines the word <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t18">brūhito tehi tehi guṇavisesahī ti=Brahma. Ayaṁ pana Brahmasaddo</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span><span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t18">-Brāhmaṇa-Tathāgata-mātāpitu-seṭṭhādisu dissati</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samantapāsādikā</span> (i.131) speaks of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span> who was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t18">khiṇāsava</span>, living for sixteen thousand <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span>. When the Buddha, immediately after his birth, looked around and took his steps northward, it was this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> who seized the babe by his finger and assured him that none was greater than he.

The names of several <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahmas</span> occur in the books – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tudu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭikāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Baka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sanaṅkumāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span>. To these should be added the names of seven <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmīs</span> resident in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avihā</span> and other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> worlds: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phalagaṇḍu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khaṇḍadeva</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhuraggi</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṅgiya</span> (SN.i.35, 60; SN­a.i.72 etc.).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Baka</span> speaks of seventy-two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahmas</span>, living, apparently, in his world, as his companions (SN.i.142).

See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>.

These are described as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahmas</span>. Mention is also made of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pacceka Brahmā</span> – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subrahma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhavāsa</span> (SN.i.146f).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tudu</span> is also sometimes described as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Brahma</span> (e.g., SN.i.149). Of the Pacceka <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahmas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subrahma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhavāsa</span> are represented as visiting another <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>, who was infatuated with his own power and glory, and as challenging him to the performance of miracles, excelling him therein and converting him to the faith of the Buddha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tudu</span> is spoken of as exhorting <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span> to put his trust in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t18">loc.cit.</span>)

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahmas</span> are represented as visiting the earth and taking an interest in the affairs of men. Thus, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span> descends from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>-world to dispel the heresies of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgati</span> (Ja.vi.242f). When the Buddha hesitates to preach his doctrine, because of its profundity, it is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) who visits him and begs him to preach it for the welfare of the world. The explanation given (e.g., at SN­a.i.155) is that the Buddha waited for the invitation of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> that it might lend weight to his teaching. The people were followers of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span>’s acceptance of the Buddha’s leadership would impress them deeply.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> is mentioned as visiting the Buddha several times subsequently, illuminating <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> with the effulgence of his body. It is said that with a single finger he could illuminate a whole <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavāḷa</span> (SN­a.i.158). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sanaṅkumāra</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) was also a follower of the Buddha. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahmas</span> appear to have been in the habit of visiting the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="119" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span> worlds too, for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="120" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sanaṅkumāra</span> is reported as being present at an assembly of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="121" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> gods and as speaking there the Buddha’s praises and giving an exposition of his teaching. But, in order to do this, he assumed the form of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="122" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span> (DN.ii.211ff).

The books refer to the view held, at the Buddha’s time, of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="123" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> as the creator of the universe and of union with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="124" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> as the highest good, only to be attained by prayers and sacrifices (e.g., at DN.i.18, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="125" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> is described as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="126" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vasavattī issaro kattā nimmātā</span>, etc.). But the Buddha himself did not hold this view and does not speak of any single <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="127" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> as the highest being in all creation. See, however, AN.v.59f., where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="128" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span>, is spoken of as the highest denizen of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="129" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sahassalokadhātu</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="130" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yāvatā sahassalokadhātu,</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="131" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="132" data-dppn-term-class="t18">tattha aggaṁ akkhāyati</span>); but he, too, is impermanent (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="133" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Brahmūno pi... atthi eva aññathattaṁ, atthi viparināmo</span>).

There are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="134" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahmas</span>, mighty and powerful (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="135" data-dppn-term-class="t18">abhibhū anabhibhūto aññadatthudaso vasavattī</span>), but they too, all of them, and their world are subject to the laws of Kamma, e.g., at SN.v.410 (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="136" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahmaloko pi āvuso anicco adhuvo sakkāyapariyāpanno sādhāyasmā Brahmalokā cittaṁ vuṭṭhāpetvā sakkāyanirodhacittaṁ upasaṁharāhi</span>). See also AN.iv.76f., 104f., where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="137" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunetta</span>, in spite of all his great powers as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="138" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span>, etc., had to confess himself still subject to suffering.

To the Buddha, union with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="139" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> seems to have meant being associated with him in his world, and this can only be attained by cultivation of those qualities possessed by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="140" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>. But the highest good lay beyond, in the attainment of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="141" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nibbāna</span>. Thus in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="142" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tevijjāsutta</span>; see also MN.ii.194f.

The word <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="143" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> is often used in compounds meaning highest and best – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="144" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahmacariyā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="145" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahmassara</span>; for details see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="146" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> in the Pāli English Dictionary (PTSPED).
