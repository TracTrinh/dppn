# Brahmaloka

## English source

Brahmaloka. The highest of the celestial worlds, the abode of the *Brahmas*. It consists of twenty heavens: the nine ordinary *Brahma*-worlds, the five <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhāvāsā</span>, the four *Arūpa* worlds, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asaññasatta</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vehapphala</span> (e.g., Vibh­a.521).

All except the four *Arūpa* worlds are classed among the *Rūpa* worlds (the inhabitants of which are corporeal). The inhabitants of the *Brahma* worlds are free from sensual desires (but see the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅgajātaka</span>, Ja.iv.378, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Diṭṭhamaṅgalikā</span> is spoken of as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span> *bhariyā*, showing that some, at least, considered that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahmas</span> had wives).

The *Brahma* world is the only world devoid of women (Dhp­a.i.270); women who develop the *jhānas* in this world can be born among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmapārisajjā</span> (see below), but not among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahmas</span> (Vibh­a.437f). Rebirth in the *Brahma* world is the result of great virtue accompanied by meditation (Vsm.415). The *Brahmas*, like the other celestials, are not necessarily *Sotāpanna* or on the way to complete knowledge (*sambodhiparāyaṇā*); their attainments depend on the degree of their faith in the Buddha, the Dhamma and the Saṅgha. See, e.g., AN.iv.76f.; it is not necessary to be a follower of the Buddha for one to be born in the *Brahma* world; the names of six teachers are given whose followers were born in that world as a result of listening to their teaching (AN.iii.371ff.; iv.135ff.).

The Jātakas contain numerous accounts of ascetics who practised meditation, being born after death in the *Brahma* world (e.g., Ja.ii.43, 69, 90; v.98, etc.). Some of the *Brahmas* – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Baka</span> (*q.v.*) – held false views regarding their world, which, like all other worlds, is subject to change and destruction (MN.i.327). When the rest of the world is destroyed at the end of a *kappa*, the *Brahma* world is saved (Vsm.415; Khp-a.121), and the first beings to be born on earth come from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ābhassara</span> *Brahma* world (Vsm.417). Buddhas and their more eminent disciples often visit the *Brahma* worlds and preach to the inhabitants, e.g., MN.i.326 f.; Thag­a.ii.184ff.; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sikhī Buddha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhibhū</span> are also said to have visited the *Brahma* world (AN.i.227f.). The Buddha could visit it both in his mind-made body and his physical body (SN.v.282f.).

If a rock as big as the gable of a house were to be dropped from the lowest *Brahma*-world it would take four months to reach the earth travelling one hundred thousand leagues a day. *Brahmas* subsist on *jhāna*, abounding in joy (*sappītikajjhāna*), this being their sole food. SN­a.i.161; food and drinks are offered to *Mahā Brahmā*, and he is invited to partake of these, but not of sacrifices (SN­a.i.158 f.). *Anāgāmīs*, who die before attaining *Arahant*-ship, are reborn in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhāvāsā</span> *Brahma*-worlds and there pass away entirely (see, e.g., SN.i.35, 60, and *Comp.*v.10). The beings born in the lowest *Brahma* world are called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmapārisajjā</span>; their life term is one third of an *asaṅkheyya kappa*; next to them come the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmapurohitā</span>, who live for half an *asaṅkheyya kappa*; and beyond these are the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahmas</span> who live for a whole *asaṅkheyya kappa* (*Comp.*v.6; but see Vibh­a.519f., where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahmas</span> are defined).

The term <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmakāyikā Devā</span> seems to be used as a class-name for all the inhabitants of the *Brahma*-worlds (AN.i.210; v.76f).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Niddesa</span> Commentary (p.109) says that the word includes all the five (?) kinds of *Brahma* (*sabbe pi pañca vokāra Brahmāno gahitā*).

The Bv­a.p.10 thus defines the word *Brahma*: *brūhito tehi tehi guṇavisesahī ti=Brahma. Ayaṁ pana Brahmasaddo* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span>*-Brāhmaṇa-Tathāgata-mātāpitu-seṭṭhādisu dissati*.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samantapāsādikā</span> (i.131) speaks of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span> who was a *khiṇāsava*, living for sixteen thousand *kappas*. When the Buddha, immediately after his birth, looked around and took his steps northward, it was this *Brahma* who seized the babe by his finger and assured him that none was greater than he.

The names of several *Brahmas* occur in the books – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tudu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭikāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Baka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sanaṅkumāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span>. To these should be added the names of seven *Anāgāmīs* resident in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avihā</span> and other *Brahma* worlds: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phalagaṇḍu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khaṇḍadeva</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhuraggi</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṅgiya</span> (SN.i.35, 60; SN­a.i.72 etc.).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Baka</span> speaks of seventy-two *Brahmas*, living, apparently, in his world, as his companions (SN.i.142).

See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> *Brahma*.

These are described as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahmas</span>. Mention is also made of *Pacceka Brahmā* – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subrahma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhavāsa</span> (SN.i.146f).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tudu</span> is also sometimes described as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Brahma</span> (e.g., SN.i.149). Of the Pacceka *Brahmas*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subrahma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhavāsa</span> are represented as visiting another *Brahma*, who was infatuated with his own power and glory, and as challenging him to the performance of miracles, excelling him therein and converting him to the faith of the Buddha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tudu</span> is spoken of as exhorting <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span> to put his trust in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> (*loc.cit.*)

The *Brahmas* are represented as visiting the earth and taking an interest in the affairs of men. Thus, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span> descends from the *Brahma*-world to dispel the heresies of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgati</span> (Ja.vi.242f). When the Buddha hesitates to preach his doctrine, because of its profundity, it is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> (*q.v.*) who visits him and begs him to preach it for the welfare of the world. The explanation given (e.g., at SN­a.i.155) is that the Buddha waited for the invitation of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> that it might lend weight to his teaching. The people were followers of *Brahma*, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span>’s acceptance of the Buddha’s leadership would impress them deeply.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> is mentioned as visiting the Buddha several times subsequently, illuminating <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> with the effulgence of his body. It is said that with a single finger he could illuminate a whole <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavāḷa</span> (SN­a.i.158). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sanaṅkumāra</span> (*q.v.*) was also a follower of the Buddha. The *Brahmas* appear to have been in the habit of visiting the *Deva* worlds too, for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sanaṅkumāra</span> is reported as being present at an assembly of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> gods and as speaking there the Buddha’s praises and giving an exposition of his teaching. But, in order to do this, he assumed the form of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span> (DN.ii.211ff).

The books refer to the view held, at the Buddha’s time, of *Brahma* as the creator of the universe and of union with *Brahma* as the highest good, only to be attained by prayers and sacrifices (e.g., at DN.i.18, where *Brahma* is described as *vasavattī issaro kattā nimmātā*, etc.). But the Buddha himself did not hold this view and does not speak of any single *Brahma* as the highest being in all creation. See, however, AN.v.59f., where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span>, is spoken of as the highest denizen of the *Sahassalokadhātu* (*yāvatā sahassalokadhātu,* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span> *tattha aggaṁ akkhāyati*); but he, too, is impermanent (*Mahā Brahmūno pi... atthi eva aññathattaṁ, atthi viparināmo*).

There are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahmas</span>, mighty and powerful (*abhibhū anabhibhūto aññadatthudaso vasavattī*), but they too, all of them, and their world are subject to the laws of Kamma, e.g., at SN.v.410 (*Brahmaloko pi āvuso anicco adhuvo sakkāyapariyāpanno sādhāyasmā Brahmalokā cittaṁ vuṭṭhāpetvā sakkāyanirodhacittaṁ upasaṁharāhi*). See also AN.iv.76f., 104f., where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunetta</span>, in spite of all his great powers as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span>, etc., had to confess himself still subject to suffering.

To the Buddha, union with *Brahma* seems to have meant being associated with him in his world, and this can only be attained by cultivation of those qualities possessed by the *Brahma*. But the highest good lay beyond, in the attainment of *Nibbāna*. Thus in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tevijjāsutta</span>; see also MN.ii.194f.

The word *Brahma* is often used in compounds meaning highest and best – e.g., *Brahmacariyā*, *Brahmassara*; for details see *Brahma* in the Pāli English Dictionary (PTSPED).
