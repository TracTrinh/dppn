# Bodhisatta

## English source

Bodhisatta. The name given to a being who aspires to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Bodhi</span> or Awakening. The Commentaries (e.g., DN­a.ii.427) define the word thus: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Bodhisatto ti paṇḍitasatto bujjhanakasatto; bodhisaṅkhātesu vā catusu maggesu āsatto laggamānaso ti Bodhisatto</span> (Bodhisatta means ‘one attached to wisdom,’ ‘a being intent on Awakening,’ or ‘one whose mind is inclined toward and attached to the four paths designated as Awakening.’) See also AN­a.i.453. For a discussion of the meaning of the word see Har Dayal: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">The Bodhisattva Doctrine</span>, pp.4ff.

The word can therefore be used in reference to all those who seek <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nibbāna</span>, including Buddhas, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>, and the disciples of Buddhas (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Buddha-paccekabuddha-Buddhasāvakā</span>), but is commonly used only of those beings who seek to become Buddhas. The word may have been used originally only in connection with the last life of a Buddha, in such contexts as “in the days before my Awakening, when as yet I was only a Bodhisatta”, e.g., MN.i.17, 114, 163; so also in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāpadānasutta</span> (DN.ii.13) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Acchariya-abbhutadhammasutta</span> (MN.iii.119).

But already in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kathāvatthu</span> (e.g., 283-90, 623) the previous lives of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> and other saints had begun to excite interest and speculation.

In the developed form of the ideas regarding Bodhisattas, a Bodhisatta’s career started with his making a resolution before a Buddha (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">abhinīhārakaraṇa</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">mūlapaṇidhāna</span>) to become a Buddha for the welfare and liberation of all creatures. In later literature, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">abhinīhāra</span> is preceded by a period during which the Bodhisatta practises <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">manopaṇidhi</span>, when he resolves in his mind to desire to become a Buddha without declaring this intention to others.
