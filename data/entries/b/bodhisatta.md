# Bodhisatta

## English source

Bodhisatta. The name given to a being who aspires to *Bodhi* or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>. The Commentaries (e.g., DN­a.ii.427) define the word thus: *Bodhisatto ti paṇḍitasatto bujjhanakasatto; bodhisaṅkhātesu vā catusu maggesu āsatto laggamānaso ti Bodhisatto* (Bodhisatta means ‘one attached to wisdom,’ ‘a being intent on Awakening,’ or ‘one whose mind is inclined toward and attached to the four paths designated as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>.’) See also AN­a.i.453. For a discussion of the meaning of the word see Har Dayal: *The Bodhisattva Doctrine*, pp.4ff.

The word can therefore be used in reference to all those who seek *Nibbāna*, including Buddhas, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>, and the disciples of Buddhas (*Buddha-paccekabuddha-Buddhasāvakā*), but is commonly used only of those beings who seek to become Buddhas. The word may have been used originally only in connection with the last life of a Buddha, in such contexts as “in the days before my <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>, when as yet I was only a Bodhisatta”, e.g., MN.i.17, 114, 163; so also in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāpadānasutta</span> (DN.ii.13) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Acchariya-abbhutadhammasutta</span> (MN.iii.119).

But already in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kathāvatthu</span> (e.g., 283-90, 623) the previous lives of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> and other saints had begun to excite interest and speculation.

In the developed form of the ideas regarding Bodhisattas, a Bodhisatta’s career started with his making a resolution before a Buddha (*abhinīhārakaraṇa* or *mūlapaṇidhāna*) to become a Buddha for the welfare and liberation of all creatures. In later literature, the *abhinīhāra* is preceded by a period during which the Bodhisatta practises *manopaṇidhi*, when he resolves in his mind to desire to become a Buddha without declaring this intention to others.

For the *abhinīhāra* to be effective, eight conditions should be fulfilled (Bv.ii.59; explained at Bv­a.75f. and Snp­a.i.48f): the aspirant should be

- (1) a human being,
- (2) a male,
- (3) sufficiently developed to become an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Arahant</span> in that very birth,
- (4) a recluse at the time of the declaration,
- (5) he should declare his resolve before a Buddha,
- (6) should be possessed of attainments such as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t23">jhānas</span>,
- (7) be prepared to sacrifice all, even life, and
- (8) his resolution should be absolutely firm and unwavering.

In the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>, his *abhinīhāra* was made at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amaravatī</span> in the presence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīpaṅkara Buddha</span>. His name at that time was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumedha</span> (*q.v.*). The Buddha, before whom the *abhinīhāra* is made, looks into the future and, if satisfied, declares the fulfilment of the resolve, mentioning the particulars of such fulfilment. This declaration is called *vyākaraṇa*, and is made also by all subsequent Buddhas whom the Bodhisatta may meet during his career. Having received his first *vyākaraṇa*, the Bodhisatta proceeds to investigate the qualities which should be acquired by him for the purposes of Buddhahood (*buddhakārakadhammā*), in accordance with the custom of previous Bodhisattas. These he discovers to be ten in number, the Ten Perfections (*dasapāramī*): *dāna*, *sīla*, *nekkhamma*, *paññā*, *viriya*, *khanti*, *sacca*, *adhiṭṭhāna*, *mettā* and *upekhā*. Bv.ii.116ff.

Sometimes thirty *pāramī* are spoken of, each of the ten being divided into three, varying in kind and degree. Thus, in the case of *dāna*, the *dānapāramī* consists in giving one’s limbs, *dāna-upapāramī* in giving away one’s external possessions and *dānapāramatthapāramī* in giving one’s life, this last being the most excellent.

In the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>, examples of births in which the ten *pāramī* were practised to the highest degree are as follows: the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekarāja</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khantivādī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷasaṅkhapāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sutasoma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mūgapakkha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lomahaṁsa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sattubhattaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sasa</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sutasomajātaka</span>s (Bv­a. 50; Ja.i.44f).

He also develops the four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhabhūmi</span> (*catasso buddhabhūmiyo*) – *ussāha*, *ummagga*, *avaṭṭhāna* and *hitacariyā* – explained respectively as zealousness (*viriya*), wisdom (*paññā*), resolution (*adhiṭṭhāna*) and compassion (*mettābhāvanā*).

He cultivates the six *ajjhāsayas* which conduce to the maturing of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> (*bodhiparipākiyā saṁvattanti*), these six being: *nekkhammajjhāsaya*, *pavivekajjhāsaya*, *alobhajjhāsaya*, *adosajjhāsaya*, *amohajjhāsaya* and *nissaravajjhāsaya*. Snp­a.i.50.

A Bodhisatta, during his career, escapes from being born in eighteen inauspicious states (*aṭṭhārasa abhabbaṭṭhānāni*). He is never born blind, deaf, insane, slobbery (*eḷamūga*) or crippled, or among savages (*milakkhesu*), in the womb of a slave, or as a heretic. He never changes his sex, is never guilty of any of the five *ānantarikakamma*s, and never becomes a leper. If born as an animal, he never becomes less than a quail or more than an elephant. He is never born either among various classes of *Petas* nor among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷakañjakas</span>, neither in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avīci</span> nor in the *lokantarakanirayas*, neither as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>, nor in worlds where there is no perception (*asaññībhava*), nor in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhāvāsas</span>, nor in the *Arūpa* worlds, nor ever in another <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavāḷa</span>. Snp­a.i.50 f.

Besides practising the (thirty) *pāramī*, all Bodhisattas must make the five great sacrifices (*mahāpariccāgā*) – giving up wife, children, kingdom, life and limb (Ja.vi.552) and must fulfil the three kinds of conduct (*cariyā*): *ñātatthacariyā*, *lokatthacariyā* and *buddhi-atthacariyā* and the seven *mahādanas* as practised by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span>, which caused the earth to quake seven times. DN­a.ii.427; Dhp­a.iii.441; the Bv­a. (116 f.) gives a story about <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṅgala Buddha</span> which corresponds to that of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span> in regard to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>. See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kharadāṭhika</span>.

The length of a Bodhisatta’s career varies; some practice the *pāramī* for at least four *asaṅkheyya*s and one hundred thousand *kappas*, others for at least eight *asaṅkheyya*s and one hundred thousand *kappas*, and yet others for sixteen *asaṅkheyya*s and one hundred thousand *kappas*. The first of these periods is the very least that is required and is intended for those who excel in wisdom (*paññā*), like our <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>. The middle is for those who excel in faith (*saddhā*); and the last and highest for those whose chief feature is perseverance (*viriya*) (Snp­a.i.47 f) like <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Metteyya Buddha</span>.

In their penultimate life all Bodhisattas are born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>), where life lasts for fifty-seven crores and six million years, but most Bodhisattas leave <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> before completing their life-span. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī</span>, e.g., was among the exceptions (DN­a.ii.427).

As the time for the announcement of their last birth approaches, all is excitement because of various signs appearing in the ten thousand world systems. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> of all the worlds assemble in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> and request the Bodhisatta to seek birth as a human being, that he may become the Buddha. The Bodhisatta withholds his reply until he has made the Five Great Investigations (*pañcamahāvilokanā*) regarding time, continent, place of birth, his mother and the life-span left to her. Buddhas do not appear in the world when men live to more than one hundred thousand years or to less than one hundred. They are born only in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> and in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhimadesa</span>, and only of a *Khattiya* or Brahmin clan. The Bodhisatta’s mother in his last birth must not be passionate or given to drink; she should have practised the *pāramī* for one hundred thousand *kappas*, have kept the precepts inviolate from birth, and should not be destined to live more than ten months and seven days after the conception of the Bodhisatta.

Having satisfied himself as to these particulars, the Bodhisatta goes with the other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandanavana</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span>, where he announces his departure from their midst and disappears from among them while playing. On the day of his conception, the Bodhisatta’s mother takes the vows of fasting and celibacy at the conclusion of a great festival, and when she has retired to rest, she dreams that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Four Regent Gods</span> take her with her bed, bathe her in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anotatta</span> Lake, clad her in divine garments, and place her in a golden palace surrounded by all kinds of luxury. As she lies there the Bodhisatta in the form of a white elephant enters her womb through her right side. The earth trembles and all the ten thousand world systems are filled with radiance. Immediately the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Four Regent Gods</span> assume guard over mother and child. Throughout the period of pregnancy, which lasts for ten months exactly, the mother remains free from ailment and sees the child in her womb sitting cross-legged (like a preacher on a dais, says the Commentary DN­a.ii.436). At the end of the ten months; she gives birth to the child, standing in a grove, never indoors. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhāvāsa</span> Brahmins, free from all passion, first receive the child in a golden net, and from them the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Four Regent Gods</span> take him on an antelope skin and present him to his mother. Though the Bodhisatta is born free of the mucous otherwise present at birth, two showers of water – one hot, the other cold – fall from the sky and bathe mother and child. The child then takes seven strides to the north, standing firmly on his feet, looks on all sides, and seeing no one anywhere to equal him, announces his supremacy over the whole world and the fact that this is his last birth. (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> as the Bodhisatta, spoke, in three different births, as soon as born – as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span>, as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span>, and in his last birth, Ja.i.53).

Seven days after birth his mother dies. She dies because she must bear no other being. The Bodhisatta’s time of conception is so calculated that the mother’s destined life-span completes itself seven days after his birth. From the Commentary account it would appear that the age of the Bodhisatta’s mother at the time of his birth is between fifty and sixty (*majjhimavayassa pana dve koṭṭhāsā atikkamma tatiye koṭṭhāse*) (DN­a.ii.437; Ud­a.278).

The Bodhisatta’s last birth is attended by various miracles. The Commentaries see, in the various incidents connected with the Bodhisatta’s last birth, signs of various features, which came, later, to be associated with the Buddha and his doctrine; for details see DN­a.ii.439ff.

Soothsayers, being summoned, see on the child’s body the thirty-two marks of a Great Man (*mahāpurisa*), and declare that the child will become either a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span> or a Buddha (for details of these marks see DN.ii.17ff.; MN.ii.136f. The reasons for these marks are given at DN.iii.145ff). His father, desiring that his child shall be a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span> rather than a Buddha, brings him up in great luxury, hiding from him all the sin and ugliness of the world. But the destiny of a Bodhisatta asserts itself, and he becomes aware of the presence in the world of old age, disease, death and the freedom of mind to be found in the life of a recluse. In the case of some Bodhisattas (e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī</span>) these four signs (*nimittāni* as they are called) are seen by them at different times, but in the case of others on one and the same day (DN­a.ii.457).

Urged by the desire to discover the cause of suffering in the world and the way out of it, the Bodhisatta leaves the world on the day of his son’s birth.

- Some Bodhisattas leave the world riding on an elephant (e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Dīpaṅkara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sumana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sumedha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Phussa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sikhī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Koṇāgamana</span>),
- some on a chariot (e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Koṇḍañña</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Revata</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Paduma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Piyadassī</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kakusandha</span>),
- some on a horse (e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Maṅgala</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sujāta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Atthadassī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Tissa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Gotama</span>), and
- some in a palanquin (e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Anomadassī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Siddhattha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Vessabhū</span>).
- Some, like <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Nārada</span>, go on foot,
- while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sobhita</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Dhammadassī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kassapa</span> travelled in the palaces of their lay life.

Having left the world, the Bodhisatta practises the austerities, the period of such practices varying.

- In the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Dīpaṅkara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Koṇḍañña</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sumana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Anomadassī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sujāta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Siddhattha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kakusandha</span> it was ten months;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Maṅgala</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sumedha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Tissa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sikhī</span> it was eight;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Revata</span> seven;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Piyadassī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Phussa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Vessabhū</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Koṇāgamana</span> six;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sobhita</span> four;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Paduma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Atthadassī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Vipassī</span> two weeks;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Nārada</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Padumuttara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Dhammadassī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kassapa</span> one week;
- and for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Gotama</span> six years (for the reason for this great length in the last case, see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Gotama Buddha</span>).

On the day the Bodhisatta attains to Buddhahood, he receives a meal of milk-rice (*pāyāsa*) from a woman and a gift of *kusa*-grass, generally from an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ājīvaka</span>, which he spreads under the Bodhi-tree (the Bodhi-tree is different for each Bodhisatta) for his seat. The size of this seat varies;

- the seats of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Dīpaṅkara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Revata</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Piyadassī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Atthadassī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Dhammadassī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Vipassī</span> were fifty-three hands in length;
- those of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Koṇḍañña</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="119" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Maṅgala</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="120" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Nārada</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="121" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sumedha</span> fifty-seven hands;
- that of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="122" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sumana</span> sixty hands;
- those of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="123" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sobhita</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="124" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Anomadassī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="125" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Paduma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="126" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Padumuttara</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="127" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Phussa</span> thirty-eight;
- of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="128" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sujāta</span> thirty-two;
- of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="129" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kakusandha</span> twenty-six;
- of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="130" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Koṇāgamana</span> twenty;
- of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="131" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kassapa</span> fifteen;
- of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="132" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Gotama</span> fourteen (Bv­a. 247).

Before the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="133" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> the Bodhisatta has five great dreams:

- (1) That the world is his couch with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="134" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Himālaya</span> as his pillow, his left hand resting on the eastern sea, his right on the western, and his feet on the southern;
- (2) That a blade of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="135" data-dppn-term-class="t23">tiriyā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="136" data-dppn-term-class="t23">kusa</span>) grass growing from his navel touches the clouds;
- (3) That white worms with black heads creep up from his feet, covering his knees;
- (4) That four birds of varied hues from the four quarters of the world fall at his feet and become white;
- (5) That he walks to and fro on a heap of dung, by which he remains unsoiled.

For the explanations of these dreams see AN.iii.240f.; these dreams are referred to at Ja.i.69.

The next day the Bodhisatta sits cross-legged on his seat facing the east, determined not to rise till he has attained his goal. The gods of all the worlds assemble to do him honour, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="137" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> (*q.v.*) comes with his mighty hosts and the gods flee. All day, the fight continues between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="138" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> and the Bodhisatta; the *pāramī* alone are present to lend their aid to the Bodhisatta, and when the moment comes, the Goddess of the Earth bears witness to his great sacrifices, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="139" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> and his armies retire discomfited at the hour of sunset, the gods then returning and singing a paean of victory. Meanwhile the Bodhisatta spends the night in deep concentration; during the first watch he requires knowledge of past lives, during the second watch he develops the divine eye, while during the last watch he ponders over and comprehends the *Paṭiccasamuppāda* doctrine. Backwards and forwards his mind travels over the chain of causation and twelve times the earth trembles. With sunrise, omniscience dawns on him, and he becomes the Supremely Awakened Buddha, uttering his *udāna* of victory, while the whole world rejoices with him.

For the *Paṭiccasamuppāda* see DN.ii.31ff.; for the other details see Ja.i.56ff., where the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="140" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> is given. DN­a.ii.462ff gives similar details regarding <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="141" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī</span>; Bv­a.248 says it is the same for all Bodhisattas.

The above is a brief account, as given in the books, of certain features common to all Bodhisattas. In addition to these, particulars of the personal career of the Bodhisatta who became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="142" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>, are found, chiefly in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="143" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhavaṁsa</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="144" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka Commentary</span>. It has already been stated that each Bodhisatta receives the *vyākaraṇa* from every Buddha whom he meets, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="145" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> was no exception.

- He received his first <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="146" data-dppn-term-class="t23">vyākaraṇa</span> as the ascetic <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="147" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sumedha</span>, from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="148" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Dīpaṅkara</span>;
- and then, as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="149" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Cakkavatti</span>, from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="150" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Koṇḍañña</span>;
- as the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="151" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Suruci</span>, from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="152" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Maṅgala</span>;
- as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="153" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Nāga</span> king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="154" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Atula</span>, from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="155" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sumana</span>;
- as the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="156" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Atideva</span>, from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="157" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Revata</span>;
- as the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="158" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Ajita</span>, from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="159" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sobhita</span>;
- as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="160" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Yakkha</span> chief, from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="161" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Anomadassī</span>;
