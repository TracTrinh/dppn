# Bālakaloṇakārāma

## English source

Bālakaloṇakārāma, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bākaloṇakārāgama</span>. A locality near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>. When the monks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> started quarrelling, the Buddha left them and went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bālakaloṇakārāma</span>, where he visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhagu</span> and preached to him on the virtues of solitude. From there the Buddha proceeded to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pācīnavaṁsadāya</span> (Vin.i.350; MN.iii.154; Dhp­a.i.47; Ja.iii.489).

The readings of the texts are uncertain, and it is impossible to say whether a village (*gāma*) is meant or only a grove (*ārāma*).

The reading <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bālakaloṇakāragāma</span> occurs in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhima Commentary</span> (MN­a.ii.596); but even here two explanations are given: one to the effect that Bālaka was the name of a village of salt makers (? *loṇakāragāma*) belonging to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli</span> *gahapati*. When the inhabitants of the village came to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli</span> with their taxes, he went with them (*bālakagāmavāsiniyā... parisāya*) to see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta</span>.

The other explanation is that the word *bālakiniyā* in the text is an adjective meaning “composed of fools” (*bālavatiyā bālussannāya*, cp. Ja.i.246, where mention is made of *bālagāmikamanussā* who were obviously fools). The confusion seems, therefore, to have arisen very early. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli</span>’s village (of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāka</span>), if such a place existed, was probably near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālandā</span>.
