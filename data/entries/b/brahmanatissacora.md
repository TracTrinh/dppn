# Brāhmaṇatissacora

## English source

Brāhmaṇatissacora. *v.l.* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brāhmaṇatiyacora</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brāhmaṇatissabhaya</span>. Reference is made in the Ceylon Chronicles and in some of the Commentaries to a period of great distress in Ceylon, owing to the activities of a *cora* (? brigand) called Brāhmaṇatissa in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṭṭagāmaṇī-Abhaya</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pitirājā</span>).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsa</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsaṭīkā</span> (Mhv.xxxiii.37ff.; Mhv­ṭ.613), Tissa was a Brahmin youth of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohaṇa</span>. One day he heard a Brahmin soothsayer announce that if a brigand were to commence his activities under a certain combination of planets, he would conquer the whole of Ceylon. Tissa, acting on this idea, turned robber and sent word to the king that he should hand over his throne to him. At the same time seven <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Damiḷas</span>, with their followers, arrived in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Tittha</span> with the same demand. The king thereupon sent word to Tissa that the kingdom would be his if he could defeat the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Damiḷas</span>. Tissa agreed to this and marched against them, but was taken captive in a battle near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅketahāla</span>. The Pāli Commentaries give further details. Tissa plundered the land for twelve long years; food became so scarce that, owing to starvation, people lost even their sexual desires, and the birth of a child was such a rare occurrence that all the land rejoiced over such a birth (SN­a.ii.83). Tissa’s activities were at their height when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṭṭagāmaṇī</span> was in hiding. The stores of food in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittalapabbata</span> *vihāra* and in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissamahārāma</span> were laid waste by enormous rats and the monks could obtain no food, Tissa having ravished the land. They therefore sent eight Theras to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, begging him to rid the country of Tissa; but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> sent reply that he was powerless, and suggested that the monks should go over the seas. Some took his advice and sailed from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambukola</span>, but the leaders of the community – *Saṁyuttabhāṇaka* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Sīva</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Soṇa</span> – remained behind awaiting better times (see also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgā</span> Therī, whose story is given in AN­a.ii.654f.; also MN­a.i.546). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> was deserted; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> was overgrown with trees. The monks had to live on lotus stalks and fruit rinds thrown away by the people. When Brāhmaṇatissa died, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṭṭagāmaṇī</span> once more came to the throne (Vibh­a.445-51). *v.l.* Brāhmaṇatiyacora (from which the Ceylon Chronicles derive the form Bæmiṇitiyā). About the date of the Bæmiṇisāya (the Brahmin famine as it was called in Sinhalese), see Cv.Trs. Introd. xvii., section 4.

See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍāla Tissa</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍāla Tiya</span>) which evidently refers to this same “*bhaya*.”
