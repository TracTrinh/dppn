# Bandhula

## English source

Bandhula. Son of a chieftain of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>. He studied at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāli</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>. On his return home, he wished to give an exhibition of his skill, and the princely families of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> bound sticks of bamboo in bundles of sixty, inserting a strip of iron in each bundle; they then suspended the bundles in the air and challenged <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span> to cut them down. He leapt up in the air and smote them with his sword, but on discovering the treachery of his kinsmen, he threatened to kill them all; his parents, however, dissuaded him, and he went to live in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> appointed him <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Senāpati</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span>’s wife was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span> (known as Bandhula <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span> in order to distinguish her from the wife of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>). As she bore no children, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span> wished to send her back to her people; but when she went to bid farewell to the Buddha before her departure, he asked her to return to her husband. He accepted her, thereby showing his faith in the Buddha. Soon after she conceived a child, and her pregnancy-longing was to enter the lotus tank used by the princes of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> on their coronation and to drink its water. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span> took her to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, drove away the strong guards who were posted at the lotus tank, and let <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span> enjoy it to her heart’s content. When the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span> princes heard of this, they were greatly enraged and pursued <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span>’s chariot, in spite of the warning of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāli</span>. When the chariots of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> came into line, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span>, in order to frighten them, twanged his bow; but as they still pursued him, he shot a single arrow, which pierced each of the five hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> through his girdle without their being aware of the wound. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span> told them of their plight; but they refused to believe him until they loosed the girdle of the foremost and he fell down dead. Thereupon they returned to their homes, bade farewell to their families, and fell dead on the moment of loosening their armour.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span> bore twin sons sixteen times; each of them became perfect in the various arts, and each had a retinue of one thousand men. One day, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span> retried a case, which had been unjustly decided by the judge and his decision was greatly applauded. The king, hearing the applause and learning the reason, appointed him judge. It is probably this incident, which is referred to at SN.i.74 (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṭṭhakaraṇasutta</span>); see also KS.i.101, n.3.

But the former judges poisoned the king’s mind against <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span>, and the king, listening to them, sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span> and his sons to quell a frontier rebellion, giving orders that they should all be murdered on the way home. This was done, and the news of the massacre was brought to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span> while she was entertaining five hundred monks led by the two Chief Disciples (according to MN­a.ii.753 the Buddha was also present). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span> read the message, and placing it in a fold of her dress, went on with her duties. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> discovered her fortitude at the end of the meal and greatly praised her. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span> sending for her daughters-in-law, broke the news to them, urging them to harbour no resentment against the king. The king’s spies, discovering this, brought the news to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>. The king was greatly moved, and having sent for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span>, begged her forgiveness and granted her a boon. She chose as her boon that she and her thirty-two daughters in law should be allowed to return home to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span>’s nephew, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha Kārāyaṇa</span>, was appointed commander-in chief, but he never forgave the injury to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span>, and, in the end, brought about <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>’s deposition and consequent death (Dhp­a.i.228f., 349-56; Ja.iv.148 ff.; MN­a.ii.753f). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span> is sometimes referred to as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhulamalla</span>. (e.g., Ja.iv.148.)

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span>’s wife, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span>, was one of the three persons possessing the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Latāpasādhana</span>, the others being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadāniyacora</span> (but see Dhp­a.i.412, where the daughter of Bārānasī <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span> is substituted for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadāniya</span>).

From the time of her husband’s death <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span> laid aside the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pasādhana</span>, but, on the day on which the Buddha’s body was being removed for cremation, she washed the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pasādhana</span> in perfumed water and placed it on the body, which it completely covered. She expressed the wish that, as long as she remained in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Saṁsāra</span>, her body should need no ornament. DN­a.ii.597.
