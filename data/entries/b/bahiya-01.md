# Bāhiya 01

## English source

Bāhiya 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhiya Dārucīriya</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhiya Dārucīriya</span>. An *Arahant*. He was born in the family of a householder of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhiya</span> – hence his name – and engaged himself in trade, voyaging in a ship (Ap.ii.476 says he was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhārukaccha</span>). Seven times he sailed down the Indus and across the sea and returned safely home. On the eighth occasion, while on his way to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suvaṇṇabhūmi</span>, his ship was wrecked, and he floated ashore on a plank, reaching land near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppāraka</span>. Having lost all his clothes, he made himself a bark garment, and went about, bowl in hand, for alms in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppāraka</span>. Men, seeing his garment and struck with his demeanour, paid him great honour. Though they offered him costly robes and many other luxuries, he refused them all and his fame increased. Because of his bark garment he was known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dārucīriya</span>. In due course he came himself to believe that he had attained *Arahant*-ship, but a *Devatā* (a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhāvāsa</span> *Brahmā*, who had been his fellow celibate in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>, says the Commentary, see below and also MN­a.i.340), reading his thoughts and wishing him well, pointed out to him his error and advised him to seek <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>. By the power of the *Devatā*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhiya</span> reached <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> in one night, a distance of one hundred and twenty leagues, and was told that the Buddha was in the city begging alms. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhiya</span> followed him thither and begged to be taught something for his salvation. Twice he asked and twice the Buddha refused, saying that it was not the hour for teaching. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhiya</span> insisted, saying that life was uncertain and that the Buddha or he might die.

The Commentaries say that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhiya</span> was excited by his meeting with the Buddha and that the Buddha wished to give him time to regain his calm, hence his refusal. The Buddha knew of his impending death and of his *upanissaya* (potential) for Arah*ant*-ship. He was a *pacchimabhavika* (someone in his last existence).

The Buddha then taught him the proper method of regarding all sense experiences – namely, as experiences and no more. Even as he listened, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhiya</span> became an *Arahant* and the Buddha left him. Shortly after, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhiya</span> was gored to death by a cow with calf (cp. the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span>). The Buddha, seeing his body lying on the dung heap, asked the monks to remove it and to have it burnt, erecting a *Thūpa* over the remains. In the assembly he declared <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhiya</span> to be foremost among those who instantly comprehended the Truth (*khippābhiññānaṁ*) (AN.i.24; Ud.i.10).

Bāhiya’s resolve to attain to this eminence was made in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> when he heard the Buddha declare a monk foremost in instantaneous comprehension. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>, when the Buddha’s teachings were fading from the minds of men, Bāhiya was one of seven monks who climbed a rock, determined not to leave it until they had attained their goal. Their leader became an *Arahant* and the second an *Anāgāmī* – passing into the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhāvāsa</span> world; the rest were reborn in this age as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumāra Kassapa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba Mallaputta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiya</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhiya</span>. Although Bāhiya had kept the precepts in previous births, he had never given a bowl or a robe to a monk. For this reason the Buddha did not, at the end of his sermon, ordain him by the “*ehibhikkhupabbajā*.” The Buddha knew that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhiya</span> had not sufficient merit to obtain divine robes. Some say that he was once a brigand and had shot a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> with an arrow and had taken possession of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>’s begging bowl and robe.

Bāhiya met his death while searching for a robe in which to be ordained (Ud­a.77ff.; AN­a.i.156ff.; Dhp­a.ii.209ff.; Ap.ii.475ff). The cow, which killed Bāhiya was identical with the one which killed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tambadāṭhika</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppabuddha</span> (for her story see Dhp­a.ii.35f).
