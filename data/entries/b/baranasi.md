# Bārāṇasī

## English source

Bārāṇasī (Benares). The capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">janapada</span>. It was one of the four places of pilgrimage for the Buddhists – the others being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhagayā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusināra</span> – because it was at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migadāya</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> near Bārāṇasī that the Buddha preached his first sermon to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcavaggiyā</span> (DN.ii.141). This was the spot at which all Buddhas set in motion the Wheel of the Dhamma (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Dhammacakka</span>). It is the custom of Buddhas to travel by air from the Bodhi-tree to the scene of their first sermon, a distance of eighteen leagues (MN­a.i.388; Bv.A.242, etc.), but the present Buddha did all the journey on foot in order to be able to meet on the way the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ājīvaka</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upaka</span>.

Benares was an important centre of trade and industry. There was direct trade between there and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> (Dhp­a.iii.429), the road passing through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span> (Vin.i.189), and between there and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> (Dhp­a.i.123). It was the custom for enthusiastic young men of Benares to go to the university at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> (e.g., Ja.ii.4; Dhp­a.i.250), but there seem to have been educational institutions at Benares also, some of which were older than even those of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> (Khp­a.198; see also Dhp­a.iii.445, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Susīma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkha</span>’s son, goes from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> to Benares for purposes of study).

From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verañjā</span> to Benares there seem to have been two routes: one rather circuitous, passing through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soreyya</span>, and the other direct, crossing the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Payāgatittha</span>. From Benares the road continued to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> (Vin-a.i.201). On the road from Benares to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhakavinda</span> (Vin.i.220). There seems to have been friendly intercourse between the chieftains of Benares and the kings of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, as shown by the fact that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> sent his own physician, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span>, to attend to the son of the Treasurer of Benares (Vin.i.275). The distance from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> to Benares was thirty leagues by river (MN­a.ii.929).

The extent of the city of Benares, including its suburbs, at the time when it was the capital of an independent kingdom, is often stated to have been twelve leagues (e.g., Ja.iv.377; vi.160; MN­a.ii.608). The names of several kings are mentioned in the Jātakas, among them being those of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uggasena</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udaya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kikī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sīlava</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissasena</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyama</span> (the Snp­a on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khaggavisāṇasutta</span> contains the names of several kings of Benares who renounced the world and became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>).

The name which occurs most frequently, however, is that of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span>, which seems to have been the dynastic name of the Benares kings. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Govindasutta</span>, the foundation of Bārāṇasī is attributed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Govinda</span>, its first king being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhataraṭṭha</span>, contemporary of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Reṇu</span> (DN.ii.235). The Ceylon Chronicles (Mhv­ṭ. 127,129,130) mention the names of others who reigned in Benares, e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duppasaha</span> and sixty of his descendants; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>, son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samaṅkara</span>, and eighty-four thousand of his descendants; also sixteen kings, ancestors of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Okkāka</span>. The city itself had been known by different names at different periods; thus, in the time of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udayajātaka</span> it was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Surundhana</span>; in that of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sutasoma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudassana</span>; in that of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇananda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmavaḍḍhana</span>; in that of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khaṇḍahāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pupphavatī</span>; in that of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yuvañjaya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rammanagara</span> (Ja.iv.119f); and in that of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Molinī</span> (Ja.iv.15). It was also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsinagara</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsipura</span> (e.g., Ja.v.54; vi.165; Dhp­a.i.87), being the capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhojājāniyajātaka</span> (Ja 23, Ja.i.178) says that “all the kings around coveted the kingdom of Benares.” In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahāchattajātaka</span> (Ja 336, Ja.iii.116), the king of Benares is mentioned as having captured the whole of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>. At the time of the Buddha, however, Benares had lost its great political importance. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> was already the paramount power in India, and several successful invasions of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span> by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosalans</span> under their kings <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabbasena</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṁsa</span>, are referred to. The final conquest would seem to be ascribed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṁsa</span> because the epithet <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bārāṇasiggaha</span> (conqueror of Benares) is an established addition to his name (Ja.ii.403).

Later, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> succeeded in establishing his sway over <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>, with the help of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsī</span>, too, was included in his kingdom. Even in the Buddha’s time the city of Benares was wealthy and prosperous and was included in the list of great cities suggested by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> as suitable places for the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Parinibbāna</span> of the Buddha (DN.ii.146).

Mention is also made of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Bārāṇasīseṭṭhi</span> (e.g., Dhp­a.i.412; iii. 87, 365) and a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Santhāgārasālā</span> (Mote Hall), which was then, however, no longer being used so much for the transaction of public business as for public discussions on religious and philosophical questions, e.g., Ja.iv.74; ascetics who came to the city found lodging for the night in the Potters’ Hall (e.g., Dhp­a.i.39).

Near Benares was a grove of seven <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sirīsaka</span>-trees where the Buddha preached to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Erakapatta</span> (Dhp­a.iii.230), and also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kemiyambavana</span> where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> met <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghoṭamukha</span> (MN.ii.158); on the other side of the river was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsabhagāma</span>, and beyond that another village called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cundaṭṭhila</span> (Pv-a.168).

The Buddha is several times spoken of as staying in Benares, where he preached several sermons (e.g., AN.i.110f., 279f.; iii.392ff., 399ff.; SN.i.105; v.406; Vin.i.189, 216f., 289) and converted many people including <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span>, whose home was in Benares (Vin.i.15), and his friends <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vimala</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subāhu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇajī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gavampati</span>, all members of eminent families (Vin.i.19). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) became a monastic centre in the Buddha’s time and continued so for long after. From there came twelve thousand monks under the leadership of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammasena</span> to be present at the ceremony of the foundation of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> (Mhv.xxix.31).

In the past, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bārāṇasī</span> was the birthplace of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> (Bv.xxv.33). In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Metteyya Buddha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bārāṇasī</span> will be known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ketumatī</span> at the head of eighty-four thousand towns. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkha</span> will be <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span> there, but he will renounce the world and will become an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Metteyya</span> (DN.iii.75f). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bārāṇasī</span> evidently derives its name from the fact that it lies between the two rivers Barṇā and Asi (CAGI.499f).
