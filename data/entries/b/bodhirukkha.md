# Bodhirukkha

## English source

Bodhirukkha. The generic name given to the tree under which a Buddha attains <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> (DN.A.ii.416). The tree is different in the case of each Buddha. Thus,

- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Gotama</span> and also for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Koṇḍañña</span> it was an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t23">asvattha</span>;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Dīpaṅkara</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kakusandha</span> a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t23">sirīsa</span>;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Maṅgala</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sumana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Revata</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sobhita</span> a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Nāga</span>;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Anomadassī</span> an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t23">ajjuna</span>;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Paduma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Nārada</span> a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t23">mahā soṇa</span>;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Padumuttara</span> a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t23">salala</span>;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sumedha</span> a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t23">nimba</span>;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sujāta</span> a bamboo;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Piyadassī</span> a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t23">kakudha</span>;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Atthadassī</span> a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t23">campaka</span>;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Dhammadassī</span> a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t23">bimbajāla</span>;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Siddhattha</span> a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t23">kaṇikāra</span>;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Tissa</span> an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t23">asana</span>;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Phussa</span> an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t23">āmaṇḍa</span>;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Vipassī</span> a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t23">pāṭalī</span>;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sikhī</span> a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t23">puṇḍarīka</span>;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Vessabhū</span> a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t23">sāla</span>;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Koṇāgamana</span> an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t23">udumbara</span>;
- for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kassapa</span> a banyan (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t23">passim</span>; the details differ somewhat at Bv­a.247).

The site of the Bodhi-tree is the same for all Buddhas (Bv­a.247), and it forms the navel of the earth (Ja.iv.233) (*puthuvinābhi*). No other place can support the weight of the Buddha’s attainment. Ja.iv.229.

When no Bodhi-tree grows, the Bodhimaṇḍa (ground round the Bodhi-tree), for a distance of one royal *karīsa*, is devoid of all plants, even of any blade of grass, and is quite smooth, spread with sand like a silver plate, while all around it are grass, creepers and trees. None can travel in the air immediately above it, not even <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> (Ja.iv.232f).

When the world is destroyed at the end of a *kappa*, the Bodhimaṇḍa is the last spot to disappear; when the world emerges into existence again, it is the first to appear. A lotus springs there bringing it into view and if during the *kappa* thus begun a Buddha will be born, the lotus puts forth flowers, according to the number of Buddhas (DN­a.ii.412).

In the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>, his Bodhi-tree sprang up on the day he was born (DN­a.ii.425; Bv­a.248). After his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>, he spent a whole week in front of it, standing with unblinking eyes, gazing at it with gratitude. A shrine was later erected on the spot where he so stood, and was called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Animisacetiya</span> (*q.v.*). The spot was used as a shrine even in the lifetime of the Buddha, the only shrine that could be so used. While the Buddha was yet alive, in order that people might make their offerings in the name of the Buddha when he was away on pilgrimage, he sanctioned the planting of a seed from the Bodhi-tree in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā</span> in front of the gateway of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>. For this purpose <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> took a fruit from a tree at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā</span> as it dropped from its stalk, before it reached the ground. It was planted in a golden jar by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span> with great pomp and ceremony. A sapling immediately sprouted forth, fifty cubits high, and in order to consecrate it the Buddha spent one night under it, wrapt in meditation. This tree, because it was planted under the direction of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, came to be known as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānandabodhi</span> (Ja.iv.228ff).

According to the Ceylon Chronicles (e.g., Mhv.xv), branches from the Bodhi-trees of all the Buddhas born during this *kappa* were planted in Ceylon on the spot where the sacred Bodhi-tree stands today in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>. The branch of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakusandha</span>’s tree was brought by a nun called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rucānandā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇāgamana</span>’s by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇṭakānandā</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kanakadattā</span>), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>’s by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhammā</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> was most diligent in paying homage to the Bodhi-tree, and held a festival every year in its honour in the month of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kattika</span> (Mhv.xvii.17). His queen, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissarakkhā</span> was jealous of the Tree, and three years after she became queen (i.e., in the nineteenth year of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s reign), she caused the tree to be killed by means of *maṇḍu*-thorns (Mhv.xx.4f). The tree, however, grew again, and a great monastery was attached to the Bodhimaṇḍa. Among those present at the foundation of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> are mentioned thirty thousand monks from this *Vihāra*, led by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittagutta</span> (Mhv.xxix.41).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kittisirimegha</span> of Ceylon, contemporary of Samudragupta, erected with the permission of Samudragupta, a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghārāma</span> near the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Bodhivihāra</span>, chiefly for the use of the Sinhalese monks who went to worship the Bodhi-tree. The circumstances in connection with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghārāma</span> are given by Xuanzang who gives a description of it as seen by himself (Beal-Xuan, op.cit., 133ff). It was probably here that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> met the Elder <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span> who persuaded him to come to Ceylon.

In the twelfth year of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s reign the right branch of the Bodhi-tree was brought by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghamittā</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> and placed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Meghavana</span>.

The Buddha, on his death bed, had resolved five things, one being that the branch which should be taken to Ceylon should detach itself (Mhv.xvii.46f). From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā</span>, the branch was taken to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span>, thence to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāmalittī</span>, where it was placed in a ship and taken to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambukola</span>, across the sea; finally it arrived at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>, staying on the way at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tivakka</span>. Those who assisted the king at the ceremony of the planting of the Tree were the nobles of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kājaragāma</span> and of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candanagāma</span> and of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tivakka</span>. From the seeds of a fruit which grew on the tree sprang eight saplings, which were planted respectively at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambukola</span>, in the village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tivakka</span>, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thūpārāmā</span>, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Issaramaṇārāma</span>, in the court of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭhamacetiya</span>, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyagiri</span>, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kājaragāma</span> and in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candanagāma</span> (Mhv.xix.60ff.; for details in connection with the bringing of the Bodhi-tree, see Mbv.144 ff).

Thirty-two other saplings, from four other fruits, were planted here and there at a distance of one *yojana*. Ceremonies were instituted in honour of the Tree, the supervision of which was given over to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bodhāhārakula</span>, at the head of which were the eight ministers of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> who, led by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bodhigutta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumitta</span>, were sent as escorts of the Tree (see Mbv.165f., for the names of the others). Revenues were provided for these celebrations.

Later, King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhātusena</span> built a *Bodhighara* or roof over the Tree (Cv.xxxviii.431) while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Silākāla</span> made daily offerings at the shrine (see Cv.Trs.i.32, n.6; Cv.xli.29), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kittisirimegha</span> had the *Bodhighara* covered with tin plates (Cv.xli.65). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāga</span> had the roof of the *Bodhighara* gilded, built a trench round the courtyard and set up Buddha images in the image house (Cv.xli.94). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggabodhi I</span>. erected a stone terrace round the Tree and placed, at the bottom of it, an oil pit to receive the oil for illuminations on festival days (Cv.xlii.19). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggabodhi II</span>. had a well dug for the use of pilgrims (Cv.xlii.66), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna III</span>. held a great celebration in the Tree’s honour (Cv.xliv.45).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggabodhi VII</span>. found the *Bodhighara* in ruins and had it rebuilt (Cv.xlviii.70); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda II</span>. instituted a regular offering in its honour (Cv.xlviii.124), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udaya III</span>. gave a village near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> to the service of the Bodhi-tree. Cv.liii.10.
