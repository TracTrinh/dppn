# Bhaddaji Thera

## English source

Bhaddaji Thera. The son of a *seṭṭhi* in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span>. He was worth eighty crores (800 million), and was brought up in luxury like that of the Bodhisatta in his last birth. When Bhaddaji was grown up, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> came to Bhaddiya to seek him out, and stayed at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātiyāvana</span> with a large number of monks. Thither Bhaddaji went to hear him preach. He became an *Arahant*, and, with his father’s consent, was ordained by the Buddha. Seven weeks later he accompanied the Buddha to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṭigāma</span>, and, while the Buddha was returning thanks to a pious donor on the way, Bhaddaji retired to the bank of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span> outside the village, where he stood wrapt in *jhāna*, emerging only when the Buddha came by, not having heeded the preceding chief Theras. He was blamed for this; but, in order to demonstrate the attainments of Bhaddaji, the Buddha invited him to his own ferry boat and bade him work a wonder. Bhaddaji thereupon raised from the river bed, fifteen leagues into the air, a golden palace twenty leagues high, in which he had lived as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Panāda</span>. On this occasion the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Panāda</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Surucijātaka</span> (Ja 489) was preached.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsa</span> account (xxxi.37ff) says that, before raising <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Panāda</span>’s palace, Bhaddaji rose into the air to the height of seven palmyra trees, holding the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dussa Thūpa</span> from the *Brahma* world in his hand. He then dived into the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span> and returned with the palace. The Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanduttara</span>, whose hospitality the Buddha and his monks had accepted, saw this miracle of Bhaddaji, and himself wished for similar power by which he might procure relics in the possession of others. He was reborn as the novice <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇuttara</span>, who obtained the relics for the *Thūpas* of Ceylon.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>, Bhaddaji was a Brahmin ascetic who, seeing the Buddha travelling through the air, offered him honey, lotus stalks, etc. Soon after he was struck by lightning and reborn in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span>. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span> he was a very rich *seṭṭhi* and fed sixty-eight thousand monks, to each of whom he gave three robes. Later, he ministered to five hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>. In a subsequent birth his son was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>, and he looked after him and built a *cetiya* over his remains after his death. Thag.vs.163f.; Thag­a.i.285ff.; also Ja.ii.331ff., where the details vary slightly; Ja.iv.325; also Mhv­ṭ.560f.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddaji</span> is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāradakassapajātaka</span> (Ja 544, Ja.vi.255).

He is probably identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhisadāyaka</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (Ap.ii.420f). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddaji</span> is mentioned among those who handed down the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span> to the Third Council (Dhs-a.32). See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddajisutta</span>.
