# Bimbisāra

## English source

Bimbisāra. King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> and patron of the Buddha. He ascended the throne at the age of fifteen and reigned in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> for fifty-two years. The Buddha was five years older than Bimbisāra, and it was not until fifteen years after his accession that Bimbisāra heard the Buddha preach and was converted by him. It is said that the two were friends in their youth owing to the friendship which existed between their fathers (Mhv.ii.25ff.; Dpv.iii.50ff). Bimbisāra’s father was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhāti</span> (Mhv­ṭ.137; Dpv.iii.52); according to Tibetan sources he was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Paduma</span> and his Mother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbī</span> (Rockhill, op.cit., 16).

But according to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pabbajāsutta</span> the first meeting between the Buddha and Bimbisāra took place in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> under the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍava</span> *pabbata*, only after the Buddha’s Renunciation (Snp.vs.405ff.; also Ja.i.66 and Dhp­a.i.85; also Rockhill, p. 27). The king, seeing the young ascetic pass below the palace windows, sent messengers after him. On learning, that he was resting after his meal, Bimbisāra followed him and offered him a place in his court. This the Buddha refused, revealing his identity. The Commentary adds that Bimbisāra wished him success in his quest and asked him to visit first <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> as soon as he had attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> (Snp­a.ii.386). It was in fulfilment of this promise that the Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> immediately after his conversion of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tebhātikajaṭilā</span>. He stayed at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppatiṭṭha</span> *cetiya* in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Laṭṭhivanuyyāna</span>, whither Bimbisāra, accompanied by twelve *nahutas* of householders, went to pay to him his respects. The Buddha preached to them, and eleven *nahutas*, with Bimbisāra at their head, became *Sotāpannas*. On the following day the Buddha and his large retinue of monks accepted the hospitality of Bimbisāra. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, in the guise of a young man, preceded them to the palace, singing songs of glory of the Buddha. At the conclusion of the meal, Bimbisāra poured water from a golden jar on the Buddha’s hand and dedicated <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span> for the use of him and of his monks (Vin.i.35ff).

It was this gift of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span>, which formed the model for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span>’s gift of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Meghavana</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> (Mhv.xv.17). The gift of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span> was one of the incidents sculptured in the Relic chamber of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> (Mhv.xxx.80). It may have been in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span> that the king built for the monks a storeyed house, fully plastered (Vin.ii.154). With the attainment of *Sotāpatti*, the king declared that all the five ambitions of his life had been fulfilled: that he might become king, that the Buddha might visit his realm, that he might wait on the Buddha, that the Buddha might teach him the doctrine, that he might understand it (Vin.i.36). The king became a *Sotāpanna* after listening to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāradajātaka</span> according to Bv­a.p.18f.

From this moment up till the time of his death, a period of thirty-seven years, Bimbisāra did all in his power to help the new religion and to further its growth. He set an example to his subjects in the practice of the precepts by taking the *uposatha* vows on six days, of each month (Pv-a.209).

Bimbisāra’s chief queen was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosaladevī</span> (*q.v.*), daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kosala</span> and sister of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>. On the day of her marriage she received, as part of her dowry, a village in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span>, for her bath money. Her son was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> (also Ja.iii.121). Bimbisāra had other wives as well; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemā</span>, who, at first, would not even visit the Buddha till enticed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>’s descriptions of the beauties of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span>; and the courtesan <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumavatī</span>, who was brought from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>, with the help of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>, so that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> might not lack a *Nagarasobhinī*. Both these later became nuns. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumavatī</span>’s son was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> had another son by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambapālī</span>, known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vimala Koṇḍañña</span>, and two others, by different wives, known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīlava</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayasena</span>. A daughter, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cundī</span>, is also mentioned.

Bimbisāra’s death, according to the Commentaries, was a sad one (e.g., DN­a.i.135 ff.; see also Vin.ii.190f). Soothsayers had predicted, before the birth of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, that he would bring about the death of his father, for which reason his mother had wished to bring about an abortion. But Bimbisāra would not hear of this, and when the boy was born, treated him with the greatest affection (for details see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>). When the prince came of age, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>, by an exhibition of his *iddhi*-power, won him over to his side and persuaded him to encompass the death of his father, Bimbisāra’s patronage of the Buddha being the greatest obstacle in the path of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>. The plot was discovered, and Bimbisāra’s ministers advised him to kill <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> and their associates. But Bimbisāra sent for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> and, on hearing that he desired power, abdicated in his favour. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> chided <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> for a fool. “You are like a man who puts a skin over a drum in which is a rat,” and he urged on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> the need for the destruction of Bimbisāra.

But no weapon could injure Bimbisāra (probably because he was a *Sotāpanna*, he also had the power of judging the status of anyone by his voice – e.g., in the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumbhaghosa</span>, Dhp­a.i.233), it was therefore decided that he should be starved to death, and with this end in view he was imprisoned in a hot house (*tāpanageha*) with orders that none but the mother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> should visit him. On her visits she took with her a golden vessel filled with food which she concealed in her clothes. When this was discovered she took food in her head dress (*molī*), and, later, she was obliged to take what food she could conceal in her footgear. But all these ways were discovered, and then the queen visited Bimbisāra after having bathed in scented water and smeared her person with *catumadhura* (the four kinds of sweets). The king licked her person and that was his only sustenance. In the end the visits of the queen were forbidden; but the king continued to live by walking about his cell meditating. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, hearing of this, sent barbers to cut open his feet, fill the wounds with salt and vinegar, and burn them with coals. It is said that when the barbers appeared Bimbisāra thought his son had relented and had sent them to shave him and cut his hair. But on learning their real purpose, he showed not the least resentment and let them do their work, much against their will (in a previous birth he had walked about in the courtyard of a *cetiya* with shoes on, hence this punishment)! Soon after, Bimbisāra died, and was reborn in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cātummahārājika</span> world as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janavasabha</span>, in the retinue of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janavasabhasutta</span> records an account of a visit paid by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janavasabha</span> to the Buddha some time after.

A son was born to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> on the day of Bimbisāra’s death. The joy be experienced at the birth of his son made him realize something of the affection his own father must have felt for him, and he questioned his mother. She told him stories of his childhood, and he repented, rather belatedly, of his folly and cruelty. Soon after, his mother died of grief, and her death gave rise to the protracted war between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, as mentioned elsewhere (Ja.ii.237, 403).

The books contain no mention of any special sermons preached by the Buddha to Bimbisāra nor of any questions asked by him of the Buddha. When he heard that the Buddha intended to perform a miracle, although he had ordered his disciples to refrain from doing so, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> had doubts about the propriety of this and questioned the Buddha who set his doubts at rest (Dhp­a.iii.204; Ja.iii.263f.). It was also at the request of Bimbisāra that the Buddha established the custom of the monks assembling on the first, eighth, and either the fourteenth or fifteenth days of each month (Vin.i.101f.).

Perhaps, like <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>, his equal in devotion to the Buddha, he refrained from giving the Buddha extra trouble, or perhaps the affairs of his kingdom, which was three hundred leagues in extent, did not permit him enough leisure for frequent visits to the Buddha (Dhp­a.iii.205; the kingdom included eighty thousand villages, *gāma*, Vin.i.179).

It is said that he once visited four monks – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godhika</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subāhu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Valliya</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttiya</span> – and invited them to spend the rainy season at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. He built for them four huts, but forgot to have them roofed, with the result that the gods withheld the rains until the king remembered the omission (Thag­a.i.125). He similarly forgot his promise to give <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pilindavaccha</span> a parkkeeper, if the Buddha would sanction such a gift. Five hundred days later he remembered his promise and to make amends, gave five hundred parkkeepers with a special village for their residence, called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ārāmikagāma</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pilindagāma</span> (Vin.i.207f.).

Bimbisāra’s affection for the Buddha was unbounded. When the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāli</span>, who was a member of Bimbisāra’s retinue, to beg the Buddha to visit <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, Bimbisāra did not himself try to persuade the Buddha to do so, but when the Buddha agreed to go he repaired the whole road from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span> – a distance of five leagues – for the Buddha to walk upon; he erected a rest house at the end of each league, and spread flowers of five different colours knee deep along the whole way. Two parasols were provided for the Buddha and one for each monk. The king himself accompanied the Buddha in order to look after him, offering him flowers and perfume and all requisites throughout the journey, which lasted five days. Arrived at the river, he fastened two boats together decked with flowers and jewels and followed the Buddha’s boat into the water up to his neck. When the Buddha had gone, the king set up an encampment on the river bank, awaiting his return; he then escorted him back to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> with similar pomp and ceremony (Dhp­a.iii.438 ff).

Great cordiality existed between Bimbisāra and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>. They were connected by marriage, each having married a sister of the other. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> once visited Bimbisāra in order to obtain from him a person of unbounded wealth (*amitabhoga*) for his kingdom. Bimbisāra had five such – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jaṭila</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meṇḍaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kākavaliya</span>; but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> had none. The request was granted, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meṇḍaka</span>’s son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya</span>, was sent back to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> (Dhp­a.i.385f.; AN­a.i.220). Some of these were richer than Bimbisāra, e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotiya</span> (*q.v.*), whose house was built entirely of jewels while the king’s palace was of wood; but the king showed no jealousy (Dhp­a.iv.211).

Bimbisāra also maintained friendly relations with other kings, such as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍappajjota</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>, to whom he sent his own physician <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span> to tend in his illness – and Rudrāyaṇa of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Roruka</span> (Divy.545).

Among the ministers and personal retinue of Bimbisāra are mentioned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa Koḷivisa</span>, the flower-gatherer <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span> who supplied the king with eight measures of jasmine flowers, the minister <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiya</span>, the treasurer <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumbhaghosaka</span> and his physician <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span>. The last named was discovered for him by the prince <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span> when he was suffering from a fistula. The king’s garments were stained with blood and his queens mocked him. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span> cured the king with one single anointing; the king offered him the ornaments of the five hundred women of the palace, and when he refused to take these, he was appointed physician to the king, the women of the seraglio and the fraternity of monks under the Buddha (Vin.i.272f).

When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammadinnā</span> wished to leave the world, Bimbisāra gave her, at her husband’s request, a golden palanquin and allowed her to go round the city in procession (MN­a.i.516).

Bimbisāra is generally referred to as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Seniya Bimbisāra</span>. The Commentaries explain <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Seniya</span> as meaning “possessed of a large following” or as “belonging to the *Seniyagotta*,” and Bimbisāra as meaning “of a golden colour,” *bimbī* meaning gold (e.g., Ud­a.104). According to Tibetan sources, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbī</span> was the name of his mother, and from this his own name was derived; but another reason was that he was radiant like the morning sun (Rockhill 16, see also MN­a.i.292).

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phussa Buddha</span>, when the Buddha’s three step-brothers, sons of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayasena</span>, obtained their father’s leave to entertain the Buddha for three months, Bimbisāra, then head of a certain district, looked after all the arrangements. His associates in this task were born as *Petas*, and he gave alms to the Buddha in their name in order to relieve their sufferings.

See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tirokuḍḍasutta</span>, also Pv-a.21ff.; for his intercession on behalf of another *peta*. see Pv-a.89.

During his lifetime, Bimbisāra was considered the happiest of men, but the Buddha declared (e.g., MN.i.95) that he himself was far happier than the king.

The *kahāpaṇa* in use in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> during Bimbisāra’s time was the standard of money adopted by the Buddha in the formation of those rules into which the matter of money entered (Vin-a.ii.297).

Bimbisāra had a white banner and one of his epithets was Paṇḍaraketu (Thag.vs.64; Thag­a.i.147). Nothing is said about his future destiny, but he is represented in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janavasabhasutta</span> as expressing the wish to become a *Sakadāgāmī*, and this wish may have been fulfilled (DN.ii.206).
