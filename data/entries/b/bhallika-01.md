# Bhallika 01

## English source

Bhallika 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhallika Thera</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhalliya Thera</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhalluka Thera</span>. The younger brother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tapassu</span>, their father being a caravan leader of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pokkharavatī</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkalā</span> (AN­a.i.207 calls their birthplace <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asitañjana</span>). While they were going along with five hundred trading carts, these stopped near the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājāyatana</span> tree where the Buddha was sitting, eight weeks after his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>. When they investigated the cause for their carts thus stopping, a deity, their kinsman in a former life (their mother according to AN­a.i.207), pointed out to them the Buddha and asked them to give him a meal as he had eaten nothing for seven weeks. Not waiting to cook, the merchants gave the Buddha some butter and honey in a bowl provided by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Four Regent Gods</span>. At the end of the meal the Buddha talked to them. They accepted the Buddha and the Dhamma as their Refuge (the *Dvevācikasaraṇa*, they thus became the Buddha’s first lay disciples; AN.i.26), and obtained from the Buddha a few hairs as an object of worship. Later, when the Buddha was in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> after the preaching of his First Sermon, the merchants visited him and listened to his preaching. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tapassu</span> became a *Sotāpanna*, and Bhallika entered the Saṅgha and became an *Arahant*.

In the past, Bhallika had given fruit to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span>. During this life of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sikhī Buddha</span>, he was a Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aruṇavatī</span>, and hearing that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujita</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ojita</span> had given the Buddha his first meal, he and his friend invited the Buddha to eat at their house, and resolved to win a similar distinction for themselves in the future. They were herdsmen in the life of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>, and for many years supplied milk-rice to the Buddha and the monks (Thag­a.i.48f.; AN­a.i.207f.; Vin.i.3f.; Ja.i.80. Mhv.iii.303f).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> (vs.7), contains a verse spoken by Bhallika when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> tried to frighten him by assuming a hideous form.

It is said that the hair (eight handfuls) given by the Buddha was deposited in a *cetiya* in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asitañjana</span> and that on fast days blue rays shone from the *cetiya*. AN­a.i.208. There is a tradition in Ceylon (recorded in the *Pūjāvaliya*) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tapassu</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhalluka</span> visited the east coast of Ceylon and built a *cetiya*, there. An inscription makes a similar record.
