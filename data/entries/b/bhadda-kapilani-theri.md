# Bhaddā Kapilānī Therī

## English source

Bhaddā Kapilānī Therī. The daughter of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosiyagotta</span> Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgala</span>, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span> country. Ap.ii.583 (vs. 57) says that her mother was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sucīmatī</span> and her father <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapila</span>, whence, probably, her title of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāpilānī</span>. When the messengers sent by the parents of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pipphali</span>-*māṇava* (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span>) were wandering about seeking for a wife for him to resemble the image they carried with them, they discovered <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā</span> and informed Pipphali’s parents. The parents arranged the marriage without the knowledge of the young people and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā</span> went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pipphali</span>’s house. There they lived together, but, by mutual consent, the marriage was never consummated. It was said that, she brought with her, on the day of her marriage, fifty thousand cartloads of wealth. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pipphali</span> desired to leave the world, making over to her his wealth, she wished to renounce it likewise, and together they left the house in the guise of recluses, their hair shorn, unobserved by any. In the village, however, they were recognized by their gait, and the people fell down at their feet. They granted freedom to all their slaves, and set forth, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pipphali</span> leading and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā</span> following close behind. On coming to a fork in the road, they agreed that he should take the right and she the left. In due course she came to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Titthiyārāma</span> (near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>), where she dwelt for five years, women not having yet been admitted to the Buddha’s Saṅgha. Later, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī Gotamī</span> had obtained the necessary leave, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā</span> joined her and received ordination, attaining *Arahant*-ship not long after. Later in the assembly, the Buddha declared her foremost of nuns who could recall former lives.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> she was the wife of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span>, a *seṭṭhi* of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsavatī</span>, and having heard a nun proclaimed in the first rank of those who could recall former lives, she resolved to acquire a similar rank, while her husband (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> in this life) resolved to be chief among those who practise austere vows (*dhutavādinaṁ*). Together they did many good deeds and were reborn in heaven.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span>, the husband was the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekasāṭaka</span> and she was his wife. In his next birth he was king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> and she his chief queen. Together they entertained eight <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> on a very lavish scale. In the interval between the appearance in the world of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇāgamana Buddha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>, the husband was a clansman and she his wife. One day a quarrel arose between her and her sister-in law. The latter gave alms to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> and Bhaddā, thinking, “She will win glory for this,” took the bowl from her hand and filled it with mud. But later she was filled with remorse, took back the bowl, emptied it, scrubbed it with scented powder and, having filled it with the four sweet foods, sprinkled over the top ghee of the colour of a lotus calyx. Handing it back to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>, she prayed to herself “May I have a shining body like this offering.”

In a later birth, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā</span> was born as the daughter of a wealthy treasurer of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>; she was given in marriage, but her body was of such evil odour that she was repulsive to all and was abandoned by several husbands. Much troubled, she had her ornaments made into an ingot of gold and placed it on the shrine of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>, which was in process of being built, and did reverence to it with her hands full of lotuses. Her body immediately became fragrant and sweet, and she was married again to her first husband. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> account mentions two other lives: one when she was the wife of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumitta</span> and gave a blanket to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>, and again when she was born among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyans</span> and attended on one hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiya</span>.

Later, she was the queen of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span>, according to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>, which gives King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span> as the name of her husband in another life), with whom she ministered to five hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>, sons of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumavatī</span>. When they passed away she was greatly troubled and left the world to give herself up to ascetic practices. She dwelt in a grove, developed *jhāna*, and was reborn in the *Brahma* world. (Thīg­a.67ff.; Ap.ii.578ff.; AN­a.ii.93ff., 203f.; AN.i.25; Thīg.vs.63-6).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā Kāpilānī</span>’s name is mentioned several times in the Vinaya rules in connection with her pupils who were found guilty of transgressing them (e.g., Vin.iv.227, 268, 269, etc.). She and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thullanandā</span> were both famous as preachers, and the latter, being jealous of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā</span>, went out of her way to insult her (Vin.iv.290). Once <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā</span> sent word to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span> asking <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thullanandā</span> if she could find her a lodging in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandā</span> agreed to do this, but made things very unpleasant for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā</span> when she arrived (Vin.iv.292).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā Kāpilānī</span> is identified with the Brahmin woman in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthipālajātaka</span> (Ja 509, Ja.iv.491) and with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāma</span>’s mother in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmajātaka</span> (Ja 540, Ja.vi.95).
