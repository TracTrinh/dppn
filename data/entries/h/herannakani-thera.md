# Heraññakāni Thera

## English source

Heraññakāni Thera. He was the son of an official of the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> who turned bandit. On his father’s death he succeeded to his place. He was present when the Buddha accepted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, and, filled with wonder, he entered the Saṅgha, where he soon attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship. Later he persuaded his brother too, with some difficulty, to become a monk.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> he was a labourer (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">bhataka</span>), and seeing the Buddha’s disciple, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujāta</span>, looking for rags for his robe, gave him half a garment (Thag.vs.145-6; Thag­a.i.266f).

He is evidently identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upaḍḍhadussadāyaka</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>. Ap.ii.435f.
