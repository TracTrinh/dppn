# Himavā

## English source

Himavā, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya, Himācala</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavanta</span>, etc. The name given to the Himālayas. It is one of the seven mountain ranges surrounding <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhamādana</span> (Snp­a.i.66). It is three hundred thousand leagues in extent (Snp­a.i.224), with eighty-four thousand peaks, its highest peak being five hundred *yojanas* (Snp­a.ii.443). In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span>, are seven great lakes, each fifty leagues in length, breadth and depth – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anotatta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇṇamuṇḍa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rathakāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chaddanta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mandākinī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīhappapātaka</span>; these lakes are never heated by the sun (AN.iv.101; Snp­a.ii.407; cf. AN­a.ii.759). From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span> flow five hundred rivers. Snp­a.ii.437; but according to Mil.114, only ten of these are to be reckoned, the others flowing only intermittently. These ten are: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamunā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aciravatī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhū</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sindhu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarassatī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vettavatī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vītaṁsā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candabhāgā</span>.

In numerous Jātakas <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span> is mentioned as the place to which ascetics retire when they leave household life. It is full of woodlands and groves, suitable for hermits (e.g., SN­a.i.265). In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span> is a peak named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Papāta</span> where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> die (Snp­a.i.129). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> go to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span> to give birth to their young (SN­a.iii.120; cf. SN.v.63). The mountain is often used in similes; it is then referred to as *pabbatarājā* (e.g., SN.ii.137; v.464; AN.iii.311; MN.iii.166, etc.). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalī Thera</span> once went there from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> with five hundred others. The journey took them eight days (details are given at Thag­a.ii.138; Paṭis-a.252).

The country round <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span> was converted by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhima Thera</span> (Mhv.xii.41). He was accompanied by four others: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapagotta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mūladeva</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Alakadeva</span>), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahadeva</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dundubhissara</span> (Dpv.viii.10; Mhv­ṭ.317). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhima</span> preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammacakkappavattanasutta</span> and eighty crores attained salvation. These five Theras converted five kingdoms and each ordained one hundred thousand persons (Mhv.xii.42f).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> brought for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s use, from the Himālaya, twigs of the *Nāgalatā* to clean his teeth, healthful fruits, myrobalan, teminalia and mango fruit (Mhv.v.25f), while, for the foundation of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span>, *sāmaṇeras* with *iddhi*-power brought sweet scented *marumba* (Mhv.xxix.9).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇālajātaka</span> (Ja 536, *q.v.*) was preached in the region of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span>. The Buddha took the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> princes there and showed them the various features, including many mountain peaks, such as: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇipabbata</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hiṅgulapabbata</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Añjanapabbata</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sānupabbata</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phalikapabbata</span> (Ja.v.415). On fast days the gods assemble in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span> and hold discourses. Vin-a.iv.759.
