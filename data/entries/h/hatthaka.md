# Hatthaka

## English source

Hatthaka, called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthakāḷavaka</span> An eminent lay disciple of the Buddha declared foremost among those who gather a following by means of the four bases of sympathy (*catūhi vatthūhi parisaṁ saṅgaṇhantānaṁ*) (AN.i.26). He was the son of the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ālavī</span> (hence his name <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span>), and the Buddha saved him from being eaten by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span>. He was given the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthaka</span> because he was handed to the Buddha by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>, after the latter’s conversion, and by the Buddha to the king’s messengers. He was thus “handed” from one to another (*hatthato hatthaṁ gatattā*) (AN­a.i.212; Snp­a.i.240).

When he grew up <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthaka</span> heard the Buddha preach, and, in due course, became an *Anāgāmī*. He was always accompanied by five hundred lay disciples, and was one of seven laymen who had such a following (SN­a.iii.223). The books record several conversations between the Buddha and Hatthaka. He once saw the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gomagga</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siṁsapavana</span>, near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ālavī</span>, and asked him if he were one of those who lived happily. The Buddha said he was always happy in any circumstances (AN.i.136f). On another occasion the Buddha asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthaka</span> how he could command the allegiance of such a large company. “By the four bases of sympathy,” he answered, by giving gifts, by kindly words, by kindly deeds, by equality of treatment. And when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthaka</span> had gone, the Buddha praised him for his eminence, in that he possessed eight marvellous qualities: faith, virtue, conscientiousness, fear of blame, ability to listen well, charity, wisdom, modesty (AN.iv.218f; his modesty is especially mentioned elsewhere also e.g., at AN.iv.216). Together with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittagahapati</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthaka</span> is often held up as an example to be copied by others (e.g., at SN.ii.235; AN.i.88; ii.164; iii.451, etc.).

After death, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthaka</span> was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avihā</span>, there to pass away entirely. From there he once visited the Buddha and tried to stand in his presence, but collapsed and could not remain upright. The Buddha then asked him to create a gross body form, and when he did this he was able to stand. He told the Buddha that he was constantly surrounded by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> wishing to learn the Dhamma from him, and confessed that he had died regretting three things – of not having seen enough of the Buddha, of not heard enough of the Dhamma, of not served enough the Saṅgha (AN.i.278f). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhavaṁsa</span> (Bv.xxvi.19), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthakāḷavaka</span> are mentioned as the chief lay patrons (*aggupaṭṭhākā*) of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>.
