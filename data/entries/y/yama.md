# Yama

## English source

Yama. The god of death (see, e.g., Dhp­a.iii.337; *Yamassa santikaṁ = Maraṇasantikaṁ*). When beings die they are led before him to be judged according to their deeds. Birth, old age, illness, punishment for crime and death, are regarded as his messengers, sent among men as a warning to abstain from ill and do good. Yama questions beings brought before him as to whether they have seen these messengers and profited by them. If the answer is in the negative, the *nirayapālas* take them away to the different hells (MN.iii.179ff).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Samayasutta</span> (DN.ii.259) mention is made of two Yamas (*duve Yamā*), which the Commentary explains (DN­a.ii.690) by “*dve Yamakadevatā*” (the twins, whom Rhys Davids calls the Castor and Pollux of Indian Mythology, in Dial.ii.290, n.1). Elsewhere (AN­a.i.374; MN­a.ii.953) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> speaks of four Yamas (*na cesa eko va hoti, catusu pana dvāresu cattāro janā honti*) at the four gates (of the *Nirayas*?). He says that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yama</span> is a *Vemānikapetarājā*, who sometimes enjoys all the pleasures of heaven, in a celestial mansion, surrounded by *kapparukkhas*, and at other times experiences the fruits of his *kamma*. He is a good king.

In the Jātakas the *Nirayas* are particularly mentioned as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yama</span>’s abode (*Yamakkhaya*, *Yamanivesana*, *Yamasādana*, etc.), e.g., Ja.ii.318; iv.273; v.268, 274, 304. The Vetaraṇī is mentioned as forming the boundary of Yama’s kingdom (Ja.i.21; Ja.ii.317; iii.472; but see ii.318). At Ja.iv.405, Yama’s abode is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ussadaniraya</span>. Dhp­a.i.334 explains <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamaloka</span> by *Catubbidhaṁ apāyalokaṁ*. Cp. Pv-a.33 (*Yamaloko ti petaloko*); Pv-a.107 (*Yamavisayaṁ = Petalokaṁ*). But, more generally, all *Saṁsāra* is considered as subject to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yama</span>’s rule, and escape from *Saṁsāra* means escape from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yama</span>’s influence, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yama</span> being the god of Death. It is evidently in this sense that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yama</span> is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesāyi</span> (*q.v.*, Ja.ii.317, 318).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yama</span> is sometimes mentioned with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Varuṇa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span>, etc., as a god to whom sacrifices are offered (e.g., Ja.vi.201; DN.i.244; at Mil.37 the list includes <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuvera</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suyāma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Santusita</span>; cp. *Mahā Vastu* Mvu.i.265; Mvu.iii.68, 77; 77, 307). There is a tradition (AN.i.142) that once <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yama</span> longed to be born as a human being and to sit at the feet of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tathāgata</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yama</span>’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nayanāyudha</span> is mentioned (Snp­a.i.225) among the most destructive of weapons.
