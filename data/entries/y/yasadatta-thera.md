# Yasadatta Thera

## English source

Yasadatta Thera. He belonged to a family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malla</span> chieftains and was educated at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span>, where he attained great proficiency. Later, while journeying in the company of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiya</span>, he came to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, where he was present at the discussion between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiya</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>. It was his purpose to try and discover flaws in the Buddha’s argument. The Buddha knew what was in his mind, and at the end of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiyasutta</span> admonished him in five verses (Thag.360-4). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasadatta</span> was greatly moved and entered the Saṅgha, winning *Arahant*-ship in due course.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span> Buddha, he had been a very learned Brahmin, living as an ascetic in the forest. One day he saw the Buddha, and, with clasped hands, praised his virtues (Thag.i.427f). He is evidently identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ñāṇathavika</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>. (Ap.ii.392f)
