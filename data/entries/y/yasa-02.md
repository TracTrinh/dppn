# Yasa 02

## English source

Yasa 02. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa Kākaṇḍakaputta</span>. He was the son of the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kākaṇḍaka</span> and was a pupil of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>. It is said he was fortunate enough to see the Buddha alive (Mhv.iv.57f). When he arrived at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kūṭāgārasālā</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vana</span>, he discovered that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjī</span> monks had raised the “Ten Points” (*dasavatthu*) contrary to the Buddha’s teachings, and that they were publicly asking for money from their lay disciples. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span> thereupon protested against such misdemeanours, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjī</span> monks, hoping to win him over, offered him a share of the money they had collected. This offer he rejected with scorn, and the monks passed on him the *Paṭisāraṇīyakamma* (craving of pardon from lay folk). This necessitated that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span> should be sent among the laymen, accompanied by a messenger, presumably to ask their pardon for having misinformed them. But instead of this, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span> told the lay people that the behaviour of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjī</span> monks was completely at variance with the rules laid down by the Buddha, and quoted the Buddha’s discourses to prove his charge.

When the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjī</span> monks heard of this, they pronounced on him the *Ukkhepaniyakamma* (Act of Suspension), but when they assembled to carry it out, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span> disappeared through the air to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>, from where he sent messengers to the monks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>, of the west (*Pāṭheyyakā* or *Pāveyyakā*) and of the south (*Dakkhiṇāpatha*), asking for their assistance in checking the corruption of the religion. With them he visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambhūta</span> *Sānavāsī* at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ahogaṅgapabbata</span>, and there they decided to consult <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span> who lived in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soreyya</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span>, therefore, went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span>, following him through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇṇakujja</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udumbara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggaḷapura</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahajāti</span>. Having found <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span>, he questioned him regarding the ten points, and obtained from him promise of assistance.

Together they returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, where lived <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabbakāmī</span>, the oldest Thera of the day. After obtaining his opinion on the matter, an assembly of the monks was held and a committee was appointed (to settle the matter by an *Ubbāhikā*) of four from the East: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabbakāmī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāḷha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khujjasobhita</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsabhagāmika</span>; and four from the West: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambhūta</span> *Sānavāsī*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span>. They debated the question at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vālikārāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span> acting as questioner and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabbakāmī</span> answering his questions. At the end of the enquiry the decision was given against the ten points of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjī</span> monks, and this decision was conveyed to the assembly. Then the recital of the Vinaya was held in which seven hundred monks participated; this recital was called the *Sattasatī*. Vin.ii.294ff.; Mhv.iv.9ff.; Dpv.iv.45ff.; Dpv.v.23. The Mhv. says that at first the king (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷāsoka</span>) was inclined to support the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjians</span>, but his sister, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandā</span> Therī, warned him against this (Mhv.iv. 37ff.).

The monks who refused to accept the findings of the committee held another convocation, which was called the *Mahā Saṅgīti* (Dpv.v.30ff.). The *Sattasatī* Recital (Recital of the Seven Hundred, also called The Second Recital) was also named (e.g., AN­a.i.251; MN­a.ii.880) *Yasatherasaṅgīti*, evidently because of the prominent part played by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span> is ranked (See, e.g., DN­a.ii.525) among the great benefactors of the religion.
