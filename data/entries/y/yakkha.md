# Yakkha

## English source

Yakkha, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span>. A class of non-human beings. They are mentioned with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, *Rakkhasas*, *Dānavas*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhabbas</span>, *Kinnaras*, and *Mahoragas* (? <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span>) (e.g., Ja.v.420). In other lists (e.g., Pv-a. 45, 55) they range immediately above the *Petas*; in fact, some of the happier *Petas* are called Yakkhas. Elsewhere (e.g., AN.ii.38) they rank, in progressive order, between *manussā* and *gandhabbā*. They are of many different kinds: spirits, ogres, dryads, ghosts, spooks. In the early records, Yakkha, like <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgā</span>, as an appellative, was anything but depreciative. Thus not only is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, king of the gods, so referred to (MN.i.252; Ja.iv.4; DN­a.i.264), but even the Buddha is spoken of as a Yakkha in poetic diction (MN.i.386). Many gods, such as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakudha</span>, are so addressed (SN.i.54).

According to a passage in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vimānavatthu</span> Commentary (Vv-a.333), which gives illustrations, the term is used for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Four Regent Gods</span> (*Mahā Rājāno*), the followers of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span>, and also for *puriso* (individual soul?). In the scholiast to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayaddisajātaka</span> (Ja 513, Ja.v.33), the figure of the hare in the moon is also called Yakkha. Of these above named, the followers of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> appear to be the Yakkhas proper. The term Yakkha as applied to *purisa* is evidently used in an exceptionally philosophical sense as meaning “soul” in such passages as *ettāvatā yakkhassa suddhi* (Snp.vs.478), or *ettāvat’ aggaṁ no vadanti hekā, yakkhassa suddhiṁ idha paṇḍitāse* (Snp.vs.875).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Niddesa</span> (MNid.282), Yakkha is explained by *satta*, *nara*, *māṇava*, *posa*, *puggala*, *jīva*, *jagu*, *jantu*, *indagu*, *manuja*. The last term is significant as showing that Yakkha also means “man.”

The cult of Yakkhas seems to have arisen primarily from the woods and secondarily from the legends of sea-faring merchants. To the latter origin belong the stories connected with *vimānas* found in or near the sea or in lakes. The worship of trees and the spirits inhabitating them is one of the most primitive forms of religion. Some, at least, of the Yakkhas are called *rukkhadevatā* (spirits of trees) (e.g., Ja.iii.309, 345; Pv.i.9; Pv-a.5), and others *bhummadevatā* (spirits of the earth) (Pv-a.45,55), who, too, seem to have resided in trees. Generally speaking, the Yakkhas were decadent divinities, beings half deified, having a *Deva*’s supernormal powers, particularly as regards influencing people, partly helpful, partly harmful. They are sometimes called *Devatā* (e.g., SN.i.205), or *Devaputta* (e.g., Pv-a. 113, 139). Some of these, like <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indakūṭa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suciloma</span>, are capable of intelligent questioning on metaphysics and ethics. All of them possess supernatural powers; they can transfer themselves at will, to any place, with their abodes, and work miracles, such as assuming any shape at will. An epithet frequently applied is *mahiddhika* (e.g., Pv.ii.9; Ja.vi.118). Their appearance is striking as a result of former good *kamma* (Pv.i.2, 9; ii.11; iv.3, etc.). They are also called *kāmakāmī*, enjoying all kinds of luxuries (Pv.i.3), but, because of former bad *kamma*, they are possessed of odd qualities, thus they are shy, they fear palmyra leaf and iron. Their eyes are red and they neither wink nor cast a shadow. Ja.iv.492; v.34; vi.336, 337; these various characteristics are, obviously, not found in all Yakkhas. The Yakkhas are evidently of different grades – as is the case with all classes of beings – the highest among them approximate very nearly to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> and have *Deva*-powers, the lowest resemble *Petas*. The Yakkhas are specially mentioned as being afraid of palm leaves (Ja.iv.492).

Their abode is their self-created palace, which is anywhere, in the air, in trees, etc. These are mostly *ākāsaṭṭha* (suspended in the air), but some of them, like the abode of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span>, are *bhūmaṭṭha* (on the ground) and are described as being fortified (Snp­a.i.222). Sometimes whole cities e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷakamandā</span> stand under the protection of, or are inhabited by, Yakkhas.

In many respects they resemble the Vedic *Pisācas*, though they are of different origin. They are evidently remnants of an ancient demonology and have had incorporated in them old animistic beliefs as representing creatures of the wilds and the forests, some of them based on ethnological features (see Stede: *Gespenstergeschichten des Petavatthu* v.39ff).

In later literature the Yakkhas have been degraded to the state of red-eyed cannibal ogres. The female Yakkhas (*Yakkhinī*) are, in these cases, more fearful and evil-minded than the male. They eat flesh and blood (Ja.iv.549; v.34); and devour even men (DN.ii.346; Ja.ii.15ff.) and corpses (Ja.i.265). They eat babies (Ja.v.21; vi.336) and are full of spite and vengeance (Dhp­a.i.47; ii.35f.). The story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhūta Thera</span> is interesting because his elder brothers and sisters were devoured by a hostile Yakkha, so the last child is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhūta</span> to propitiate the Yakkha by making him the child’s sponsor!

Ordinarily the attitude of the Yakkhas towards man is one of benevolence. They are interested in the spiritual welfare of the human beings with whom they come in contact and somewhat resemble tutelary *genii*. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āṭānāṭiyasutta</span> (DN.iii.194f), however, the Yakkha king, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span>, is represented as telling the Buddha that, for the most part, the Yakkhas believe neither in the Buddha nor in his teachings, which enjoin upon his followers abstention from various evils and are therefore distasteful to some of the Yakkhas. Such Yakkhas are disposed to molest the followers of the Buddha in their woodland haunts. Cp. the story of the Yakkha who wished to kill <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> (Ud.iv.4). But the *Mahā* Yakkhas (a list in DN.iii.204f), the generals and commanders among Yakkhas, are always willing to help holy men and to prevent wicked Yakkhas from hurting them. Among Yakkhas are some beings who are *Sotāpannas* – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janavasabha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suciloma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khara</span> (*s.v.*). Some Yakkhas even act as messengers from another world, and will save prospective sinners from committing evil (Pv.iv.1). The case of the Yakkha <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajirapāṇī</span> is of special interest. DN.i.95. The Commentary (DN­a.i.264) says he is not an ordinary Yakkha, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> himself.

He is represented as a kind of mentor, hovering in the air, threatening to kill <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭha</span>, if he does not answer the Buddha’s question the third time he is asked. In many cases the Yakkhas are “fallen angels” and come eagerly to listen to the word of the Buddha in order to be able to rise to a higher sphere of existence e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piyaṅkaramātā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Punabbasumātā</span>, and even <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span>, listening to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷukaṇḍakī Nandamātā</span> reciting the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārāyaṇavagga</span> (AN.iv.63). At the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Samayasutta</span> (*q.v.*) many hundreds of thousands of Yakkhas were present among the audience.

It has been pointed out (Stede, op. cit) that the names of the Yakkhas often give us a clue to their origin and function. These are taken from (a) their bodily appearance e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuvaṇṇā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kharaloma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kharadāṭhika</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittarāja</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Silesaloma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sūciloma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hāritā</span>; (b) their place of residence, attributes of their realms, animals, plants, etc. e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajakalāpaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span> (forest dweller), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uppala</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakudha</span> (name of plant), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumbhīra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gumbiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Disāmukha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamamoli</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajira</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajirapāṇī</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajirabāhu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sātāgira</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Serīsaka</span>; (c) qualities of character, etc. e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Adhamma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭattha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhamma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakata</span>; (d) embodiments of former persons e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janavasabha</span> (lord of men = <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Naradeva</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Serī</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> (*q.v.*) is often mentioned as king of the Yakkhas. He is one of the four Regent Gods, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āṭānāṭiyasutta</span> (DN.iii.199ff) contains a vivid description of the Yakkha kingdom of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span>, with its numerous cities, crowds of inhabitants, parks, lakes and assembly halls. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> is also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuvera</span>, and the Yakkhas are his servants and messengers. They wait upon him in turn. The *Yakkhinīs* draw water for him, and often are so hard-worked that many die in his service, e.g., Ja.iv.492. Mention is also made (e.g., DN­a.ii.370) of *Yakkhadāsīs* who have to dance and sing to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> during the night. Early in the morning they drink a cup of toddy (*surā*) and go off into a deep sleep, from which they rise betimes in the evening ready for their duties.

No one, apparently, is free from this necessity of waiting upon the king even <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janavasabha</span> has to run errands for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> (DN.ii.207). Among the duties of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> is the settling of disputes between the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, and this keeps him (Ja.vi.270) much occupied. In this work he is helped by the *Yakkhasenāpati*, whose business it is to preside over the courts during eight days of each month (Snp­a.i.197). The Yakkhas hold regular assemblies on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manosilātala</span> on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhagalavatī</span> *pabbata* (Snp­a.i.187; cp. DN.iii.201 and DN­a.iii.967). As followers of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuvera</span>, lord of riches, the Yakkhas are the guardians and the liberal spenders of underground riches, hidden treasures, etc., with which they delight men, e.g., Pv.ii.11; Pv-a.145; Pv.iv.12; Pv-a.274. These were seven Yakkhas who guarded the wealth of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotiya</span> *seṭṭhi* (Dhp­a.iv.208f.).

It is difficult to decide whether the Yakkhas, who are the aborigines of Ceylon (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Laṅkā</span>), were considered human or non-human. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuveṇī</span>, one of their princesses, and her maid, can both assume different forms, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijaya</span> marries <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuveṇī</span> and has two children by her (cp. Vin.iii.37; iv. 20; where sexual intercourse with a Yakkha is forbidden). The Yakkhas are invisible, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijaya</span> is able to kill them only with the help of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuveṇī</span> (Mhv.vii.36); but their clothes are found fit for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijaya</span> and his followers to wear (Mhv.vii.38). Again, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyā</span> (*q.v.*) could make herself invisible and assume the form of a mare, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍukābhaya</span> lived with her for four years and she gave him counsel in battle. Later, when he held festivities, he had the Yakkha <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittarāja</span> on the throne beside him (Mhv.x.87). In all probability these Yakkhas were originally considered as humans, but later came to be confused with non-humans. Their chief cities were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Laṅkāpura</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirīsavatthu</span>.

The commonly accepted etymology of Yakkha is from the root *yaj*, meaning to sacrifice. Thus: *yajanti tattha baliṁ upaharantī ti Yakkha* (Vv-a.224), or *pūjanīyabhāvato yakkho ti uccati* (Vv-a.333).
