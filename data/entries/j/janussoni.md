# Jāṇussoṇī

## English source

Jāṇussoṇī. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Sāla</span> Brahmin, ranking with eminent Brahmins such as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṅkī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tārukkha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pokkharasāti</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Todeyya</span> (Snp. p.115). He is mentioned as staying in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Icchānaṅgala</span> (MN.ii.196), where he evidently took part in the periodical gatherings of Brahmin leaders – and also at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manasākaṭa</span> (DN.i.235). He was a follower of the Buddha, of whom he was a great admirer. He appears to have been in the habit of talking to well-known teachers of other schools and hearing their opinion of the Buddha, either for the purpose of comparing his own faith in him or of discovering their views. Two such conversations are recorded – one with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subha Todeyyaputta</span> (MN.ii.209), the other with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pilotika</span> (MN.i.175ff). His discussion with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pilotika</span> he reported to the Buddha, who expanded it to form the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷahatthipadopamasutta</span>. The Buddha also preached to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jāṇussoṇī</span> the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhayabheravasutta</span> (MN.i.16ff). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jāṇussoṇī</span>’s permanent residence was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> (DN­a.ii.399), and he often visited the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, consulting him on many topics, such as: results of actions (AN.i.56), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sandiṭṭhakanibbāna</span> (AN.i.157), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">tevijja</span>-Brahmins (AN.i.166), fearlessness of death (AN.ii.173), the ideals of various classes of persons (AN.iii.362), true celibacy (AN.iv.54), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paccārohaṇī</span> ceremony (AN.v.233ff., 249ff.), the efficacy of gifts (AN.v.269ff.), and eternalism and annihilation (SN.ii.76). He had a white chariot with silver fittings and white trappings drawn by four pure white mares. He would drive about in this, wearing white garments, turban-cloths and sandals and fanned by a white fan. The reins, the goads and the canopy were also of white. His chariot was considered the finest in all <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> (SN.v.4f; cp. MN.i.175 and ii.208). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jāṇussoṇī</span> was not his personal name but the name of the rank he held as chaplain to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> king. MN­a.i.90; according to AN­a. (i.308) it was the name of any noble family, members of which held this rank. Cp. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Govindiye abhisiñci</span> (at DN.ii.231).
