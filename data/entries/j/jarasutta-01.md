# Jarāsutta 01

## English source

Jarāsutta 01. Once, when the Buddha was on a visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span>, a rich Brahmin and his wife, seeing him, called him their son and ministered to him with great affection. It is said that for five hundred births they had been the parents of the Bodhisatta. At the conclusion of a meal the Buddha preached to them and they became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpannas</span>. After the Buddha left <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span> they continued to lead pious lives and became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span> before death. At their funeral they were accorded all the honours due to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span>, and at the conclusion of the ceremonies the Buddha, who was present, preached this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> to those assembled there (Snp­a.ii.531ff; Dhp­a.iii.317ff; cp. – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketajātaka</span> (Ja 68)).

From selfishness come grief and avarice. The monk who lives away from the world, unsmeared by it, is independent and becomes purified. Snp.804-813 explained at MNid.i.117ff.
