# Jātaka

## English source

Jātaka. The tenth book of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khuddakanikāya</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttapiṭaka</span> containing tales of the former births of the Buddha. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> also forms one of the nine <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">aṅgas</span> or divisions of the Buddha’s teachings, grouped according to the subject matter (DN­a.i.15, 24).

The canonical book of the Jātakas (so far unpublished) contains only the verses, but it is almost certain that from the first there must have been handed down an oral commentary giving the stories in prose. This commentary later developed into the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka Commentary</span>.

Some of the Jātakas have been included in a separate compilation, called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cariyāpiṭaka</span>. It is not possible to say when the Jātakas in their present form came into existence nor how many of these were among the original number. In the time of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Niddesa</span>, there seem to have been five hundred Jātakas, because reference is made to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pañcajātakasatāni</span> (p.80; five hundred was the number seen by Faxian in Ceylon (p.71)). Bas-reliefs of the third century have been found illustrating a number of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> stories, and they presuppose the existence of a prose collection. Several Jātakas exist in the canonical books which are not included in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> collection. For a discussion on the Jātakas in all their aspects, see Rhys Davids <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Buddhist India</span>, pp.189ff.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Dīghabhāṇakas</span> included the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhammapiṭaka</span> (DN­a.i.15; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samantapāsādikā</span> (i.251) contains a reference to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Jātakanikāya</span>).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> consists of twenty-two sections or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nipātas</span>.
