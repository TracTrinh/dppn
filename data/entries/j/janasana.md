# Janasāna

## English source

Janasāna (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jarasāna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jarasoṇa</span>). An <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ājīvaka</span>. He it was who predicted the glory of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> by explaining the prenatal desires of his mother. The queen promised him great honour if his predictions should prove true. Later, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> became king and heard the story, he sent a golden palanquin to fetch <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janasāna</span> to the palace. On the way, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janasāna</span> visited the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vattaniya</span> hermitage where lived <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assagutta</span>, and having heard the latter talk of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">āyatana</span>, his earlier <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kamma</span> asserted itself and he became a monk, attaining <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> he was a python who died while listening to some monks reciting a chapter on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">āyatanas</span>. Mhv­ṭ.190ff.
