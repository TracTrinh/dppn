# Jīvakambavana

## English source

Jīvakambavana. A mango-grove in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, belonging to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span>, which he made over to the Buddha and his monks. He built a monastery in the grove, and there the Buddha stayed several times. On one such occasion <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> visited the Buddha and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmaññaphalasutta</span> was preached (DN 2) (DN.i.47ff). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvakasutta</span>s were also preached there. The mango-grove was near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maddakucchi</span>, so thither they carried the Buddha when his foot was injured by a splinter from the rock hurled by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> (Dhp­a.ii.164, etc.).

It was in this grove that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Panthaka</span> attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship, and, at that time, there were five hundred monks there (Ja.i.114f., etc.). Nuns, too, appear to have gone there for their siesta. Thīg­a.245f.
