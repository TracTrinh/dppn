# Juṇhajātaka

## English source

Juṇhajātaka (Ja 456). Once the Bodhisatta was born as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Juṇha</span>, son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. He studied in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> and, on one occasion, while walking in the dark, he ran up against a Brahmin, knocking him down and breaking his bowl. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Juṇha</span> raised the Brahmin to his feet and, on being asked for the price of a meal, told the Brahmin who he was. He had no money with him, but requested the Brahmin to remind him of the circumstance when he should become king. In due time <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Juṇha</span> was anointed, and the Brahmin stood one day by the road when the king was passing on his elephant. The Brahmin stretched out his hand, crying, “Victory to the king.” <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Juṇha</span> took no notice, so the Brahmin uttered a stanza to the effect that a king should not neglect a Brahmin’s request. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Juṇha</span> then turned back, and the man explained who he was, asking <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Juṇha</span> for five villages, one hundred slave girls, one thousand ornaments and two wives, all of which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Juṇha</span> gave him.

The story was related in reference to the eight boons granted by the Buddha to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> when the latter became his constant attendant. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> is identified with the Brahmin (Ja.iv.95-100).

See also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāṇacchandajātaka</span> (Ja 289).
