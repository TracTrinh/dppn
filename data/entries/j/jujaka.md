# Jūjaka

## English source

Jūjaka. A Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dunniviṭṭha</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāliṅga</span>. He was given a young maiden in repayment of a debt, but because she was praised for her virtues, the other wives in the village grew jealous of her and mocked her as an old man’s darling. Thereafter she refused to go to the village well, and suggested that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jūjaka</span> should obtain as slaves the children of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span>, then living as an ascetic in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅkagiri</span>.

After many adventures <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jūjaka</span> found <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span>, was allowed to have the two children, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jāli</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇhajinā</span>, and having tied their hands together, took them away. After he had travelled sixty leagues, the gods led him to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetuttara</span>, where the children’s grandfather reigned as king. The king bought the children back from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jūjaka</span> at a very great price and gave him choice foods to eat. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jūjaka</span>, having over-eaten and being unable to digest the food, died on the spot (Ja.vi.521-81). He is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> (Ja.vi.593).

The wife of the Brahmin who went for alms to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span> was a descendant of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jūjaka</span>. His descendants were still living in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dunniviṭṭha</span>, even in the Buddha’s day (AN­a.i.183).
