# Jambudīpa

## English source

Jambudīpa. One of the four *Mahā Dīpas*, or great continents, which are included in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavāḷa</span> and are ruled by a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>. They are grouped round Mount <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span>. In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span> with its eighty-four thousand peaks, its lakes, mountain ranges, etc. This continent derives its name from the *Jambu*-tree (also called *Naga*) which grows there, its trunk fifteen *yojanas* in girth, its outspreading branches fifty *yojanas* in length, its shade one hundred *yojanas* in extent and its height one hundred *yojanas* (Vin.i.30; Snp­a.ii.443; Vsm.i.205f; Vin-a.i.119, etc.). On account of this tree, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> is also known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambusaṇḍa</span> (Snp.vs.552; Snp­a.i.121). The continent is ten thousand *yojanas* in extent; of these ten thousand, four thousand are covered by the ocean, three thousand by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span> mountains, while three thousand are inhabited by men (Snp­a.ii.437; Ud­a.300).

Sometimes in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> there are as many as eighty-four thousand cities; this number is sometimes reduced to sixty thousand, forty thousand, or even twenty thousand, but never to less (Snp­a.i.59; Ja.iv.84 says sixty-three thousand; Pv-a.111). In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> there were eighty-four thousand cities, in each of which he built a monastery (Mhv.v.176; Vsm.201). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (i.35) it is said that, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>, trifling in number are the parks, groves, lakes, etc., more numerous the steep, precipitous places, unfordable rivers, inaccessible mountains, etc.

At the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Metteyya Buddha</span>’s appearance on earth <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> will be pervaded by mankind even as a jungle is by reeds and rushes. There will be eighty-four thousand cities with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ketumātī</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>) at the head (DN.iii.75).

The Buddha once declared that the people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> excel those of both <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> in three respects – courage, mindfulness and religious life (AN.iv.396; Kv.99).

Buddhas (and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavattis</span>) are born only in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> (Bv­a.48; MN­a.ii.917).

There were four sounds heard throughout <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>:

- the shout uttered by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Puṇṇaka</span> proclaiming his victory over <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Dhanañjaya Koravya</span> in a game of dice;
- the bark of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Vissakamma</span> when taken about in the guise of a dog by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sakka</span>, threatening to devour all wicked beings after the decay of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kassapa</span>’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Sāsana</span>;
- the roar of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kusa</span>, challenging to battle the seven kings who sought the hand of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Pabhāvatī</span>;
- the yell of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Āḷavaka</span>, proclaiming his name from the top of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kelāsa</span>, on hearing that the Buddha had visited his abode (SN­a.i.248, etc.).

When opposed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīhaladīpa</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tambapaṇṇidīpa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> indicates the continent of India (e.g., Mhv.v.13; xiv.8; Cv.xxxvii.216, 246).

For the purposes of *cārikā*, the monks divided their tours in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> into three circuits or *maṇḍalas* – the *Mahāmaṇḍala* which extended over nine hundred leagues, the *Majjhimamaṇḍala* which extended over six hundred, and the *Antimamaṇḍala* over three hundred. Those who wish to tour the first, start after the *Mahā Pavāraṇa* and complete their journey in nine months, for the *Majjhimamaṇḍala* they start after the *Pavāraṇa*, on the full-moon day of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kattika</span>, completing the tour in eight months, while for the *Antimamaṇḍala* they start on the first day of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phussa</span> and return after seven months (Vin-a.i.197).

In each <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavāḷa</span> there is a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> (AN.i.227). Mention is made in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kākātijātaka</span> (Ja 327, Ja.iii.91) of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> *samudda*, beyond which was the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kebuka</span>.
