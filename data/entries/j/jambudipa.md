# Jambudīpa

## English source

Jambudīpa. One of the four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Dīpas</span>, or great continents, which are included in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavāḷa</span> and are ruled by a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>. They are grouped round Mount <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span>. In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span> with its eighty-four thousand peaks, its lakes, mountain ranges, etc. This continent derives its name from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Jambu</span>-tree (also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Naga</span>) which grows there, its trunk fifteen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojanas</span> in girth, its outspreading branches fifty <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojanas</span> in length, its shade one hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojanas</span> in extent and its height one hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojanas</span> (Vin.i.30; Snp­a.ii.443; Vsm.i.205f; Vin-a.i.119, etc.). On account of this tree, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> is also known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambusaṇḍa</span> (Snp.vs.552; Snp­a.i.121). The continent is ten thousand <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojanas</span> in extent; of these ten thousand, four thousand are covered by the ocean, three thousand by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span> mountains, while three thousand are inhabited by men (Snp­a.ii.437; Ud­a.300).

Sometimes in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> there are as many as eighty-four thousand cities; this number is sometimes reduced to sixty thousand, forty thousand, or even twenty thousand, but never to less (Snp­a.i.59; Ja.iv.84 says sixty-three thousand; Pv-a.111). In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> there were eighty-four thousand cities, in each of which he built a monastery (Mhv.v.176; Vsm.201). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (i.35) it is said that, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>, trifling in number are the parks, groves, lakes, etc., more numerous the steep, precipitous places, unfordable rivers, inaccessible mountains, etc.

At the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Metteyya Buddha</span>’s appearance on earth <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> will be pervaded by mankind even as a jungle is by reeds and rushes. There will be eighty-four thousand cities with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ketumātī</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>) at the head (DN.iii.75).

The Buddha once declared that the people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> excel those of both <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> in three respects – courage, mindfulness and religious life (AN.iv.396; Kv.99).

Buddhas (and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavattis</span>) are born only in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> (Bv­a.48; MN­a.ii.917).
