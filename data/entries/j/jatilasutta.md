# Jaṭilasutta

## English source

Jaṭilasutta. Once when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> was talking with the Buddha in the loggia outside the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāramātupāsāda</span>, there passed close by thirty-five ascetics of various denominations – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Jaṭilas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span>, etc. – and the king saluted them respectfully. Later, he asked the Buddha whether they were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span> or on the way to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship. The Buddha explained to him how hard it was for a layman, with all his encumbrances, to find an opportunity to learn the truth about <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span>; much time and care and attention were necessary. The king agreed and mentioned how he gathered information through his spies. SN.i.77f.
