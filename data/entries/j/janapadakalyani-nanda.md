# Janapadakaḷyānī Nandā

## English source

Janapadakaḷyānī Nandā. One of the three Nandās who became Bhikkhunīs – the others being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandā</span>, sister of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandatthera</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhirūpanandā</span>. Because of her very great beauty she earned the sobriquet of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Janapadakaḷyānī</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> Commentary (170) gives details of her beauty, which justified her title; see also Ja.i.394.

Janapadakaḷyānī was engaged to be married to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span>, but on the day fixed for the marriage the Buddha induced <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span> to join the Saṅgha, in spite of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span>’s wishes, and in due course he became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. Later, when women were admitted to the Saṅgha, Janapadakaḷyānī, feeling she had nothing to look forward to, became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Bhikkhunī</span> under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span>. For a long time she would not visit the Buddha, having heard that he spoke disparagingly of physical beauty, but one day, inspired by curiosity, she accompanied her colleagues to hear the Buddha preach. He, being aware of her thoughts, created the form of a most beautiful maiden who stood fanning him. As Janapadakaḷyānī sat gazing at her, enraptured by her beauty, she saw her gradually reach extreme old age, passing through all the stages, until at last she saw her die, leaving her body to decompose and become a mass of filth. At the critical moment, the Buddha uttered the appropriate words and Janapadakaḷyānī became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>. The Buddha then preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāyavicchandanikasutta</span> and she became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> (Ud.iii.2; Ja.i.91; Snp­a.i.241f, 243f, 254, 273; Dhp­a.i.97, 100).

She seems to have been known also as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rūpanandā</span>. Dhp­a.iii.113f; but see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rūpanandā</span>; perhaps here we have a confusion of legends. In the northern books she is called Bhadrā. (Rockhill, p.55.)

In one of her previous lives, Janapadakaḷyānī was born as a she-mule; she sorely tempted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span>, who was then a mule belonging to a merchant named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kappaṭa</span> (Dhp­a.i.105). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sundarīnandā</span> also seems to have been called Janapadakaḷyānī.
