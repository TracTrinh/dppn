# Jotika 01

## English source

Jotika 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotiya</span>. A treasurer of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> who later became an *Arahant*. In the past he had been a householder of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> who, with his elder brother, owned a field of sugar-cane. One day, when returning from the field with some stalks of sugar-cane, he saw a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhamādana</span> to whom he gave a bowl full of sugar-cane juice for him to drink, and when he had drunk it, gave him another bowlful which the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> took to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhamādana</span> to share with his colleagues. The householder, when proffering his gifts, wished for glory and for *Nibbāna*. His elder brother, hearing of this, was likewise filled with joy and he, too, wished for *Nibbāna*.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span> they were again brothers in a rich family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhumatī</span>, the elder being called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sena</span> and the younger <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparājita</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sena</span> entered the Saṅgha and became an *Arahant*. At his suggestion, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparājita</span> built for the Buddha a very costly <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhakuṭi</span>, with the seven kinds of precious things (according to Dhp­a.iii.364, both uncle and nephew were called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avaroja</span>). His nephew, also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparājita</span>, built an elephant stable in front of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhakuṭi</span> and was reborn in this age as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meṇḍaka</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhakuṭi</span> and its surroundings contained all kinds of luxuries and splendours beyond description (for details see Dhp­a.iv.203f). Within and without the chamber he caused jewels, pounded and otherwise, to be strewn knee deep. Those who came to listen to the Buddha went away, their hands full of jewels. One day a Brahmin tried to carry away a very costly jewel, which much annoyed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparājita</span>. On mentioning it to the Buddha, the Buddha taught him to make a wish that his property should not be taken away by thieves or lost by fire or water. For nine months <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparājita</span> held the ceremony of dedication of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhakuṭi</span>.

In this age he was born as the son of a *seṭṭhi* in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. On the day of his birth the whole city became one blaze of light, hence his name, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotika</span>. The king, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, gave one thousand a day for his milk-money. When the time came for his marriage, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> provided for him a palace, seven storeys high, made entirely of precious minerals. Four urns of treasure stood at the four corners and four stalks of sugar-cane made of solid gold, each the size of a palmyra tree with leaves of precious stones, to remind <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotika</span> of his good deed in the past. Seven <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> guarded the seven gates – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamakoḷī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uppala</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajira</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajirabāhu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kasakanda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭattha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Disāpāmukha</span> – each with numerous followers. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, hearing of his splendour, appointed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotika</span> to the rank of *seṭṭhi*.

The gods brought him a wife from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> and lodged her in an apartment in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotika</span>’s palace. Her name was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Satulakāyī</span> (Dhp­a.iv.223). She brought with her a pint-pot of rice and three crystal fire-stones. Whenever they wished to eat, they would put rice over the boiler and set it over the crystals. The stones would immediately become alight and, when the meal was cooked, would extinguish themselves. The pot was inexhaustible. All who came to visit Jotika were provided with food and were allowed to take anything they wished from the treasure, the treasure never growing less. So great were the crowds which flocked to Jotika’s palace that for a long time <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> had no chance of seeing it. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> did in the end visit him with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, Jotika invited him to a meal which was the most dainty the king had ever tasted. After the meal Jotika presented his wife to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>; so delicately was she nurtured that the perfume with which the king was scented hurt her eyes! There were no lamps in Jotika’s house; only the light of jewels was made use of, and as a memento of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>’s visit Jotika gave him a huge jewel to be used as a lamp (this account is taken from Dhp­a.iv.199-213).

Jotika was a very pious follower of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>. Once, when he was away listening to the Buddha’s preaching, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> – who even when visiting the palace as a boy with his father had coveted Jotika’s wealth – went with his soldiers to attack the palace in an attempt to take possession of it. But the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamakoḷī</span> routed the army, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> fled for refuge to the *vihāra* where Jotika was listening to the Dhamma. On being charged by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> with hypocrisy in that he was there listening to the Dhamma, after having charged his guards to set upon the king, Jotika’s answer was that he had no need of guards since nobody could take anything of his without his sanction. He then challenged the king to remove the rings from his (Jotika’s) fingers. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, trying with all his might, failed. Jotika then held out his hands and his rings all fell off. Agitated by the king’s desire to possess his wealth, Jotika asked for permission to become a monk. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> agreed, hoping thus to get possession of his riches. Jotika entered the Saṅgha and soon became an *Arahant*, but at the moment of his attainment of *Arahant*-ship all his wealth and earthly glory vanished and his wife returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> (Dhp­a.iv.221-4).

Jotika is included among the five persons who possessed great merit and had *puññiddhi*. Dhp­a.i.385; Vsm.383; Paṭis-a.502. These five persons are described as *amitabhogā* (AN­a.i.220).
