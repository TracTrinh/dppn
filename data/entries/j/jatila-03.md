# Jaṭila 03

## English source

Jaṭila 03. (*v.l.* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jaṭilaka</span>). A *seṭṭhi* of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, one of the five *seṭṭhis* of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> (Dhp­a.i.385). His mother was a *seṭṭhi*’s daughter in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, who had illicit relations with a *Vijjādhara*, and when the child was born she placed it in a vessel which she handed to her slave, to be floated down the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span>. Two women, while bathing, saw the vessel, discovered what it contained and each claimed the child. The dispute was settled by the king and the child was given to the woman who happened to be a disciple of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span>. The child was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jaṭila</span> because the first time he was bathed after birth his hair became matted. When able to walk, he was given to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span> to be ordained, but the Thera took him to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> and handed him over to one of his supporters, a merchant, who adopted him as his son. Years passed, and one day the merchant, having to go on a journey, made a list of the goods which he had accumulated in his house during twelve years and asked Jaṭila to sell them if he could find buyers. Such was the lad’s fortune that in one day they were all disposed of. The merchant, realising the young man’s destiny, gave him his daughter in marriage and provided him with a house. As Jaṭila stepped into the house, the earth behind it was rent asunder and a mountain of gold, eighty cubits in height, appeared for his use. Thereupon the king made him a Treasurer. Later, wishing to retire from the world, Jaṭila sent out messengers to discover if there were others as rich as he, in case the king should raise objections to his going away. When news was brought back of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meṇḍaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotika</span>, he knew there would be no opposition and obtained the king’s permission. He had three sons, but, having tested them, came to know that only the youngest had the necessary good fortune to enjoy his vast wealth. Jaṭila thereupon handed over to him his wealth and entered the Saṅgha, becoming an *Arahant* within a few days. Some time afterwards the Buddha, with Jaṭila and other monks, was entertained for a fortnight by Jaṭila’s sons, and in answer to the monks’ questions Jaṭila declared that he felt no desire to re-enter household life. The monks found this hard to believe till assured by the Buddha that it was so.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>, Jaṭila was a goldsmith. One day, an *Arahant*, seeking for gold wherewith to complete the shrine erected over the Buddha’s remains, came to the goldsmith’s house; the latter, having just quarrelled with his wife, was in a surly mood and said to the *Arahant*, “Throw your teacher into the water and get away.” His wife told him how wicked were his words, and he, realising his fault, asked pardon of the *Arahant* and made valuable offerings at the Buddha’s shrine, by way of amends. Of his three sons whom he asked, in turn, to help him with the preparations, only the youngest consented to go with him. Therefore it was that in seven successive states <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jaṭila</span> was thrown into the water on the day of his birth and only his youngest son could enjoy his wealth (Dhp­a.iv.214ff; Paṭis-a.502f).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jaṭila</span>’s possession of a golden mountain is given as an example of *puññiddhi*, he being one of the five persons of great merit. Vsm.383; Bv­a.24.
