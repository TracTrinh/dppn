# Jīvaka 01

## English source

Jīvaka 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka Komārabhacca</span>. A celebrated physician. He was the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sālavatī</span>, a courtesan of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> (AN­a. i.216 says that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayarājakumāra</span> was his father). Directly after birth the child was placed in a basket and thrown on a dust-heap, from where he was rescued by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayarājakumāra</span>. When questioned by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span>, people said “he was alive” (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jīvati</span>), and therefore the child was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span>; because he was brought up by the prince (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kumārena posāpito</span>), he was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Komārabhacca</span>. It has been suggested, however, that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Komārabhacca</span> meant master of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kaumārabhṛtya</span> science (the treatment of infants); VT.ii.174; in Divy. (506-18) he is called Kumārabhūta.

When grown up, he learnt of his antecedents, and going to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> without <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span>’s knowledge, studied medicine for seven years. His teacher then gave him a little money and sent him away as being fit to practise medicine. His first patient was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span>’s wife at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span>, and for curing her he received sixteen thousand <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kahāpaṇas</span>, a manservant, a maid-servant and a coach with horses. When he returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span> established him in his own residence. There he cured <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> of a troublesome fistula and received as reward all the ornaments of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>’s five hundred wives. He was appointed physician to the king and the king’s women and also to the fraternity of monks with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> at its head. Other cures of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span>’s included that of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> on whom he performed the operation of trepanning, and of the son of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> who had suffered from chronic intestinal trouble due to misplacement, and for this case <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span> received sixteen thousand <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kahāpaṇas</span>.

When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍappajjota</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>, was ill, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> lent Jīvaka to him. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍappajjota</span> hated ghee, which was, however, the only remedy. Jīvaka prepared the medicine, prescribed it for the king, then rode away on the king’s elephant <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddavatikā</span> before the king discovered the nature of the medicine. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajjota</span>, in a rage, ordered his capture and sent his slave <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāka</span> after him. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāka</span> discovered Jīvaka breakfasting at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> and allowed himself to be persuaded to eat half a myrobalan, which purged him violently. Jīvaka explained to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāka</span> that he wished to delay his return; he told him why he had fled from the court and, having returned the elephant, proceeded to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajjota</span> was cured and, as a token of his favour, sent Jīvaka a suit of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sīveyyaka</span> cloth, which Jīvaka presented to the Buddha (Vin.i.268-81; AN­a.i.216). Jīvaka was greatly attracted by the Buddha. Once when the Buddha was ill, Jīvaka found it necessary to administer a purge, and he had fat rubbed into the Buddha’s body and gave him a handful of lotuses to smell. Jīvaka was away when the purgative acted, and suddenly remembered that he had omitted to ask the Buddha to bathe in warm water to complete the cure. The Buddha read his thoughts and bathed as required. Vin.i.279f; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp-a.ii.164f), relates a like occurrence in another connection. When the Buddha’s foot was injured by the splinter from the rock hurled by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>, he had to be carried from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maddakucchi</span> to Jīvaka’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambavana</span>. There Jīvaka applied an astringent, and having bandaged the wound, left the city expecting to return in time to remove it. But by the time he did return, the city gates were closed and he could not enter. He was greatly worried because he knew that if the bandage remained on all night the Buddha would suffer intense pain. But the Buddha read his thoughts and removed the bandage. See also Ja.v.333.

After Jīvaka became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>, he was anxious to visit the Buddha twice a day, and finding <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span> too far away, he built a monastery with all its adjuncts in his own <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambavana</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, which he gave to the Buddha and his monks (DN­a.i.133; MN­a.ii.590). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> died, Jīvaka continued to serve <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, and was responsible for bringing him to the Buddha after his crime of parricide (for details see the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmaññaphalasutta</span>; also Ja.i.508f; v.262, etc.).

Jīvaka’s fame as a physician brought him more work than he could cope with, but he never neglected his duties to the Saṅgha. Many people, afflicted with disease and unable to pay for treatment by him, joined the Saṅgha in order that they might receive that treatment. On discovering that the Saṅgha was thus being made a convenience of, he asked the Buddha to lay down a rule that men afflicted with certain diseases should be refused entry into the Saṅgha (Vin.i.71ff). Jīvaka was declared by the Buddha chief among his lay followers loved by the people (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t18">aggaṁ puggalappasannānaṁ</span>) (AN.i.26). He is included in a list of good men who have been assured of the realisation of deathlessness (AN.iii.451; Dhp­a.i.244, 247; Ja.i.116f).

At a meal once given by Jīvaka, the Buddha refused to be served until <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Panthaka</span>, who had been left out of the invitation, had been sent for (for details see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Panthaka</span>). It may have been the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvakasutta</span> which effected <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span>’s conversion. One discussion he had with the Buddha regarding the qualities of a pious lay disciple is recorded in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (AN.iv.222f). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span> was Jīvaka’s youngest sister (Snp­a.i.244; Dhp­a.iii.106).

At Jīvaka’s request, the Buddha enjoined upon monks to take exercise; Jīvaka had gone to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> on business and had noticed their pale, unhealthy took (Vin.ii.119).
