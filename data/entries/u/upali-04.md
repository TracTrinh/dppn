# Upāli 04

## English source

Upāli 04. Distinguished as Upāli <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Gahapati</span>. He lived at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālandā</span> and was a follower of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta</span>.

He was present when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha Tapassī</span> reported to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nātaputta</span> an account of his visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvārika</span> Mango-grove. Upāli undertook to go himself to the Buddha and refute his views, in spite of the protestations of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha Tapassī</span>. At the end of his discussion with the Buddha, which is recorded in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upālisutta</span>, Upāli is converted and invites the Buddha to a meal. Although the Buddha enjoins upon Upāli that his benefactions to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span> should not cease because of his conversion, Upāli gives instructions that no <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nigaṇṭha</span> be admitted to his presence, but that if they need food it shall be given to them. Hearing a rumour of his conversion, first <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tapassī</span>, and later <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nātaputta</span> himself, go to Upāli’s house, where they learn the truth. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nātaputta</span> is finally convinced that Upāli has become a follower of the Buddha, hot blood gushes from his mouth (MN.i.371ff).

According to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (MN­a.ii.621, 830), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nātaputta</span> had to be carried on a litter to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span>, where he died shortly after.

Upāli became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span> (MN­a.ii.620).

He is mentioned, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittagahapati</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammika</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">upāsaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khujjuttarā</span>, as one who had acquired the four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Paṭisambhidā</span> while being yet a learner (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sekha</span>). Vsm.ii.442; Vibh­a.388.
