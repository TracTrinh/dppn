# Upavāṇa 01

## English source

Upavāṇa 01. A Thera. He belonged to a very rich Brahmin family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, and having seen the Buddha’s majesty at the dedication of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, he entered the Saṅgha and became an *Arahant* with sixfold *aññā*. For some time, before <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> was appointed *upaṭṭhāka*, Upavāṇa waited on the Buddha. Once when the Buddha was attacked by cramp, Upavāṇa, with the help of his lay-friend <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devahita</span>, obtained hot water and suitable medicines, with which the ailment was healed; the Buddha, thereupon, expressed his gratitude. Thag­a.i.308ff; this ailment does not seem to be mentioned in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milinda</span> 134f. where several others are given. This incident is given at greater length in SN.i.174f; see also Dhp­a.iv.232f.

When the Buddha lay on his death-bed at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusināra</span>, Upavāṇa was by his side fanning him; the Buddha, seeing that he obstructed the vision of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> who had come to pay their last homage to the Teacher, asked Upavāṇa to move away (DN.ii.138f).

Two occasions are mentioned on which Upavāṇa consulted the Buddha on matters of doctrine, once regarding the arising of suffering (SN.ii.41-2) and once on the immediate and practical use of the Dhamma (*sandiṭṭhikadhamma*) (SN.iv.41). There is also recorded a visit of Upavāṇa to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> when they were both staying in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghositārāma</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> asks him about the *bojjhaṅgas* as being conducive to a happy life and Upavāṇa explains (SN.v.76). On another occasion Upavāṇa is the enquirer, and he asks <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> about the “end-maker” (*antakara*); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> explains that the “end-maker” is the one who knows and sees things as they really are (AN.ii.163).

When an unpleasant interview took place between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lāḷudāyī</span> (*q.v.*) and no one was found to support <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, the matter is reported to the Buddha, who declares that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> should have taken <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>’s side. Soon afterwards <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> seeks Upavāṇa and tells him that he was too timid to interfere, and if the Buddha referred to the matter again, would Upavāṇa undertake to answer? In the evening the Buddha engages Upavāṇa in conversation and asks him to explain the five qualities which make a monk esteemed and loved by his colleagues. At the end of the discourse the Buddha applauds Upavāṇa (AN.iii.195f).

In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span>’s time Upavāṇa had been a poor man. Seeing people making great offerings at the Buddha’s *Thūpa*, he was much touched, and having washed his upper garment, he hung it as a flag over the *Thūpa*. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhisammataka</span>, who was the guardian of the *cetiya*, took the flag three times round the *cetiya*, he himself remaining invisible.

A monk whom the man consulted after this miracle foretold that for thirty thousand *kappas* he would be in the *Deva*-worlds and that he would be *Deva*-king eighty times. One thousand times he was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>. In his last life his wealth was eighty crores. When he was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>, his banner was held aloft, three leagues in height. Ap.i.70ff.
