# Uttarakuru 01

## English source

Uttarakuru 01. A country often mentioned in the *Nikāyas* and in later literature as a mythical region. A detailed description of it is given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āṭānāṭiyasutta</span>. (DN.iii.199ff; here <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> is spoken of as a city, *pura*; see also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> in Hopkins: *Epic Mythology*, especially p.186). The men who live there own no property nor have they wives of their own; they do not have to work for their living. The corn ripens by itself and sweet-scented rice is found boiling on hot oven-stoves. The inhabitants go about riding on cows, on men and women, on maids and youths. Their king rides on an elephant, on a horse, on celestial cars and in state palanquins. Their cities are built in the air, and among those mentioned are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āṭānāṭā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusināṭā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāṭapuriyā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakusināṭā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapīvanta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janogha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Navanavatiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaraambaravatiya</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷakamandā</span>, the last being the chief city.

The king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuvera</span>, also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span>, because the name of his citadel (? *rājadhāni*) is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visāṇa</span>. His proclamations are made known by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tatolā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tattalā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tatotalā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ojasi</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tejasi</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tetojasi</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sūra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāja</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariṭṭha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nemi</span>. Mention is also made of a lake named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dharaṇī</span> and a hall named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhagalavati</span> where the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span>, as the inhabitants of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> are called, hold their assemblies.

The country is always spoken of as being to the north of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>. It is eight thousand leagues in extent and is surrounded by the sea (DN­a.ii.623; Bv­a.113). Sometimes it is spoken of (e.g., AN.i.227; v.59; Snp-a.ii.443) as one of the four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Dīpā</span> – the others being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparagoyāna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pubbavideha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> – each being surrounded by five hundred minor islands. These four make up a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavāḷa</span>, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mount Meru</span> in their midst, a flat-world system. A *cakkavattī*’s rule extends over all these four continents (DN.ii.173; DN­a.ii.623) and his chief queen comes either from the race of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span> or from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span>; in the latter case she appears before him of her own accord, urged on by her good fortune (DN­a.ii.626; Khp­a.173).

The trees in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> bear perpetual fruit and foliage, and it also possesses a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapparukkha</span> which lasts for a whole *kappa* (AN.i.264; MN­a.ii.948). There are no houses in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span>, the inhabitants sleep on the earth and are called, therefore, *bhūmisayā* (Thag­a.ii.187-8).

The men of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> surpass even the gods of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> in four things:

- they have no greed (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t23">amamā</span>) (the people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Uttarakuru</span> are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t23">acchandikā</span>, Vibh­a.461),
- no private property (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t23">apariggahā</span>),
- they have a definite term of life (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t23">niyatāyukā</span>) (one thousand years, after which they are born in heaven, says <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Buddhaghosa</span>, AN­a.ii.806)
- and they possess great elegance (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t23">visesabhuno</span>).

They are, however, inferior to the men of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> in courage, mindfulness and in the religious life (AN.iv.396; Kv.99).

Several instances are given of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> having gone to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> for alms. Having obtained his food there, he would go to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anotatta</span> lake, bathe in its waters and, after the meal, spend the afternoon on its banks (See, e.g., Vin.i.27-8; Dhs­a.16; Dhp­a.iii.222). The power of going to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> for alms is not restricted to the Buddha; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> and various ascetics are mentioned as having visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> on their begging rounds (See, e.g., Ja.v.316; vi.100; MN­a.i.340; Snp-a.ii.420). It is considered a mark of great *iddhi*-power to be able to do this (e.g., Rohita, SN­a.i.93; also Mil.84).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotika</span>’s wife was a woman of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span>; she was brought to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotika</span> by the gods. She brought with her a single pint pot of rice and three crystals. The rice-pot was never exhausted; whenever a meal was desired, the rice was put in a boiler and the boiler set over the crystals; the heat of the crystals went out as soon as the rice was cooked. The same thing happened with curries (Dhp­a.iv.209ff). Food never ran short in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span>; once when there was a famine in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verañjā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> and his monks were finding it difficult to get alms, we find <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> suggesting that they should go to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> for alms (Vin.iii.7). The clothes worn by the inhabitants resembled divine robes (See, e.g., Pv-a.76).

It was natural for the men of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> not to transgress virtue, they had *pakati-sīla* (Vsm.i.15).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> is probably identical with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuru</span> country mentioned in the *Ṛg-Veda* (See Vedic Index).
