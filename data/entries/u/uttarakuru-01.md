# Uttarakuru 01

## English source

Uttarakuru 01. A country often mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nikāyas</span> and in later literature as a mythical region. A detailed description of it is given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āṭānāṭiyasutta</span>. (DN.iii.199ff; here <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> is spoken of as a city, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pura</span>; see also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> in Hopkins: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Epic Mythology</span>, especially p.186). The men who live there own no property nor have they wives of their own; they do not have to work for their living. The corn ripens by itself and sweet-scented rice is found boiling on hot oven-stoves. The inhabitants go about riding on cows, on men and women, on maids and youths. Their king rides on an elephant, on a horse, on celestial cars and in state palanquins. Their cities are built in the air, and among those mentioned are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āṭānāṭā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusināṭā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāṭapuriyā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakusināṭā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapīvanta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janogha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Navanavatiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaraambaravatiya</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷakamandā</span>, the last being the chief city.

The king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuvera</span>, also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span>, because the name of his citadel (? <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">rājadhāni</span>) is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visāṇa</span>. His proclamations are made known by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tatolā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tattalā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tatotalā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ojasi</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tejasi</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tetojasi</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sūra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāja</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariṭṭha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nemi</span>. Mention is also made of a lake named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dharaṇī</span> and a hall named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhagalavati</span> where the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span>, as the inhabitants of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> are called, hold their assemblies.

The country is always spoken of as being to the north of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>. It is eight thousand leagues in extent and is surrounded by the sea (DN­a.ii.623; Bv­a.113). Sometimes it is spoken of (e.g., AN.i.227; v.59; Snp-a.ii.443) as one of the four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Dīpā</span> – the others being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparagoyāna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pubbavideha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> – each being surrounded by five hundred minor islands. These four make up a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavāḷa</span>, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mount Meru</span> in their midst, a flat-world system. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cakkavattī</span>’s rule extends over all these four continents (DN.ii.173; DN­a.ii.623) and his chief queen comes either from the race of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span> or from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span>; in the latter case she appears before him of her own accord, urged on by her good fortune (DN­a.ii.626; Khp­a.173).

The trees in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> bear perpetual fruit and foliage, and it also possesses a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapparukkha</span> which lasts for a whole <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappa</span> (AN.i.264; MN­a.ii.948). There are no houses in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span>, the inhabitants sleep on the earth and are called, therefore, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t18">bhūmisayā</span> (Thag­a.ii.187-8).

The men of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> surpass even the gods of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> in four things:
