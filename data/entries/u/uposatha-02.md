# Uposatha 02

## English source

Uposatha 02. The name of the Elephant Treasure of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sudassana</span>. He was “all white, sevenfold firm (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sattappatiṭṭha</span>), wonderful in power, flying through the sky.” (DN.ii.174; MN.iii.173f). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Lalitavistara</span> his name is given as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bodhi</span>.

Uposatha is also the name of a tribe of elephants, the ninth in a series of ten tribes, of ascending importance (DN­a.ii.573; Ud­a.403). It is said that a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>’s elephant belongs either to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chaddanta</span> tribe or to the Uposatha. If a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chaddanta</span> elephant comes to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>, it is the youngest of the tribe that comes, if an Uposatha elephant, then it will be the foremost (DN­a.ii.624; Ja.iv.232, 234; Khp­a.172). When the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span> dies, the elephant goes back to his fellows (DN­a.ii.635). The strength of an Uposatha elephant is equal to that of one thousand million men (Bv­a.37). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span> (p.282), the king of the Uposatha elephants is described as being gentle and handsome, eight cubits in height and nine in girth and length, showing signs of rut in three places on his body, all white, sevenfold-firm. Just as this elephant could never be put into a cow-pen or covered with a saucer, so could no one keep as slaves the children of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span>.
