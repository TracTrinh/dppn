# Udumbara Devī

## English source

Udumbara Devī. Wife of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṅguttara</span>. She was the daughter of a teacher in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> and was given to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṅguttara</span> because he was the eldest pupil. But he was unhappy with her, and on the way to his home, when she climbed up a fig (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">udumbara</span>) tree to pluck fruits for herself, he put thorns round the tree and ran away, leaving her. The king, coming along, saw her and married her. She was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udumbara Devī</span> because of the circumstances in which she was found. When the king suspected her of infidelity to him, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> saved her from ignominy, and she became thereafter his best friend and helped him in all his doings, treating him, with the king’s permission, as her younger brother. When the king planned to kill <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udumbara Devī</span> warned him in time and enabled him to evade the treachery of his enemies at court (Ja.vi.348, 352, 355, 363, 368, 384).

In the present age she was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Diṭṭhamaṅgalikā</span> (Ja.vi.478).
