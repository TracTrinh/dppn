# Upāli 01

## English source

Upāli 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli Thera</span>. One of the most eminent of the Buddha’s immediate disciples. He belonged to a barber’s family in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> and entered the service of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> princes. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> and his cousins left the world and sought ordination from the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anupiyā</span> Grove, Upāli accompanied them. They gave him all their valuable ornaments, but, on further consideration, he refused to accept them and wished to become a monk with them. The reason given for his refusal is that he knew the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> were hot-headed, and feared that the kinsmen of the princes might suspect him of having murdered the young men for the sake of their belongings.

At the request of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> youths, the Buddha ordained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli</span> before them all, so that their pride might be humbled. (Vin.ii.182; Dhp­a.i.116f; see also Bv.i.61; but see Bv­a.44; the Tibetan sources give a slightly different version, see Rockhill, op.cit., pp. 55-6; according to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Vastu</span> iii.179, Upāli was the Buddha’s barber, too).

Upāli’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">upajjhāya</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kappiṭaka</span> (Vin.iv.308). When Upāli went to the Buddha for an exercise for meditation, he asked that he might be allowed to dwell in the forest. But the Buddha would not agree, for if Upāli went into the forest he would learn only meditation, while, if he remained amongst men, he would have knowledge both of meditation and of the word of the Dhamma. Upāli accepted the Buddha’s advice and, practising insight, in due course won <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship. The Buddha himself taught Upāli the whole of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vinayapiṭaka</span> (Thag­a.i.360f, 370; AN­a.i.172).

In the assembly of the Saṅgha, the Buddha declared him to be the most proficient of those who were learned in the Vinaya (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vinayadharānaṁ</span>) (AN.i.24; see also Vin.iv.142, where the Buddha is mentioned as speaking Upāli’s praises). He is often spoken of as having reached the pinnacle of the Vinaya, or as being its chief repository (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Vinaye agganikkhitto</span>) (e.g., Dpv.iv.3, 5; v.7, 9) and three particular cases – those of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajjuka</span> (Vin.iii.66f), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhārukacchaka</span> monk (Vin.iii.39) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumāra Kassapa</span> (AN­a.i.158; MN­a.i.336; Ja.i.148; Dhp­a.iii.145) – are frequently mentioned in this connection as instances where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli</span>’s decisions on Vinaya rules earned the special commendation of the Buddha. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> Council, Upāli took a leading part, deciding all the questions relative to the Vinaya, in the same way as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> decided questions regarding the Dhamma (Vin.ii.286f; DN­a.i.11f; Mhv.iii.30).

In accordance with this tradition, ascribing to Upāli especial authority regarding the rules of the Saṅgha, various instances are given of Upāli questioning the Buddha about the Vinaya regulations. Thus we find him consulting the Buddha as to the legality or otherwise of a complete congregation performing, in the absence of an accused monk, an act at which his presence is required (Vin.i.325f). Again, he wishes to know if, in a matter which has caused altercations and schisms among members of the Saṅgha, the Saṅgha declares re-establishment of concord without thorough investigation, could such a declaration be lawful? (Vin.i.358f). When a monk intends to take upon himself the conduct of any matter that has to be decided, under what conditions should he do so? What qualities should a monk possess in himself before he takes upon himself to warn others? (Vin.ii.248f). In what case can there be an interruption of the probationary period of a monk who has been placed on probation? (Vin.ii.33f).

A whole list of questions asked by Upāli and answers given by the Buddha on matters pertaining to the Vinaya rules is found in the chapter called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upālipañcaka</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parivāra</span> (Vin.v.180-206; see also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upālivagga</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> v.70ff).

It is not possible to determine which of these and other questions were actually asked by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli</span>, and which were ascribed to him on account of his traditional reputation.

It is said (e.g., Vin.iv.142; Vin-a.iv.876) that even in the Buddha’s lifetime monks considered it a great privilege to learn the Vinaya under Upāli. The monks seem to have regarded Upāli as their particular friend, to whom they could go in their difficulties. Thus, when certain monks had been deprived by thieves of their clothes, it is Upāli’s protection that they seek (Vin.iii.212; see also the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ramanīyavihārī</span>, Thag­a.i.116).

The canon contains but few records of any discourses connected with Upāli, apart from his questions on the Vinaya. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (AN.iv.143f) he is mentioned as asking the Buddha for a brief sermon, the Buddha telling him that if there were anything that did not conduce to revulsion and detachment, Upāli could be sure that such things did not form part of the Buddha’s teaching. There is a record of another sermon (AN.v.201ff) which the Buddha is stated to have preached when Upāli expressed the desire to retire into the solitude of the forest. The Buddha tells him that forest-life is not for the man who has not mastered his mind or attained to tranquillity.

For other sermons see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upālisutta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ubbāhikasutta</span>.

Three verses are ascribed to Upāli in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> (vv. 249-51; but see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Gotama the Man</span>, p.215; another verse ascribed to Upāli, but so far not traced elsewhere, is found in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milinda</span> p.108) where he admonishes the brethren to seek noble friends of unfaltering character, to learn the monks’ code of discipline and to dwell in solitude.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span>, Upāli was a very rich Brahmin named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujāta</span>. When the Buddha came to his father’s city in order to preach to him the Dhamma, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujāta</span> saw him, and in the assembly be noticed an ascetic named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunanda</span>, holding over the Buddha for seven days a canopy of flowers. The Buddha declared that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunanda</span> would, in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>, become famous as the Elder <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa Mantānīputta</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujāta</span>, too, wished to see the future <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>, and having heard <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span> praise the monk <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭika</span> as chief of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Vinayadharas</span>, he wished to hear, regarding himself, a similar declaration from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>. With this end in view he did many deeds of merit, chief of which was the erection of a monastery named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sobhana</span>, for the Buddha and his monks, at an expense of one hundred thousand.

As a result he was born in heaven for thirty thousand <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> and was one thousand times king of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>. One thousand times, too, he was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>.

Two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> ago there was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Khattiya</span> named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Añjasa</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli</span> was born as his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunanda</span>. One day he went to the park riding an elephant named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirika</span>, and met, on the way, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devala</span>, whom he insulted in various ways. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunanda</span> was, thereupon, seized with a sensation of great heat in his body, and it was not till he went with a large following to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> and asked his pardon that the sensation left him. It is said that if the Buddha had not forgiven him, the whole country would have been destroyed. This insult paid to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> was the cause of Upāli having been born as a barber in his last birth (Ap.i.37ff).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (Vin-a.i.272, 283) that while the Buddha was yet alive Upāli drew up certain instructions according to which future <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Vinayadharas</span> should interpret Vinaya rules, and that, in conjunction with others, he compiled explanatory notes on matters connected with the Vinaya.

In direct pupillary succession to Upāli as head of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Vinayadharas</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dāsaka</span>, whom Upāli had first met at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Valikārāma</span>, where Upāli was staying (Mhv.v.10). Upāli taught him the whole of the Vinaya.

Upāli’s death was in the sixth year of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyibhadda</span>’s reign. Dpv.v.7ff.
