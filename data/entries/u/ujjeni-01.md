# Ujjenī 01

## English source

Ujjenī 01. The capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>. In the Buddha’s time, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍappajjota</span> (Vin.i.276; Dhp­a.i.192) was king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span> and there was friendly intercourse between that city and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, whose king was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Seniya Bimbisāra</span>. After <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>’s death, however, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajjota</span> seems to have contemplated a war against <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>. See MN.iii.7.

There was an old trade-route from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> and the merchants of the two cities showed healthy rivalry not only in trade, but also in matters of culture. See, e.g., Ja.ii.248ff., where the merchants of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> compare their musician <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Guttila</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mūsila</span>, the chief fiddler of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>.

It was while going with a caravan to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>, that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa Kuṭikaṇṇa</span> (4) met the *Peta*, whose words made him decide to renounce household life (Ud­a.307f).

The road taken by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span>’s disciples ran through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span> (Sn.vs.1011).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span> was also the birthplace of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span> (Thag­a.i.483), of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidāsī</span> (Thīg.v.405), of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span> (Thag­a.41) and of the courtesan <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumavatī</span>, mother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayā</span> (Thīg­a.39).

Before succeeding to his father’s throne at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> reigned for several years as Viceroy at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>, and it was during this period that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghamittā</span> were born (Mhv.xiii.10ff; Mbv.99; Vin-a.i.70).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> spent six months in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇagirivihāra</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>, prior to his visit to Ceylon (Mhv.xiii.5).

From the same *vihāra* forty thousand monks were present, under the leadership of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Saṅgharakkhita</span>, at the foundation of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> (Mhv.xxix.35).

The Jātakas speak of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span> as having been the capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> from very ancient times, e.g., in Ja.iv.390, where Avanti Mahā Rāja rules in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span> as capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>. But in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Govindasutta</span> (DN.ii.235), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māhissatī</span> is mentioned as the capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>. Perhaps <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māhissatī</span> lost its importance later and gave place to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>, for we find <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māhissatī</span> mentioned just before <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span> among the places passed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span>’s pupils on their way to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> (Sn.vs.1011).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span> is identical with the Greek Ozene, about 77° E. and 23° n.(*Buddhist India*, p.40; see also CAGI.560, and Beal-Xuan.ii.270 for Xuanzang’s description of it).
