# Upāli 03

## English source

Upāli 03. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli Thera</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (i.91f) contains the story of a Thera named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli</span>, who is to be distinguished from the eminent disciple of that name, though the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> verses obviously point to a confusion of the legends of the two. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna Commentary</span> distinguishes this monk as “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Bhāgineyya</span> Upāli,” and states that he was a nephew of the Venerable <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli</span>. He was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> and was ordained by his uncle, who later became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Bhāgineyya</span> Upāli had been a householder in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span>. Later he left the world and became an ascetic in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span>. There he met the Buddha and the monks, and uttered their praises in song. As a result he was eighteen times king of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> and twenty-five times king of men.
