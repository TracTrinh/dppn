# Ubbirī Therī

## English source

Ubbirī Therī. She was born in the family of a very rich burgess of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> and was married to the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> (probably <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>). After a few years a daughter was born to her, whom she named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">v.l.</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvantī</span>). The king was so pleased with the child that he had <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ubbirī</span> anointed as queen. But the girl died soon afterwards, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ubbirī</span>, distracted, went daily to the charnel-field. One day, as she sat lamenting on the bank of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aciravatī</span>, the Buddha appeared before her in a ray of glory. Having listened to her story, the Master pointed out to her that in that same burial-ground, eighty-four thousand of her daughters, all named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvā</span>, had been burnt. Pondering on the Master’s words, she developed insight and became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>.

When she was a young girl in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsavatī</span> in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>, she was one day left alone in the house. Seeing an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> begging for alms, she invited him in and gave him food. As a result she was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>. Eighty times she reigned as queen in heaven and seventy times as queen among men (Thīg.vv.51-2; Thīg­a.53ff).

No mention is made in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Therīgāthā</span> Commentary of her having joined the Saṅgha, but the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (ii.525f) states that she did so.
