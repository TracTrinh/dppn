# Ummadantījātaka

## English source

Ummadantījātaka (Ja 527). The story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ummadantī</span>. The story was related in connection with a backsliding brother who, having seen a very beautiful woman as he was going on his alms-rounds in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, gave himself up to despair and had to be led to the Buddha for admonition (Ja.v.209ff).

The story is also found, with certain slight variations, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Jātakamālā</span> under the title of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ummādayantījātaka</span> (Ja xiii).

The tale of a maiden who made all who saw her mad, and of the love-smitten monarch who preferred walking in the right path and even meeting death itself to indulging in passion, is found also in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kathāsaritsāgara</span>, e.g., in the 15th, 23rd and 91st <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">taraṅga</span>.
