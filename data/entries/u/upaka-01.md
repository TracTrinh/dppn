# Upaka 01

## English source

Upaka 01. An <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ājīvaka</span> whom the Buddha met on his way between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā</span> and the Bodhi Tree, after he set out from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> for the preaching of the First Sermon. Upaka questioned the Buddha on his attainments, and when the Buddha told him what he had accomplished he asked the Buddha if he were “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anantajina</span>.” When the Buddha acknowledged it, Upaka shook his head saying, “It may be so, friend,” and went along by another road (Ja.i.81; Vin.i.8; MN.i.170-1; Dhp­a.iv.71-2). It is said (DN­a.ii.471) that the Buddha walked all the way from the Bodhi Tree to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> – instead of flying through the air, as is the custom of Buddhas – because he wished to meet Upaka.

After this meeting Upaka went to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅkahāra</span> country and there, having fallen desperately in love with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cāpā</span>, the daughter of a huntsman who looked after him, starved for seven days and in the end persuaded the huntsman to give her to him in marriage. For a living, Upaka hawked about the flesh brought by the huntsman. In due course <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cāpā</span> bore him a son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhadda</span>. When the baby cried, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cāpā</span> sang to him saying, “Upaka’s son, ascetic’s son, game-dealer’s boy, don’t cry,” thus mocking her husband. In exasperation he told her of his friend <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anantajina</span>, but she did not stop teasing him. One day, in spite of her attempts to keep him, he left her and went to the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>. The Buddha, seeing him coming, gave orders that anyone asking for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anantajina</span> should be brought to him. Having learnt from Upaka his story, the Buddha had him admitted to the Saṅgha. As a result of his meditation, Upaka became an *Anāgāmī* and was reborn in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avihā</span> heaven (Thīg­a.220ff; MN­a.i.388f. Upaka’s story is also given in Snp-a.i.258ff, with several variations in detail). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (i.35, 60) records a visit paid to the Buddha by Upaka and six other beings born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avihā</span>. According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhima Commentary</span> (i.389), Upaka became an *Arahant* as soon as he was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avihā</span>.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Therīgāthā</span> he is also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷa</span> (v.309. This may have been a term of affection used because of his dark colour) and his birth-place is given as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāla</span>, a village near the Bodhi Tree, where he is said to have been living with his wife at the time he left her (Thīg­a.225).

Later, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cāpā</span>, too, left the world and became an *Arahant* Therī.

The *Divyāvadāna* (p.393) calls him Upaka Upagana.

The enumeration of the Buddha’s virtues which was made to Upaka is not regarded as a real *dhammadesanā* because it took place before the preaching of the first sermon. It produced only a *vāsanā-bhāgiya* result, not *sekha-bhāgiya* or *nibaddha-bhāgiya* (Ud­a.54).

The words of the Buddha’s speech to Upaka are often quoted (e.g., Kv.289).
