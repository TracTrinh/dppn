# Uṇṇābha

## English source

Uṇṇābha. A Brahmin. He once visited the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> and asked him whether the five sense-faculties (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">indriyāni</span>), which were of different scope and range, had any common ground of resort (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">paṭisaraṇa</span>). The Buddha replies that the mind is their common resort and, in answer to further questions, explains that there is nothing beyond <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nibbāna</span>; that the holy life has <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nibbāna</span> for its ending.

When the Brahmin, greatly pleased with the Buddha’s teaching, goes away, the Buddha tells the monks that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uṇṇābha</span> has become an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmī</span> and would, therefore, after death, no longer return to this world (SN.v.217f).

The same <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nikāya</span> (SN.v.272f) records a visit of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uṇṇābha</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>. He asks <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> what is the aim of holy life and, on being told that it is the abandoning of desire by means of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jhāna</span>, suggests that it would be a task without end. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, by means of an illustration, explains how the task does come to an end, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uṇṇābha</span> expresses great satisfaction with the answer. Perhaps this refers to another Brahmin of the same name.
