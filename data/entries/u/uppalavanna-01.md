# Uppalavaṇṇā 01

## English source

Uppalavaṇṇā 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uppalavaṇṇā Therī</span>. One of the two chief women disciples of the Buddha. She was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> as the daughter of a banker, and she received the name of Uppalavaṇṇā because her skin was the colour of the heart of the blue lotus. When she was come of age, kings and commoners from the whole of India sent messengers to her father, asking for her hand. He, not wishing to offend any of them, suggested that Uppalavaṇṇā should leave the world. Because of her *upanissaya* (potential), she very willingly agreed and was ordained a nun. Soon it came to her turn to perform certain services in the *uposatha*-hall. Lighting the lamp, she swept the room. Taking the flame of the lamp as her visible object, she developed *tejokasiṇa* and, attaining to *jhāna*, became an *Arahant* possessed of the four special attainments (*paṭisambhidā*). She became particularly versed in the mystic potency of transformation (*iddhivikubbana*). When the Buddha arrived at the *Gaṇḍamba*-tree to perform the Twin Miracle, Uppalavaṇṇā offered to perform certain miracles herself, if the Buddha would give his consent, but this he refused (Thīg­a.190, 195). Later, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, in the assembly of the Saṅgha, he declared her to be the chief of the women possessed of *iddhi*-power (AN.i.25). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Therīgāthā</span> (vv.234-5) contains several verses attributed to her.

Three of them had been uttered in anguish by a mother who had been unwittingly living as her daughter’s rival with the man who later became the monk <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgātīriya</span> (*q.v.*). Uppalavaṇṇā repeated them to help her to reflect on the harm and vileness of sensual desires. Two others are utterances of joy on the distinctions she had won and another records a miracle she performed before the Buddha, with his consent. The rest contain a conversation between Uppalavaṇṇā and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> (a conversation, more or less identical with the foregoing, is recorded in SN.i.131f), wherein she tells him that she has passed completely beyond his power.

The books give several episodes connected with Uppalavaṇṇā. Once a young man named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, who was her cousin and had been in love with her during her lay-life, hid himself in her hut in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhavana</span> and, in spite of her protestations, deprived her of her chastity. It is said that he was swallowed up by the fires of *Avīci*. From that time onwards, nuns were forbidden to live in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhavana</span> (Dhp­a.ii.49f; the incident is referred to in Vin.iii.35). It is said (e.g., Dhp­a.iv.166f) that this incident gave rise to the question whether even *Arahants* enjoyed the pleasures of love and wished to gratify their passions. Why should they not? For they are not trees nor ant-hills, but living creatures with moist flesh. The Buddha most emphatically declared that thoughts of lust never entered the hearts of the saints. On another occasion, Uppalavaṇṇā came across, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhavana</span>, some meat left behind, obviously for her, by some kind-hearted thief; having cooked the meat, she took it to the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span>. Finding him away on his alms-rounds, she left the meat with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span>, who was looking after the *vihāra*, to be given to the Buddha, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span> insisted on Uppalavaṇṇā giving him her inner robe as a reward for his services (Vin.iii.208f).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp-a.iii.211), the miracle which Uppalavaṇṇā volunteered to perform at the *Gaṇḍamba*-tree, was the assumption of the form of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>, with a retinue extending for thirty-six leagues and the paying of homage to the Buddha, with all the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>’s followers, in the presence of the multitude.

Mention is made of a pupil of Uppalavaṇṇā, who followed the Buddha for seven years, learning the Vinaya (Vin.ii.261).

The Buddha declares that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uppalavaṇṇā</span> are the measure of his women disciples, and that the believing nun, if she would aspire perfectly, should aspire to be like them (AN.i.88; ii.164; SN.ii.236).

In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span>’s time Uppalavaṇṇā saw a woman disciple who was declared to be the best of those possessed of supernormal power, and wished for herself a similar rank in the dispensation of a future Buddha. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>, she was one of the seven daughters of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kikī</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, and having done many good deeds, was born in heaven. Later, she was born in the world of men and had to work for her own living. One day she gave to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>, who had just risen from *samādhi*, a meal of fried rice in his bowl and covered it with a beautiful lotus; the meal had been prepared for herself. The lotus she afterwards took back but again replaced it, asking the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>’s forgiveness. She expressed a wish that she should beget as many sons as there were grains of rice in her gift, and that lotuses should spring up under her feet as she walked. In her next birth she was born in a lotus. An ascetic adopted her as his daughter, but when she grew up, the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, hearing of her beauty, asked the ascetic for her hand and made her his chief queen, under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumavatī</span>. The king’s other wives were jealous of her beauty, and when the king was away, quelling a rising of the border tribes, they concealed in caskets the five hundred sons, chief of whom was the prince <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Paduma</span> (*q.v.*), that were born to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumavatī</span>, and told the king that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumavatī</span> was a non-human and had given birth to a log of wood. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumavatī</span> was sent away in disgrace, but later, through the instrumentality of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, the trick was exposed, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumavatī</span> regained all her former power and glory (her temporary downfall was due to her having withdrawn her gift of a lotus to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>.) Later, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Paduma</span> and his brothers became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumavatī</span> died of a broken heart and was born in a village outside <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. There some of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> who had been her sons discovered her, and they all came to a meal at her house. At the conclusion of the meal she offered them blue lotuses, and expressed the wish that her complexion should be like the matrix of the blue lotus.

This account is a summary of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Therīgāthā</span> Commentary, pp.182ff; AN­a.i.188ff; but see also Dhp­a.ii.48f.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> account of the past lives of Uppalavaṇṇā differs from the above in several details (ii.551. But vv.1-15 quoted in the Thīg­a. differ from those in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>, and agree with the Thīg­a. account). According to this account, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span>’s time she was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> maiden named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vimalā</span> and was impressed by the *iddhi*-powers displayed by a nun, hence her wish for similar powers. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> also mentions Uppalavaṇṇā’s birth as the daughter of a banker of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī</span>. She gave great alms to the Buddha and the monks and made offerings of lotuses. She was the second daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kikī</span> and her name was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samaṇaguttā</span>. In her next birth she became the ravishing daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tirīṭavaccha</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariṭṭhapura</span>. In her last birth she became an *Arahant* within a fortnight of her ordination.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uppalavaṇṇā</span>’s name occurs several times in the Jātakas.

- In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kharādiyajātaka</span> (Ja.i.160) she was a deer, the sister of the Bodhisatta;
- in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Tipallatthamigajātaka</span> (Ja.i.164) she was the mother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Rāhula</span>, then born as a stag.
- She is identified with the old woman, the foster-mother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Ayyakālaka</span> (Ja.i.196),
- with the queen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mudulakkhaṇā</span> (Ja.i.306),
- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Brāhmiṇī</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sārambha</span> (Ja.i.375),
- the courtesan in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kurudhamma</span> (Ja.ii.381),
- the Brahmin’s daughter (and sister of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Rāhula</span>) in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Dhonasākha</span> (Ja.iii.168),
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Siridevī</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sirikālakaṇṇi</span> (Ja.iii.264),
