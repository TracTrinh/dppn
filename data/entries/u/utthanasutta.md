# Utthāṇasutta

## English source

Utthāṇasutta. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> was once staying in the upper storey of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāramātupāsāda</span> when he heard the new entrants to the Saṅgha, in the cells below, making a great uproar, talking about the food they had eaten, and other such worldly topics. The Buddha desired <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> to come and, when he appeared, the Buddha asked him to frighten the monks by a display of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span>-power.

By his psychic power <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> caused the whole building to rock to and fro like a ship, and when the monks, in terror, sought the Buddha’s protection, he explained to them that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> gave them the fright as a lesson to them to lead active and energetic lives, for death lays hold of the slothful.

The monks having listened to the Buddha’s sermon, concentrated their minds on it, and soon after became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span>. Sn.vv.331-4; Snp-a.i.336f; cf. SN.v.269ff. See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāsādakampanasutta</span>.
