# Uruvelā Kassapa

## English source

Uruvelā Kassapa. One of three brothers, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tebhātikajaṭilas</span>, living at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span>. He lived on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nerañjarā</span> with five hundred disciples. Further down the river lived his brothers <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nadī Kassapa</span> with three hundred disciples and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā Kassapa</span> with two hundred.

The Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā Kassapa</span> and took lodging for the night where the sacred fire was kept, in spite of Kassapa’s warning that the spot was inhabited by a fierce <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>. The Buddha, by his magical powers, overcame, first this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> and then another, both of whom vomited fire and smoke. Kassapa being pleased with this exhibition of *iddhi*-power, undertook to provide the Buddha with his daily food. Meanwhile the Buddha stayed in a grove near by, waiting for the time when Kassapa should be ready for conversion. Here he was visited by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Four Regent Gods</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, *Brahma* and others. The Buddha spent the whole rainy season there, performing, in all, three thousand five hundred miracles of various kinds, reading the thoughts of Kassapa, splitting firewood for the ascetics’ sacrifices, heating stoves for them to use after bathing in the cold weather, etc. Still Kassapa persisted in the thought, “The great ascetic is of great magic power, but he is not an *Arahant* like me.” Finally the Buddha decided to startle him by declaring that he was not an *Arahant*, neither did the way he followed lead to *Arahant*-ship. Thereupon Kassapa owned defeat and reverently asked for ordination. The Buddha asked him to consult with his pupils, and they cut off their hair and threw it with their sacrificial utensils into the river and were all ordained. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nadī Kassapa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā Kassapa</span> came to inquire what had happened, and they, too, were ordained with their pupils. At <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayāsīsa</span> the Buddha preached to them the Fire Sermon (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ādittapariyāya</span>), and they all attained *Arahant*-ship.

From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayāsīsa</span> the Buddha went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapas</span> and their pupils, and in the presence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> and the assembled populace <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā Kassapa</span> declared his allegiance to the Buddha. This story of the conversion of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapas</span> is given in Vin.i.24ff and in AN­a.i.165f; also in Thag­a.i.434ff.

Later, in the assembly of monks, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā Kassapa</span> was declared to be the chief of those who had large followings (*aggaṁ mahāparisānaṁ*) (AN.i.25). Six verses attributed to him are found in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> (vv.375-80), wherein he reviews his achievement and relates how he was won over by the Buddha.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> he was a householder, and having seen the Buddha declare a monk (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīhaghosa</span> was his name, Ap.ii.481) to be the best of them with large followings, wished for himself to be so honoured in a future life, and did many works of merit towards that end.

Later, he was born in the family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phussa Buddha</span> as his younger step-brother, his father being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> (according to Bv.xix.14, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phussa</span>’s father was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayasena</span>). He had two other brothers. The three quelled a frontier disturbance and, as a reward, obtained the right to entertain the Buddha for three months. They appointed three of their ministers to make all the arrangements and they themselves observed the ten precepts. The three ministers so appointed were, in this age, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Raṭṭhapāla</span>.

Having sojourned among gods and men, the three brothers, in their last birth, were born in a Brahmin family, the name of which was Kassapa. They learnt the three Vedas and left the household life (AN­a.i.165f; Dhp­a.i.83ff; Ap.ii.481ff).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāradakassapajātaka</span> (Ja 544, Ja.vi.220ff; Ap.ii.483), Uruvelā Kassapa was once born as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgati</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span> country. He listened to the teachings of a false teacher called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Guṇa</span> and gave himself up to pleasure, till he was saved by his wise daughter <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rujā</span>, with the help of the *Brahma* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span>, who was the Bodhisatta.

Uruvelā Kassapa was so-called partly to distinguish him from other Kassapas and partly because he was ordained at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span>. At first he had one thousand followers, and after he was ordained by the Buddha all his followers stayed with him and each of them ordained a great number of others, so that their company became very numerous (AN­a.i.166).

The scene of the conversion of Uruvelā Kassapa is sculptured in Sanchi. According to Tibetan sources, Kassapa was one hundred and twenty years old at the time of his conversion (Rockhill, op.cit., 40).

Xuanzang found a *stūpa* erected on the spot where the Buddha converted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> (Beal-Xuan.ii.130).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Belaṭṭhasīsa</span> was a disciple of Uruvelā Kassapa and joined his teacher when the latter was converted (Thag­a.i.67). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Senaka</span> Thera was Kassapa’s sister’s son (Thag­a.i.388). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vacchapāla</span> was among those who joined the Saṅgha, after having seen Kassapa pay homage to the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> (Thag­a.i.159).
