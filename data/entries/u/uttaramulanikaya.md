# Uttaramūlanikāya

## English source

Uttaramūlanikāya. One of the fraternities of monks in Ceylon, an off-shoot of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> sect. Their headquarters were probably at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttaroḷa</span> *Vihāra*, built by King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māṇavamma</span>, and given to the monks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri Vihāra</span>, for having consented to take into the Saṅgha his elder brother, in spite of the fact that he had lost one eye as the result of some yoga practices.

The first chief of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttaroḷa</span> was the king’s brother himself and he was in charge of six hundred monks. He was granted great honours and privileges together with five classes of servants to minister to him. He was also appointed to supervise the guardians of the Tooth Relic (Cv.lvii.7-11, and 16-26; also Geiger’s Trs.i.194, n.2 and 3).

From a Tamil inscription of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māṇavamma</span> we find that he kept up his patronage of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttaramūlanikāya</span>, and it is recorded that he gave over the custodianship of the Tooth Relic to a monk of this fraternity, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> (Epi. Zey., vol.ii., pt. vi., pp.250ff).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span>, author of the *Anuruddhaśataka* and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhammatthasaṅgaha</span>, describes himself in the colophon to the former work as an “*Upasthavira*” of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttaramūlanikāya</span>.

In later years this *Nikāya* produced many an illustrious star in Celyon’s literary firmament, among them the grammarian <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vilgammūla</span> *Mahā* Thera and Sri <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span>. For details about them see PLC., *passim*.
