# Ussada

## English source

Ussada. A *Niraya*. It resembled a city with four gates and a wall. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mittavindaka</span>, arriving at Ussaka in his wanderings, saw there a man supporting a wheel as sharp as a razor, which to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mittavindaka</span> appeared like a lotus-flower. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mittavindaka</span> took it from him, and realising then what it was, tried to escape, but was unsuccessful. This was the suffering undergone by those who had smitten their mothers. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, during a visit to Ussaka, saw <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mittavindaka</span>, but could do nothing for him (Ja.iv.3f; iii.206f).

Ussada was considered a place of great suffering (e.g., Ja.iv.403), and also a place where those who, having promised a gift fail to give it, are born (Ja.iv.405). Once the Bodhisatta was born in Ussada, for cruelty during his reign as king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, and he suffered for eighty thousand years (Ja.vi.2). Beings born there have their tongues pierced with glowing hooks and are dragged about on a floor of heated metal (Ja.vi.112).

In the scholiast to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Matakabhattajātaka</span> (Ja.i.168) reference is made to sixteen *Ussada-nirayā*.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revatī</span> (*q.v.*) was once cast into *Ussada-nirayā*. Vv-a.223.
