# Ukkaṭṭhā

## English source

Ukkaṭṭhā. A town in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>, near the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span>. It has been given, free from all taxes (as *brahmadeyya*), to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pokkharasāti</span> by the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>, in recognition of the former’s skill. It was thickly populated and had much grassland, woodland and corn (DN.i.87; DN­a.i.245). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Icchānaṅgala</span> wood was in the neighbourhood, and when the Buddha was staying in the wood <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pokkharasāti</span> first sent his pupil <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭha</span> and then went himself to visit the Buddha (see the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭhasutta</span>).

There was a road which connected <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkaṭṭhā</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setavyā</span> (AN.ii.37) and with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> (Ja.ii.259). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chatta</span> goes from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setavyā</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkaṭṭhā</span> to learn under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pokkharasāti</span> (Vv-a.229).

It was in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhagavana</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkaṭṭhā</span> that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mūlapariyāyasutta</span> (MN.i.1ff) was preached and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mūlapariyāyajātaka</span> (Ja.ii.259ff) was related in connection with it. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkaṭṭhā</span> was the residence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgaṇika Bhāradvāja</span> (Thag­a.339).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> explains (MN­a.i.9; AN­a.ii.504) that the city was so-called because it was built by the light of torches (*ukkā*) at night, in order that it might be completed within the auspicious time.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmanimantikasutta</span> (MN.i.326; but see SN.i.142; Ja.iii.359), the Buddha says that it was while he was residing at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhagavana</span> that be became aware of the erroneous views of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Baka</span>-*Brahma* and went to the *Brahma*-world to teach <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Baka</span> the truth. The *Divyāvadāna* calls the city <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkatā</span> (p.621).
