# Upaka 02

## English source

Upaka 02. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upaka Maṇḍikāputta</span>. He once visited the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span> and stated before him his view that whoever starts abusive talk of another, without being able to make good his case, is blameworthy. The Buddha agrees and says that Upaka himself has been guilty of this offence. The Commentary (AN­a.ii.554) explains that Upaka was a supporter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>. Upaka protests against being caught in a big noose of words, like a fish caught as soon as he pops up his head. The Buddha explains that it is necessary for him to teach with endless variations of words and similes. Upaka is pleased with the Buddha’s talk and reports the conversation to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>. The king shows his anger at the man’s presumption in having remonstrated with the Buddha (AN.ii.181f), and the Commentary adds that he had him seized by the neck and cast out.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (AN­a.ii.554-5) that Upaka went to visit the Buddha in order to find out whether the Buddha would blame him for being a supporter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>. According to others, he came to abuse the Buddha because he had heard that the Buddha had consigned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> to hell. He was apparently of low caste, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> addresses him as “salt-worker’s boy” (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">loṇakārakadāraka</span>) (AN.ii.182).
