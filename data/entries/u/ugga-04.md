# Ugga 04

## English source

Ugga 04. *v.l.* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uggagahapati</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uggata</span>. A householder of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthigāma (Hatthigāmaka)</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjī</span> country. Among householders he was declared by the Buddha to be the best of those who waited on the Saṅgha (*saṅghupaṭṭhākānaṁ*) (AN.i.26). On his father’s death he was appointed to the post of *seṭṭhi*. Once when the Buddha went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthigāma</span> during a tour and was staying in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgavanuyyāna</span> there, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span> came to the pleasance, with dancers, at the conclusion of a drinking-feast of seven days’ duration. At the sight of the Buddha he was seized with great shame and his intoxication vanished. The Buddha preached to him and he became an *Anāgāmī*. Thereupon he dismissed his dancers and devoted himself to looking after members of the Saṅgha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> visited him at night and told him of the attainments of various monks, suggesting that he should choose only the eminent ones as the recipients of his gifts. But what he gave, he gave to all with equal delight (AN­a.i.214-5).

The Buddha once stated that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span> was possessed of eight special and wonderful qualities. One of the monks, hearing the Buddha’s statement, went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span> and asked him what these qualities were. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span> replied that he was not aware of what the Buddha had in mind and proceeded to explain eight wonderful things that had happened to him, viz.:

- (1) As soon as he saw the Buddha, his state of drunkenness vanished and he made obeisance to the Buddha, who talked to him on various topics, such as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t23">dāna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t23">sīla</span>, etc.
- (2) When the Buddha saw that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Ugga</span>’s mind was ready, he preached to him the Four Truths, which he understood and realised.
- (3) He had had four young and beautiful wives; when he took the vow of celibacy, he made ample provision for them; for one of them he obtained the husband of her choice, because she so desired, and this he did with no tinge of jealousy.
- (4) All his immense wealth he shared with men of good and lovely conduct.
- (5) On whatever monk he waited, he did it with whole-heartedness; to the monk’s preaching he listened earnestly; if the monk did not preach, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Ugga</span> himself taught him the doctrine.
- (6) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Devas</span> told him of the different attainments of various monks, but he gave to all alike, without distinction.
- (7) He felt no pride that he should hold converse with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Devas</span>.
- (8) He did not worry about death because the Buddha had assured him that he would never more return to this world.

The monk reports this conversation to the Buddha and the Buddha tells him that these were the very qualities he had in mind when praising <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span> (AN.iv.212-6).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (SN.iv.109f) records a visit paid to the Buddha by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span>, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthigāmaka</span>. He asked the Buddha why it was that some beings attained full freedom in this very life, while others did not. Because of grasping, says the Buddha.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span> had been a householder in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>. He once heard the Buddha preach and declare, at the end of his sermon, one of his lay disciples to be the best of those who waited on the Saṅgha. He wished for himself a similar attainment and did many good deeds towards that end (AN­a.i.214).
