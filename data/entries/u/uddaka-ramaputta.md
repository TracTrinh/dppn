# Uddaka Rāmaputta

## English source

Uddaka Rāmaputta. One of the teachers under whom <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>, after leaving the world and before he became the Buddha, received instruction (Ja.i.66, 81). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uddaka</span> taught him the doctrine which had been realised and proclaimed by his father <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāma</span>, which was the attainment of the state of “neither-perception-nor-non-perception” (corresponding to the fourth <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arūpajjhāna</span>). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> had mastered this, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uddaka</span> made him more than his own equal by setting him over the whole company of his disciples as their teacher. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>, finding this doctrine unsatisfactory, abandoned it (MN.i.165ff., 240ff.; Dhp­a.i.70-1).

The Buddha evidently had a high regard for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uddaka Rāmaputta</span>, for after the Awakening, when looking for someone to whom the Dhamma might be preached, and who was capable of realising its import at once, his thoughts turned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uddaka</span>, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uddaka</span> was already dead (Vin.i.7).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakārasutta</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (ii.180) it is mentioned that King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Eḷeyya</span>, together with his bodyguard, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggalla</span> and others, were followers of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāmaputta</span> and that they held him in great esteem.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (iv.83f) the Buddha says that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uddaka</span> claimed to be “versed in lore and to have conquered everything, digging out the root of suffering,” though he had no justification for such a claim.

Again, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāsādikasutta</span> (DN.iii.126-7), the Buddha tells <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span> that when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uddaka</span> said “seeing, he seeth not,” he had in mind a man who saw the blade of a sharpened razor but not its edge – a low, pagan thing to speak about.

In the Sanskrit books <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uddaka Rāmaputta</span> is called Udraka. Mvu.ii.119-20; Divy.392; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Lalitavistara</span> 306f.
