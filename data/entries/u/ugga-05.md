# Ugga 05

## English source

Ugga 05. A householder of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, declared by the Buddha to be the best of those who gave agreeable gifts (*manāpadāyakānaṁ*) (AN.i.26; in SN­a.iii.26 he is wrongly described as *aggo paṇītadāyakānaṁ* – the title of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span>).

His original name is not known. He came to be called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span> *seṭṭhi*, because he was tall in body, lofty in morals and of striking personality. The very first time he saw the Buddha, he became a *Sotāpanna* and later an *Anāgāmī*. When he was old, the thought came to him one day, while he was alone, “I will give to the Buddha whatever I consider most attractive to myself and I have heard from him that such a giver obtains his wishes. I wish the Buddha would come to my house now.” The Buddha, reading his thoughts, appeared before his door with a following of monks. He received them with great respect and, having given them a meal, announced to the Buddha his intention of providing him and the monks with whatever they found agreeable (AN­a.i.213-4).

While staying at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kūṭāgārasālā</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, the Buddha once declared to the monks that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span> was possessed of eight marvellous qualities. The rest of the story is very similar to that of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthigāmaka</span>, given above. This <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span> states as the first wonderful thing which happened to him, the faith he found in the Buddha at their very first meeting; three and four are the same; the fifth is that whatever monk he waits on, he does it whole-heartedly; the sixth, that if the monk preaches he would listen with attention, if the monk does not preach, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span> would teach to him the doctrine; the seventh is the same; the eighth that he has got rid of all the *orambhāgiya-saṁyojanas* mentioned by the Buddha. The conversation is reported to the Buddha who agrees that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span> does possess the qualities mentioned (AN.iv.208-12).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (SN.iv.109f) repeats under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> the same discussion with the Buddha as was given in connection with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthigāma</span>, regarding the reason why some beings do not attain complete freedom in this very life. This is perhaps due to uncertainty on the part of the compilers as to which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span> took part in the original discussion.

A *Sutta* in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (AN.iii.49-51) gives a list of things of which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span> himself was fond. We are told that he offered these things to the Buddha. The list includes rice-cakes made in the shape of *Sāla*-blossoms, the flesh of sucking pig and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span> robes. These and other things were given not only to the Buddha, but, according to the Commentary (AN­a.ii.602), also to five hundred monks. The *Sutta* goes on to say that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span> died soon after and was born among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manomayadevā</span>. He visited the Buddha from the *Deva*-world and stated that he had achieved his goal (of reaching *Arahant*-ship).

He is included in a list of householders who possessed six special qualities: unwavering loyalty to the Buddha, the Dhamma and the Saṅgha, Ariyan conduct, insight and liberation (AN.iv.451).

His desire to become chief of those who give agreeable things was first conceived in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>, when he was a householder in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsavatī</span>; he heard the Buddha describe one of his disciples as being a giver of such gifts (AN­a.i.213).
