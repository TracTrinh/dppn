# Ukkhepakaṭa Vaccha Thera

## English source

Ukkhepakaṭa Vaccha Thera. He was the son of a Brahmin of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaccha</span> family. Having heard the Buddha preach, he entered the Saṅgha and dwelt in a village settlement in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>. He learnt the doctrine from the various monks who came there from time to time, but it was not until he learnt from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> that he was able to distinguish between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Vinaya</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span>. He thus became versed in the Three <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Piṭakas</span> even before the First Council (On this see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brethren</span>, 66. n.1). He practised meditation and soon attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship (Thag.v.65; Thag­a.i.147f). Later he became a teacher of the doctrine. According to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapāla</span> (Thag­a.i.149), the soubriquet <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ukkhepakaṭa</span> was given to him because he was able to teach and recite passages from the three <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Piṭakas</span> “casting them in their proper setting, according as they belonged to each <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Piṭaka</span>.” The title was meant to emphasise his eminent repertory of orally-learnt doctrine.

He had been a householder in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siddhattha Buddha</span> and had helped a guild who built a hall for the Buddha by giving them a pillar for the building.

Fifty-five <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> ago he was a king named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasodhara</span> and twenty-one <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> ago another king named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>. His seven-storied palaces were all built on one pillar. He is probably to be identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekatthambhika</span> Thera of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (i.56-7).
