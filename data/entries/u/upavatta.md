# Upavatta

## English source

Upavatta (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upavattana</span>). The *sāla*-grove of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusināra</span>, on the further side of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hiraññavatī</span>. This was the last resting-place of the Buddha on his last tour, and here he passed away, lying on a bed placed between two *sāla* trees (DN.ii.137ff; Dpv.xv.70).

Here <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhadda</span> visited the Buddha in the earlier part of the last night of his life, was converted and gained admission into the Saṅgha, afterwards winning *Arahant*-ship (see also Dhp­a.iii.377). It was here, too, that the Buddha asked the monks if they had any doubts they wished to hear solved regarding the Buddha, the Dhamma and the Saṅgha, *magga* and *paṭipadā*, or any questions they wished to ask (AN.ii.79), and here he gave his last admonition to the monks (SN.i.157; see also Ud.37f). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> tried to persuade him to die in a place of greater importance, and the Buddha, in order to disabuse his mind, preached to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sudassanasutta</span> (DN.ii.169f).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (DN­a.ii.572f) that the road to the *sāla*-grove from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hiraññavatī</span> led from the further bank of the river, like the road from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kadambanadī</span> to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thūpārāma</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> which led through the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājamātuvihāra</span>. The row of *sāla*-trees stretched from south to east and then continued to the north (“like the chief street in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>”). Hence the name <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upavattana</span>. The grove was to the southwest of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusināra</span>. Ud­a.238.
