# Udena 01

## English source

Udena 01. King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>. He was the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parantapa</span>. His mother, when pregnant with him, was carried off by a monster-bird and deposited on a tree near the residence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Allakappa</span>. The child was born in a storm (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">utu</span>?) – hence the name. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Allakappa</span>, having discovered the mother and child, took them under his protection. One day, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> was grown up, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Allakappa</span> saw by the conjunction of the planets that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parantapa</span> had died. When he announced the news, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>’s mother revealed to him her identity. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Allakappa</span> taught <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> the various charms he knew for taming elephants and sent him to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>, with a large following of elephants, to claim the kingdom. Some time after he became king, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> appointed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span> as his treasurer, and one day, having seen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span>’s adopted daughter, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>, going to the river to bathe, sent for her and married her. Later he married, in very romantic circumstances, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsuladattā</span>, daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍappajjota</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp-a.i.161ff) contains a whole story-cycle of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> from which these details, except where otherwise stated, are taken. For details of other persons mentioned in the article and their encounters with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>, see under their respective names.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> had another wife, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span>, who took advantage of her new position to wreak vengeance on the Buddha for having once slighted her. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> was converted to the Buddha’s faith by her handmaiden <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khujjuttarā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span> tried to poison the king’s mind against her, but the attempt was frustrated, though <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> very nearly lost her life at the king’s hand. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> realised how grievously he had wronged her, he promised to grant her a boon, and, as the result of her choice, the Buddha sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> with five hundred monks to the palace every day, to preach to the women of the court. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> himself does not seem to have been interested in religion. Once when he discovered that the women of the court had given five hundred costly robes to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, he was annoyed, but when in answer to his questions <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> explained to him that nothing given to members of the Saṅgha was wasted, he was pleased and himself made a similar offering of robes to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>. Mentioned also in Vin.ii.291. The incident took place after the Buddha’s death.

His encounter in his park the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udakavana</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍola Bhāradvāja</span>, in somewhat similar circumstances, did not, however, end so happily. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>’s women had given <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍola</span> their robes, and when the king questioned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍola</span> as to the appropriateness of the gift, he remained silent. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> threatened to have him bitten by red ants; but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍola</span> vanished through the air (Snp-a.ii.514-5; SN­a.iii.27; in a previous birth too, as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇḍavya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> had been guilty of abusing holy men, see the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅgajātaka</span> (Ja 497), Ja.iv.375ff). Later (SN.iv.110f) we find him visiting <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍola</span> again on friendly terms and receiving information as to how young members of the Saṅgha succeeded in curbing their passions in spite of their youth. In this context <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> calls himself a follower of the Buddha.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> had a son named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bodhi</span> (Ja.iii.157), among whose activities the building of a palace, called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokanada</span>, is specially recorded. It is clear from the incident of the presentation of robes to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, referred to above, as well as by a definite statement to that effect contained in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Petavatthu</span> Commentary (p.140), that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> survived the Buddha; but whether his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bodhi</span> succeeded him or not is not known.

Among <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>’s possessions mention is made of his bow, requiring one thousand men to string it (Dhp­a.i.216), and of his elephant <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddavatikā</span> (Ja.iv.384).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> is sometimes referred to as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṁsarājā</span> (king of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṁsas</span>) (e.g., Ja.iv.375; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Divyāvadāna</span> e.g., 528, calls him <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vatsarājā</span>), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṁsas</span> or the Vacchas being the inhabitants of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> Commentary (p.382) he is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjirājā</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span> (p.291) tells a story of a woman called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gopālamātā</span>, who became a queen of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>. She was the daughter of peasant-folk, and, being poor, she sold her hair for eight pennies, with which she gave a meal to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span> and his seven companions. That very day she became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>’s queen.

Chưa dịch.
