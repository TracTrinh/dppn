# Uttara 01

## English source

Uttara 01. A Thera. He was the son of an eminent Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> (of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, according to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>). He became proficient in Vedic lore and renowned for his breeding, beauty, wisdom and virtue. The king’s minister, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span>, seeing his attainments, desired to marry him to his daughter; but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span>, with his heart set on release, declined, and learnt the Dhamma under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>. Later he entered the Saṅgha and waited on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>.

One day <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> fell ill and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span> set out early to find a physician. On the way he set down his bowl by a lake and went down to wash his mouth. A certain thief, pursued by the police, dropped his stolen jewels into the novice’s bowl and fled. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span> was brought before <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> who, to satisfy his grudge, ordered him to be impaled. The Buddha, seeing the ripeness of his insight, went to him and placing a gentle hand, “like a shower of crimson gold,” on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span>’s head, spoke to him and encouraged him to reflection. Transported with joy and rapture at the Master’s touch, he attained sixfold <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">abhiññā</span> and became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. Rising from the stake, he stood in mid­air and his wound was healed. Addressing his fellow-celibates, be told them how, when he realised the evils of rebirth, he forgot the lesser evil of present pain (Thag.vv.121-2; Thīg­a.i.240ff).

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumedha Buddha</span>, he had been a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Vijjādhara</span>. Once, while flying through the air, he saw the Buddha at the foot of a tree in the forest and, being glad, offered him three <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kaṇikāra</span> flowers.

By the Buddha’s power, the flowers stood above him forming a canopy. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Vijjādhara</span> was later born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, where his palace was known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇikāra</span>.

He was king of the gods one hundred and five times, and king of men one hundred and three times.

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (quoted in Thīg­a.), he became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> at the age of seven. This does not agree with the rest of the story and is probably due to a confusion with some other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span> is probably to be identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tīṇikaṇikārapupphiya</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>. Ap.ii.441ff. Ras.i.52f.
