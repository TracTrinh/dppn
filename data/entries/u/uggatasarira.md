# Uggatasarīra

## English source

Uggatasarīra. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Sāla</span> Brahmin, so-called because he was tall in person and eminent in wealth (AN­a.ii.714). Having made preparations for a great sacrifice, in which numerous animals were to be slaughtered, he visited the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> to consult him as to the efficacy of the sacrifice. Three times he told the Buddha that he had heard that the laying down (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ādhāna</span>) of the fire and the setting up (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ussāpana</span>) of the sacrificial post bore great fruit. Three times the Buddha agreed that it was so, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uggatasarīra</span> was about to conclude that the Buddha approved of his sacrifice, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> intervened and suggested that the Buddha should be asked to explain his meaning and to give his advice as to the efficacy of the sacrifice. The Buddha thereupon declared that there were three fires to be cast off: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">rāga</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dosa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">moha</span>; and three fires that should be honoured: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">āhuneyyaggi</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gahapataggi</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dakkhiṇeyyaggi</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">āhuneyyaggi</span> was represented by the parents; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gahapataggi</span>, by wife, children, servants and retainers; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dakkhiṇeyyaggi</span>, by holy men and recluses.

At the end of the discourse, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uggatasarīra</span> became a convert to the Buddha’s faith and set free the animals destined for the sacrifice. AN.iv.416.
