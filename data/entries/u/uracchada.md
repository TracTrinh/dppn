# Uracchadā

## English source

Uracchadā. One of the nine daughters of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">King Kikī</span>. She was born with the semblance of a necklet upon her neck and shoulders, as though drawn by a painter, hence her name, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uracchadā</span>. When sixteen years old she heard <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> preach, and became a *Sotāpanna*. That same day she attained *Arahant*-ship, entered the Saṅgha and passed into *Nibbāna* (Ja.vi.481).

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī</span>, both <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uracchadā</span> and Queen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māyā</span> (mother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>) were born as the daughters of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhumā</span>. One day the king received a present of a golden wreath, worth a thousand, and a box of precious sandalwood. He gave the sandalwood to the elder daughter and the wreath to the younger. The two girls, wishing to present their gifts to the Buddha, obtained the king’s consent. The elder princess powdered the sandalwood and filled a golden box with it. The younger had the wreath made into a necklet and placed it in a golden casket. They then went to the Buddha, and the elder reverently sprinkled his body with sandalwood and scattered it in his cell with the prayer, “May I, in time to come, be the mother of a Buddha like you.” The younger reverently placed the necklet on the Buddha and prayed, “Until I attain *Arahant*-ship, may this ornament never part from my body.” (Ja.vi.481)

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vimānavatthu</span> Commentary (pp. 270f), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uracchadā</span>’s name was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uracchadamālā</span> and her teacher was a Brahmin named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gopāla</span>, who was also present when the Buddha preached to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uracchadamālā</span>. But he did not acquire any special attainments.
