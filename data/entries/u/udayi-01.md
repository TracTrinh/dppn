# Udāyī 01

## English source

Udāyī 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī Thera</span>. Also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lāḷudāyī</span> (and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍita Udāyī</span>), to distinguish him from others. He was the son of a Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>. He saw the power and majesty of the Buddha when the latter visited his kinsmen and, entering the Saṅgha, in due course became an *Arahant*. When the Buddha preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgopamasutta</span> (see AN.iii.344f), on the occasion when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Seta</span>, King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>’s elephant, was publicly admired, Udāyī was stirred to enthusiasm by thoughts of the Buddha and uttered sixteen verses, extolling the virtues of the Buddha, comparing him to a great and wondrous elephant. (Thag.vv.689-704; Thag­a.ii.7f.; Udāyī’s verses are repeated in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttara</span> (iii.346-7) but the Commentary (ii.669) attributes them to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷudāyī</span>).

Once when Udāyī was staying at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāmaṇḍā</span>, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Todeyya</span>’s mango-grove, he converted a pupil of a Brahmin of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verahaccāni</span> clan and, as a result, was invited by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verahaccānī</span> herself to her house. It was only on his third visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verahaccānī</span> that Udāyī preached to her and she thereupon became a follower of the Faith (SN.iv.121-4).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (iv.166f.; another discussion with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> is mentioned in AN.iv.426f) also records a conversation between Udāyī and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, when Udāyī asks if it is possible to describe the consciousness, too, as being without the self. On another occasion Udāyī has a discussion with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcakaṅga</span> on *vedanā* (MN.i.396ff; SN.iv.223-4; the Commentary SN­a.iii.86 and MN­a.ii.629 here describes Udāyī as “*Paṇḍita*”). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> overhears their conversation and reports it to the Buddha, who says that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span>’s explanation is true, though not accepted by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcakaṅga</span>.

Elsewhere (SN.v.86ff) Udāyī is mentioned as asking the Buddha to instruct him on the *bojjhaṅgas*, and once, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Desaka</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setaka</span>?) in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumbha</span> country, he tells the Buddha how he cultivated the *bojjhaṅgas* and thereby attained to final emancipation (SN.v.89).

He is rebuked by the Buddha for his sarcastic remark to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> had failed to benefit by his close association with the Master. The Buddha assures him that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> will, in that very life, become an *Arahant* (AN.i.228).

Udāyī was evidently a clever and attractive preacher, for he is mentioned as having addressed large crowds, a task demanding great powers, as the Buddha himself says when this news of Udāyī is reported to him (AN.iii.184).

According to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (DN­a.iii.903), it is this same Udāyī (*Mahā Udāyī*) who, having listened to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sampasādaniyasutta</span>, is beside himself with joy at the contemplation of the wonderful qualities as set forth in that *Sutta*, and marvels that the Buddha does not go about proclaiming them. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (MN­a.i.526) seems to identify him also with the Udāyī to whom the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Laṭukikopamasutta</span> (MN.i.447ff) was preached.
