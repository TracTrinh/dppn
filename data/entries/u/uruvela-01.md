# Uruvelā 01

## English source

Uruvelā 01. A locality on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nerañjarā</span>, in the neighbourhood of the Bodhi-tree at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhagayā</span>. Here, after leaving <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uddaka</span>, the Bodhisatta practised during six years the most severe penances. His companions were the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcavaggiya</span> monks, who, however, left him when he relaxed the severity of his austerities (MN.i.166). The place chosen by the Bodhisatta for his penances was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Senānigama</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> version (Ja.i.67f) contains additional particulars. It relates that once the Bodhisatta fainted under his austerities, and the news was conveyed to his father that he was dead. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span>, however, refused to believe this, remembering the prophecy of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷadevala</span>. When the Bodhisatta decided to take ordinary food again, it was given to him by a girl, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujātā</span>, daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Senānī</span> of the township of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Senānī</span>. In the neighbourhood of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span> were also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajapāla</span> Banyan-tree, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mucalinda</span>-tree and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājāyatana</span>-tree, where the Buddha spent some time after his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>, and where various shrines, such as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Animisacetiya</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratanacaṅkamacetiya</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratanaghara</span> later came into existence.

From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvela</span> the Buddha went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>, but after, he had made sixty-one *Arahants* and sent them out on tour to preach the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span>, he returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span>, to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kappāsikavanasaṇḍa</span> and converted the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddavaggiyā</span> (Vin.i.23f; Dhp­a.i.72). At <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span> dwelt also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tebhātikajaṭilas</span>: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā Kassapa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nadī Kassapa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā Kassapa</span>, who all became followers of the Buddha (Vin.i.25).

According to the Ceylon Chronicles (e.g., Mhv.i.17ff; Dpv.i.35, 38, 81), it was while spending the rainy season at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span>, waiting for the time when the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> brothers should be ripe for conversion, that the Buddha, on the full-moon day of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phussa</span>, in the ninth month after the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>, paid his first visit to Ceylon.

Mention is made of several temptations of the Buddha while he dwelt at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span>, apart from the supreme contest with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>, under the Bodhi-tree. Once <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> came to him in the darkness of the night in the guise of a terrifying elephant, trying to frighten him. On another dark night when the rain was falling drop by drop, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> came to the Buddha and assumed various wondrous shapes, beautiful and ugly. Another time <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> tried to fill the Buddha’s mind with doubt as to whether he had really broken away from all fetters and won complete <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> (SN.i.103ff). Seven years after the Buddha’s Renunciation, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> made one more attempt to make the Buddha discontented with his lonely lot and it was then, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> had gone away discomfited, that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>’s three daughters, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Taṇhā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ragā</span>, made a final effort to draw the Buddha away from his purpose (SN.i.124f).

It was at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span>, too, that the Buddha had misgivings in his own mind as to the usefulness of preaching the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span> which he had realised, to a world blinded by passions and prejudices. The *Brahma* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> thereupon entreated the Buddha not to give way to such diffidence (SN.i.136ff; Vin.i.4f). It is recorded that either on this very occasion or quite soon after, the thought arose in the Buddha’s mind that the sole method of winning *Nibbāna* was to cultivate the four *satipaṭṭhāna*s and that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> visited the Blessed One and confirmed his view (SN.v.167; and again, 185). A different version occurs elsewhere (SN.v.232), where the thought which arose in the Buddha’s mind referred to the five controlling faculties (*saddhindriya*, etc.), and *Brahma* tells the Buddha that in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> he had been a monk named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahaka</span> and that then he had practised these five faculties.

The name <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span> is explained as meaning a great sandbank (*mahāvelā, mahanto vālikarāsi*). A story is told which furnishes an alternative explanation: Before the Buddha’s appearance in the world, ten thousand ascetics lived in this locality, and they decided among themselves that if any evil thought arose in the mind of any one of them, he should carry a basket of sand to a certain spot. The sand so collected eventually formed a great bank (AN­a.ii.476; Ud­a.26; MN­a.i.376; Mhv­ṭ.84). In the *Divyāvadāna* (p.202), the place is called Uruvilvā. The *Mahā Vastu* (Mvu.ii.207) mentions four villages as being in Uruvelā: Praskandaka, Balākalpa, Ujjaṅgala and Jaṅgala.
