# Uttarā 04

## English source

Uttarā 04. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā Nandamātā</span>. Chief of the lay-women disciples who waited on the Buddha (Bv.xxvi.20). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (i.26), she is described as the best of women disciples in meditative power (*jhāyīnaṁ*), but this may refer to another <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span>. She is again mentioned (AN.iv.347; AN­a.ii.791) in a list of eminent lay-women disciples, who observed the fast (*uposatha*) of the eight precepts.

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttara Commentary</span> (i.240ff), she was the daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇasīha</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇaka</span>) (*q.v.*), a servitor of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span> *seṭṭhi* of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. Later, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇasīha</span> was made *dhana-seṭṭhi* because of the immense wealth he gained by virtue of a meal given to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, he held an almsgiving for the Buddha and his monks for seven days. On the seventh day, at the end of the Buddha’s sermon of thanksgiving, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇasīha</span>, his wife and daughter, all became *Sotāpanna*.

When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span> *seṭṭhi* asked for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span>’s hand for his son, his request was refused because <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span>’s family did not belong to the Buddha’s faith. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span> sent word to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span> that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span> was the Buddha’s disciple and daily offered flowers to the Buddha, costing a *kahāpaṇa*. Later, however, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span> promised that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span> should be given flowers worth two *kahāpaṇas*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span> agreed and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span> was married. After several unsuccessful attempts to obtain her husband’s permission to keep the fast, as she had done in her parents’ house, she got from her father fifteen thousand *kahāpaṇas* and with these she purchased the services of a prostitute named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span>, to look after her husband for a fortnight, and with his consent she entered on a fortnight’s *uposatha*. On the last day of the fast, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span> was busy preparing alms for the Buddha, her husband, walking along with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span>, saw her working hard and smiled, thinking what a fool she was not to enjoy her wealth. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span>, seeing him, smiled at the thought of his folly in not making proper use of his wealth. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span>, thinking that husband and wife were smiling at each other, regardless of her presence, flew into a fury and, seizing a pot of boiling oil, threw it at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span>’s head. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span> was at that time full of compassion for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span>, and the oil, therefore, did not hurt her at all. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span>, realising her grievous folly, begged forgiveness of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span>, who took her to the Buddha and related the whole story, asking that he should forgive her. The Buddha preached to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span> and she became a *Sotāpanna*.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vimānavatthu</span> Commentary (pp.631ff; Vv.11f) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp-a.iii.302ff; see also iii.104) give the above story with several variations in detail. According to these versions, at the end of the Buddha’s sermon to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span> became a *Sakadāgāmī* and her husband and father-in-law *Sotāpannas*.

After death <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span> was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> in a *vimāna*. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> saw her in one of his visits to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> and, having learnt her story, repeated it to the Buddha.

It is curious that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span> is not mentioned in either account. It has been suggested (e.g., *Brethren*, 41, n.1) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā Nandamātā</span> may be identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷukaṇṭakī Nandamātā</span>, but I do not think that the identification is justified. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span>’s story is given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visuddhimagga</span> (p.313) to prove that fire cannot burn the body of a person who lives in love, and again (p.380-1; also Paṭis.ii.212; Paṭis-a.497), as an instance of psychic power being diffused by concentration.
