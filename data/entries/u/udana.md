# Udāna

## English source

Udāna. A short collection of eighty stories, in eight <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vaggas</span>, containing solemn utterances of the Buddha, made on special occasions. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> proper, comprising the Buddha’s utterances, is mostly in verse, in ordinary metres (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sloka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Triṣṭubh</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Jagatī</span>), seldom in prose (e.g., iii.10; viii.1, 3, 4). Each <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> is accompanied by a prose account of the circumstances in which it was uttered.

The book forms the third division of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khuddakanikāya</span> (DN­a.i.17; but see p.15, where it is the seventh).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> is also the name of a portion of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Piṭakas</span> in their arrangement according to matter (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">aṅga</span>). Thus divided, into this category fall eighty-two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span>, containing verses uttered in a state of joy (DN­a.i.23-4; see also Ud­a. pp.2-3).

The prose­and-verse stories of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> seem to have formed the model for the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Buddhist Legends</span>, i.28).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> is also the source of twelve stories of the same Commentary and contains parallels for three others. About one-third of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> is embodied in these stories. See, ibid., i.47-8, for details.
