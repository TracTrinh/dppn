# Āriyakkhattayodhā

## English source

Āriyakkhattayodhā. The mercenary soldiers employed in Ceylon. Their chief was a general called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ṭhakuraka</span>. When the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Senapāti</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mitta</span> obtained possession of the throne, he sought to win the favour of these soldiers by giving them money. This they refused to accept and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ṭhakuraka</span>, going up to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mitta</span> as he sat on the throne, cut off his head. On being questioned, he said that he had done the deed at the command of the lawful king, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhuvanekabāhu I</span>., who had become a refugee. The Āriya soldiers then joined forces with the Sīhala army and restored <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhuvanekabāhu</span> to the throne (Cv.xc.16-30).

Geiger (Cv.Tra.ii.202, n.3) thinks that these mercenaries must have come from South India. The name of their general, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ṭhakuraka</span>, however, seems to indicate that they were Rajputs.
