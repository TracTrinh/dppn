# Ajita Kesakambala

## English source

Ajita Kesakambala (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajita Kesakambalī</span>). Head of one of the six heretical sects mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Piṭakas</span> as being contemporaneous with the Buddha. He is described as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Titthakara</span> (heretical teacher), leader of a large following, virtuous and held in esteem by the people (SN.i.68).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmaññaphalasutta</span> (DN.i.55), where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> describes a visit paid to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajita</span>, he taught the doctrine of “cutting off,” i.e. annihilation at death. He was a nihilist who believed in neither good nor evil. In Tibetan sources he is stated to have taught that all beings must dwell in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Saṁsāra</span> for 84,000 <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">mahākalpas</span> before they come to an end; nothing can prevent that (Rockhill: 103-4). The answer <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajita</span> gave to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> is given elsewhere (e.g., SN.iii.207; MN.i.515) as being the view of a typical sophist. His name is often introduced into the stereotyped list of the six teachers even where the views they are alleged to have expressed do not coincide with those attributed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajita</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmaññaphalasutta</span>, e.g., SN.iv.398, where he is represented as talking about the rebirths of his adherents – he who denied rebirth. In AN.i.286 he seems to have been confused with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makkhali Gosāla</span>. He was called Kesakambalī because he wore a blanket of human hair, which is described as being the most miserable garment. It was cold in cold weather, hot in the hot, evil-smelling and uncouth (DN­a.i.144; MN­a.i.422-3).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Bodhijātaka</span> (Ja 528) the Buddha had already refuted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajita</span>’s view in previous births (Ja.v.246). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajita</span> was evidently much older in years than the Buddha, for we find <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, in the early years of his friendship with the Buddha, telling him that he was a young novice compared with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajita</span>, SN.i.68.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span> the king says that he had visited a teacher named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajita Kesakambala</span>. This cannot possibly refer to our <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajita</span>; the reference is probably to a teacher belonging to the same school of thought (“there is neither fruit nor result of good or evil karma,” p.4. His views are given on p.25 without mention being made of his name. But see note 2 to the Mil. trans., p.8.).

References to ascetics wearing hair garments are found in several passages of the Pāḷi canon. DN.i.167; MN.i.77, 238; AN.i.240; for a discussion of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajita</span>’s views see Barua: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pre-Buddhistic Indian Philosophy</span>, pp.287ff.
