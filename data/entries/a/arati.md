# Aratī

## English source

Aratī. One of the three daughters of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>, the others being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Taṇhā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ragā</span>. (In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Buddhacarita</span> (xiii.), their names are Ratī, Prītī and Tṛṣṇā; in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Lalitavistara</span> (353), Ratī, Aratī and Tṛṣṇā).

Seeing their father disconsolate after his repeated attempts to foil <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>’s quest for Awakening, they offered to tempt the Buddha with their wiles. This was in the fifth week after the Awakening. With <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>’s approval, they came to the Buddha in various forms and in various guises, as he sat at the foot of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajapāla</span> banyan tree, and danced and sang before him. In the end the Buddha told them that he was beyond temptation by the pleasures of the senses and they went back to their father (SN.i.124-7; Ja.i.78-80, 469; Dhp­a.i.201f., iii.196,199; Snp.v.835).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta</span> account, they are said to have asked the Buddha questions regarding himself and his teachings. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aratī</span>’s question was how a man who had already crossed the five floods could cross the sixth. For explanation see KS.i.158, n.3.
