# Aṅgulimāla

## English source

Aṅgulimāla (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgulimālaka</span>). A robber who was converted by the Buddha in the twentieth year of his ministry, and who, later, became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. His story appears both in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhima Commentary</span>, 743ff., and in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragātha</span> Commentary, ii.57ff. The two accounts differ in certain details; I have summarised the two versions.

He was the son of the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaggava</span>, chaplain to the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>, his mother being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mantāṇī</span>. He was born under the thieves’ constellation, and on the night of his birth all the armour in the town shone, including that belonging to the king. Because this omen did no harm to anyone the babe was named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ahiṁsaka</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragātha</span> Commentary says he was first called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hiṁsaka</span> and then <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ahiṁsaka</span>. See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brethren</span>, 323, n.3.

At <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> he became a favourite at the teacher’s house, but his jealous fellow-students poisoned his teacher’s mind, and the latter, bent on his destruction, asked as his honorarium a thousand human right-hand fingers. Thereupon <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ahiṁsaka</span> waylaid travellers in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jālinī</span> forest in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> and killed them, taking a finger from each. The finger-bones thus obtained he made into a garland to hang round his neck, hence the name Aṅgulimāla.

As a result of his deeds whole villages were deserted, and the king ordered a detachment of men to seize the bandit, whose name nobody knew. But Aṅgulimāla’s mother, guessing the truth, started off to warn him. By now he lacked but one finger to complete his thousand, and seeing his mother coming he determined to kill her. But the Buddha, seeing his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">upanissaya</span> (potential), went himself to the wood, travelling thirty <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojanas</span>, (DN­a.i.240; Ja.iv.180) and intercepted Aṅgulimāla on his way to slay his mother. Aṅgulimāla was converted by the Buddha’s power and received the “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ehi bhikkhu</span>” pabbajjā (Thag.868-70) while the populace were yelling at the king’s palace for the robber’s life. Later, the Buddha presented him before King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> when the latter came to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, filled with wonder, offered to provide the monk with all requisites. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgulimāla</span>, however, had taken on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dhutaṅgas</span> and refused the king’s offer.

When he entered <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> for alms, he was attacked by the mob, but on the admonition of the Buddha, endured their wrath as penance for his former misdeeds.

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp-a.iii.169) he appears to have died soon after he joined the Saṅgha.

There is a story of how he eased a woman’s labour pains by an act of truth. The words he used in this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">saccakiriyā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yato ahaṁ sabbaññutabuddhassa ariyassa ariyāya jātiyā jāto</span>) have come to be regarded as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">paritta</span> to ward off all dangers of childbirth and constitute the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgulimālaparitta</span>. The water that washed the stone on which he sat in the woman’s house came to be regarded as a panacea (MN.ii.103-4; MN­a.747f).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgulimālasutta</span> he is addressed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenādi</span> as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gagga Mantānīputta</span>, his father being a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gagga</span>. The story is evidently a popular one and occurs also in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Avadānaśataka</span> (No. 27).

At the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> king’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asadisadāna</span>, an untamed elephant, none other being available, was used to bear the parasol over <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgulimāla</span>. The elephant remained perfectly still – such was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgulimāla</span>’s power (Dhp­a.iii.185; also DN­a.ii.654).

The conversion of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgulimāla</span> is often referred to as a most compassionate and wonderful act of the Buddha’s, e.g. in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sutasomajātaka</span>, (Ja.v.456f.; see also Ja.iv.180; Snp-a.ii.440; Dhp­a.i.124) which was preached concerning him. The story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgulimāla</span> is quoted as that of a man in whose case a beneficent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kamma</span> arose and destroyed former evil <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kamma</span> (AN­a.i.369).

It was on his account that the rule not to ordain a captured robber was enacted (Vin.i.74).

For his identification with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalmāsapāda</span> see JPTS, 1909, pp. 240ff.
