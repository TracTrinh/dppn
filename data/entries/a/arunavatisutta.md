# Aruṇavatīsutta

## English source

Aruṇavatīsutta. Records the incident of the visit of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhibhū</span> to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>-world (SN.i.154f., etc.; see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhibhū</span> 1). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhibhū</span> chose as his theme action and energy, and the verses he uttered on that occasion, beginning “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ārabhatha, nikkhamatha, yuñjatha Buddhasāsane</span>, arouse (yourself), go forth, strive in the teaching of the Buddha,” are often quoted.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (SN­a.i.172-3) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhibhū</span> chose this theme out of all the doctrines to be found in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Tipiṭaka</span> because he knew that the subject would commend itself to all his hearers, human and non-human.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milakkhatissa Thera</span> of Ceylon, hearing a novice in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pācīnapabbata</span> recite the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aruṇavatīsutta</span>, listened to the stanzas, and feeling that they had been preached to encourage zealous monks like himself, he exerted himself and became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmī</span>. Soon afterwards he became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> (AN­a.i.21-2).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> is said to have been preached by the Buddha on the fullmoon day of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Jeṭṭhamāsa</span> (AN­a.i.436).
