# Āgantuka

## English source

Āgantuka. A banker of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>. He was rich, but he neither enjoyed his wealth himself nor gave it to others; he ate rice-dust with sour gruel, wore coarse clothes and went about in an old chariot with a parasol of leaves over his head. After death he was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Roruva</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">niraya</span>.

He died heirless and it took seven days and seven nights for the king’s men to remove his wealth to the royal treasury.

In reply to a question of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, the Buddha revealed why <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āgantuka</span> had been a miser: in a past birth, while going to the king’s court, he had met the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tagarasikhī</span> begging for alms and had ordered his servant to give the food prepared for himself (Āgantuka) to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>. On his way back, seeing the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> returning with the excellent food from the merchant’s house in his alms-bowl, he wished he had distributed it among his own servants instead, as they would have done some work in return (Ja.iii.199-300).

The reason for Āgantuka being heirless is related in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mayhakajātaka</span> (Ja 390).
