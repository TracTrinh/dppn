# Apadāna

## English source

Apadāna. The thirteenth division of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khuddakanikāya</span>. It is a Buddhist <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Vitae Sanctorum</span> and contains 547 biographies of monks and forty biographies of nuns, all mentioned as having lived in the time of the Buddha. The Commentary gives details of eleven more Theras not found in the text: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nadī Kassapa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā Kassapa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbila</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjiputta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apara Uttara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddaji</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sivika</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upavāṇa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Raṭṭhapāla</span>.

In addition to these, there are two introductory chapters, the Buddhāpadāna and the Paccekabuddhāpadāna, dealing with the Buddha and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> respectively. It is worth noting that the Buddhāpadāna contains no account of the Buddha’s life, either as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> or earlier, as Bodhisatta (see, however, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pubbakammapiloti</span>). Nor does the Paccekabuddhāpadāna contain any life-histories. The stanzas are what might be more appropriately described as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">udāna</span>, and appear in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khaggavisāṇasutta</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span>. Cp. the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāpadānasutta</span> (DN.ii.1ff), where the word <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> is used as meaning the legend or life-story of a Buddha or a Great One – in this case the seven Buddhas. Or does <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāpadāna</span> mean the Great Story, i.e. the story of the Dhamma and its bearers and promulgation: cp. the title of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Vastu</span> (Dial.ii.3).

Most of the stories are found in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paramatthadīpanī</span>, the Commentary to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Therīgāthā</span>, extracted from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> with the introductory words, “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">tena vuttaṁ Apadāne</span>.” But in numerous instances the names under which the verses appear in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paramatthadīpanī</span> differ from those subjoined to the verses in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>. In several cases it is a matter of the Commentary giving a name while the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> gives only a title, e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Usabha</span> Thera (Thag­a.i.320), called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosumbaphaliya</span> (Ap.ii.449); and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidinna</span> (Thag­a.i.312), called (Ap.ii.415) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanavījaniya</span>.

Sometimes the stories are duplicated in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> itself, the same story occurring in two places with a very slight alteration in words, even the name of the person spoken of being the same. Most often no reason can be assigned for this, except, perhaps, careless editing, e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Annasaṁsāvaka</span> Ap.i.78 and again Ap.i.261; see also the Introduction to the PTS Edition.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> is regarded as one of the very latest books in the Canon, one reason for this view being that while later books like the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhavaṁsa</span> mention only twenty-four Buddhas previous to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> contains the names of thirty-five. It is very probable that the different legends in the collection are of different dates. On these and other matters connected with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>, see Rhys Davids article in ERE and Muller’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Les Apadānas du Sud</span> (Congress of Orientalists, Leyden, 1895).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumaṅgalavilāsinī</span> (i.15. See also Przyluski: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t18">La Legende de l’Empereur Açoka</span>, pp. viii f., 214), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Dīghabhāṇakas</span>, who included the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khuddakanikāya</span> in the Abhidhammapiṭaka, did not recognise the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Majjhimabhāṇakas</span> included it in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khuddakanikāya</span>, which they regarded as belonging to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttapiṭaka</span>. There is a Commentary to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visuddhajanavilāsinī</span>.

According to Gv (p. 69) the Commentary on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> was written by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> at the request of five monks.
