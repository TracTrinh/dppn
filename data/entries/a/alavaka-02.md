# Āḷavaka 02

## English source

Āḷavaka 02. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>. King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span>, with the help of the Mayor of the town (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nagaraguttika</span>) and his ministers, was able to keep his promise for some time, by sending criminals to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>’s power was such that at the sight of him men’s bodies became as soft as butter. Soon there were no criminals left, and each household was forced to contribute one child for sacrifice to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>.

Then women, about to bring forth children, began to leave the king’s capital. Twelve years passed in this manner and the only child left was the king’s own son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kumāra</span>. When the king learnt this, he ordered the child to be dressed in all splendour and taken to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>. The Buddha, with his Eye of Compassion, saw what was going to happen and went to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>’s abode.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span> was away at a meeting of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span>. His doorkeeper <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gadrabha</span> admitted the Buddha, after warning him of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>’s unmannerly nature. The Buddha went in and sat down on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span>’s throne while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gadrabha</span> went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span> to announce to his master the Buddha’s arrival. While the Buddha was there, preaching to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span>’s women-folk, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sātāgira</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemavata</span>, passing through the air on their way to the assembly in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span>, being made aware of the Buddha’s presence by their inability to fly over him, descended to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span>’s palace and made obeisance to the Buddha before resuming their journey.

When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span> heard from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gadrabha</span> and from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sātāgira</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemavata</span> of the Buddha’s visit, he was greatly incensed and uttering aloud his name, he hurried to his abode. There with all the various supernatural powers he could command he tried to dislodge the Buddha from his seat, but without success, even his special weapon, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dussāvudha</span> being of no avail against the Buddha. Then, approaching the Buddha, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span> asked him to leave his house, which the Buddha did. He then summoned the Buddha back and he came. Three times this happened and three times the Buddha obeyed, judging compliance to be the best way of softening his wrath, but the fourth time the Buddha refused to return. Thereupon <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span> expressed his desire to ask questions of the Buddha, hoping thereby to fatigue him. The Buddha agreed, and when he had answered all the questions to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span>’s satisfaction, the latter became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span> (Snp-a.i.239).

At dawn of day, King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span>’s men brought the young prince, Āḷavaka Kumāra to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>, as sacrifice. Hearing the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>’s shouts of joy at the close of the Buddha’s sermon, they greatly marvelled. When they announced to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span> that they had brought their offering, and handed him the child, he was much ashamed because of the Buddha’s presence. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span> gave the child to the Buddha, who blessed him and gave him back to the king’s messengers. The boy, having passed from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>’s hands to those of the Buddha, and from there to the king’s men, thereafter became known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthaka Āḷavaka</span> (Snp-a.i.239-40).

When the king and the citizens heard that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> had become a follower of the Buddha, they built for him a special abode near that of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> and provided him with endless gifts of flowers, perfumes, etc., for his use. The story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span>, of which the above is a summary, is given in full in Snp-a.i.217-40 and in SN­a.i.244-59. It is also given in brief in AN­a.i.211-12 and with some difference in detail.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span>’s abode was thirty leagues from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, and the Buddha covered the whole journey in one day (Snp-a.i.220). The abode was near a banyan tree and on the ground (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t18">bhummaṭṭhaṁ</span>) well protected with walls, etc., and covered on the top by a metal net, it was like a cart enclosed on all sides. It was three leagues in extent, and over it lay the road to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span> by air (Snp-a.i.222). Ascetics, having seen the glittering palace, often called to find out what it was. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span> would ask them questions regarding their faith, and when they could not answer he would assume a subtle form and, entering their hearts, would drive them mad (Snp-a.i.228).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span> shouted his name before starting from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span> to vanquish the Buddha. He stood with his left foot on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manosilātala</span> and his right on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kelāsakūṭa</span>. His shout was heard throughout <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> and was one of the four shouts, mentioned in tradition, as having travelled so far (Snp-a.i.223; for the others see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusā</span>). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span> had a special weapon, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dussāvudha</span>, comparable to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajirāvudha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span>’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gadāvudha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yama</span>’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nayanāvudha</span>. It had the power, if it were thrown into the sky, of stopping rain for twelve years and if cast on the earth of destroying all trees and crops for a like period. If hurled into the sea it would dry up all the water, and it could shatter <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span> into pieces. It was made of cloth and is described as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vatthāvudha</span>, and it was worn as a part of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>’s upper garment (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t18">uttariya</span>).

There are three salient features in the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span> which link it closely to the large circle of stories grouped by Professor Watanabe (JPTS1909-10, pp.240ff) under the title of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalmāsapāda</span> stories:

## Vietnamese translation

Chưa dịch.
