# Ayojjhā

## English source

Ayojjhā. A city of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span> (but see below in this article). Two visits of the Buddha to this city are recorded in the Canon; on one occasion he preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phenasutta</span> (SN.iii.140ff) and on the other the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dārukkhandhasutta</span> (SN.iv.179f). In both these references the city is said to be on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span>; the town usually called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ayojjhā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ayodhya</span>) is certainly not on this river. The records, therefore, go back either to a confused or an unintelligent tradition (see Thomas: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Life and Legend of the Buddha</span>, 15; cf. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span>), or may possibly refer to another settlement made by colonists from the original <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ayojjhā</span>. It is worthy of note that in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dārukkhandhasutta</span> some of the MSS. read <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ayojjhā</span>. But even <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) was on the Jumnā and not on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span>.

During the Buddhist period, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ayojjhā</span> on the Sarayū was the capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇa Kosala</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">janapada</span> roughly corresponding to modern Oudh. This, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ayodhyā</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Rāmāyaṇa</span>, is about a mile from the modern Fyzabad. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka Commentary</span> (Ja.iv.82) there is a mention of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ayojjhā</span>, which here evidently refers to the city of the Sanskrit epics. It is called the capital of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷasena</span>. It was besieged by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhaveṇhuputtā</span>, who breached the wall and took the king prisoner. Having thus subjugated the city, they went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dvāravatī</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīpavaṁsa</span> (iii.15) mentions <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ayujjhanagara</span> as the capital of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Arindama</span> and of fifty-five of his descendants.

According to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (SN­a.ii.233-4), the people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ayujjhanagara</span> built for the Buddha a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> in a spot surrounded by forest near a curve of the river. Once a warrior named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jagatipāla</span>, of the race of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāma</span>, came to Ceylon from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ayojjhā</span>, and having slain <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vikkampaṇḍu</span>, the heir­apparent to the throne, ruled in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohaṇa</span> for five years. Cv.lvi.13ff. </p>
