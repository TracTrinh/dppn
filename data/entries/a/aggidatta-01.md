# Aggidatta 01

## English source

Aggidatta 01. Chaplain to the King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>, first to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kosala</span>, and then to his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>. Later he renounced the world and, with a large band of followers, wandered about <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kururaṭṭha</span>, teaching a cult of nature-worship. The Buddha, seeing his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">upanissaya</span> (potential), sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> to convert him. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggidatta</span>’s hermitage, but being refused shelter there, vanquished, by a display of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span>-power, a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nāgarāja</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ahicchatta</span>, who lived in the neighbourhood, and occupied the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>’s abode. While <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggidatta</span> and his followers stand awestruck at this event, the Buddha appears, and realising that the Buddha is even greater than <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>, they pay homage to him. The Buddha preaches to them on the error of their ways. At the end of the discourse they become <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span> (Dhp­a.iii.241-7).
