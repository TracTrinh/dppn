# Ākaṅkheyyasutta 01

## English source

Ākaṅkheyyasutta 01. The sixth <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhimanikāya</span>, preached at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>.

A monk must conform to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sīla</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pātimokkha</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sikkhāpadas</span>, whatever be his yearnings, whether to be beloved of his fellows, to be given robes etc., to gain the four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jhānas</span>, to make an end of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dukkha</span> or to be possessed of such powers as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dibbacakkhu</span>, etc. (MN.i.33-6)

This <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> is often mentioned (e.g., DN­a.i.50; MN­a.i.13) as an example of a discourse preached by the Buddha of his own accord (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">attano ajjhāsayeneva</span>).
