# Āṭānāṭiyasutta

## English source

Āṭānāṭiyasutta. The thirty-second *Sutta* of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghanikāya</span>, preached at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span> (DN.iii.194ff).

The Four Great Kings (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cattāro Mahā Rājāno</span>) having set a guard over the four quarters, visited the Buddha. Having saluted him and sat down with hosts of other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> told the Buddha that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> did not, for the most part, believe in the Buddha for the reason that they did not find it pleasant or agreeable to abstain from the things which he declared to be evil – such as the taking of life, theft, etc. And in order that the Buddha’s disciples, haunting lonely and remote parts of the forest where the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> dwelt, might find protection from them, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> suggested that the Buddha might learn the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āṭānāṭiya</span> word-rune (*rakkha*). The Buddha agreeing, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> proceeded to recite it.

It opens with a salutation to the seven Buddhas, beginning with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī</span>. The remainder contains a list of the gods and other superhuman beings, the Four Great Kings heading the list; these last are described at some length; forty-one other gods are mentioned as a kind of appendix or afterthought, all mentioned one after another with no attempt at group division and without any details, in what are, apparently, mnemonic doggerels.

A part of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Samayasutta</span> (sections 10-20) looks very much like an improved and enlarged edition of this list of bare names.

The Buddha learnt the word-rune and taught it to the monks.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āṭānāṭiyasutta</span> is now regarded as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paritta</span>, and its influence pervades a hundred million world systems (Vibh­a.430).

In Ceylon, for instance, it is recited with great fervour at the conclusion of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paritta</span> ceremonies, particularly in times of illness, in order to ward off evil spirits.

It is included in the list of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parittas</span> found in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span>, p.151; on the importance of this *Sutta* in the history of India, see Rhys Davids, *Buddhist India*, pp.219-37.

DN­a.iii.969 gives a long description of the ritual to be followed when reciting the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āṭānāṭiyaparitta</span>.
