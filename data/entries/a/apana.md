# Āpaṇa

## English source

Āpaṇa. A city in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttarāpa</span> country (probably its capital). The Buddha once visited the city with 1,250 monks and the whole company was entertained by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jaṭila Keniya</span> (Vin.i.245ff). From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āpaṇa</span> the Buddha went on to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> (Vin.i.247). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (SN.v.225), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āpaṇa</span> is spoken of as a township of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgas</span> (*Aṅgānaṁ nigamo*) and the Buddha is mentioned as having stayed there with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>.

Several *Suttas* were preached at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āpaṇa</span>, among them

- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Poṭaliyasutta</span> (regarding <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Poṭaliya</span>), (MN.i.359ff)
- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Laṭukikopamasutta</span> (to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Udāyī</span>) (MN.i.447ff),
- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Selasutta</span> (regarding <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sela</span>) (MN.ii.146ff; Sn.pp.102ff) and
- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Saddhasutta</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Āpaṇasutta</span> (SN.v.225-7).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āpaṇa</span> was a Brahmin village and was the home of the Elder <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sela</span> (Thag­a.ii.47). On the occasion of the Buddha’s visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āpaṇa</span>, during which he converted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sela</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Keniya</span>, he seems to have stayed at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āpaṇa</span> for over a week and ordained three hundred monks in the company of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sela</span> (Sn., p.112).

According to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (MN­a.ii.586), the village was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āpaṇa</span> because it had twenty thousand bazaars (*āpaṇā*) and was therefore distinguished for its shops (*āpaṇānaṁ ussannattā*). Near the village, on the banks of the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahī</span>, was the woodland where the Buddha stayed during his visits.
