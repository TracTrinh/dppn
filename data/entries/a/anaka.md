# Ānaka

## English source

Ānaka (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">v.l.</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āṇaka</span>). A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">mutiṅga</span> (kettle-drum) belonging to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dasārahas</span>. As it grew old and began to split, they fixed in another peg, and this process was continued, until, at last, the original drumhead vanished, leaving only the framework of pegs (SN.ii.266). The origin of the drum is related in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakkaṭajātaka</span>. When the Golden Crab, there mentioned, was trampled to death by the elephants, his two claws broke away from his body and lay apart in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kulīradaha</span>, where he lived. During the floods the water flowed from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span> into this lake, running back again when the floods subsided. The two claws were thus carried into the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span>. One of them reached the sea, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span>, picking it up, made thereof the drum named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷambara</span>. The other was picked up by the Ten Royal Brothers (evidently the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dasārahas</span> mentioned above) while playing in the river, and they made of it the little drum <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānaka</span> (Ja.ii.344; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> is quoted in SN­a.ii.167-8, with several variations in detail).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta Commentary</span> (ii.167-8) it is said that the drum was like molten wax in colour, because the crab’s claw had been dried by wind and sun. The sound of the drum was heard for twelve leagues, and it was, therefore, used only on festive occasions. On hearing it, the people assembled hurriedly, in various conveyances, decked with splendour. It was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānaka</span> because it brought the people together as if summoning them (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">mahājanaṁ pakkositvā viya ānetī ti Ānako</span>).

Later, when the original drumhead had vanished, it could hardly be heard even inside a hall. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānaka</span> drum is used as a simile in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āṇisutta</span> (SN.ii.266-7; see also KS.ii.178, n.4).
