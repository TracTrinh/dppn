# Assaka 04

## English source

Assaka 04. The country of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaka</span> is one of the sixteen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janapadas</span> mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (AN.i.213; iv.252, 256, 260). It does not, however; occur in the list of twelve countries given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janavasabhasutta</span>. The Assakas are said to have had settlements on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godhāvarī</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span>’s hermitage (Sn.vs.977) was in their territory, in close proximity to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aḷaka</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mulaka</span> (the district round Paithan) (Law, *Geography of Early Buddhism*, 21).

The country is mentioned with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> (Ja.v.317) in the same way as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, and its position in the list between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sūrasena</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> makes it probable that when the list was drawn up, its position was immediately to the north-west of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>. It is probable, in that case, that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godhāvarī</span> settlement, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇāpatha</span>, was a later colony.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assakajātaka</span> (Ja.ii.155) mention is made of a king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaka</span> whose realm was in the kingdom of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsī</span>. It is significant, in this connection, that the capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaka</span>, variously called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Potana</span> (e.g., DN.ii.235; Ja.iii.3) or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Potali</span> (e.g., Ja.ii.155), is not mentioned in the reference to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godhāvarī</span>.

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Kāliṅgajātaka</span> (Ja.iii.3-5), at one time the King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaka</span> (Arum) accepted the challenge of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāliṅga</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dantapura</span> to war, and defeated him. Later <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaka</span> married <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāliṅga</span>’s daughter and the relations between the two countries were amicable. In the Hāthigumphā Inscription of Khāravela it is related that Khāravela, regardless of King Sātakarṇī, sent a large army to the west (*pacchime disaṁ*) to strike terror into <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaka</span> (or *Asika*) *nagara*. Law (op.cit., p.21) thinks that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaka</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Kāliṅgajātaka</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asikanagara</span> of the Hāthigumphā Inscription and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaka</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span> are one and the same place. This would probably be correct if <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Potana</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Potali</span> were regarded as two different cities, capitals of two different settlements having the same name.

Sanskrit authors speak of both Asmakā and Asvakā. It is not possible to say whether these represent two distinct tribes or whether they are variant names for the same people. Asaṅga mentions Asmaka in his *Sūtrālaṅkāra* as a territory on the basin of the Indus. This would make it identical with the Assakenus of Greek writers, that is to the east of the Sarasvati, about twenty-five miles from the sea on the Swat valley. Pāṇini mentions the Asmakas (iv.173). The *Mārkaṇḍeya Purāṇa* and the *Bṛhat Saṁhitā* place <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaka</span> to the north-west. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaka</span> capital, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Potana</span>, it has been suggested, is the Paudanya of the *Mahā Bhārata* (i.77, 47). In the Commentary to Kautilya’s *Arthaśāstra*, Bhaṭṭasvāmi identifies Asmaka with Mahā Rāstra (Law, op.cit., 22).

Soon after the Buddha’s death, a King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaka</span> was the ruler of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Potali</span>, and he and his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sūjata</span> were converted by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span> (Vv-a.259-67).

In the time of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Reṇu</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaka</span> king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Potana</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span> (DN.ii.236).

In the Buddha’s time the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaka</span> king is described as an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhakarājā</span>. He took a thousand for the plot of land sold for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span>’s hermitage (Snp-a.ii.581).
