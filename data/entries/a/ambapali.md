# Ambapālī

## English source

Ambapālī (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambapālikā</span>). A courtesan of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>. She is said to have come spontaneously into being at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> in the gardens of the king. The gardener found her at the foot of a mango tree – hence her name – and brought her to the city. She grew up so full of beauty and of grace that many young princes vied with each other for the honour of her hand. Finally, in order to end their strife, they appointed her courtesan. Later she became a devout follower of the Buddha, and building a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> in her own garden, gave it to him and the Saṅgha. This was during the Buddha’s last visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> shortly before his death. It is said that when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambapālī</span> heard of the Buddha’s visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṭigāma</span> near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> she and her retinue drove out of the city in magnificent chariots to meet him, and, after hearing a discourse, invited him and the monks to a meal the next day. The Buddha accepted this invitation and had, as a result, to refuse that of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>.

While returning from her visit to the Buddha, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambapālī</span> was so elated at the idea of having the Buddha to a meal the next day, that she refused to make way for the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span> princes who were on their way to the Buddha. She refused to give up her invitation for anything in the world. The commentary says that just before <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambapālī</span>’s visit to him, the Buddha admonished the monks to be steadfast and mindful, lest they should lose their heads about her (DN­a.ii.545).

It was after this meal that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambapālī</span> gave over her park, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambapālivana</span>, to the Buddha and the Saṅgha. The Buddha accepted the gift and stayed there some time before going on to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Beluva</span>. Vin.i.231-3; DN.ii.95-8; the two accounts vary in details, e.g. in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha</span> version the Buddha was already in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambapālivana</span>, and not in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṭigāma</span>, when the courtesan visited him.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambapālī</span> had a son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vimala Koṇḍañña</span>, who was an eminent Elder. Having heard him preach one day, she renounced the world and, working for insight by studying the law of impermanence as illustrated in her own ageing body, she attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship (Thīg­a.206-7).

Nineteen verses ascribed to her are found in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Therīgāthā</span> (252-70).

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sikhī Buddha</span> she had entered the Saṅgha. While yet a novice, she took part in a procession of Bhikkhunīs, and were doing homage at a shrine when an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> Therī in front of her hastily spat in the court of the shrine. Seeing the spittle and not knowing who had committed the fault, she said in reproof, “What prostitute has been spitting here?” It was owing to this remark that she was born as a courtesan in her last birth (Thīg­a.206-7).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (quoted also in Thīg­a) gives some more details about her. She had been a daughter of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Khattiya</span> family in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phussa Buddha</span> and had done many good deeds in order to be beautiful in later births. As a result of the abuse of the nun (referred to above) she had been born in hell and later had, for ten thousand lives, been a courtesan. In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>’s time she had practised celibacy (Ap.ii.613ff.; Thīg­a.213f).

It is said that she charged fifty <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kahāpaṇas</span> a night from her patrons and that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> became very prosperous through her. It was this that prompted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> to get a courtesan for his own city of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> (Vin.i.268).

Among <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambapālī</span>’s patrons was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, and he was the father of her son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vimala Koṇḍañña</span> (Thag­a.i.146).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> (vv.1020-21; Thag­a.ii.129) there are two verses which, according to tradition, were spoken by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> in admonition of monks who lost their heads at the sight of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambapālī</span>. Whether this was before or after she joined the Saṅgha we are not told.
