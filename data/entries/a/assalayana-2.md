# Assalāyana

## English source

Assalāyana sits silent and upset at the end of the discourse, but when the Buddha relates to him a story of the past where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asitadevala</span> had defeated Brahmins who held these same views, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assalāyana</span> feels relieved and expresses his admiration of the Buddha’s exposition. He declares himself a follower of the Buddha (MN.ii.147ff). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (MN­a.ii.785) tells us further that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assalāyana</span> became a devoted follower of the faith and built a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span> in his own residence for worship, and that all his descendants, down to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>’s day, built similar <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiyas</span> in their houses.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assalāyana</span> is probably to be identified with the father of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Koṭṭhita</span>, his wife being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candavatī</span>. There is, however, one difficulty connected with this theory: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Koṭṭhita</span> says that he was won over to the faith after hearing the same sermon of the Buddha as converted his father (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yadā me pitaraṁ Buddho vinayī sabbasuddhiyā</span>) (Thag­a.i.31; Ap.ii.480). It is unlikely, if the identification be correct, that this refers to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assalāyanasutta</span>, because at the time of that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assalāyana</span> was only sixteen years old; but there exists no record of any other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> preached to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assalāyana</span>, dealing with “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sabbasuddhi</span>.”

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assalayāna</span>’s name occurs in a list of eminent Brahmins found in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span> Commentary (i.372).
