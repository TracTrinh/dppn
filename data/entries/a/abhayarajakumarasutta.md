# Abhayarājakumārasutta

## English source

Abhayarājakumārasutta. It contains the episode of Prince <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span> visiting <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> and setting him the questions suggested by Nigaṇṭha <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nātaputta</span>:

- Would a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Tathāgatha</span> say anything unpleasant or disagreeable to others?
- If he did, how would he differ from ordinary men?
- If he did not, how was it that the Buddha spoke of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Devadatta</span> as a reprobate, a child of perdition, etc. – words which angered and upset <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Devadatta</span>?

The Buddha answered that the question needed qualification and, noticing that the prince was nursing his little boy, who lay in his lap, asked him what he would do if a pebble or a stick got into his mouth. “I should pull it out even if the blood flowed.” – “Just so would a Buddha state unpleasant truths in due season if necessary and profitable.”

At the end of the discourse <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span> accepts the Buddha as his Teacher (MN.i.391ff).
