# Anāthapiṇḍika 01

## English source

Anāthapiṇḍika 01. A banker (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span>) of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> who became famous because of his unparalleled generosity to the Buddha. His first meeting with the Buddha was during the first year after the Awakening, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> (the story is given in Vin.ii.154ff; SN­a.i.240ff, etc.), whither Anāthapiṇḍika had come on business.

His wife was the sister of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, and when he arrived he found the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span> preparing a meal for the Buddha and his monks on so splendid a scale that he thought that a wedding was in progress or that the king had been invited. On learning the truth he became eager to visit the Buddha, and did so very early the next morning (Vin.ii.155-6). He was so excited by the thought of the visit that he got up three times during the night. When, at last, he started for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sītavana</span>, the road was quite dark, but a friendly <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvaka</span>, sped him on with words of encouragement. By force of his piety the darkness vanished.

The Buddha was staying in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sītavana</span>, and when Anāthapiṇḍika reached there spirits opened the door for him. He found the Buddha walking up and down, meditating in the cool air of the early dawn. The Buddha greeted him and talked to him on various aspects of his teaching. Anāthapiṇḍika was immediately converted and became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>. He invited the Buddha to a meal the next day, providing everything himself, although the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span>, the Mayor of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> and King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> asked to be allowed to help. After the meal, which he served to the Buddha with his own hand, he invited the Buddha to spend the rainy season at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, and the Buddha accepted, saying “the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tathāgata</span>s, O householder, take pleasure in solitude.” “I understand, O Blessed One, I understand,” was the reply.

When Anāthapiṇḍika had finished his business at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> he set out towards <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, giving orders along the way to his friends and acquaintances to prepare dwellings, parks, rest-houses and gifts all along the road to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> in preparation for the Buddha’s visit. He had many friends and acquaintances and he was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ādeyyavaco</span> (his word was held to be of weight), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">loc.cit.</span>, p.158. But see Ja.i.92, where it is said that Anāthapiṇḍika bore all the expenses of these preparations. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Vihāras</span> were built costing l,000 pieces each, a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojana</span> apart from each other.

Understanding the request implied in the Buddha’s words when he accepted the invitation, Anāthapiṇḍika looked out for a quiet spot near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> where the Buddha and the monks might dwell, and his eye fell on the park of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetakumāra</span>. He bought the park at great expense and erected therein the famous <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavanārāma</span>. As a result of this and of his numerous other benefactions in the cause of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sāsana</span>, Anāthapiṇḍika came to be recognised as the chief of alms-givers (AN.i.25).

Anāthapiṇḍika’s personal name was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudatta</span>, but he was always called Anāthapiṇḍika (feeder of the destitute) (AN­a.i.208; MN­a.i.50) because of his munificence; he was, however, very pleased when the Buddha addressed him by his own name (Vin.ii.156). He spent eighteen crores on the purchase of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> and a like sum on the construction of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span>; another eighteen crores were spent in the festival of dedication. He fed one hundred monks in his house daily in addition to meals provided for guests, people of the village, invalids, etc. Five hundred seats were always ready in his house for any guests who might come (AN­a.i.208-9. He fed 1,000 monks daily says Dhp­a.i.128; but see Ja.iii.119, where a monk, who had come from far away and had missed the meal hour, had to starve.).

Anāthapiṇḍika’s father was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span> (AN­a. loc.cit.). The name of Anāthapiṇḍika’s brother was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhūti</span>.

Anāthapiṇḍika married a lady called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puññalakkhaṇā</span> (Ja.ii.410; Ja.iii.435, she was the sister of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. SN­a.i.240); he had a son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷa</span> and three daughters, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Subhaddā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Subhaddā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanā</span>. (Besides <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷa</span>, Anāthapiṇḍika had another son, who joined the Saṅgha under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhūti Thera</span><span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t18">;</span> AN­a.ii.865). Mention is also made of a daughter-in law, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujātā</span> by name, daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya</span> and the youngest sister of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>. She was very haughty and ill-treated the servants (Ja.ii.347).

The son, in spite of his father’s efforts, showed no piety until he was finally bribed to go to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> and listen to the Buddha’s preaching (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷa</span>). The daughters, on the other hand, were most dutiful and helped their father in ministering to the monks. The two elder ones attained to the First Fruit of the Path, married, and went to live with the families of their husbands. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanā</span> obtained the Second Fruit of the Path, but remained unmarried. Overwhelmed with disappointment because of her failure in finding a husband, she refused to eat and died; she was reborn in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> (Dhp­a.i.128f).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhadraghaṭajātaka</span> (Ja 291, Ja.ii.431) tells us of a nephew of Anāthapiṇḍika who squandered his inheritance of forty crores. His uncle gave him first one thousand and then another five hundred with which to trade. This also he squandered. Anāthapiṇḍika then gave him two garments. On applying for further help the man was taken by the neck and pushed out of doors. A little later he was found dead by a side wall.

The books also mention a girl, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇā</span>, who was a slave in Anāthapiṇḍika’s household. On one occasion when the Buddha was starting on one of his periodical tours from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, the king, Anāthapiṇḍika, and other eminent patrons failed to stop him; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇā</span>, however, succeeded, and in recognition of this service Anāthapiṇḍika adopted her as his daughter (MN­a.i.347-8). On <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t18">uposatha</span> days his whole household kept the fast; on all occasions they kept the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pañcasīla</span> inviolate (Ja.iii.257).

A story is told of one of his labourers who had forgotten the day and gone to work; but remembering later, he insisted on keeping the fast and died of starvation. He was reborn as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span> (MN­a.i.540-1).

Anāthapiṇḍika had a business village in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span> and the superintendent of the village had orders to feed any monks who came there (Vin.iv.162f). One of his servants bore the inauspicious name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷakaṇṇi</span> (curse); he and the banker had been playmates as children, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷakaṇṇi</span>, having fallen on evil days, entered the banker’s service. The latter’s friends protested against his having a man with so unfortunate a name in his household, but he refused to listen to them. One day when Anāthapiṇḍika was away from home on business, burglars came to rob his house, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷakaṇṇi</span> with great presence of mind drove them away (Ja.i.364f).

A similar story is related of another friend of his who was also in his service (Ja.i.441).

All his servants, however, were not so intelligent. A slave woman of his, seeing that a fly had settled on her mother, hit her with a pestle in order to drive it away, and killed her (Ja.i.248f).

A slave girl of his borrowed an ornament from his wife and went with her companions to the pleasure garden. There she became friendly with a man who evidently desired to rob her of her ornaments. On discovering his intentions, she pushed him into a well and killed him with a stone (Ja.iii.435).

The story of Anāthapiṇḍika’s cowherd, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span>, is given elsewhere.

All the banker’s friends were not virtuous; one of them kept a tavern (Ja.i.251). As a result of Anāthapiṇḍika’s selfless generosity he was gradually reduced to poverty. But he continued his gifts even when he had only bird-seed and sour gruel. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Devatā</span> who dwelt over his gate appeared before him one night and warned him of his approaching penury; it is said that every time the Buddha or his monks came to the house she had to leave her abode over the gate and that this was inconvenient to her and caused her to be jealous. Anāthapiṇḍika paid no attention to her warnings and asked her to leave the house. She left with her children, but could find no other lodging and sought counsel from various gods, including <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> advised her to recover for Anāthapiṇḍika the eighteen crores that debtors owed him, another eighteen that lay in the bottom of the sea, and yet eighteen more lying unclaimed. She did so and was readmitted (Dhp­a.iii.10ff; Ja.i.227ff).

Anāthapiṇḍika went regularly to see the Buddha twice a day, sometimes with many friends (Ja.i.95ff.; he went three times says Ja.i.226), and always taking with him alms for the young novices. But we are told that he never asked a question of the Buddha lest he should weary him. He did not wish the Buddha to feel obliged to preach to him in return for his munificence (Dhp­a.i.3). But the Buddha of his own accord preached to him on various occasions; several such sermons are mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span>:
