# Aṭṭhaka 01

## English source

Aṭṭhaka 01. A celebrated sage, composer and reciter of sacred runes, mentioned together with nine others (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāmaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāmadeva</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessāmitta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamataggi</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgīrasa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhāradvāja</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāseṭṭha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhagu</span>. Vin.i.245; DN.i.104; DN­a.i.273), as the ancient <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ṛsis</span> of the Brahmins. They abstained from food at unseasonable times. They were the first teachers of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tevijja</span> Brahmins (DN.i.238) and great sacrifices were conducted by them (AN.iv.61).

Various teachings are attributed to them, e.g. that they recognised five kinds of Brahmins – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahmasama</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Devasama</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">mariyāda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sambhinnamariyāda</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brāhmaṇacaṇḍāla</span> (AN.iii.224ff). These sages did not claim to have discerned and realised the five qualities – truth, austerities, chastity, study and munificence – specified by the Brahmins for the attainment of merit and the achievement of what is right (MN.ii.199-200), though their followers behaved as if they did. Nor did they claim that they personally saw and knew that “here alone resides the truth and everything else is vain” (MN.ii.169). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vimānavatthu</span> Commentary it is said that the Buddha had realised those things of which these sages thought and for which they wished (p.265) (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahmacintitan-ti brahmehi Aṭṭhakādīhi cintitaṁ, pañcacakkhunā diṭṭhaṁ</span>).

It is said that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṭṭhaka</span> and the other seers had the divine eye and had incorporated the teachings of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> into their own scriptures. Thus (at that time) the three Vedas were in conformity with the Dhamma. But later the Brahmins went back on these teachings (DN­a.i.273).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṭṭhaka</span> is generally identified with Aṣṭaka mentioned as the author of Ṛg-veda x.104, unless the name be taken as a corrupt reading under which some representation of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atri</span> may lurk. VT.ii.130, n.2.
