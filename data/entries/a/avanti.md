# Avanti

## English source

Avanti. One of the four great monarchies in the time of the Buddha, the other three being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṁsa</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vatsa</span>). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> is also mentioned among the sixteen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janapadā</span> (AN.i.213; iv.252, 256, 260).

Its capital was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>. But according to another account (DN.ii.235), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māhissati</span> is mentioned as having been, at least for some time, the capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>. It is quite likely that ancient <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> was divided into two parts, the northern part having its capital at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span> and the southern part (also called 2. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti Dakkhiṇāpatha</span>) at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māhissati</span> (Māhiśmatī) (Bhaṇḍarkar: *Carmichael Lectures* (1918), p.54). This theory is supported by the fact that in the *Mahā Bhārata* (ii.31, 10), Avanti and Māhiśmatī are referred to as two different countries.

In the Buddha’s time, the King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajjota</span>, a man of violent temper (Vin.i.277), and therefore known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍappajjota</span>. He wished to conquer the neighbouring kingdom of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>, of which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> was king, but his plans did not work out as he had anticipated. Instead, his daughter <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsuladattā</span> became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>’s wife and the two countries continued to be on friendly terms. The romantic story of this marriage is given in Dhp­a.i.191ff. For a summary see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsuladattā</span>.

The kingdom of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaka</span> is invariably mentioned in connection with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>. Even in the Buddha’s life-time, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> became a centre of Buddhism. Among eminent monks and nuns who were either born or resided there, are to be found

- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mahā Kaccāna</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Nanda Kumāraputta</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Soṇa Kuṭikaṇṇa</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Dhammapāla</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Abhaya</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Rājakumāra</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Isidatta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Isidāsī</span>

It is said that when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajjota</span> heard of the Buddha’s advent to the world, he sent his chaplain’s son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāna</span>, with seven others, to invite him to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>.

Having listened to the Buddha’s teaching, the messengers became *Arahants*, and when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāna</span> conveyed to the Buddha the king’s invitation to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>, he was asked by the Buddha to return and represent him. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāna</span> returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> and converted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajjota</span> to the faith of the Buddha (Thag­a.i.485). Henceforward <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span> seems to have spent a good deal of his time in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>, dwelling in the city of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuraraghara</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Papāta</span> *Pabbata* (SN.iii.9, 12; iv.115-16; AN.v.46; also Ud­a.307).

The religion thus introduced, however, does not seem to have spread to any extent until much later; for we find <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span> experiencing great difficulty in collecting ten monks, in order that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa Kuṭikaṇṇa</span> might receive the higher ordination; in fact it was not until three years had elapsed that he succeeded (Vin.i.195). Later, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa Kuṭikaṇṇa</span> visited the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, he conveyed to the Buddha <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span>’s request that special rules might be laid down for the convenience of the monks of Avanti Dakkhiṇāpatha and of other border countries (Vin.i.197-8). The Buddha agreed, and among the rules so laid down were the following:

- (1) The higher ordination could be given with only four monks and a Vinayadhara.
- (2) Monks are allowed the use of shoes with thick linings (because in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Avanti</span> the soil is black on the surface, rough and trampled by cattle).
- (3) Monks are enjoined to bathe frequently (the men of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Avanti</span> attaching great importance to bathing).
- (4) Sheepskins, goatskins, etc., could be used as coverlets.
- (5) Robes could be accepted on behalf of a monk who has left the district, and the ten days’ rule with regard to such a gift will not begin until the robes have actually reached the monk’s hands (Cp. the first <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t23">nissaggiya</span> rule, Vin.iii.195-6) (this, evidently, because of difficulty of access).

By the time of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> Council, however, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> had become one of the important centres of the orthodox school, for we find <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa Kākaṇḍakaputta</span> sending messengers to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> to call representatives to the Council, and we are told that eighty-eight *Arahants* obeyed the summons (Vin.ii.298-9).

Among other localities in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> (besides those mentioned above) were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghanaselapabbata</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makkarakaṭa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷugāma</span>, and, in Jaina works, we find mention also of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudarśanapura</span> (Law: *Kṣatriya Tribes*, p.148).

Even in the Buddha’s day there were rumours of the King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> making preparations to attack <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, but we are not told that he ever did so (e.g.,MN.iii.7). Subsequently, however, before the time of Candagupta, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> became incorporated with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>. Before <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> became King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> he was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> Viceroy of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> and ruled in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>, and it was in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span> that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghamittā</span> were born and grew up (Mhv.xiii.8ff). But the country seems to have retained its name at least as late as the second century CE, as may be seen from Rudradāman’s Inscription at Junagaḍh (*Buddhist India*, p.28).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> is now identified with the country north of the Vindhaya Mountains and north-east of Bombay, roughly corresponding to modern Mālwa, Nimār and adjoining parts of the Central Provinces Law: *Geography of Early Buddhism*, p.22).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span> (Trs.ii.250, n.1) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> is mentioned as one of the three *maṇḍalas* or great divisions of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>, the other two being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pācīna</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇāpatha</span>.

According to a late tradition recorded in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhavaṁsa</span> (Bv.xxviii.10), the Buddha’s mat (*nisīdana*) and rug were deposited, after his death, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>.

It has sometimes been suggested that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> was the home of modern Pāli (e.g.,in *Buddhist India*, pp.153-4). It has further been suggested that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> school of monks – founded by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span>, who was considered the greatest analytical exponent of the Buddha’s time – living in comparative isolation (as seen above) on account of difficulty of access (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>, however, lay on the road taken by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvari</span>’s ten disciples on their way from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Patiṭṭhāna</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>), and laying special stress on *dhutavāda* practices (Vin.ii.299) – developed branches of knowledge dealing mainly with grammar and doctrinal interpretation by ways of exegetical analysis. The Pāli grammar ascribed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāyana</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nettippakaraṇa</span> were both works of this school. For a discussion of this see PLC.181ff.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> was one of the parts into which the earth was divided by King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Reṇu</span>, with the help of his Great Steward, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Govinda</span>. The King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> at the time was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessabhū</span> and his capital <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māhissati</span>. DN.ii.235-6.
