# Avanti

## English source

Avanti. One of the four great monarchies in the time of the Buddha, the other three being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṁsa</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vatsa</span>). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> is also mentioned among the sixteen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janapadā</span> (AN.i.213; iv.252, 256, 260).

Its capital was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>. But according to another account (DN.ii.235), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māhissati</span> is mentioned as having been, at least for some time, the capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>. It is quite likely that ancient <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> was divided into two parts, the northern part having its capital at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span> and the southern part (also called 2. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti Dakkhiṇāpatha</span>) at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māhissati</span> (Māhiśmatī) (Bhaṇḍarkar: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Carmichael Lectures</span> (1918), p.54). This theory is supported by the fact that in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Bhārata</span> (ii.31, 10), Avanti and Māhiśmatī are referred to as two different countries.

In the Buddha’s time, the King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajjota</span>, a man of violent temper (Vin.i.277), and therefore known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍappajjota</span>. He wished to conquer the neighbouring kingdom of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>, of which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> was king, but his plans did not work out as he had anticipated. Instead, his daughter <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsuladattā</span> became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>’s wife and the two countries continued to be on friendly terms. The romantic story of this marriage is given in Dhp­a.i.191ff. For a summary see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsuladattā</span>.

The kingdom of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaka</span> is invariably mentioned in connection with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>. Even in the Buddha’s life-time, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> became a centre of Buddhism.
