# Akaniṭṭhā Devā

## English source

Akaniṭṭhā Devā. A class of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, living in the highest of the five <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhāvāsā</span> (Pure Mansions) (DN.iii.237). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāpadānasutta</span> (DN.ii.52f) the Buddha mentions that he visited their abode and conversed with beings who were born there as a result of the holy lives they had lived under various Buddhas.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakkapañhasutta</span> (DN.ii.286) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> speaks of them as the highest <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, and expresses his satisfaction that he, too, will be born among them in his last life.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says they are so-called because of their supremacy in virtue and in happiness, and because there are no juniors among them (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sabbeh’eva saguṇehi ca bhavasampattiyā ca jeṭṭhā n’atth’ettha kaniṭṭhāti akaniṭṭhā</span>). DN­a.ii.480. VbhA.521 (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">āyunā ca paññāya ca Akaniṭṭhā jeṭṭhakā sabba-devehi paṇītatarā devā</span>, the Akaniṭṭha gods are the eldest in lifespan and wisdom, and are the most exalted among all the Devas). (=DN­a.iii.739).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visuddhimagga</span> (p. 634) their world is spoken of as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma loka</span> where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmīs</span> are born and enter complete <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nibbāna</span> (p. 710) (also Iti-a.40; DN­a.iii.740). The duration of life among these <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> is 16,000 Kalpas (Kv.207). Sometimes <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmī</span> are born among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avihā</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> and finish their existence, in a subsequent birth, among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Akaniṭṭhās</span>. These are called “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Uddhaṁsotā</span>” (Dhp­a.iii.289f.; see also SN.v.201).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Akaniṭṭhabhavana</span> is the upper limit of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">rūpāvacārabhūmi</span> (Paṭis.i.84); it is also spoken of as the highest point of the universe, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avīci</span> being the lowest. Thus the quarrel among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> monks spread even up to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Akaniṭṭhā devā</span> (Ja.iii.487), as did the shouts of the assembly at the severing of the branch of the Bodhi-tree (Mbv. 150-1; see also Mil. 284).
