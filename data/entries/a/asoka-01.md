# Asoka 01

## English source

Asoka 01. King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>. He was the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Biṇḍusāra</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Biṇḍusāra</span> had sixteen wives who bore him 101 sons. The chief Pāli sources of information regarding <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīpavaṁsa</span> (chaps. i., v., vi., vii., xi., etc.), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsa</span> (v., xi., xx., etc.), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samantapāsādikā</span> (pp.35 ff.). Other sources are the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Divyāvadāna</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">passim</span>, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Avadānaśataka</span> ii.200ff. For an exhaustive discussion of the sources and their contents see Prszlyski, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">La Legende de l’Empereur Asoka</span>.

The Pāli Chronicles (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīpavaṁsa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsa</span>) mention only three of the sons, viz. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Susīma</span> according to the northern legends) the eldest, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> (uterine brother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>) the youngest. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsaṭīkā</span> gives the name of his mother as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammā</span> and calls her Aggamahesī (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Biṇḍusāra</span>’s chief queen); she belonged to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moriyavaṁsa</span> (p.125; Mbv.98. In the northern tradition, e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asokāvadānamālā</span>, she is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhadrāṅgī</span>, daughter of a Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Campā</span>). The preceptor of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammā</span>’s family was an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ājīvaka</span> called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janasāna</span> (which probably explains <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s earlier patronage of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ājīvakas</span>).

In his youth <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> was appointed Governor of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> with his capital at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Divyāvadāna</span> says he was in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> with headquarters in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarāpatha</span>, where he superseded <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Susīma</span> and quelled a rebellion. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Biṇḍusāra</span> lay on his death-bed, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> left <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span> and came to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span> where he made himself master of the city and possessor of the throne. He is stated in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsa</span> (v.20; Mbv.98) to have killed all his brothers except <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> that he might accomplish his purpose, and to have been called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍāsoka</span> on account of this outrage (Mhv.v.189). It is impossible to say how much truth there is in this account of the accession. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s Rock Edicts seem to indicate that he had numerous brothers, sisters and relations alive at the time they were written in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span> and other towns (see Mookherji, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asoka</span>, pp. 3-6). His brother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> he appointed as his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Uparāja</span> (Mhv.v.33), but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) became a religious devotee attaining <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> Commentary refers to another younger brother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vītasoka</span>, who also became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> (i.295f. The northern works give quite a different account of his brothers. See Mookherji, p.6).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> had several wives. His first wife was the daughter of a merchant of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vedisagiri</span>, whom he met when stopping at the merchant’s house on his way to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span> (Mhv.xiii.8ff). Her name was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devī</span>, also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vedisa Mahā Devī</span>, and she was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span>, descended from a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> family who migrated to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vedisa</span> to escape from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Viḍūḍabha</span> (Mbv., pp.98, 116). Of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devī</span> were born a son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span>, and a daughter <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghamittā</span>, who became the wife of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggibrahmā</span> and mother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devī</span> evidently did not follow <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span>, for his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t18">aggamahesī</span> there was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asandhamittā</span> (Mhv.v.85). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asandhamittā</span> died in the thirtieth year of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s reign, and four years later he raised <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissarakkhā</span> to the rank of queen. Mhv.xx.1-3. The Allahabad Pillar Inscription mentions another queen, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāruvākī</span>, mother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tivara</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Divyāvadāna</span> (chap. xxvii.) gives another, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padmāvatī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇāla</span>’s mother. Besides the children mentioned above, names of others are given: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jalauka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cārumatī</span> (Mookherji. p.9).

According to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsa</span> (v.21, 22), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s accession was 218 years after the Buddha’s death and his coronation was four years later. The chronicles (v.22ff) contain various stories of his miraculous powers. His command spread a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojana</span> into the air and a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojana</span> under the earth. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> supplied him daily with water from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anotatta</span> Lake and with other luxuries from elsewhere. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> and even mice and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t18">karavīka</span> birds ministered to his comfort, and thoughtful animals came and died outside his kitchen in order to provide him with food.

At first <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> maintained the alms instituted by his father, but soon, being disappointed in the recipients, he began looking out for holy men. It was then that he saw from his window, his nephew, the young novice <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodha</span>. Owing to their friendship in a past birth (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodha</span> had been brothers, traders in honey, and they gave honey to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asandhamittā</span> had been the maiden who showed the honey-shop to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>. The story is given in Mhv.v.49ff), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> was at once drawn to him and invited him into the palace. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodha</span> preached to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Appamādavagga</span> and the king was greatly pleased. He ceased his benefactions to other religious orders and transferred his patronage to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodha</span> and members of the Buddhist Saṅgha. His wealth, which, according to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samantapāsādikā</span> (i.52), amounted to 500,000 pieces daily, he now spent in doing acts of piety – giving 100,000 to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodha</span> to be used in any manner he wished, a like sum for the offering of perfumes and flowers at the Buddha’s shrines, 100,000 for the preaching of the Dhamma, 100,000 for the provision of comforts for members of the Saṅgha, and the remainder for medicines for the sick. To <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodha</span>, in addition to other gifts, he sent sets of robes three times each day, placing them on the back of an elephant, adorned by festoons of flowers. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodha</span> gave these robes to other monks (MN­a.ii.931).

Having learnt from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggaliputtatissa</span> that there were 84,000 sections of the Dhamma, he built in various towns an equal number of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāras</span>, and in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span> he erected the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asokārāma</span>. With the aid of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kāḷa</span>, he created a life-size figure of the Buddha, to which he made great offerings.

His two children, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghamittā</span>, aged respectively twenty and eighteen, he ordained under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="119" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggaliputtatissa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="120" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapālā</span>, in the sixth year of his reign (MN­a.v.197, 209). This raised him from a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="121" data-dppn-term-class="t18">paccadāyaka</span> to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="122" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sāsanadāyādin</span>.

In order to purge the Saṅgha of undesirable monks and heretical doctrines, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="123" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggaliputtatissa</span> held the Third Council under the king’s patronage. It is said that the pious monks refused to hold the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="124" data-dppn-term-class="t18">uposatha</span> with those they considered unworthy. The king, desirous of bringing about unity in the Saṅgha, sent a minister to restore amity, but the minister, misunderstanding his orders, beheaded many holy monks, being at last stopped by the king’s brother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="125" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span>, who was then a monk (MN­a.vs.240ff).

At the conclusion of the Council, held in the seventeenth year of his reign, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="126" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> sent forth Theras to propagate the Buddha’s religion:
