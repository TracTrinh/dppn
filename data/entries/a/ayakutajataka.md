# Ayakūṭajātaka

## English source

Ayakūṭajātaka (Ja 347). The Bodhisatta was once born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> and became its king. At that time people were in the habit of sacrificing animals to the gods in order to win their favour, but the Bodhisatta proclaimed that no living creature should be slain. Being enraged at the loss of their food, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> sent one of their number to the Bodhisatta. He came to the Bodhisatta’s bed at night meaning to strike him a deadly blow. Thereupon <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s throne grew hot, and learning the cause, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> himself came and stood guard over the Bodhisatta. The latter saw the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> standing over him ready to strike but powerless, and only learnt later, to his great encouragement, that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> had been there to protect him (Ja.iii.145-7).

The reason for the telling of the story is given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaṇhajātaka</span>.
