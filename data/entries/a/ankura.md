# Aṅkura

## English source

Aṅkura. Tenth son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devagabbhā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upasāgara</span> (Ja.iv.81f), and one of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhakaveṇhudāsaputtā</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅkura</span> gave his share of the kingdom, won by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dāsaputtā</span>, to his sister <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Añjanā</span>, and started in trade (Ja.iv.81f). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Petavatthu</span> (Pv.23ff.; Pv-a.111ff) contains an account of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅkura</span>’s later career. Once he took a caravan of a thousand carts from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dvāravatī</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kamboja</span>, led by himself and a Brahmin colleague. On the way their water supply fails, but they are befriended by a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> of great power, who, in his previous life, had been one of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅkura</span>’s trusted and loyal servants. Annoyed by the suggestion of the Brahmin that instead of proceeding to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kamboja</span> they should entice the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> back with them to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dvāravatī</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> appears before them in person, and in answer to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅkura</span>’s questions, tells him that he had been a tailor in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bheruva</span>, where lived the generous <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asayha</span>. When suppliants came in search of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asayha</span>’s house, the tailor showed them the way. Impressed by the story, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅkura</span> returns forthwith to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dvāravatī</span>, and spends the rest of his life, 60,000 years (10,000 says <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">loc infra</span>); Vin-a.i.245), in acts of unparalleled munificence (there were as many as 3,000 cooks to supply food in his alms-halls and 60,000 youths to cut firewood.) He is reborn in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>.

In the assembly of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> who gather to listen to the Buddha’s preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅkura</span> occupies a place in the back row (12 leagues away says Dhp­a.iii.219; 10 leagues away says Pv.28, v.65) while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indaka</span>, who had given but one spoonful of rice to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha Thera</span><span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t18">,</span> sits quite close to the Buddha. The Buddha notices this and remarks that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indaka</span> had been lucky in finding a worthy donee; the recipients of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅkura</span>’s gifts had not been distinguished for their holiness. Gifts should, therefore, be given discriminately. At the end of this discourse <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅkura</span> becomes a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>. Dhp­a.iii.222; ibid., iv.82. See also Lüders, ZDMG. 58, 700.
