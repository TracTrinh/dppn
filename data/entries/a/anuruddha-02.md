# Anuruddha 02

## English source

Anuruddha 02. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha Thera</span>. First cousin of the Buddha and one of his most eminent disciples. He was the son of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amitodana</span> and brother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span>. When members of other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> families had joined the Saṅgha of their distinguished kinsman, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> was grieved that none had gone forth from his own. He therefore suggested to his brother that one of them should leave the household life. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> was at first reluctant to agree, for he had been reared most delicately and luxuriously, dwelling in a different house for each season, surrounded by dancers and mimes. But on hearing from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> of the endless round of household cares he agreed to go. He could not, however, get his mother’s consent until he persuaded his cousin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span> to go with him. Together they went with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhagu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbila</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> and their barber <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli</span>, to the Blessed One at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anupiya</span> Mango Grove and were ordained. Before the rainy season was over <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> acquired the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dibbacakkhu</span> (Vin.ii.180-3; Mvu.iii.177f), and he was later ranked foremost among those who had obtained this attainment (AN.i.23).

He then received from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, as topic of meditation, the eight thoughts of a great man. The list is given in AN.iv.228ff. Another conversation he had with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> before becoming an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> is reported in AN.i.281-2. He went into the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pācīnavaṁsadāya</span> in the Ceṭi country to practise these. He mastered seven, but could not learn the eighth. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>, being aware of this, visited him and taught it to him. Thereupon <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> developed insight and realised <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship in the highest grade (AN.iv. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">loc.cit.</span>; AN­a.108-9; Thag.901).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> appears in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span> as an affectionate and loyal comrade-bhikkhu, full of affection to his kinsman, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>, who returned his love. In the assembly he stood near the Buddha (Bv.v.60). When the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>, disgusted with the quarrels of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> monks, went away to seek more congenial surroundings, it was to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pācīnavaṁsadāya</span> that he repaired, where were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandiya</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbila</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upakkilesasutta</span> (MN.iii.153f.), was preached on the sweets of concord and freedom from blemish. And again in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Naḷakapānasutta</span> (MN.i.462ff.), though a large number of distinguished monks are present, it is to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> that the Buddha directly addresses his questions, and it is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> who answers on behalf of them all. See also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Gosiṅgasutta</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Gosiṅgasutta</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> was present when the Buddha died at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>, and knew the exact moment of his death; the verse he uttered on that occasion is thoughtful and shows philosophic calm, in contrast, for example, with that of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>. DN.ii.156-7. On this see Oldenberg, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nachrichten der Wissenschaften zu Goettingen</span>, 1902, pp.168f.; and Przyluski JA. mai-juin, 1918, pp.486ff.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> was foremost in consoling the monks and admonishing them as to their future course of action. It was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> again that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> consulted regarding the Buddha’s last obsequies (DN.ii.160f). Later, at the First Council, he played a prominent part and was entrusted with the custody of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (DN­a.i.15).

In one of the verses ascribed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> (904; Thag­a.ii.72) it is said that for twenty-five years he did not sleep at all, and that for the last thirty years of his life he slept only during the last watch of the night. The same source (Thag.908; also SN.i.200) mentions an occasion where a goddess, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jālinī</span>, who had been his wife in a previous birth, seeing him grown old and grey with meditation, seeks to tempt him with the joys of heaven, but he tells her he has no need of such things, having attained to freedom from rebirth (Thag­a.ii.73; this story is given in detail in SN­a.i.225-6).

His death took place in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvagāma</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjī</span> country, in the shade of a bamboo thicket. Thag.919. See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brethren</span>, 331, n.1. I cannot trace the reference to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthigāma</span>. He was one hundred and fifteen years old at the time of his death (DN­a.ii.413).

In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>’s time he had been a rich householder. Hearing one of the monks declared best among possessors of the celestial eye, he wished for a similar honour for himself in the future. He did acts of great merit towards that end, including the holding of a great feast of light in front of the Buddha’s tomb. In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>’s age he was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>; one day he placed bowls filled with clarified butter all round the Buddha’s tomb and lighted them, himself walking round the tomb all night, bearing on his head a lighted bowl.

Later he was reborn in a poor family in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> and was named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Annabhāra</span> (lit. “food-bearer”). One day, while working for his master, the banker <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span>, he gave his meal to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upariṭṭha</span>. The banker, having heard from the deity of his parasol of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Annabhāra</span>’s pious deed, rewarded him and set him up in trade. The king, being pleased with him, gave him a site for a house, the ground of which, when dug, yielded much buried treasure. On account of this great accretion of wealth he was given the rank of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanaseṭṭhi</span> (Thag­a.ii.65ff.; Thag.910; Dhp­a.iv.120ff).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp-a.i.113), as a result of his gift to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> never lacked anything he desired – such had been the wish he expressed. A charming story is related in this connection. Once when playing at ball with his friends he was beaten and had to pay with sweets. His mother sent him the sweets, but he lost over and over again until no more sweets were to be had. His mother sent word to that effect, but he did not know the meaning of the words “there isn’t.” When his mother, to make him understand, sent him an empty bowl, the guardian deity of the city filled it with celestial cakes, so that he should not be disappointed. Thereafter, whenever <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> sent for cakes, his mother would send him an empty vessel, which became filled on the way. See also Dhp­a.iv.124ff.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (i.35) mentions another incident of his past. Once, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumedha Buddha</span>’s time, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span>, having seen the Buddha meditating alone at the foot of a tree, set up lights round him and kept them burning for seven days. As a result he reigned for thirty <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> as king of the gods, and was king of men twenty-eight times. He could see a distance of a league both by day and night.

On various occasions <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> had discussions with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>, and he was consulted by disciples, both monks and laymen, on points of doctrine and practice. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddhasutta</span> (MN.iii.144f) he goes with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhiya Kaccāna</span> and two others to a meal at the house of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcakaṅga</span>, the king’s carpenter. At the end of the meal the carpenter asks him the difference between that deliverance of the heart (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetovimutti</span>) that is boundless (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t18">appamāṇa</span>) and that which is vast (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t18">mahaggata</span>). The discussion leads on to an account of the four states of rebirth among the brilliant gods (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ābhā</span>), and in reply to the questions of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhiya Kaccāna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> proceeds to explain their nature. At the end of the discourse we find <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> acknowledging that he himself had lived among these gods.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (SN.iv.240-5) he is mentioned as questioning the Buddha about women, how they come to be born in happy states and how in woeful purgatory. A similar inquiry is mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> had been visited by some <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manāpakāyikā</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, who had played and sung to him and shown their power of changing their complexions at will. He comes to the Buddha and asks how women could be born among these <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> (AN.iv.262ff).

We find him (SN.v.174-6, also 299f) being asked by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> about the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sekha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t18">asekha</span> and about super-knowledge (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t18">abhiññā</span>). In dealing with this passage the Commentary (SN­a.iii.183) states that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> used to rise early, and that after ablutions he sat in his cell, recalling up a thousand <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> of the past and the future. With his clairvoyant eye he knew the thousand-fold universe and all its workings.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha Saṁyutta</span> (SN.v.294) gives an account of a series of questions asked by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t18">satipaṭṭhānā</span>, their extent, etc. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> evidently laid great emphasis on the cultivation of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t18">satipaṭṭhānā</span>, for we find mention of them occurring over and over again in his discourses. He attributes all his powers to their development, and admonishes his hearers to practise them. SN.v.299-306. He himself considered the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dibbacakkhu</span> as the highest attainment. Thus in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Gosiṅgasutta</span> (MN.i.213) he declares it to be more worthy than knowledge of the doctrine, meditation, forest-life, discourse on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span> or self-mastery.

Once he lay grievously ill in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhavana</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, but the pain made no impression on his mind, because, he says, his mind was well grounded in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t18">satipaṭṭhānā</span> (SN.v.302, but see Dhp­a.iv.129, where he suffered from wind in the stomach). Apart from his teaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t18">satipaṭṭhānā</span>, he does not seem to have found fame as a teacher. He was of a retiring disposition and never interfered in any of the monks’ quarrels.

Mention is often made of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span>’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span>-powers. Thus, he was one of those who went to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>-world to curb the pride of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> who had thought that no ascetic could reach his world (SN.i.145. The others being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kappina</span>). The mother of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="119" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="120" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piyaṅkara</span>, while wandering in search of food, heard him at night reciting some verses from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="121" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada</span> and stood spellbound listening (SN.i.209; SN­a.i.237-8).

His <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="122" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span>, however, does not seem to have enabled him to prevent his fellow-dweller <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="123" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhiñjika</span> from talking too much (SN.ii.203-4), nor his other fellow-dweller <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="124" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhiya</span> from attempting to create dissension in the Saṅgha (AN.ii.239). Among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="125" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjians</span> he seems to have been held particularly in esteem, together with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="126" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandiya</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="127" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbila</span>. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="128" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="129" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha</span> tells the Buddha how the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="130" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjians</span> are envied by the inhabitants of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="131" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="132" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> worlds on account of the presence of these distinguished monks in their country (in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="133" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Gosiṅgasutta</span>, MN.i.210).

In numerous Jātakas <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="134" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> is identified with personalities occurring in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="135" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Atītavatthu</span>. In several cases he is mentioned as having been <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="136" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="137" data-dppn-term-class="t18">deus ex machina</span> of the story in question. Thus in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="138" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇicora</span> (Ja.ii.125); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="139" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Guttila</span> (ii.257); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="140" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ayakūṭa</span> (iii.147); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="141" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sūka</span> (iii.494); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="142" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Sūka</span> (iii.496); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="143" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇha</span> (iv.14); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="144" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Akitti</span> (iv.242); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="145" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sādhīna</span> (iv.360); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="146" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siri</span> (iv.412); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="147" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sutasoma</span> (v.511); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="148" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāma</span> (vi.95); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="149" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nimi</span> (vi.129); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="150" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sumagga</span> (vi.329); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="151" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span> (vi.593).
