# Asadisajātaka

## English source

Asadisajātaka (Ja 181). The story of the prince <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asadisa</span>. It was told in reference to the Great Renunciation to show that in former lives also the Bodhisatta had renounced a royal state (Ja.ii.86-92). The latter part of the story is given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Vastu</span> and is called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Śarakṣepanajātaka (</span>Mvu.ii.82-3).

The story is figured in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Bharhut Stūpa</span> (Cunningham, p.70, and Plate xxvii.13) and in the Sanchi Tope (Fergusson, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Tree and Serpent Worship</span>, p.181, Plate xxxvi). King Kittisiri of Ceylon wrote a beautiful poem in Sinhalese based on this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span>. Cv. Supplement 101, vs.13.
