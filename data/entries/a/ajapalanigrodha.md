# Ajapālanigrodha

## English source

Ajapālanigrodha. A banyan tree which is famous in Buddhist literature. It was in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span>, on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nerañjarā</span>, near the Bodhi-tree, and a week after the Awakening the Buddha went there and spent a week cross-legged at the foot of the tree. There he met the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Huhuṅkajātika</span> Brahmin (Vin.i.2-3). Two weeks later he went there again from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājāyatana</span> (Vin.i.4). It was then that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahma Sahampati</span> appeared to him and persuaded him to preach the doctrine, in spite of the difficulty of the task (Vin.i.5-7; in the eighth week after the Awakening, says <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>, SN­a.i.152). This was immediately after the meal offered by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tapassu</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhalluka</span>, so says the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhima Commentary</span> (i.385; Ja.i.81). When the Buddha wishes to have someone as his teacher, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> appears again and suggests to him that the Dhamma be considered his teacher (AN.ii.20f.; SN.i.138f).

By <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajapālanigrodha</span> it was, too, that, immediately after the Awakening, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> tried to persuade the Buddha to die at once (DN.ii.112). Several other conversations held here with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> are recorded in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta</span> (SN.i.103f).

Here, also, the Buddha spent some time before the Awakening (DN.ii.267), and it was here that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujāta</span> offered him a meal of milk-rice (Ja.i.16, 69).

Here, in the fifth week after the Awakening, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>’s daughters tried to tempt the Buddha (Ja.i.78, 469).

Several etymologies are suggested for the name: (a) in its shadow goatherds (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ajapālā</span>) rest; (b) old Brahmins, incapable of reciting the Vedas, live here in dwellings protected by walls and ramparts (this derivation being as follows: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">na japantī ti = ajapā, mantānaṁ anajjhāyakā = ajapā, ālenti arīyan-ti nivāsam etthā ti = Ajapālo ti</span>); (c) it shelters the goats that seek its shade at midday (Ud­a.51). The northern Buddhists say that the tree was planted by a shepherd boy, during the Bodhisatta’s six years’ penance, to shelter him (Beal, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Romantic Legend of Buddha</span>,192, 238; Mhv.iii.302).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmasutta</span> (SN.v.167) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maggasutta</span> (SN.v.185), both on the four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">satipaṭṭhāna</span>, and another <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmasutta</span> (SN.v.232f) on the five <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">indriyāni</span>, were concerning thoughts that occurred to the Buddha on various occasions at the foot of this tree, when he sat there soon after the Awakening. On all these occasions <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahma Sahampati</span> appeared to him and confirmed his thoughts. Several old Brahmins, advanced in years, visited the Buddha during this period and questioned him as to whether it were true that he did not pay respect to age. To them he preached the four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Therakaraṇā Dhamma</span>. AN.ii.22.
