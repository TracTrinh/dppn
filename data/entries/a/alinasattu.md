# Alīnasattu

## English source

Alīnasattu (*v.l.* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Adīnasattu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Alīnasatta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ālīnasatta</span>). The Bodhisatta, born as son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayaddisa</span> (*q.v.*), King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarapañcāla</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kampilla</span>. When the boy grew up, fully instructed in all the arts, his father made him Viceroy. Later, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayaddisa</span>’s life having become forfeit to the man-eating ogre (*porisāda*), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Alīnasattu</span> volunteered to offer himself in his father’s place. The ogre, impressed by the prince’s fearlessness and by the readiness with which he carried out his offer, refused to eat him and absolved him from his undertaking.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Alīnasattu</span> preached to him the five moral laws and, having discovered that the ogre was really a human being, offered him the throne, which, however, the latter would not accept (Ja.v.22ff).

In lists of births in which the Bodhisatta is mentioned as having practised *sīlapāramitā*, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Alīnasattujātaka</span> is mentioned (e.g., Ja.i.45).
