# Aṭṭhakavagga

## English source

Aṭṭhakavagga. The fourth division of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span>. It consists of sixteen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span>, all of which are explained in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Niddesa</span>. It may also have been the name of divisions of other books, because we are told that once <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa</span> Thera intoned before the Buddha all the verses of the Books of the Eights (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṭṭhaka</span><span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">-vaggikāni)</span> Vin.i.196-7. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp-a.iv.101-2) says he recited the 16 portions of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṭṭhakavagga</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandamātā</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Upāsikā</span> was once reciting the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṭṭhakavagga</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārāyaṇavagga</span> on the roof of her house, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span>, while on the way with his followers to see the Buddha, listened to her recital (Snp-a.i.370; but see AN.iv.63, where only the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārāyaṇa</span> is mentioned). According to this tradition, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṭṭhakavagga</span> was already being recited in the Buddha’s own time.

In Sanskrit the title was known as Arthavarga and was so understood by the Chinese translators. No one has explained what the title means nor has interpreted the second <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Guhaṭṭhaka</span>) except as “The Eight Verses on the Cave,” and similarly with the three following <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span>: Duṭṭhaṭṭhaka, Suddhaṭṭha and Paramaṭṭhaka, each of eight verses. The fact that it is commented on separately in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Niddesa</span> and was translated into Chinese makes it appear probable that it was once a separate work. See Thomas: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Life and Legend of the Buddha</span>, 274.
