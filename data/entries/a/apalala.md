# Apalāla

## English source

Apalāla. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nāga</span> king, converted by the Buddha. He is mentioned together with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āravāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanapāla</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārileyyaka</span>. The name appears in passages where the Buddha’s powers are discussed (e.g., Bv­a.29). “Was not the Buddha honoured even by beasts such as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āravāla</span>, etc.?”

The story of the conversion of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apalāla</span> does not, as far as I can discover, occur in the canonical books. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samantapāsādikā</span> (iv.742) the story of the conversion of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apalāla</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Apalāladamana</span>) is given among the stories not included in the Three Councils (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">saṅgīti</span>), but that it was known quite early in Ceylon is evidenced by the fact that, among the scenes from the Buddha’s life represented in the relic-chamber of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span>, the conversion of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apalāla</span> is mentioned (Mhv.xxx.84). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Divyāvadāna</span> (pp.348, 385) makes reference to the story, and states that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nāga</span> was converted shortly before the Buddha’s death. Xuanzang gives the story in detail (Beal-Xuan.i.122; also Legge: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Faxian’s Travels</span>, p.29n.). During <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>’s time, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apalāla</span> had been a powerful man called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgi</span>. By means of his charms he subdued the dragons that attacked the country, and the people, in gratitude, agreed to give him tribute. Later some of them forgot their promise and he, in wrath, became a dragon after his death.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> visited him and preached to him. He was converted, but, for his sustenance, he was allowed to have one gathering of the crops every twelve years. It is for this reason that the White River (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhavastu</span>) overflows every twelfth year. The story is found in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sūtrālaṅkāra</span> and other Mahāyāna books. See Nariman: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sanskrit Buddhism</span>, pp.194, 274.

According to the Vinaya of the Mūlasarvāstivādins, the Buddha converted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apalāla</span> during a visit to Kashmir in the company of the Yakṣa <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajrapānī</span> (JA.1914, vol. iv.510).
