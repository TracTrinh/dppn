# Abhibhū 01

## English source

Abhibhū 01. Chief disciple of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sikhī Buddha</span> (DN.ii.9; Ja.i.41; Bv.xxi.20). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aruṇavatīsutta</span> it is said that he went with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sikhī</span> to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>-world and, at the Buddha’s request, preached a sermon to the accompaniment of great magical powers. He proved that by using just such speech as if he were preaching to a gathering of monks, he could, standing in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>-world, make his voice heard by its thousand realms (SN.i.154f). The verses spoken on this occasion are, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span>, ascribed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhibhūta</span> (v.1147-8).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (AN.i.226f.; AN­a.i.436f) we find <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> asking the Buddha how far <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhibhū</span>’s powers bore relation to those of a Buddha, and the Buddha replying that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhibhū</span> was a mere disciple, and proceeding to describe the immeasurable powers of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tathāgata</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhibhū</span> was a Brahmin because we find him so addressed in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aruṇavatīsutta</span> referred to above, but in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhavaṁsa Commentary</span> (p.202) he is spoken of as a rājaputta.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭisambhidhāmagga</span> Commentary (488f) his story is given as an example of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vikubbana-iddhi</span> whereby a person could make himself seen in many places at the same time. We are told that he developed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nīlakasiṇa</span>, to attract to himself the attention of the world systems.

The Thera <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Adhopupphiya</span> had been a hermit in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span> during the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sikhī Buddha</span> and had offered flowers to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhibhū</span>. Ap.i.128-9.
