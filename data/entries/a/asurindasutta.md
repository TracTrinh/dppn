# Asurindasutta

## English source

Asurindasutta (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asurindakasutta</span>). Recounts an interview between the Buddha and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asurinda Bhāradvāja</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span>. When Asurinda heard that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhāradvāja</span> (probably the chief of the clan) had entered the Saṅgha, he was greatly vexed, and going up to the Buddha he abused him. The Buddha remaining silent, *Asurinda* thought that he acknowledged defeat. But the Buddha enlightened him, saying that the worse of the two is he who, when reviled, reviles back; he who does not so revile wins a twofold victory: he seeks the good both of himself and of the other (SN.i.163f.; SN­a.i.178).
