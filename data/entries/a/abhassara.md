# Ābhassara

## English source

Ābhassara. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>-world where live radiant <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> from whose bodies rays of light are emitted, like lightning. It belongs to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rūpaloka</span> and is in the plane of second <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jhāna</span> (Abhs. v.3; Comp.138, n.4). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> living there subsist on joy

(<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pītibhakkha</span>) (SN.i.114.; Dhp­a.iii.258; Ja.vi.55). Their span of life is two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> and there is no guarantee that a person who has been born there may not later be reborn in an unhappy condition (AN.ii.127; but see Abhs. v.6, where their life-span is given as eight <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span>). From time to time these <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> utter shouts of joy saying “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">aho sukhaṁ, aho sukhaṁ</span>.” This sound is the best of sounds. These <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> are completely enveloped in ease (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sukhena abhisaññā parisaññā</span>) (AN.iii.202; DN. iii.219). Their world forms the third station of consciousness (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">viññāṇaṭṭhiti</span>), they are of uniform body, but their perceptions are diverse (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ekattakāyā nānattasaññino</span>) (AN.iv.40, 401; DN.ii.69; DN.iii.253). During the periods of the development of the world many beings are born in the Ābhassara realm and they are then called the highest of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, yet even they change their condition (AN.v.60). In lists of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> (e.g., MN.i.289) they are given below the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Appamāṇābhā</span> and above the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhā</span>.

Bodhisattas are sometimes born in the Ābhassara world (AN­a.i.73; Ja.i.406, 473; MN.i.329; MN­a.i.553; SN­a.i.162), but they are never born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arūpa</span> worlds even when they have developed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arūpa</span>-<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jhānas</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bakabrahma</span> was born in Ābhassara after having passed through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vehapphala</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhakiṇṇa</span>, and it was then that he conceived the belief that he was eternal.

The Buddha visited him and convinced him of the error of his belief (Ja.iii.359). When the universe is dissolved after the lapse of a long epoch and is again evolved, beings are mostly born in the Ābhassara world. When, sooner or later, the world begins to re-evolve (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vivaṭṭati</span>), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmavimāna</span> appears, but it is empty. Then some being or other, either because he has finished his life there or because his merit is exhausted, leaves the Ābhassara world and is reborn in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmavimāna</span>. Others follow his example, and it is then that the first to be reborn in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>-world thinks of himself as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>, the eternal, etc. (DN.iii.29).

When inhabitants of the Ābhassara-world are reborn as humans, their existence continues to be like that which they had in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>-world itself. As time goes on, however, they lose their qualities and develop the characteristics, both physical and mental, of human beings (for details see DN.iii.84ff., Paṭis-a.253). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (DN­a.iii.865) says that their birth on earth is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t18">opapātika</span> (by spontaneous regeneration) and they are mind-born (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t18">manomaya</span>).

On the occasions when the world is destroyed by fire, the fire spread up to the Ābhassara-world; when by water, the water rises to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhakiṇṇa</span>; when by wind, the wind reaches to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vehapphala</span> (Cp-a.9).

According to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (MN­a.i.29; Vibh­a.520; cp. DN­a.ii.510), the Ābhassaras are so-called because radiance spreads from their bodies in all directions, like flames from a torch (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t18">daṇḍadīpikāya acci viya etesaṁ sarīrato ābhā chijjitvā chijjitvā patantī viya sarati visaratī ti Abhassarā</span>).

According to the scholiast of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candābhajātaka</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), beings who meditate on the Sun and Moon are born in this world. The Moon appears at the wish of the Ābhassara <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahmas</span>. See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candimā</span>.
