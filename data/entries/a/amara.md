# Amarā

## English source

Amarā (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amarādevī</span>). Wife of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span>. She was the daughter of a merchant who had fallen on evil days. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span>, while seeking for a wife, met her as she was taking a meal to her father and entered into conversation with her. He asked her various questions and she answered in riddles. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> went to her father’s house and plied his trade as a tailor, taking the opportunity of observing the girl’s behaviour. He tested her temper and her character in various ways, and being satisfied that she was altogether desirable, he married her with the approval of Queen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udumbarā</span>. She became popular with everybody and was of great assistance to her husband in frustrating the attempts of his enemies to work him harm (Ja.vi.364-72, 392; the story appears also in Mvu.ii.83).

In the present age <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amarā</span> was the beautiful <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbādevī</span> (Ja.vi.478).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milinda</span> (pp.205ff) the king mentions the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amarādevī</span> having been left behind in the village while her husband was away on a journey, and of her resisting a temptation to be unfaithful to him. “If that be true, how,” asks the king, “could you justify the Buddha’s statement that all women will go wrong, failing others, even with a cripple?” Incidentally, these words do not really belong to the Buddha. They appear in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇālajātaka</span> (Ja.v.435), which is a specimen of Indian folklore and not of Buddhist belief. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgasena</span> explains this by saying that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amarā</span> did not sin because she had neither real secrecy nor opportunity nor the right-wooer!
