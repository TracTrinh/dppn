# Ānandasutta 02

## English source

Ānandasutta 02. Once <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> was living in a forest tract in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> country and was much occupied in talking to the laity who came to see him. A *Deva* of the forest, desiring his welfare, came up to him and suggested that he might stop his constant babbling and meditate instead (SN.i.199). According to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (SN­a.i.225), this was soon after the Buddha’s death, shortly before <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> became *Arahant*. People, knowing of his close attendance on the Master, were ever asking for details about the *Parinibbāna* and when they mourned he had to admonish them. He used to wander about, taking with him the Buddha’s begging bowl and robe. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> (ver.119; Thag­a.i.237) the same admonition is put into the mouth of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjiputta</span> monk.
