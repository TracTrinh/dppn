# Āḷāra Kālāma

## English source

Āḷāra Kālāma. One of the two teachers to whom <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>, after his renunciation, first attached himself, the other being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uddaka Rāmaputta</span>. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span> (p.236) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span> is mentioned as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>’s fourth teacher. The Thīg­a. (p.2) says he went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaggava</span> before going to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Vastu</span> (ii.117f.) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Lalitavistara</span> (330f), give quite different accounts.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariyapariyesanāsutta</span> (MN.i.163-5; also 240ff; ii.94ff, 212ff) the Buddha describes his visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> quickly mastered his doctrine and was able to repeat it by heart; but feeling sure that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span> not only knew the doctrine but had realised it, he approached him and questioned him about it. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span> then proclaimed the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ākiñcaññāyatana</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>, putting forth energy and concentration greater than <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span>’s, made himself master of that state. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span> recognised his pupil’s eminence and treated him as an equal, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>, not having succeeded in his quest, took leave of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span> to go elsewhere (Vibh­a.432). When, after having practised austerities for six years, the Buddha attained Awakening and granted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span>’s request to preach the doctrine, it was of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span> he thought first as being the fittest to hear the teaching. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span> had died seven days earlier (Vin.i.7).

The books mention little else about <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Parinibbānasutta</span> (DN.ii.130; Vsm.330) mentions a Mallian, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusa</span>, who says he had been <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span>’s disciple, but who, when he hears the Buddha’s sermon, confesses faith in the Buddha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusa</span> describes <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span> to the Buddha as one who practised great concentration. Once <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span> was sitting in the open air and neither saw nor heard five hundred passing carts though he was awake and conscious.

As already stated above, the aim of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span>’s practices is stated to have been the attainment of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ākiñcaññāyatana</span>, the stage of nothingness. Whether this statement is handed down with any real knowledge of the facts of his teaching, it is not now possible to say. Aśvaghoṣa, in his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Buddhacarita</span> (xii.17ff), puts into the mouth of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ārāda</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span>, a brief account of his philosophy. It has some resemblance – though this is slight – to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sāṅkhya</span> philosophy, but in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span>’s teaching some of the salient characteristics of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sāṅkhya</span> system are absent. In reply to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>’s questions about the religious life and the obtaining of final release, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span> describes a system of spiritual development which is identical with the methods of the Buddhist monk up to the last attainment but one. The monk reaches the four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jhānas</span> and then attains successively to the states of space, infinity and nothingness. The last three stages are described in the terms of the first three of the four Attainments (for a discussion on this see Thomas: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Life and Legend of the Buddha</span>, p.229-30; see also MN­a.ii.881; Vibh­a.432). According to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (AN­a.i.458), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bharaṇḍu Kālāma</span> was a disciple of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span> at the same time as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> and is therefore described as the Buddha’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t18">purāṇa-sabrahmacārī</span> (AN.i.277). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> further tells us (DN­a.ii.569) that in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra Kālāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span> was his personal name. He was so-called because he was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dīgha-piṅgala</span> (long and tawny) (the name actually means “curved”).
