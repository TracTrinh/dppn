# Abhayagiri,

## English source

Abhayagiri, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagirivihāra,</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayuttaravihāra</span>. A celebrated monastic establishment on the north side of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>, consisting of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> and a mighty <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thūpa</span>. Only the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thūpa</span> now stands. It was built by King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṭṭagāmaṇī Abhaya</span> on the site of the ancient <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Titthārāma</span>, 217 years, 10 months and 10 days after the founding of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> (Mhv.xxxvii.78-83). Tradition states that when the king was fleeing from the Tamils he passed the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Titthārāma</span> on his way, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nigaṇṭha</span> Giri, who then lived there, made insulting remarks about him. The king vowed, if he were returned to the throne, to build a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> on that spot (Mhv.xxxvii.43-4); he fulfilled his vow, and the name of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> was a combination of his own name and of that of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nigaṇṭha</span>. The monastery was given in charge of the Thera <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Tissa</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuppikala</span> and of two other monks, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuppikala</span> having befriended the king in his misfortunes.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> advanced rapidly in wealth and in power, but quite soon the monks seceded from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> fraternity because, according to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsa</span> (Mhv.xxxvii.95ff), an incumbent of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Tissa</span> by name, was expelled from the monastery for frequenting lay families. His disciple, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bahalamassutissa</span>, went in anger to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> and formed a separate faction.

A Sinhalese chronicle, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nikāya Saṅgraha</span> (pp.11, 12; also PLC.42), states that these dissentients were soon after joined by a body of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjiputtaka</span> monks from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pallārāma</span> in India, under the leadership of a teacher called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammaruci</span>, and the sect which they together founded in Ceylon became known as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammarucinikāya</span>, with headquarters in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span>.

For quite a long while the two fraternities, that of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> and that of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span>, seem to have lived in amity, alike enjoying the munificence of patrons (Ibid., 52f.; Mhv.xxxv.20, 57, 119-22; xxxvi.7-14). Thus, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhukagāmaṇi</span> raised the height of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayuttarathūpa</span> (as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thūpa</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> seems to have been called) and made the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gāmaṇitissa</span> tank to be used for the cultivation of land for the maintenance of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> (ibid., xxxv.119-22); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaniṭṭhatissa</span> built a splendid structure in the same <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> for the Thera <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāga</span>; it was called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratanapāsāda</span> (xxxvi.7, 8.).

But in the reign of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vohārakatissa</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> monks openly adopted the heretical <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Vaitulya Piṭaka</span> (of the Mahāyānists see Mhv. trans. 259, n.2). An inquiry was held by the king with the help of his minister <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapila</span>, the heretical books were burnt and the monks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> disgraced (Mhv.xxxvi.40-1).

Soon afterwards, however, the heretics won over the king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sena</span> to their side and destroyed the establishment of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>, carrying away all the materials to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> (PLC. 53; Mhv.xxxvii.10-16). Later, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sena</span> repented of his ways, burnt the books of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> monks and transferred his patronage to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>. But the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> fraternity must soon have recovered its prestige, for we find <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sena</span>’s successor, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimeghavaṇṇa</span>, planting a Bodhi tree (called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissavasabha</span>) (Cv.trans. i.9, n.3) in Abhayagiri and surrounding it with a stone terrace (Cv.xxxvii.91). A few years later both <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> (409-31) and his queen became active supporters of Abhayagiri (Cv.xxxvii.212). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhātusena</span> is stated to have enlarged the Abhayuttaravihāra (Cv.xxxviii.61), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Silākāla</span> is credited with several benefactions to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> and its Bodhi tree (Cv.xli.31-2); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāga</span> gave the weaver’s village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambela</span> to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttaravihāra</span> (another name for Abhayagiri; see Cv. trans. i.8, n.2; 61, n.6.); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggabodhi I</span>. built a bathing-tank there (Cv.xlii.28), while his successor, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggabodhi</span> II, built the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dāṭhāggabodhi</span> house, so-called after himself and his queen (Cv.xlii.63-5).

In the monastery at Abhayagiri there seems to have been a stone image of the Buddha, referred to under various names, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Silāsambuddha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷasela</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷasatthā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Silāsatthā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Silāmayamuninda</span>. Cv.xxxix.7; xxxviii.65; 61.2; see also vv.51, 77, 87. There was also in Abhayagiri another image called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhiseka</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>).

It was evidently held peculiarly sacred. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhadāsa</span> placed a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nāgamaṇi</span> in its eye (Cv.xxxvii.123); this was soon lost, and we find <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhātusena</span> replacing it, adorning and decorating the statue in various ways (for details see Cv.xxxviii.62ff). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Silāmeghavaṇṇa</span> had it restored and redecorated and made provision for its maintenance (Cv.xliv.68). The same king, we are told, attempted to carry out a reform of the Abhayagiri monks, but this attempt ultimately brought disaster on him (Cv.xliv.75ff). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jeṭṭhatissa</span> gave to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> the village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Dāragiri</span> (Cv.xliv.96). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dāṭhopatissa</span> built the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kappūrapariveṇa</span> attached to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span>, and also a monastery <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tiputthulla</span>, encroaching on the precincts of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>, notwithstanding the protests of the monks belonging to the latter (Cv.xlv.29ff). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggabodhi</span> VII, added the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhattudesabhoga</span> (Cv.xlviii.64), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda II</span>. the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Lekha</span> pariveṇa as well as the many-storeyed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratanapāsāda</span> with its costly ornamentation (Cv.xlviii.135-40; see also Geiger’s trans. 123, n.2).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sena I</span>. built the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Viraṅkurārāma</span> and gave it to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Saṅghikas</span> (Cv.l.68-9), while his consort, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghā</span>, erected a dwelling house, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahindasena</span> (Cv.l.79), and his courtier, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span>, yet another dwelling house, called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarasena</span>, for the maintenance of which he provided. Two other courtiers, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajira</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rakkhasa</span>, built two dwelling houses, called respectively <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajirasenaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rakkhasa</span> (Cv.l.83).

In the reign of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sena II</span>. the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṁsukūlika</span> monks, who till then had evidently lived in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> (Cv. trans. i.108, n.1), separated and formed special groups. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghā</span>, queen of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udaya II</span>., erected and endowed the building known as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghasenapabbata</span> (Cv.li.86-7). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa IV</span>. built a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pāsāda</span> bearing his name and assigned to it a village (Cv.lii.13; Cv.trs. i.162, n.4), while his successor, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa V</span>., erected the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaṇḍikāpariveṇa</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Silāmeghapabbata</span>, endowing each with a village (Cv.lii.58-9).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sena III</span>. spent 40,000 <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kahāpaṇas</span> for a stone paving round the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> monks befriended both <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijayabāhu I</span>. (then known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="119" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kitti</span>) and his brother, and out of gratitude <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="120" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijayabāhu</span> built the Uttaramūla <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="121" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pariveṇa</span>, which was probably attached to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="122" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihara</span> itself (Cv.lvii.18, 23).

In the reign of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="123" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu I</span>., when that monarch had established himself on the throne, it is said that he tried to reform the monks of the Abhayagiri, but he found the task hopeless (Cv.lxxviii.21ff). He found that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="124" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagirithūpa</span> had been destroyed by the vandalism of the Tamils, and he had it restored to a height of 160 cubits (Cv.lxxviii.98). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="125" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> was finally abandoned, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="126" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> fell into ruin and decay, the monastery being completely destroyed.

It is clear that even at the outset there was considerable rivalry between the monks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="127" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> and those of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="128" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>. The rivalry seems originally to have been mainly personal, but it later developed into differences in doctrinal opinion. Of the exact nature of these latter we have no information, owing, chiefly, to the book-burnings carried out by pious kings in the excess of their zeal for the purity of the Faith. For the same reason we are unable to ascertain what part, if any, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="129" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> fraternity played in literary activity. It has been suggested, however, that both the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="130" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka Commentary</span> (PLC.124, 125) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="131" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahassavatthuppakaraṇa</span> (PLC.128), another compilation of tales, were the work of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="132" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> monks.

Fa-Hsien evidently spent the two years of his stay in Ceylon with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="133" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> fraternity because the books he took away with him were those of the unorthodox schools. According to him, there were, at this time, 5,000 monks in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="134" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> (Faxian’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="135" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Travels</span>, 67ff).

In the chronicles <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="136" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> is referred to under several names <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="137" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayuttara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="138" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayavihāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="139" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayācala</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="140" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttaravihāra</span>.
