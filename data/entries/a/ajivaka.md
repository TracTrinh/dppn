# Ājīvaka

## English source

Ājīvaka, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ājīvakas</span>. A class of naked ascetics (see, e.g., Vin.i.291), followers of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makkhali Gosāla</span>, regarded, from the Buddhist point of view, as the worst of sophists. Numerous references to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ājīvakas</span> are to be found in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Piṭakas</span>, only a few of them being at all complimentary. Thus in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Saccakasutta</span> they are spoken of as going about naked, flouting life’s decencies and licking their hands after meals. MN.i.238; see also SN.i.66, where a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span> praises <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gosāla</span> as a man who had attained to perfect self-control by fasting and austere practices. He had abandoned speech and wordy strife with any person, was equable, a speaker of truth, a doer of no evil. That the life of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ājīvakas</span> was austere may be gleaned from their condemnation of monks carrying parasols (Viii.ii.130).
