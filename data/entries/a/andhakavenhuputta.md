# Andhakaveṇhuputtā

## English source

Andhakaveṇhuputtā, or, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhakaveṇhudāsaputtā</span>. Ten brothers, sons of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devagabbhā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upasāgara</span>. As it had been foretold at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devagabbhā</span>’s birth that one of her sons would destroy the lineage of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṁsa</span>, each time a son was born to her, fearing lest he be put to death, she sent him secretly to her serving-woman, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandagopā</span>; the latter had married <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhakaveṇhu</span> and, by good fortune, daughters were born to her at the same time as sons to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devagabbhā</span>; these daughters she sent to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devagabbhā</span> in exchange for the latter’s sons.

The ten sons were named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsudeva</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Baladeva</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candadeva</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suriyadeva</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggideva</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Varuṇadeva</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajjuna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajjuna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghatapaṇḍita</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅkura</span>. Cowell sees in this story the kernel of a nature-myth (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span>, trans. iv. 51 n.); cf. with this the Kṛṣṇa legend in the Harivaṁsa; see also Wilson’s Viṣṇu <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Purāṇa</span> (Hall’s Ed.), v. 147f.; and the article on Kṛṣṇa in Hopkins’ <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Epic Mythology</span>, pp.214f.

They had also a sister, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Añjanadevī</span>. When they grew up they became highway robbers, seizing even a present sent to their uncle, King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṁsa</span>. Thus they became notorious as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andakaveṇhudāsaputtā</span>. The king, having learnt of their true descent, devised various plans for their destruction. Two famous wrestlers, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cānura</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Muṭṭhika</span>, were engaged to have a public wrestling match with them. The brothers accepted the challenge and looted several shops for clothes, perfumes, etc., to be used for the occasion. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Baladeva</span> killed both the wrestlers. In his death-throes <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Muṭṭhika</span> uttered a prayer to be born as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>; his wish was fulfilled and he was born as such in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷamattiya</span> forest. When the king’s men attempted to seize the brothers, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsudeva</span> threw a wheel which cut off the heads of both the king and his brother the viceroy, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upakaṁsa</span>.

The populace, terrified, begged the brothers to be their guardians. Thereupon they assumed the sovereignty of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asitañjana</span>. From there they set out to conquer the whole of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>, starting with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ayojjhā</span> (whose king, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷasena</span>, they took prisoner) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dvāravatī</span>, which they captured with the help of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇhadīpāyana</span>.

They made <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dvāravatī</span> their capital and divided their kingdom into ten shares, forgetting their sister, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Añjanadevī</span>. When they discovered their mistake, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅkura</span> gave her his share and took to trade. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅkura</span>’s later history is found in Pv-a.111ff. See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅkura</span>.

In course of time the brothers had many sons and daughters, the average human age at that time being 20,000 years. Later their sons annoyed the sage <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇhadīpāyana</span> by dressing up a lad as a woman and asking him what child she would bring forth. “A knot of acacia wood,” he answered, “with which will be destroyed the line of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vasudeva</span>.”

They laughed at the sage and kicked him. On the seventh day the lad voided from his belly a knot of acacia wood which they burnt, casting the ashes into the river. From those ashes, which stuck near the citygate, an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Eraka</span>-plant sprang up. One day, while disporting themselves in the water, the kings, with their families and followers, started a sham quarrel and plucked leaves from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Eraka</span>-plant to use as clubs. The leaves turned into weapons in their hands, and they were all killed except <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsudeva</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Baladeva</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Añjanadevī</span>, and their chaplain, all of whom fled in a chariot. Thus were the words of the sage fulfilled.

In their flight they reached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷamattiya</span> forest in which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Muṭṭhika</span> had been born as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Muṭṭhika</span> saw <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Baladeva</span> he assumed the shape of a wrestler and challenged him to a fight. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Baladeva</span> accepted the challenge and “was gobbled up like a radish-bulb.”

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsudeva</span> proceeded on his way with the others and at night lay in a bush for shelter. A huntsman, mistaking him for a pig, speared him; when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsudeva</span> heard that the huntsman’s name was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jarā</span> (Old Age) he reconciled himself to death. Thus they all perished except <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Añjanadevī</span> (Ja.iv.79ff), of whose later history nothing is mentioned.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumbhajātaka</span> (Ja 512, Ja.v. p.18) it is suggested that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhakaveṇhus</span> were destroyed as a result of indulging in drink. This story was evidently well-known to tradition as it is so often referred to, e.g., in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkiccajātaka</span> (Ja 530). (v. 267) and in Vv., p.58.
