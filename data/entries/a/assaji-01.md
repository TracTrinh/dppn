# Assaji 01

## English source

Assaji 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji Thera</span>. The fifth of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcavaggiya</span> monks. When the Buddha preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammacakkappavattanasutta</span>, he was the last in whom dawned the eye of Truth, and the Buddha had to discourse to him and to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> while their three colleagues went for alms (Vin.i.13. He became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span> on the fourth day of the quarter, AN­a.i.84). He became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>, together with the others, at the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anattalakkhaṇasutta</span> (Vin.i.14; Ja.i.82).

He was responsible for the conversion of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, in the course of his wanderings in search of Eternal Truth, saw <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji</span> begging for alms in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, and being pleased with his demeanour, followed him till he had finished his round. Finding a suitable opportunity, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji</span> about his teacher and the doctrines he followed. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji</span> was at first reluctant to preach to him, because, as he said, he was but young in the Saṅgha. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> urged him to say what he knew, and the stanza which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji</span> uttered then, has, ever since, been famous, as representing the keynote of the Buddha’s teaching:

“<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ye dhammā hetuppabhavā tesaṁ hetuṁ Tathāgato āha tesañ ca yo nirodho, evaṁvādī Mahā Samaṇo</span>.”

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> immediately understood and hurried to give the glad tidings to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> that he had succeeded in his quest. Vin.i.39ff.; the incident is related in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp-a.i.75ff.) with slight variations as to detail.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> held <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji</span> in the highest veneration, and we are told that from the day of this first meeting, in whatever quarter he heard that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji</span> was staying, in that direction he would extend his clasped hands in an attitude of reverent supplication, and in that direction he would turn his head when he lay down to sleep (Dhp­a.iv.150-1).

One day when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji</span> was going about in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> for alms, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saccaka</span>, who was wandering about in search of disputants to conquer, saw him, and questioned him regarding the Buddha’s teaching because he was a well-known disciple (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ñātaññatara-sāvaka</span>). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji</span> gave him a summary of the doctrine contained in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anattalakkhaṇasutta</span>. Feeling sure that he could refute these views attributed to the Buddha, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saccaka</span> went with a large concourse of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> to the Buddha and questioned him. This was the occasion for the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Saccakasutta</span> (MN.i.227ff). The Commentary (MN­a.i.452) tells us that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji</span> decided on this method of exposition because he did not wish to leave <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saccaka</span> any loophole for contentious questioning.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (SN.iii.124ff) records a visit paid by the Buddha to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji</span> as he lay grievously sick in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapārāma</span> near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. He tells the Buddha that he cannot enter into <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jhāna</span> because of his difficulty in breathing and that he cannot win balance of mind. The Buddha encourages him and asks him to dwell on thoughts of impermanence and non-self.
