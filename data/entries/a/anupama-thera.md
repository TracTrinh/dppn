# Anūpama Thera

## English source

Anūpama Thera. He belonged to a wealthy family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> and obtained his name (“Peerless”) because of his beauty. When he came of age, urged by the workings of his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">upanissaya</span> (potential) (or, efficient cause), he left the world and dwelt in the forest, practising insight. For some time his mind wandered, but later he put forth great effort and became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> (Thag.vv.213-14).

Thirty-seven <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> ago he had offered flowers to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paduma</span> (Thag­a.i.334-6). He is evidently to be identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅkolapupphiya Thera</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> i.287.
