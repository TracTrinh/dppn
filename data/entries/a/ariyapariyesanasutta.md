# Ariyapariyesanāsutta

## English source

Ariyapariyesanāsutta. Preached in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> in the hermitage of the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rammaka</span>. Some monks expressed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> their desire to hear a discourse from the Buddha, as it was so long since they had heard one. He advised them to go to the hermitage of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rammaka</span> where their wishes might be fulfilled. The noontide of that same day <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> spent with the Buddha at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pubbārāma</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāramātupāsāda</span> and in the evening, after the Buddha had bathed in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pubbakoṭṭhaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> suggested to him that he might go to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rammaka</span>’s hermitage. The Buddha assenting, they went together. The Buddha, finding the monks engaged in discussing the Dhamma, waited till their discussion was over. Having inquired the topic thereof, he praised them and proceeded to tell them of the two quests in the world – the noble and the ignoble. He described how he, too, before his Awakening, had followed the quest, apprenticing himself to various teachers, such as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra Kālāma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uddaka Rāmaputta</span>, and how, on discovering that they could not give him what he sought, he went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span> and there found the consummate peace of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nibbāna</span>. This biographical account is also found in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Saccaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bodhirājakumāra</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅgāravasutta</span>s. It is in part repeated in the Vinaya and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghanikāya</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> then proceeds to give an account of the Buddha’s first reluctance to preach, of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span>’s intervention, of the meeting with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ājīvaka Upaka</span> and the first sermon preached to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcavaggiyas</span>. Finally the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> expounds the pleasures of the senses, the dangers therefrom and the freedom and confidence which ensue when one has overcome desire (MN.i.160-75).

In the Commentary (MN­a.i.369ff) the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāsarāsi</span>, evidently because of the simile found at the end of the discourse where the pleasures of the senses are compared to baited traps.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atthasālinī</span> quotes it (p.35).
