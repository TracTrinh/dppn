# Ānandamāṇava

## English source

Ānandamāṇava. Called *māṇava*, in order to distinguish him from other Ānandas. He was a Brahmin youth, maternal cousin of the Therī <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uppalavaṇṇā</span>, with whom he had been in love when she was a laywoman. One day when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uppalavaṇṇā</span> returned from her alms-rounds to her hut in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhavana</span>, where she was living at the time, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānandamāṇava</span>, who was hiding under her bed, jumped up and seized her. In spite of her protestations and admonitions, he overcame her resistance by force and, having worked his will of her, went away. As if unable to endure his wickedness, the earth burst asunder and he was swallowed up in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avīci</span> (Dhp­a.ii.49-50).

In order that such assaults should not be repeated, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi Kosala</span> erected, at the Buddha’s suggestion, a residence for the nuns within the city gates, and henceforth they lived only within the precincts of the city (Dhp­a.ii.51f).
