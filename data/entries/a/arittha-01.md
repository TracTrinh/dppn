# Ariṭṭha 01

## English source

Ariṭṭha 01. A monk. He had been subjected by the Saṅgha to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ukkhepanīyakamma</span> for refusal to renounce a sinful doctrine, namely, that the states of mind declared by the Buddha to be stumbling-blocks are not such at all for him who indulges in them. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariṭṭha</span> left the Saṅgha and would not come back until the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ukkhepanīyakamma</span> was revoked (Vin.ii.25-8). He was a vulture-trainer (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gaddhabādhiputta</span>) (See note in VT.ii.377).

His case is cited as that of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pācittaya</span>-offence because he refused to give up a wrong doctrine even after the monks had three times requested him to do so (Vin.iv.135).

In spite of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ukkhepanīyakamma</span> the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Chabbaggiya</span> monks kept company with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariṭṭha</span>, thereby committing a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pācittaya</span>-offence (Vin.iv.137). We find the Buddha rebuking the nun <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thullanandā</span> for associating with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariṭṭha</span> after the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ukkhepanīyakamma</span> (Vin.iv.218).

It was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariṭṭha</span>’s heresy that led to the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Alagaddūpamasutta</span> (MN.i.130ff).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (SN.v.314-15), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariṭṭha</span> is mentioned as having said to the Buddha that he practised concentration in breathing and as having described how he did it. The Buddha, thereupon, instructs him as to how such concentration can be done perfectly and in every detail.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samantapāsādikā Ariṭṭha</span> is mentioned in a list of enemies of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sāsana</span>. Vin-a.iv.874.
