# Ariyagālatissa

## English source

Ariyagālatissa. Son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Dhanadeva</span>, of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Gāma</span>. When he was a child he was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Keḷiyatissa</span>, because he liked to play. Later he joined the Saṅgha. Dissatisfied after five years, he gave up his robes and swam down a river. Two women bathing saw him and both claimed him. When they saw he was naked one went to fetch clothes; the other gave him half her garment and took him to her parents and married him. The wife was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanā</span>. Because he was found in the river he was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgatissa</span>; but because he was lazy they called him <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nikkammatissa</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanā</span>’s people complained of him and sent them away. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> sought work under a headman, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candasuriya</span>, a friend of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Dhanadeva</span>, and reaped and threshed a field of 500 *karīsa*s in one day. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candasuriya</span>, marvelling at his strength, gave him all the grain.

Thereafter, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> and his wife gave alms daily to eight monks, but as his meals were not tasty, the monks called him <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udakaloṇatissa</span>. Discovering this, he gave them milk-rice and was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khīrabhattatissa</span>. Later he gave rice mixed with ghee and his name became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalyāṇabhattatissa</span>. In due course he fed 500 monks daily. One day, while looking for yams in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumbulapabbata</span>, he discovered 60 treasure troves and took them home. Later he fed many thousands of monks in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyambavihāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadīpa</span> and lived in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariyagālatittha</span> as a ferryman, taking people across, free of charge. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, wishing to test him, came as an old Brahmin and, having tried his patience, filled his house with valuables and gave him a field of rice.

Once a discussion arose in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piyaṅgudīpa</span>, as to where the most devout people were to be found. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Satisambodhi Thera</span> said they were in Ceylon, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yonakarājaputta Mahā Buddharakkhita</span> said they were in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yonakaraṭṭha</span>. To test this, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Satisambodhi</span> arrived in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span>’s house. His wife, having fed 12,000 monks, was resting, but having seen the Elder she prepared for him a bowl of *catumadhura*. He asked her to throw the bowl up into the air. It travelled to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piyaṅgudīpa</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanā</span> saw the monks as they ate the contents.

As <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> lay dying, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> brought him chariots from the six *Deva* worlds; he chose to be born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span>. His wife, knowing his wishes, retired into her room and died before him. They were both born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span>. In a past birth <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> had been in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chagāma</span> and honoured the Bodhi-tree there. Ras.ii.34f.
