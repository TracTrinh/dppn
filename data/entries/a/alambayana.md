# Ālambāyana

## English source

Ālambāyana (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ālambāna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ālamba</span>). Originally the name of a spell taught to an ascetic by a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Garuḍa</span> king who had unwittingly torn up by its roots a banyan tree which grew at the end of the ascetic’s walk. The ascetic taught it to a poor Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> who had gone into the forest to escape his creditors and who ministered to the ascetic.

The Brahmin became known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ālambāyana</span> after he learnt the spell. Having learnt it he left the forest and was walking along the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamunā</span>, when he came across a host of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span>, sitting, after their sports, round the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> gem which grants all desires. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span>, hearing the man repeat the charm, fled in terror, believing him to be the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Garuḍa</span>, and he took possession of their jewel. Soon after, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ālambāyana</span> met an outcast Brahmin with his son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Somadatta</span>, and on their agreeing to show him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> King, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhūridatta</span>, he gave them the jewel.

With the help of his spell <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ālambāyana</span> tamed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhūridatta</span> and went about giving exhibitions of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>’s skill. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhūridatta</span> was finally rescued by his brother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudassana</span> and his sister <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Accimukhī</span>. In the contest of skill which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ālambāyana</span> had with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudassana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Accimukhī</span> assumed the form of a frog and let drip three drops of poison on her brother’s hand, and these were allowed to fall into a hole specially prepared and filled with cow-dung. A flame burst out and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ālambāyana</span> was smitten with the heat. His skin changed colour and he became a white leper.

The story is told in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhūridattajātaka</span> (Ja 543, Ja.vi.179-97).

The name <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ālambāyana</span> appears also as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ālambāna</span> and as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ālamba</span>.
