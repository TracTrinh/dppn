# Āḷavī

## English source

Āḷavī. A town thirty <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojanas</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> (Snp-a.i.220) and probably twelve from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> (See Watters: ii.61; Fa Hsein, 60, 62). It lay between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. (The Buddha goes from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kiṭāgiri</span>, thence to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavī</span>, and finally, to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>). The Buddha, on several occasions, stayed at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavī</span> at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggāḷavacetiya</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) which was near the town. In the sixteenth year after the Awakening, the Buddha spent the whole of the rainy season at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavī</span> and preached the doctrine to 84,000 listeners (Bv­a.3). The King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavī</span> was known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span> and the inhabitants as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavakā</span>. The town later became famous as the residence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> and of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthaka Āḷavaka</span>. The Therī, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Selā</span> was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavī</span> and was therefore known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavikā</span> (Thīg­a.62-3). There was evidently a large community of monks at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavī</span>, some of whom seem to have chiefly occupied themselves with building <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāras</span> for themselves (See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavakā</span>).

Once, while at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, the Buddha saw a poor farmer of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavī</span>, ready for conversion and decided to go and preach in that town. The farmer’s ox had strayed away, and he looked for it for quite a long while before finding it; he knew that the Buddha was in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavī</span> and decided that he still had time to visit the Buddha, and he set off without taking any food. Meanwhile at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavī</span> the Buddha and his monks had been served with a meal by the people, but the Buddha waited until the farmer came before returning thanks. On the farmer’s arrival the Buddha ordered that some food should be given him, and when the man was comforted and his mind was ready the Buddha preached a sermon, at the end of which the man became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span> (Dhp­a.iii.262-3).

On another occasion the Buddha came all the way from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavī</span> for the sake of a weaver’s daughter (for the story see Dhp­a.iii.170f).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavī</span> has been identified by Cunningham and Hoernle with Newal or Nawal in the Urao district in the United Provinces, and by Nandalal Dey, with Aviwa, twenty-seven miles north-east of Etwah, Law: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Geography of Early Buddhism</span>, p.24).

Mrs. Rhys Davids states that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavī</span> was on the bank of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span> (Brethre<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t18">n</span>, 408), probably basing her view on the declaration of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span> (p.32) that he would throw the Buddha “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pāragaṅgāya</span>” (over to the other side of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span>) unless his questions were answered. I believe that here “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pāragaṅgāya</span>” is merely a rhetorical expression and has no geographical significance.
