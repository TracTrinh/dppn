# Asitābhujātaka

## English source

Asitābhujātaka (Ja 234). The Bodhisatta was once a holy ascetic living in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span>. At that time the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, growing jealous of his son Prince <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span>, banished both him and his wife, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asitābhū</span>. They went to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span> and lived in a hut of leaves. One day the prince, becoming enamoured of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candakinnarī</span>, followed her, forsaking his wife (the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kinnarī</span>’s name was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candā</span>, see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candā</span> (9). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asitābhū</span> went to the Bodhisatta and, having developed various superhuman powers, returned to her hut. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span>, having failed in his quest, returned to the hut where he found his wife poised in mid­air uttering songs of joy over her newfound freedom. When she left, he lived in solitude till, at his father’s death, he succeeded to the throne.

The story was told in reference to a young girl, the daughter of a servitor of the two chief disciples. She was married, but finding her husband neglectful of her, visited the two Chief Disciples. Under their instruction she attained the First Fruit of the Path and embraced the religious life, ultimately becoming an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>.

She was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asitābhū</span> in the previous birth (Ja.ii.229ff).

The story is referred to in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vibhaṅga</span> Commentary (p.470f) in connection with a King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> who, having gone into the forest with his queen to eat roast flesh, fell in love with a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kinnarī</span> and deserted his wife. When he returned to his queen he found her flying through the air away from him, having developed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span> powers. A tree-sprite then uttered a stanza, citing the example of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asitābhū</span>.
