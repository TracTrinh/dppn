# Aggi Bhagavā

## English source

Aggi Bhagavā. A deity (probably identical with the Vedic <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Agni</span>), worship of whom brought, as reward, birth in the *Brahma*-world. On the day a son is born, a fire (*jātaggi*) is kindled; when the son comes of age and wishes to renounce household life, this fire is taken to the forest and homage is paid to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggi Bhagavā</span> (Ja.i.285).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Naṅguṭṭhajātaka</span> (Ja 144, Ja.i.494-5) the Bodhisatta, having received an ox as a gift, wishes to offer the flesh to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggi Bhagavā</span>, but thinking that the deity will not relish a saltless meal, he goes away in search of salt. He returns to find that the ox has been eaten by hunters, only the tail, one leg and the skin being left. “If you, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggi Bhagavā</span>, have not the power to look after your own, how can you guard me?” So saying, he quenches the fire with water and becomes an anchorite. In the verses of this context <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggi</span> is addressed as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaveda</span>.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Santhavajātaka</span> (Ja 162, Ja.ii.43-5), too, the Bodhisatta is a votary of the deity. Once, when he makes an offering of milk mixed with ghee the flames blaze forth and burn his hut, and thereupon he loses faith. In this story <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggi Bhagavā</span> seems to be identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span>. See also KS.i.209, n.4.

In the exegesis to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhuridattajātaka</span> (Ja 543, Ja.vi.202), the deity is spoken of as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggideva</span>, and mention is made of an enquiry made of learned Brahmins by a king, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mujalinda</span>, as to the way to heaven. In answer he is told that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggideva</span> is the *Brāhmaṇadevatā par excellence*, and that he should be offered fresh ghee. See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaveda</span>.
