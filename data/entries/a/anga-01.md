# Aṅga 01

## English source

Aṅga 01. (see also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgā</span>). One of the stock list of the sixteen Powers or Great Countries (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janapadā</span>), mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Piṭakas</span>, e.g., AN.i.213; iv.252, 256, 260.

The countries mentioned are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṁsā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuru</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Macchā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sūrasena</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhāra</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kamboja</span>. Other similar lists occur elsewhere, e.g. DN.ii.200 (where ten countries are mentioned); see also Mvu.i.34 and Mvu.i.198; and Lalitavistara.24(22).

It was to the east of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, from which it was separated by the River <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Campā</span>, and had as its capital city <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Campā</span>, near the modern Bhagalpur (Cunningham, pp. 546-7). Other cities mentioned are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span> (DN­a.i.279; Dhp­a.i.384) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assapura</span> (MN.i.271).

The country is generally referred to by the name of its people, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgā</span>, though occasionally (e.g., Dhp­a.i.384) the name <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgaraṭṭha</span> is used. In the Buddha’s time it was subject to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> (Thag­a.i.548), whose king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> was, we are told, held in esteem also by the people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span> (MN­a.i.394), and the people of the two countries evidently used to pay frequent visits to each other (Ja.ii.211). We never hear of its having regained its former independence, but traditions of war between the two countries are mentioned (e.g., Ja.iv.454; Ja.v.316; Ja.vi.271).

In the Buddha’s time the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgarājā</span> was just a wealthy nobleman, and he is mentioned merely as having granted a pension to a Brahmin (MN.ii.163). The people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> are generally mentioned together, so we may gather that by the Buddha’s time they had become one people. They provide <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā Kassapa</span> with offerings for his great sacrifice (Vin.i.27). It was their custom to offer an annual sacrifice to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span> in the hope of gaining reward a hundred thousand fold. On one occasion <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> appears in person and goes with them to the Buddha so that they may not waste their energies in futile sacrifices (SN­a.i.269-70).

Several discourses were preached in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span> country, among them being the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇadaṇḍasutta</span> and the two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assapurasutta</span>s (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā</span> – and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa</span>). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Govindasutta</span> seems to indicate that once, in the past, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhataraṭṭha</span> was king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span>. But this, perhaps, refers to another country (Dial.ii.270 n.; see also The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Rāmāyaṇa</span> i.8, 9, 17, 25).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa Koḷivisa</span>, before he entered the Saṅgha, was a squire (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t18">paddhagu</span>) of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span>. Thag.v.632.
