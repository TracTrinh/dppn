# Aparagoyāna

## English source

Aparagoyāna. One of the four great continents into which the earth is divided. It is to the west of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span> and is seven thousand <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojanas</span> in extent. It is surrounded by five hundred islands (Snp-a.ii.443). According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (i.227; v.59), each <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cakkavāla</span> (world-system) has an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparagoyāna</span>. It is inhabited by men (Khp­a.123), but they have no houses and sleep on the ground (Thag­a.ii.187-8). In the centre of the continent is a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kadamba</span> tree, whose trunk is fifteen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojanas</span> in girth and whose trunk and arms are fifty <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojanas</span> in length.

This tree stands for a whole <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappa</span> (Dhs­a.298; AN­a.i.264; Vm.206). When the sun rises in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>, it is the middle watch of the night in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparagoyāna</span>; sunset in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparagoyāna</span> is midnight in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>, and sunrise is noon in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>, sunset in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pubbavideha</span> and midnight in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> (DN­a.iii.868). A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>-king first conquers <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pubbavideha</span> in the east and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> in the south, and then sets out to win <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparagoyāna</span> in the west and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> in the north (Mbv.73-4; Bv­a.113). Thus King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mandhātā</span>, having conquered <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>, journeys on with his retinue to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparagoyāna</span> and conquers it straight away (Divy.215).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇaka</span>, in his play with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya</span>, staked a jewel, by gazing into which the continent of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparagoyāna</span> could be seen. Ja.vi.278; so also in the necklace mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Hārapradāna Jātaka</span>. (Mvu.ii.68).

In this context the name given is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Goyāniya</span>. So also in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Vastu</span>: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparagodānika</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparagodāniya</span> (ii.159, 378, etc.). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Dulva</span> it is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparagaudani</span> (Rockhill, 84).

Some of the inhabitants came with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mandhātā</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparagoyāna</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> and settled down there. The country they colonised was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparanta</span>. DN­a.ii.482; MN­a.i.484.
