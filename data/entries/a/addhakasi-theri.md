# Aḍḍhakāsī Therī

## English source

Aḍḍhakāsī Therī. In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>’s time she had been a nun well established in the precepts. But she reviled an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> Therī by calling her a prostitute, and for this she was born in purgatory. In the present age she was the daughter of a rich and distinguished citizen of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> but, because of her former evil speech, became a prostitute in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. Having heard the Buddha preach, she entered the Saṅgha of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">bhikkhunīs</span>. Wishing to obtain the higher ordination from the Buddha, she set out for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, but was waylaid and stopped by libertines. So she sent a man to ask the Buddha’s advice and he permitted her to be ordained by a messenger (Thīg.vv.25-6; Thīg­a.30ff.; Vin.ii.277; Ap. ii. 610-11). Her case established a precedent (Vin-a.i.242). Later she attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship.

It has been suggested (VT.iii.360, n.3; and VT.ii.195-6, n.3) that her name “half <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kāsī</span>” might mean that she charged five hundred pieces from her patrons. For, according to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kāsī</span> means one thousand, and anything worth one thousand is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kāsiya</span>.

Another explanation is, however, given by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapāla</span> (Thīg­a.32). The revenue which accrued to the king for one day from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsī</span> was a thousand. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aḍḍhakāsī</span>’s patrons had to give a like sum to spend a night with her. This is referred to in one of the verses attributed to her in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Therīgāthā</span> (v.25). For this reason she was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsī</span>. But later, many men, not being able to afford a thousand, would pay half the amount and spend the day with her. As a result she became known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aḍḍhakāsī</span>.
