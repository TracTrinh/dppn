# Ariyālaṅkāra

## English source

Ariyālaṅkāra. There were four Theras of this name in Burma, all famous for their Pāli scholarship. The first (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariyālaṅkāra</span> of Ava) excelled in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dhātupaccayavibhāga</span>, i.e. was an accomplished grammarian (Sās.p.106-12). His pupil, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariyālaṅkāra</span> the younger, is credited with exegetical works on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atthasālinī</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkhepavaṇṇanā</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhammatthavibhāvanī</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vibhaṅga</span>. He also wrote a Pāli <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ṭīkā</span> called the Sāraṭṭhavikāsinī on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāyanabheda</span>, and he made in Burmese what amounted to a revised edition of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāyana</span>’s grammar (Sās.p.110-11; Bode, 52-3).

The two others do not seem to have written any works which have been preserved.
