# Abhidhammapiṭaka

## English source

Abhidhammapiṭaka. The third division of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Piṭakas</span>. It consists of seven books: the (1) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammasaṅgaṇī</span>, (2) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vibhaṅga</span>, (3) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhātukathā</span>, (4) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puggalapaññati</span>, (5) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kathāvatthu</span>, (6) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamaka</span> and (7) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭṭhāna</span>, all designated by the name of -<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">pakaraṇa</span>. Only in the Chronicles and the Commentaries is the word used as the title of a third <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Piṭaka</span> (See the discussion of this in DN­a.i.15, 18f). In the Canon itself (e.g., Vin.i.64; iii.144; iv.344) the word means “special Dhamma,” i.e. the Dhamma pure and simple (without admixture of literary treatment or personalities, etc.), and is sometimes coupled with the word <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">abhivinaya</span> (e.g., DN.iii.267; MN.i.272).

It has been suggested (New Pāli Dict. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">s.v.</span>) that, as the word <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">abhidhamma</span> standing alone is not found either in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta</span>, or the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttara</span>, and only once or twice in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhima</span>, it probably came into use only towards the end of the period in which the four great <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nikāyas</span> grew up (See Dial.iii.199 on a possible origin of the Adhidhamma).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Saṅghikas</span> refused to include the Adhidhamma in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Piṭakas</span> at all, as they did not regard it as the word of the Buddha. (Dpv.v.32-8).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Dīghabhāṇakas</span> the Abhidhammapiṭaka also included the whole of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khuddakanikāya</span> except the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cariyāpiṭaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhavaṁsa</span> (DN­a.i.15).

According to another division, the five <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nikāyas</span> are not divisions of the Dhamma but of the whole Canon, and in the fifth are included both the Vinaya and the Adhidhamma (DN­a.i.28).

There is a legend recorded by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> that the Adhidhamma was first preached by the Buddha in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> at the foot of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāricchataka</span> tree, when he was seated on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s throne, during his visit to his mother in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>. Later it was taught by him to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anotatta</span> Lake, whither <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> had gone to minister to the Buddha during the latter’s visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> (Vibh­a. p.1; AN­a.i.71, etc.).

The legend further relates that after the Awakening the Buddha spent the fourth week in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratanaghara</span>, revolving in his mind the intricate doctrines of the Adhidhamma in all their details (Ja.i.78).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Vagga</span> version of the Councils (Chaps. xi. and xii; but see DN­a.i.15 <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t18">contra</span>) the Abhidhammapiṭaka was not rehearsed at either Council.

The fact that the Adhidhamma is not mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span> and that only Dhamma and Vinaya are usually referred to, only proves that at one time the Adhidhamma did not form a separate <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Piṭaka</span>. As a matter of fact, it is not held even by the commentators to be the word of the Buddha in the same sense as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span>. One section of it, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kathāvatthu</span> (but see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kathāvatthu</span>), was taught only at the Third Council.

As far as we know, the seven books of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span> are peculiar to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theravādins</span>, though there is evidence that other schools, chiefly the Vaibhāsikas (Sarvāstivādins) and the Sautrāntikas, held the Adhidhamma books sacred. See Tāranātha: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Geschichte des Buddhismus</span> (56) 156 (296).

As far as the contents of the Adhidhamma are concerned, they do not form a systematic philosophy, but are a special treatment of the Dhamma as found in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttapiṭaka</span>. Most of the matter is psychological and logical; the fundamental doctrines mentioned or discussed are those already propounded in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span> and, therefore, taken for granted. For a discussion of the contents see article on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span> in ERE.

Apart from the Commentaries on the seven books, an exegetical work on the whole <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Piṭaka</span>, called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mūlaṭīkā</span>, was written by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> Vanaratanatissa of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vanavāsī</span> school in Ceylon.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ṭīkā</span> was evidently based on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>’s Commentaries, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> occasionally dissents from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>. The work was written at the request of an Elder, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhamitta</span>, and was revised by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulatthipura</span>.

An <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anuṭīkā</span> was written by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Dhammapāla</span>. Gv.60, 69. For details see PLC., pp. 210-12. The Gv.(72) also mentions <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhammagaṇḍhi</span>, probably a glossary.
