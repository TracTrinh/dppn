# Ambaṭṭha 01

## English source

Ambaṭṭha 01. A Brahmin youth of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭha</span> clan who lived with his teacher, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pokkharasādi</span>, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkaṭṭhā</span>. He was learned in the three Vedas and the correlated branches of knowledge, including the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Lokāyata</span>, as recorded in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭhasutta</span> (DN.i.87ff). Once, at the request of his teacher, he visited the Buddha in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Icchānaṅkala</span> wood and seems to have opened his conversation by reviling the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> and calling them menials. It appears that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭha</span> had once gone on some business of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pokkharasādi</span>’s to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, to the Mote Hall of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span>, and had been insulted there (DN.i.91).

Asked by the Buddha to what family he belonged, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭha</span> replied that he came of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kaṇhāyanagotta</span>; thereupon the Buddha traced the family back to its ancestor, who had been the offspring of a slave girl of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Okkāka</span>, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Disā</span>. The child had been able to talk as soon as he was born and, because of this devilish trait, had been called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇha</span> (devil), hence the family name. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇha</span> later became a mighty seer and married <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maddarūpī</span>, daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Okkāka</span> (DN.i.96-7).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭha</span> makes no remonstrance against this genealogy and, under pressure, accepts it as true. This gives the Buddha an opportunity of preaching on the futility of feeling vanity regarding one’s caste and on the worth of morality and conduct.

At the end of the discourse the Buddha walked up and down outside his chamber so that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭha</span> might see on his body the thirty-two signs of a great man. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭha</span> goes back to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pokkharasādi</span> and reports the whole interview. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pokkharasādi</span> is greatly incensed, abuses <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭha</span> and kicks him. Later <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pokkharasādi</span> goes himself to the Buddha and invites him for a meal. At the end of the meal the Buddha instructs him in his Dhamma and is accepted as the Teacher both of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pokkharasādi</span> himself and of his followers and dependants at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkaṭṭhā</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pokkharasādi</span> himself becomes a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span> (DN­a.i.278).

We are not told that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭha</span> became a follower of the Buddha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (DN­a.i.274) that the Buddha knew that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭha</span> would not profit by his discourse in his present life (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iminā attabhāvena magga-pātubhāvo natthi</span>), and that therefore a sermon with the idea of converting him would only have meant spending unnecessary time. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭha</span> himself only visited the Buddha on account of his interest in physiognomy. According to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> the idea of the Buddha in preaching the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭhasutta</span> at such length was that it might be repeated to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pokkharasādi</span>.

It is conjectured that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭha</span>, who is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāvinda</span>, one of the counsellors of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vedeha</span>, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ummaggajātaka</span> (Ja.vi.478), probably refers to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭha</span> of this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span>.
