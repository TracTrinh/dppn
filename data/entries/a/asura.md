# Asura

## English source

Asura, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asurakāya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span>. In Pāḷi Literature the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asuras</span> are classed among the inferior deities together with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Supaṇṇas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhabbas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> (DN­a.i.51), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷas</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> (Mil.117).

Rebirth as an Asura is considered as one of the four unhappy rebirths or evil states (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">apāyā</span>), the others being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">niraya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">tiracchānayoni</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pettivisaya</span> (e.g., It.93; Ja.vi.595; Ja.v.186; Pv.iv.11).

The fight between the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asuras</span> is mentioned even in the oldest books of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Tipiṭaka</span> and is described in identical words in several passages (e.g., DN.ii.285; SN.i.222; iv.201ff; v.447; MN.i.253; AN.iv.432; also SN.i.216ff).

A chief or king of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asuras</span> is often referred to as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asurinda</span>, several <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asuras</span> being credited with the role of leader, most commonly, however, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> (e.g., SN.i.222; iv.201ff; Ja.i.205) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhu</span> (AN.ii.17, 53; iii.243). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> was also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asurinda</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asurādhipa</span>; see, e.g., Ja.i.66 (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asurindena Paviṭṭha Devanagaraṁ viya</span>) and Ja.v.245, where we are told that from the time he conquered the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asuras</span> he was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asurādhipa</span>.

Besides these we find <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pahārāda</span> (AN.iv.197, 200) (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">v.l.</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Bhadda</span>), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambara</span> (SN.i.227), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verocana</span> (SN.i.225; probably another name for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhu</span>, see DN­a.ii.689), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bali</span> (DN.ii.259), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sucitti</span> (DN.ii.269) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Namucī</span> (DN.ii.269).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asuras</span> are spoken of as dwelling in the ocean after having been conquered by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajirahattha</span> (Indra, elsewhere, (Ja.v.139) called Asurappamaddana) and are called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsava</span>’s brethren, of wondrous powers and of great glory. They were present at the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Samayasutta</span> (see DN­a.ii.689). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says that they were all descendants of an Asura maiden named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujātā</span>. This cannot be the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujātā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span>’s daughter, whom <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> married (Ja.i.205-6). See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dānavā</span>.

There were evidently several classes of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asuras</span>, and two are mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Piṭakas</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷakañjakas</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Dānaveghasas</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Dānaveghasas</span> carried bows in their hands. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷakañjakas</span> were of fearsome shape (DN.ii.259), and were considered the lowest among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asuras</span> (DN.iii.7; see also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷaṅkajaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span>).

Once the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asuras</span> dwelt in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> together with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magha Māṇavaka</span> was born as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, he did not relish the idea of sharing a kingdom with others, and having made the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asuras</span> drunken, he had them hurled by their feet on to the steeps of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span>. There they tumbled into what came to be known as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asurabhavana</span>, on the lowest level of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span>, equal in extent to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>. Here grew the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittapātalī</span> tree, and when it blossomed the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asuras</span> knew they were no longer in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span>-world.

Wishing to regain their kingdom, they climbed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span>, “like ants going up a pillar.” When the alarm was given, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> went out to give battle to them in the ocean, but being worsted in the fight, he fled in his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vejayantaratha</span>. Fearing that his chariot might hurt the young <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷas</span>, he had it turned back. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asuras</span>, thinking that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> had obtained reinforcements, turned and fled right into the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asurabhavana</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> went back to his city and in that moment of victory, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vejayantapāsāda</span> sprang up from the ground. To prevent the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asuras</span> from coming back again, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> set up as guard in five places <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kumbhaṇḍas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> and the Four Great Kings. Everywhere were images of Indra bearing the thunderbolt in his hand. (Ja.i.202-4; Dhp­a.i.272-80; the same story, differing slightly in details, is found in Snp-a.484-5). There it is said that when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> was born among them, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asuras</span> received him with great cordiality; see also the various incidents of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asura</span> war mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya I</span>. 216ff.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asuras</span> are sometimes called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pubbadevā</span> (Snp-a.484) and their kingdom is 10,000 leagues in extent. Snp-a.485; elsewhere, in the same page, it is given as 100,000 leagues.

In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>’s time, the bygone lustre of the word <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asura</span> (as equivalent to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ahura</span>) seems to have faded. His explanation (SN­a.i.260) of the name is interesting. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> was born with his followers in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asura</span>-world (which later became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>) the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asuras</span> prepared a drink called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gaṇḍapāna</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> warned his companions not to drink it, but the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asuras</span> became drunk and were thrown down <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span>. Halfway down they regained consciousness and made a vow never to drink intoxicants (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t18">surā</span>) again; hence their name <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asura</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttara Commentary</span> (ii.526) defines <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asura</span> as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t18">bībhaccha</span>, awful, vile. They had a drum called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷambara</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), made of a crab’s claw. They left it behind in their flight from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, and since then <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> has the use of it (Ja.ii.344).

A story is told by the Buddha (SN.2, v.446) of a man who once saw a whole army with its four divisions enter a lotus stalk and the man thought he was mad. But the Buddha says that it was an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asura</span> army in flight. Here the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asuras</span> would seem to be fairies or nature spirits.
