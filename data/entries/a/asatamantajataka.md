# Asātamantajātaka

## English source

Asātamantajātaka (Ja 61). The Bodhisatta was once a famous teacher in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasīlā</span>. A young Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> came to study under him and, after completing his course, went back home. His mother, however, was anxious that he should renounce the world and tend <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggibhagavā</span> in the forest. She accordingly sent him back to the Teacher that he might learn the “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asātamanta</span>” (Dolour Text). The Teacher had a mother aged 120 years, on whom he himself waited. When the youth came back to learn the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asātamanta</span>, he was asked to look after the old woman. She, falling in love with him, hatched a plot to kill her son.

The Bodhisatta, having been told of this plot, made a wooden figure and placed it in his bed. The mother, thinking to kill her son, struck it with an axe, and discovering that she had been betrayed, fell down dead. The youth, having thus learnt the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asātamanta</span>, returned to his parents and became a hermit. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāpilānī</span> was the mother in the story, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> the father and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> the pupil.

This story, together with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ummadantījātaka</span> (Ja 527), was related to a passion-tossed monk to warn him of the evil nature of women. Ja.i.285-9.
