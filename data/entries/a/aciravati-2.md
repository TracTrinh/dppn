# Aciravatī

## English source

Aciravatī. A river, the modern Rāpti in Oudh; one of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañca Mahā Nadī</span> (Vin.ii.237), the five great rivers flowing from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span> eastwards (*pācīnaninnā*) (SN.v.39, etc.) into the sea. During the hot season it ran dry, leaving a bed of sand (AN.iv.101). It flowed through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>, and at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> an *udumbara* grove grew on its banks; it could be seen from the terrace of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>’s palace (Vin.iv.111-12; Snp-a.i.19). To the south of it was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manasākaṭa</span>, and on its southern bank was a mango grove where the Buddha sometimes resided (DN.i.235-6). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tevijjasutta</span> was preached here, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aciravatī</span> is used in a simile to prove the futility of sacrifices and prayers: it is of no use standing on one bank of the river and calling to the other bank to come over.

In the river were many bathing places, in some of which courtesans bathed naked; the Bhikkhunīs did likewise until a rule was passed prohibiting it (Vin.i.293; iv.278). The *Chabbaggiya* nuns, however, continued to do so even afterwards (Vin.iv.259f).

The river was crossed in rafts (Vin.iii.63); it sometimes became so full (DN.i.244-5; MN.iii.117; Ja.iv.167) that disastrous floods occurred, in one of which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Viḍūḍabha</span> and his army were swept into the sea (Dhp­a.i.360).

In sheltered spots monks and Brahmins used to bathe (Vin.iv.161), and once <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> himself bathed there (AN­a.i.315). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sattarasavaggiya</span> monks frequented the river for water-sports (Vin.iv.111-12).

Once the Buddha was told that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chavaggiya</span> monks were in the habit of seizing the cows that crossed the river (Vin.i.191).

The elder <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvali</span> stopped on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aciravatī</span> while on his way to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span> with five hundred monks (AN­a.i.139). In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> the river flowed round <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> and, at the eastern fort, flowed into a wide and deep lake on which separate bathing places were made for the king, the people, the Buddha and the Saṅgha respectively (MN­a.i.371).

The people on the banks were in the habit of casting nets for fish (Ud­a.366). Near the river was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Daṇḍakappa</span>, a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosalan</span> village, and while staying there <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> bathed in the river with many other monks (AN.iii.402).

Two occasions are mentioned on which monks hit geese flying over the river in the eye (Ja.i.418 and ii.366. See also Dhp­a.iv.5 and 8f). It was here that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭācārā</span>’s child was drowned (Dhp­a.ii.264).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapila</span> was born here as a golden fish as a result of his evil deeds (Dhp­a.iv.41; see also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapila S</span> ?). In the *Avadānaśataka* (Dhp­a.i.63; also ii.60) the name is given as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajiravatī</span>, and according to I Tsing (p.156) means the river of the *Aji* (dragon).
