# Aparantaka

## English source

Aparantaka (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparanta</span>). One of the countries to which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> sent missionaries after the Third Council. The leader of the mission was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yonaka Dhammarakkhita</span> (Mhv.xii.5; Dpv.viii.7). He preached to the people the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggikkhandhopamāsutta</span> and 37,000 people embraced the new faith, a thousand men and even more women entering the Saṅgha (Mhv.xii.34-6; Vin-a.i.67).

The country comprises the territory of Northern Gujarat, Kāthiāwar, Kachch and Sindh. Fleet JRAS. 1910, p.427; Bhaṇḍarkar in his Early History of Dekkan puts it in North Konkan (p.23); see also Burgess: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arch. Reports</span> ii.131.

According to Xuanzang, the country seems to comprise Sindh, Western Rājaputāna, Cutch, Gujarat and a portion of the adjoining coast on the lower bank of the Narmadā. Cunningham <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ancient Geography of India</span>, notes, p.690; and Dhamma: Early G<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">eography</span> 56ff.

Probably Buddhism was known in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparanta</span> during the time of the Buddha himself. Dutt: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Early Hist. of Bsm.</span> p.190; Divy., pp.45ff; but the reference is to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunāparanta</span>.

It is said that when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mandhātā</span> brought all the four continents under his sway people from the three other continents came over to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> and lived there. When the king died they found themselves unable to get back, and begged his minister to allow them to start settlements in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> itself. He agreed, and the settlement of those who had come from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparagoyāna</span> was for that reason called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparanta</span> (DN­a.ii.482; MN­a.i.184) (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparantaka</span>).
