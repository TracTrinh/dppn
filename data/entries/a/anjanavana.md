# Añjanavana

## English source

Añjanavana (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Añcanavana</span>). A garden at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span>. In it was a Deer-park where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> used to stay. On one such occasion <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakudha</span> came to see him (SN.i.54), and also the *Paribbājaka* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇḍaliya</span> (SN.v.73) who lived near by. Here were preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketasutta</span>, (SN.v.219) the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketajātaka</span> (Ja 68, Ja.i.308; Dhp­a.iii.317ff.; Snp-a.531) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jarāsutta</span>.

When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> was staying there a nun of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jaṭila</span> persuasion visited him and questioned him on the use of *samādhi* (AN.iv.427-8).

The Thera <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambugāmiyaputta</span> (Thag­a.i.86; Snp-a.531) dwelt there while yet a novice. Once the Buddha was staying at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Añjanavana</span> with a large company of monks and some of the monks slept on the sandbanks of the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhū</span> near by. During the night floods rose and the Thera <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gavampati</span> controlled the water by his mystic powers (Ibid., i.104; Thag.v.38).

The elder <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhūta</span> (Thag­a.i.494) stayed in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Añjanavana</span> while visiting his relatives in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span>, and the Thera <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Añjanavaniya</span> spent the rainy season there on a couch (Thag­a.i.127). There <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujātā</span> met the Buddha, and having listened to his discourse became an *Arahant* (Thīg.vv.145-50).

In ancient times the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> used to hunt in this garden, thus it was that the deer <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandiya</span> met him (Ja.iii.270f).

The garden was so-called because it was thickly covered with *añjana*-creepers that bore collyrium-coloured flowers. Others say that *añjana* is the name of a spreading tree (Thag­a.i.128; SN­a.iii.195).
