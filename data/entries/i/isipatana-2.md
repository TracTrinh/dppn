# Isipatana

## English source

Isipatana. An open space near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, the site of the famous <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migadāya</span> or Deer Park. It was eighteen leagues from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span>, and when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> gave up his austere penances his friends, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcavaggiya</span> monks, left him and went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> (Ja.i.68). After his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> the Buddha, leaving <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span>, joined them in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>, and it was there that he preached his first sermon, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammacakkappavattanasutta</span>, on the full-moon day of *Āsāḷha*. Vin.i.10f.; on this occasion 80 *koṭis* of *Brahmas* and innumerable gods attained the comprehension of the Truth (Mil.30); (130 *koṭis* says Mil.350). The *Lalitavistara* (528) gives details of the stages of this journey. The Buddha, having no money with which to pay the ferryman, crossed the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span> through the air. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> heard of this, he abolished the toll for ascetics.

There, also, the Buddha spent his first rainy season (Bv­a., p.3).

All the Buddhas preach their first sermon at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migadāya</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>; it is one of the four *avijahitaṭṭhānāni* (unchanging spots), the others being the *bodhipallaṅka*, the spot at the gate of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span>, where the Buddha first touches the earth on his return from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, and the site of the bed in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhakuṭi</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> (Bv­a.247; DN­a.ii.424).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> is mentioned by the Buddha as one of the four places of pilgrimage which his devout followers should visit (DN.ii.141).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> was so-called because sages, on their way through the air (from the Himalayas), alight here or start from here on their aerial flight (*isayo ettha nipatanti uppatanti cā ti Isipatanaṁ*).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migadāya</span> was so-called because deer were allowed to roam about there unmolested.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>, having spent seven days in contemplation in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhamādana</span>, bathe in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anotatta</span> Lake and come to the habitations of men through the air, in search of alms. They descend to earth at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> (MN­a.i.387; AN­a.i.347 adds that sages also held the *uposatha* at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>).

Sometimes the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> come to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandamūlakapabbhāra</span> (MN­a.ii.1019; Paṭis-a.437-8).

Several other incidents connected with the Buddha, besides the preaching of the first sermon, are mentioned as having taken place in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>. Here it was that one day at dawn <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span> came to the Buddha and became an *Arahant* (Vin.i.15f). It was at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>, too, that the rule was passed prohibiting the use of sandals made of talipot leaves (Vin.i.189). On another occasion when the Buddha was staying at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>, having gone there from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, he instituted rules forbidding the use of certain kinds of flesh, including human flesh (Vin.i.216ff.; the rule regarding human flesh was necessary because <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppiyā</span> made broth out of her own flesh for a sick monk). Twice, while the Buddha was at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> visited him but had to go away discomfited (SN.i.105f).

Besides the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammacakkappavattanasutta</span> mentioned above, several other *Suttas* were preached by the Buddha while staying at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>, among them

- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Pañcasutta</span> (SN.iii.66f),
- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Rathakārasutta</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Pacetanasutta</span> (AN.i.110f),
- the two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Pāsasutta</span>s (SN.i.105f),
- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Samayasutta</span> (AN.iii.320ff),
- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kaṭuviyasutta</span> (AN.i.279f.),
- a discourse on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Metteyyapañha</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Pārāyaṇa</span> (AN.iii.399f), and
- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Dhammadinnasutta</span> (SN.v.406f), preached to the distinguished layman <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Dhammadinna</span>, who came to see the Buddha.

Some of the most eminent members of the Saṅgha seem to have resided at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> from time to time; among recorded conversations at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> are several between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Koṭṭhita</span> (SN.ii.112f;iii.167f;iv.162f; 384ff), and one between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Koṭṭhita</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittahatthisāriputta</span> (AN.iii.392f).

Mention is made, too, of a discourse in which several monks staying at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> tried to help <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> in his difficulties (SN.iii.132f).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsa</span>, there was a large community of monks at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> in the second century B.C. For, we are told that at the foundation ceremony of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>, twelve thousand monks were present from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> led by the Elder <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammasena</span> (Mhv.xxix.31).

Xuanzang (Beal-Xuan.ii.45ff) found, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>, fifteen hundred monks studying the Hīnayāna. In the enclosure of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghārāma</span> was a *vihāra* about two hundred feet high, strongly built, its roof surmounted by a golden figure of the mango. In the centre of the *vihāra* was a life-size statue of the Buddha turning the wheel of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span>. To the south-west were the remains of a stone *stūpa* built by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>. The *Divyāvadāna* (389-94) mentions <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> as intimating to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upagupta</span> his desire to visit the places connected with the Buddha’s activities, and to erect *Thūpas* there. Thus he visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lumbinī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bodhimūla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migadāya</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinagara</span>; this is confirmed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s lithic records, e.g. Rock Edict, viii.

In front of it was a stone pillar to mark the spot where the Buddha preached his first sermon. Near by was another *stūpa* on the site where the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcavaggiyas</span> spent their time in meditation before the Buddha’s arrival, and another where five hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> entered *Nibbāna*. Close to it was another building where the future <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Metteyya Buddha</span> received assurance of his becoming a Buddha.

Xuanzang quotes the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhamigajātaka</span> (Ja.i.145ff) to account for the origin of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migadāya</span>. According to him the Deer Park was the forest gifted by the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> of the J<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">ātaka</span>, where the deer might wander unmolested.

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udapānajātaka</span> (Ja.ii.354ff) there was a very ancient well near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> which, in the Buddha’s time, was used by the monks living there.

In past ages <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> sometimes retained its own name, E.g., in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phussa Buddha</span> (Bv.xix.18), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammadassī</span> (Bv­a.182) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> (Bv­a.218). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> was born there (ibid., 217).

But more often <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> was known by different names (for these names see under those of the different Buddhas). Thus in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī</span>’s time it was known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemauyyāna</span>. It is the custom for all Buddhas to go through the air to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> to preach their first sermon. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>, however, walked all the way, eighteen leagues, because he knew that by so doing he would meet <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upaka</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ājīvaka</span>, to whom he could be of service (DN­a.ii.471).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> is identified with the modern Sarnath, six miles from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. Cunningham (*Arch. Reports*, i. p. 107) found the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migadāya</span> represented by a fine wood, covering an area of about half a mile, extending from the great tomb of Dhammek on the north to the Chaukundi mound on the south.

## Vietnamese translation

Chưa dịch.

## Notes
