# Isipatana

## English source

Isipatana. An open space near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, the site of the famous <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migadāya</span> or Deer Park. It was eighteen leagues from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span>, and when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> gave up his austere penances his friends, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcavaggiya</span> monks, left him and went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> (Ja.i.68). After his Awakening the Buddha, leaving <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span>, joined them in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>, and it was there that he preached his first sermon, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammacakkappavattanasutta</span>, on the full-moon day of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Āsāḷha</span>. Vin.i.10f.; on this occasion 80 <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">koṭis</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahmas</span> and innumerable gods attained the comprehension of the Truth (Mil.30); (130 <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">koṭis</span> says Mil.350). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Lalitavistara</span> (528) gives details of the stages of this journey. The Buddha, having no money with which to pay the ferryman, crossed the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span> through the air. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> heard of this, he abolished the toll for ascetics.

There, also, the Buddha spent his first rainy season (Bv­a., p.3).

All the Buddhas preach their first sermon at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migadāya</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>; it is one of the four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">avijahitaṭṭhānāni</span> (unchanging spots), the others being the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">bodhipallaṅka</span>, the spot at the gate of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span>, where the Buddha first touches the earth on his return from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, and the site of the bed in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhakuṭi</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> (Bv­a.247; DN­a.ii.424).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> is mentioned by the Buddha as one of the four places of pilgrimage which his devout followers should visit (DN.ii.141).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> was so-called because sages, on their way through the air (from the Himalayas), alight here or start from here on their aerial flight (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">isayo ettha nipatanti uppatanti cā ti Isipatanaṁ</span>).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migadāya</span> was so-called because deer were allowed to roam about there unmolested.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>, having spent seven days in contemplation in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhamādana</span>, bathe in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anotatta</span> Lake and come to the habitations of men through the air, in search of alms. They descend to earth at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> (MN­a.i.387; AN­a.i.347 adds that sages also held the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t18">uposatha</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>).

Sometimes the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> come to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandamūlakapabbhāra</span> (MN­a.ii.1019; Paṭis-a.437-8).

Several other incidents connected with the Buddha, besides the preaching of the first sermon, are mentioned as having taken place in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>. Here it was that one day at dawn <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span> came to the Buddha and became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> (Vin.i.15f). It was at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>, too, that the rule was passed prohibiting the use of sandals made of talipot leaves (Vin.i.189). On another occasion when the Buddha was staying at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>, having gone there from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, he instituted rules forbidding the use of certain kinds of flesh, including human flesh (Vin.i.216ff.; the rule regarding human flesh was necessary because <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppiyā</span> made broth out of her own flesh for a sick monk). Twice, while the Buddha was at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> visited him but had to go away discomfited (SN.i.105f).
