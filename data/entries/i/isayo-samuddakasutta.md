# Isayo Samuddakāsutta

## English source

Isayo Samuddakāsutta (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambarasutta</span>). Long ago, many virtuous seers lived in huts on the seashore. At that time there was a war between the gods and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span>. The seers considered the gods righteous but feared harm from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span>. They went, therefore, to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambara</span>, lord of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span>, and asked him for a pledge of safety; he refused, saying that the seers were followers of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>. The seers thereupon cursed him to suffer everlasting terror. It is said that that same night <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambara</span> woke up thrice, seized with fright (SN.i.227-8).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (SN­a.i.266) adds that as a result of this curse, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambara</span>’s mind became deranged and he came to be called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> (crazy-nerve).
