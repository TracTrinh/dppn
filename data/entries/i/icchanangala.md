# Icchānaṅgala

## English source

Icchānaṅgala, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Icchānaṅkala</span>. A Brahmin village in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> country. It was while staying in the woodland thicket (*vanasaṇḍa*) there that the Buddha preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭhasutta</span> (DN.i.87). From this *Sutta*, the village would seem to have been near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pokkharasādi</span>’s domain of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkaṭṭhā</span>. It was the residence of “*Mahā Sāla*” Brahmins. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span> (p.115) (which spells the name as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Icchānaṅkala</span>) mentions several eminent Brahmins who lived there, among them <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṅkī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tārukkha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pokkharasāti</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jāṇussoṇī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Todeyya</span>.

There were also two learned youths, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāseṭṭha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhāradvāja</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Icchānaṅkala</span>, who, finding it impossible to bring their discussion to a conclusion, sought the Buddha, then staying in the village. Their interview with the Buddha is recorded in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāseṭṭhasutta</span> (Ibid., 115ff.; MN ii.146ff). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (Snp-a.ii.462) says that learned Brahmins of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>, deeply versed in the Vedas, were in the habit of meeting together from time to time (once in six months, MN­a.ii.796) at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Icchānaṅgala</span> in order to recite the Vedas and discuss their interpretation. These Brahmins met at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkaṭṭhā</span>, under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pokkharasāti</span>, when they wished to cleanse their caste (*jātisodhanatthaṁ*), and at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Icchānaṅkala</span> in order to revise their Vedic hymns (*mante sodhetukāmā*), MN­a.ii.796.

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (v.325), the Buddha once stayed for three months in the jungle thicket at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Icchānaṅgala</span>, in almost complete solitude, visited only by a single monk who brought him his food. But from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (iii.30f.; cf. iii.341 and iv.340ff), it would appear that the Buddha was not left to enjoy the solitude which he desired, for we are told that the residents of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Icchānaṅgala</span>, having heard of the Buddha’s visit, came to him in large numbers and created a disturbance by their shouts. The Buddha had to send <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgita</span>, who was then his personal attendant, to curb the enthusiasm of his admirers.
