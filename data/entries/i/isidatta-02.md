# Isidatta 02

## English source

Isidatta 02. An equerry or chamberlain (*thapati*) of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span> is always mentioned with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Purāṇa</span>. Their duty was to look after the ladies of the king’s harem when these went riding the elephant into the park. This often brought them into close contact with the ladies, and they confessed to the Buddha that it was difficult not to have evil thoughts regarding them.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Purāṇa</span> were once at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sādhuka</span> on some business (their own property, according to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>, SN­a.i.215). They heard that the Buddha was having a robe made before starting on his rounds and they waited for an opportunity to talk to him. When the opportunity came they followed the Buddha and told him how glad they always were when he was near them and how sad when he was away on tour. The Buddha preaches to them the glory of the homeless life and urges them to put forth energy. He speaks very appreciatively of their loyalty to him and to his religion and congratulates them on the possession of virtuous qualities, such as sharing all their goods with holy men, a rare quality (SN.v.348-52; Netti. 134f).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta Commentary</span> (i.215), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span> was a *Sakadāgāmī* and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Purāṇa</span> a *Sotāpanna*.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammacetiyasutta</span> (MN.ii.123f), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> tells the Buddha how impressed he is by the reverence <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Purāṇa</span> show for the Buddha and his teachings. “They are my carriage-builders,” says the king, “and they depend on me for their livelihood and all their honours, yet these men do not serve me as whole-heartedly as they do the Lord.”

Once the king spent the night in a cramped little house. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Purāṇa</span>, who were with him, having spent the best part of the night in discussing the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span>, lay down to rest with their heads in the direction in which they thought the Buddha to be, and their feet towards the king!

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span> was the uncle of the woman-disciple <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migasālā</span>, whose father was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Purāṇa</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Purāṇa</span> is described as a *brahmacāri*, but not <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span>, yet, after death, they were both born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migasālā</span> asks <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> how it was that people of different characters could have the same rebirth. AN.iii.348f.; v.138f., 143f. From MN­a.ii.756, it would appear as if <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span> was the *brahmacāri*. The word cannot here mean “celibate,” for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Purāṇa</span> must have had a wife because <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migasālā</span> calls him her father (*pitā*).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span> is mentioned by the Buddha among those who had the six qualities that brought realisation of immortality – unwavering loyalty to the Buddha, the Dhamma and the Saṅgha, Ariyan virtue, wisdom and liberation (AN.iii.451).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāgatavaṁsa</span> (v.58) says that when the future <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Metteyya Buddha</span> leaves the household life, eighty-four thousand others, led by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Purāṇa</span>, will accompany him.
