# Indapatta

## English source

Indapatta (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indapattana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indapaṭṭha</span>). A town in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuru</span> country. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurudhammajātaka</span> (Ja 276, Ja.ii.365f; also Ja.iii.400; iv.361; v.457; vi.255; Cp.i.3, v.1), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya Koravya</span>, is mentioned as its king and as the owner of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Añjanavasabha</span>, the elephant of wondrous power.

The town was seven leagues in extent (Ja.v.57; 484) and there was a road that ran straight from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indapatta</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bārāṇasī</span> (Ja.v.59).

In times past, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indapatta</span> was considered one of the three chief cities of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>, the others being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarapañcāla</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kekaka</span> (Ja.ii.213, 214).

According to a verse found at the end of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhavaṁsa</span> (Bv.xxviii.11), the Buddha’s razor and needle were enshrined at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indapatta</span>.

The modern Delhi stands on the site of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indapatta</span>.
