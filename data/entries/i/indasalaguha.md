# Indasālaguhā

## English source

Indasālaguhā. A cave on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vediya</span> mountain, to the north of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambasaṇḍā</span>, which was a Brahmin village, east of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. Once, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> was staying there, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> visited him and asked him the questions recorded in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakkapañhasutta</span> (DN.ii.263).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (DN­a.iii.697) that the cave lay between two overhanging rocks, with a large <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sāla</span>-tree at the entrance. The village community had added walls with doors and windows and had ornamented it with polished plaster scroll-work and garlands and had presented it to the Buddha.

In Faxian’s time (Giles, 48f), it was still inhabited and he describes it as being one <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojana</span> north-east of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālandā</span>. Xuanzang, however, found it deserted. He calls it <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Indrakaśailagūhā</span> (Beal-Xuan.ii.180-1).

Both pilgrims noticed marks on the rock; according to Faxian they were the answers to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s questions written by the Buddha with his finger, while Xuanzang says that both questions and answers were written on the stone.

The cave is identified with one about two miles to the south-west of the modern village of Giriyek. (CAGI.539ff; Stein, Ind.An. 1901, p.54).

It is said that on the occasion of the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakkapañhasutta</span>, eight hundred million <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> realised the Truth (Mil.349).

See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Somanassa</span> (3).
