# Isayo Araññakāsutta

## English source

Isayo Araññakāsutta (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isayo Gandhasutta</span>). Long ago, many seers of virtuous conduct lived in leaf huts in a wild forest. According to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>, they lived in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span> (SN­a.i.265); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> were father- and son-in-law; sometimes they quarrelled, sometimes, as here, they were friends.

One day, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> visited them. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> entered the hermitage by the principal gate, keeping his shoes on and his sword by his side, thereby insulting the seers and committing sin. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, on the other hand, went in by the usual entrance, doffing his shoes and sword; with his canopy folded up, he stood to leeward of the seers, rendering them homage with clasped hands. The seers, addressing <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, asked him if he did not feel disgust at their smell, inasmuch as they were humans and he a god. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> answered that the scent of virtuous men is lovely, like unto a wreath of varied blossoms (SN.i.226).
