# Indriyajātaka

## English source

Indriyajātaka (Ja 423). Once an ascetic named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span>, younger brother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷadevala</span>, became a disciple of the Bodhisatta <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotipāla</span> (also called in the story <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhaṅga</span>), and lived in the mountainous country of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Arañjara</span>. Near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span>’s hermitage was a river, on the banks of which courtesans used to sit, tempting men. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span> saw one of these courtesans, and becoming enamoured of her, forsook his meditations and pined away for lack of food. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷadevala</span>, being aware of this, tried to wean him from his desires. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span>, however, refused to be comforted, even when his colleagues, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sālissara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meṇḍissara</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pabbatissara</span> admonished him. In the end <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhaṅga</span> himself was summoned and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span>, having listened to the words of his Master, was persuaded to give up his passion.

The story was told in reference to a backsliding monk. He went about for alms with his teachers and instructors but, being their junior, he received very little attention. Dissatisfied with his food and treatment, he sought his wife of former days. She provided him with every comfort and gradually tempted him with the desire to become a householder again. When the monk’s fellow-celibates discovered his wish, they took him to the Buddha who preached to him this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span>, showing that in a past life, too, he had been sorely tempted by the same woman. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span> was identified with the backsliding monk and the courtesan with the wife of his lay-days (Ja.iii.461-9).

The Buddha is stated on this occasion to have preached also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇḍinajātaka</span> (Ja.i.153ff), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rādhajātaka</span> (Ja.i.495ff), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ruhakajātaka</span> (Ja.ii.113ff), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇaverajātaka</span> (Ja.iii.58ff), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asaṅkajātaka</span> (Ja.iii.248ff) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Alambusājātaka</span> (Ja.v.152ff).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indriyajātaka</span> is also referred to in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāmavilāpajātaka</span> (Ja.ii.443ff), but the connection between the two stories is not clear; perhaps the reference is to another story of the same name.

See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhaṅgajātaka</span>.
