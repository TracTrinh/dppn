# Itivuttaka

## English source

Itivuttaka. The fourth book of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khuddakanikāya</span>, containing 110 *Suttas*, each of which begins with the words: *vuttaṁ h’etaṁ Bhagavatā*, this was spoken by the Bhagavā.

According to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapāla</span> (Iti-a.24ff), the *Suttas* were preached from time to time by the Buddha to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khujjuttarā</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>. She then repeated them to the five hundred women of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>’s palace, chief of whom was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>. In order to emphasise to her audience the fact that she was reporting the Buddha’s words and not her own, she prefaced each *Sutta* with the phrase quoted above. There was no need to describe any special circumstances in which the *Suttas* were preached, because they were familiar to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khujjuttarā</span>’s audience.

At the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> Council, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> repeated the *Suttas* to the Assembly and they were gathered into this collection.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Itivuttaka</span> is also the name given to one of the nine divisions (*aṅga*) into which the Buddha’s preaching is divided and it is defined as follows: *vuttaṁ h’etaṁ Bhagavatā ti ādinayappavattā dasuttarasataṁ suttantā* *Itivuttakam ti veditabbaṁ* (DN­a.i.24).

In the scholiast of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kummāsapiṇḍajātaka</span> (Ja 415, Ja.iii.409; l.21), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Itivuttaka</span> is mentioned in the plural (*Itivuttakesu*) and a *Sutta* is quoted from it, extolling the virtues of generosity. Perhaps, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Itivuttaka</span> was compiled as a result of a critical study of the authentic teachings of the Buddha, considered in a certain light and made for a specific purpose.
