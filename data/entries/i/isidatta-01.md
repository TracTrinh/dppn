# Isidatta 01

## English source

Isidatta 01. A Thera. He was the son of a caravan guide at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaḍḍhagāma</span> (*v.l.* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷugāma</span>) in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>. By correspondence he became the unseen friend of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittagahapati</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Macchikāsaṇḍa</span>. The latter once sent him a letter regarding the excellences of the Buddha, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span>, being pleased with the account given of the Buddha’s religion, entered the Saṅgha under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span> and in due course became an *Arahant*. Later, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span>’s leave, he visited the Buddha in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhimadesa</span> and was warmly received by him (Thag­a.i.238). A verse uttered by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span>, in response to the Buddha’s enquiry regarding his welfare, is recorded in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> (v.120).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span> had been a householder in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span> and once, having seen the Buddha walking along the street and being pleased with his demeanour, he gave him an *āmoda*-fruit (Thag­a. *loc.cit.*). He is, probably, identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amodaphaliya</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (ii.447).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (iv.283-8, also AN­a.i.210), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span> was once staying with a number of senior monks at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Macchikāsaṇḍa</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambāṭaka</span> grove. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittagahapati</span> invited the monks to a meal. On this occasion <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta</span> asked a question regarding the Buddha’s teaching on the diversity of the elements. The chief Elder, being unable to answer, remained silent. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span>, though the most junior of the whole company, obtained the chief Elder’s permission, and answered the question to the satisfaction of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta</span> likewise asked questions regarding various views, such as the infinity of the world, etc. At the end of the discourse, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta</span> discovered, by accident, that the Elder who had preached to him was none other than his unseen friend, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span>. Delighted with the discovery, he invited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span> to spend his time at a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Macchikāsaṇḍa</span>, promising to provide him with all requisites. But that same day <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span> left <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Macchikāsaṇḍa</span> and never returned, because, says <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (AN­a.i.210), he did not wish to stay after having been recognised.
