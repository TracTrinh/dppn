# Isidinna

## English source

Isidinna. A Thera. He was the son of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunāparanta</span>. He witnessed the miracle of the Buddha’s acceptance of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candanamālā</span> (probably the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candanamālaka</span>), and, having heard the Buddha preach, he became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>. While still living the life of a householder, a compassionate spirit urged him to give it up. He entered the Saṅgha and soon after became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> (Thag­a.i.312-3).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> (vv.187-8) contains two verses which he uttered in confessing <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">aññā</span>. They were the same as were spoken to him by his friendly spirit. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span>, he was a householder and did homage to the Buddha’s Bodhi-tree with a fan made of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sumana</span>-flowers (Thag­a.312f). He is probably identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanavājaniya</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (Ap.ii.415).
