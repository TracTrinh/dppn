# Issarasamaṇārāma

## English source

Issarasamaṇārāma (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Issarasamaṇavihāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Issarasamaṇaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Issaraṇimmāna</span>). One of the monasteries at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>. It was built by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span> on the spot where the prince <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariṭṭha</span> dwelt with his five hundred followers after having received their ordination from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> (Mhv.xx.14; xix.66). The building of this monastery was the seventh of the great tasks performed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span> (Mhv.xx.20).

One of the eight saplings from the Bodhi-tree at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> was planted at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Issarasamaṇārāma</span> (Mhv.xix.61; Mbv.162).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candamukhasiva</span> built a tank near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇikāragāmaka</span> and gave it for the use of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> (Mhv.xxxv.47), while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vasabha</span> built in the monastery an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">uposatha</span>-hall (Mhv.xxxv.87) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vohārakatissa</span> constructed a wall round it (Mhv.xxxvi.36). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa I</span>. restored the buildings and enlarged the grounds. He also bought villages which he presented to the monastery for its maintenance. He had two daughters, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bodhī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uppalavaṇṇā</span>, and he gave their names and his own to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span>. When the king wished to hand over the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theravāda</span> monks they refused to accept it, fearing the reproach of the people that it was the work of a parricide. Then the king dedicated it to the image of the Buddha and the monks accepted it saying that it belonged to their Master (Cv.xxxix.10-14; see also below).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsaṭīkā</span> (pp. 407 and 652), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> was also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapagiri</span>, probably after its restoration by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa I</span>., mentioned above. See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapagiri</span>. See also Cv.Trs.i.43, n.7, and Ep. Zeyl. i.31ff., where the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> is called “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Isurameṇu-Bo-Upulvan-Kasubgiri</span>” in an inscription of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda IV</span>.

It had originally been called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Issarasamaṇa</span> because of its association with the five hundred noblemen (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t18">issaradārakā</span>) who joined the Saṅgha with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariṭṭha</span> (Mhv­ṭ.416). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ṭīkā</span> adds (607) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāliya</span>, son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span>, enlarged the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> out of the tribute brought to him by the men of his tributary villages to the south of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>. He used to observe the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t18">uposatha</span> on fast days at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> and spend the day in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahindaguhā</span> there.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samantapāsādikā</span> (i.100) the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Issaraṇimmāna</span>.
