# Isigili

## English source

Isigili. One of the five mountains round <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> and one of the beauty-spots of the city (DN.ii.116). There was, on one side of it, a black stone called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷasilā</span>. This was a favourite haunt of the Buddha and the members of the Saṅgha. See e.g., Vin.ii.ii.76, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba Mallaputta</span> is asked by monks to provide for them accommodation there; see also Vin.iii.41.

It was also the scene of the suicide of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godhika</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vakkali</span> (SN.i.121; iii.121f) and of the murder of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> by the brigands (Ja.v.125f; Dhp­a.iii.65).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Dukkhakkhandasutta</span> it is said that a large number of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span> lived at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷasilā</span>, never sitting down, undergoing paroxysms of acute pain and agony, following the teachings of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta</span>. The Buddha questioned them as to their practises and preached to them the above-mentioned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span>, which he afterwards repeated to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> (MN.i.91ff).

Once when the Buddha was dwelling at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷasilā</span>, he sang the praises of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, giving <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> a chance, if he so desired, of asking him to live on for a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappa</span>; but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> did not take his opportunity (DN.iii.116).

The books refer to several other visits of the Buddha to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isigilapassa</span>. During one of these visits he heard <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅgīsa</span>’s high eulogy of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> (SN.i.194; Thag.1249ff).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isigilisutta</span> (MN.iii.68-71) the Buddha is represented as saying that while the other mountains round <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vebhāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍava</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepulla</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span> – had changed their old names, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isigili</span> retained its former name and designation.

Five hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> once resided in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isigili</span> for a long time; they could be seen entering the mountain, but once entered, there was no more sign of them. Men, observing this, said that the mountain swallowed up the sages and so it came by its name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isigili</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Isī gilatī ti Isigili</span>).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> adds (MN­a.ii.889) that when the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> returned from their begging rounds, the rock would open like a folding door to admit them. Within the rock they had made for themselves cloisters, dwelling-houses, etc.
