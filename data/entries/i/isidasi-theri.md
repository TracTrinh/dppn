# Isidāsī Therī

## English source

Isidāsī Therī. She was the daughter of a good and wealthy merchant of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>. Having come of age, she was given in marriage to the son of a merchant in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span>.

For one month she lived with him as a devoted wife; then because of her past *kamma*, her husband became estranged from her, and turned her out of the house. She was married again with the same result, and a third time to a friar. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidāsī</span>’s father persuaded him to give up the pilgrim’s life; he dwelt with his wife only for a fortnight and refused to stay with her any more. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidāsī</span> then met the Therī <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jinadattā</span>, whom she entertained to a meal at her house. Under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jinadattā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidāsī</span> joined the Saṅgha and became an *Arahant*.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Therīgāthā</span> (vv.400-47), which contains forty-seven verses ascribed to her, describes not only her present life, but also her past lives. She had been a worker in gold in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Erakaccha</span> and had committed adultery in that life. As a result she was born in hell for a long time, and, in subsequent births became an ape, a goat, an ox, a hermaphrodite slave and a carter’s daughter. In this last birth she was sold to a merchant in payment of her father’s debts. When she was sixteen, the merchant’s son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Giridāsa</span>, fell in love with her and married her. He had already one wife, and the new one caused dissension between her and her husband. Therefore it was that in this life she was hated by her husbands. This account of her sojourn in *Saṁsāra* was related by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidāsī</span> in response to a request by one of her fellow-nuns, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bodhī</span> (Thīg­a.260ff).

Mrs. Rhys Davids thinks (*Sisters*, Introd. pp.xxii f) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidāsī</span>’s verses in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Therīgāthā</span> suggest late literary craft and bear the impress of late literary creation. The scene is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span>, and not any of the usual towns mentioned in the Canon, and the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidāsī</span>’s sponsor – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jinadattā</span> – is, she says, significant. Perhaps there are traces here of Jainistic influence.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīpavaṁsa</span> (xviii.9) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidāsī</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidāsikā</span>) is mentioned in a list of eminent *Therīs* who were leaders of the Saṅgha of *bhikkhunīs*.
