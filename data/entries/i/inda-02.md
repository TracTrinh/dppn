# Inda 02

## English source

Inda 02. The Pāli equivalent of the Vedic Indra He is referred to only very seldom in the *Nikāyas*. In one such passage (DN.i.244-5) he is mentioned with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Varuṇa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isāna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahiddhi</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yāma</span>, as a god whom Brahmins invoke and pray to, for union with *Brahma* after death. In another place, he is described as being seated in the company of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span> and other gods in the Assembly Hall, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhamma</span>. Two of his companions, having listened to the admonition of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gopaka</span>, became disciples of the Buddha and, as a result, far surpassed in glory <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Inda</span> and his other companion <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>. In the same context, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsava</span>, ruler of the gods, identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, is addressed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gopaka</span> as “Indra.” (Ibid., ii.274; in MN.i.140; Ja.v.411 and vi.568, he is mentioned with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span>; in Ja.iv.568, 571 is a list in which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Inda</span> appears with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yama</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span>).

By the time of the compilation of the *Nikāyas*, the hold of the Vedic god Indra on the mind of the people seems to have become greatly weakened and Indra has been merged in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, although, strictly speaking, Indra and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> are quite different conceptions (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>).

In the later literature, however, particularly in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka Commentary</span>, Indra’s name occurs frequently, but always as identified or identifiable with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>. In one place at least (Ja.v.115) the scholiast says, “*Sakko ti Indo*.”

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ayakūṭajātaka</span> (Ja 347, Ja.iii.146), for example, Indra is called king of the gods (*devārājā*) in one verse, and in the next he is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maghavā</span>, husband of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujā</span>, and described as “*devānaṁ indo*.” Indra is most revered of the gods (Sn.vs.316). He is free from old age and death, and is, therefore, the happiest type of king (Sn.vs.515), a condition that could be attained by sacrifice (Sn.vs.517). Alone he conquered the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span> (Ja.iv.347; he is therefore called *Asurinda* and *Asurādhipa*; see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asura</span>). He is spoken of, as the lord of victors (*jayataṁ pati*, Ja.v.322), and he is the embodiment of the greatest valour (Mhv.xxx.10).

Sometimes he visits the earth in disguise (Ja.v.33). He is also represented as punishing people guilty of heinous crimes; with his thunderbolt he smites them (Dhp­a.iv.105).

The scene of his pleasures is in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandana</span> pleasance (Ja.v.158), and his is the ideal enjoyment of pleasure, surrounded by friends (Ja.v.506; Sn.vs.679) and by adoring wives (Ja.vi.240). The gods of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> are called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indapurohitā</span>, because, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Inda</span> as their chief, they seek to promote the welfare of gods and men (Ja.vi.127; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> gods are also described as being *Sa-Indakā*, ibid., 568).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Inda</span> is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tidivapuravara</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suravaratara</span> (DN.iii.176). His capital is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Masakkasāra</span> (Ja.vi.271; but see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amarāvatī</span>).

In the sacrifice the *palāsayaṭṭhi* (Butea shoot), used by the sacrificing priest, is described as Indra’s right hand (Ja.vi.212).

Indra’s *gotta*, or clan, is the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosiya</span> (Ja.vi.501); he is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vatrabhū</span> in reference to his victory over <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vatra</span> (Skt. Vṛtra, Ja.v.153), and mention is made of his thunderbolt, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indavajira</span> (Ja.i.354); thus he is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajirahattha</span> (DN.ii.259; DN­a.ii.689). The sound of Indra’s thunderbolt striking its victim, surpasses all other sounds by its intensity, its volume and its fearfulness (Ud­a.67); no obstruction can stop the progress of Indra’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajira</span> and it never misses its mark; it is *avirajjhanaka* (Vibh­a.333).

After his victory over the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span>, images of him were made (*Indapaṭimā*) and placed round <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittakūṭa</span> to frighten the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span> away, in case they attempted to retrieve their lost honour (Ja.vi.125-6; see also Ja.i.203-4; Dhp­a.i.280).

To be born into the company of Indra (*Indasahavyatā*) is considered very fortunate (e.g., Ja.v.411).

A species of coral red insect (*kimi*), noticeable after rain, are called *Indagopakā*. The reason for this name is not clear (see *Brethren*, 18n., and PTS PED).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> Commentary (p.75, n.12) seems to give <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Viḍojā</span> as an epithet of Indra; but this is probably a wrong reading, the correct one being, as in some MSS., “*Visam-ucchājapaṁ japanti*.”

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Inda</span> was a special protector of cows, and when men began to kill these creatures he visited his wrath on them. Sn.vs.310.
