# Kiṁsukopamajātaka

## English source

Kiṁsukopamajātaka (Ja 248). Four monks came to the Buddha and asked him for a topic of meditation. He gave them various topics and they, having retired to various places, all became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span>: one by understanding the six-fold sphere, the second the five <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">khandhas</span>, the third the four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">mahābhūtas</span>, and the fourth the eighteen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dhātu</span>. They returned and related to the Buddha each the particular excellence attained by him, and one of them asked the Buddha how all these methods could lead to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nibbāna</span>. The Buddha related a story of the past where four sons of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, having asked their charioteer to show them a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kiṁsuka</span>-tree, are shown the tree at four different times: when the buds were just sprouting, when the leaves were green, at the time of blossoming, and at the time of fruit-bearing.

When asked to describe the tree, the first likened it to a burnt stump, the second to a banyan tree, the third to a piece of meat, and the fourth to an acacia. The matter was referred to the king, who solved the difficulty.

The king was the Bodhisatta. Ja.ii.265f.
