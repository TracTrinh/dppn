# Kosala

## English source

Kosala, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosalā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosalans</span>. A country inhabited by the Kosalā, to the north-west of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> and next to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsī</span>. It is mentioned second in the list of sixteen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janapadas</span> (e.g., AN.i.213; iv.252, etc.). In the Buddha’s time it was a powerful kingdom ruled over by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, who was succeeded by his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Viḍūḍabha</span>. By this time <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsī</span> was under the subjection of Kosala, for we find that when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, married Kosala<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">devī</span>, daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā</span> Kosala and sister of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, a village in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsī</span> was given as part of the dowry (Ja.ii.237; iv.342f). Various Jātakas indicate that the struggle between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span> and Kosala had been very prolonged (See, e.g., Ja.ii.21f; iii.115f; 211f; v.316, 425). Sometimes the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span> king would attack Kosala, capture the king and rule over the country. At others the Kosala king would invade <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span> and annex it to his own territory. Several Kosala kings who succeeded in doing this, are mentioned by name – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabbasena</span> (Ja.iii.13), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghāvu</span> (Ja.iii.211f), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅka</span> (Ja.iii.168) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṁsa</span>; the last being given the special title of “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Bārāṇasiggāha</span>,” (Ja.ii.403; v.112) probably in recognition of the fact that he completed the conquest of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span>. Other kings of Kosala who came in conflict with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> in one way or another are mentioned – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghīti</span> (Ja.iii.211f; Vin.i.342f), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallika</span> (Ja.ii.3), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chatta</span> (Ja.iii.116). Sometimes the kings of the two countries entered into matrimonial alliances (e.g., Ja.iii.407). With the capture of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span> the power of Kosala increased rapidly, until a struggle between this country and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> became inevitable. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>’s marriage was probably a political alliance, but it only served to postpone the evil day. Quite soon after his death there were many fierce fights between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, his successor, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, these fights bringing varying fortunes to the combatants. Once <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> was captured alive, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> spared his life and gave him his daughter, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajirā</span>, in marriage and for a time all went well. Later, however, after his conquest of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> seems to have succeeded in establishing his sway in Kosala (see Vincent Smith, op.cit., 32f). In the sixth century B.C. the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> territory of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> was subject to Kosala. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span> (vs.405) speaks of the Buddha’s birthplace as belonging to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosalans</span>; see also AN.i.276, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> is mentioned as being in Kosala. Elsewhere (MN.ii.124) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> is reported as saying, “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Bhagavā pi Kosalako, ahaṁ pi Kosalako;</span> (The Bhagavā is from Kosala, and I am from Kosala).”

At the time of the Buddha <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> was the capital of Kosala. Next in importance was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span>, which, in ancient days, had sometimes been the capital (Ja.iii.270; Mvu.i.348). There was also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ayojjhā</span>, on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarayu</span>, which, judging from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Rāmāyaṇa</span>, must once have been the chief city; but in the sixth century B.C. it was quite unimportant.

The river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarayu</span> divided Kosala into two parts, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Uttara Kosala</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Dakkhiṇa Kosala</span> (Law: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Geography of Early Buddhism</span>, p.6).

Other Kosala rivers mentioned in the books are the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aciravatī</span> (DN.i.235) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sundarikā</span> (SN.i.167; Snp. p.97; but see MN.i.39, where the river is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhukā</span>).
