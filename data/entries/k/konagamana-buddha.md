# Koṇāgamana Buddha

## English source

Koṇāgamana Buddha (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Konāgamana Buddha</span>). The twenty-third in the list of the twenty-four Buddhas and the second Buddha to be born in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddakappa</span>. He was born in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhagavatī</span> Park in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sobhavatī</span>, the capital of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sobha</span>, his father being the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yaññadatta</span> and his mother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span>. He lived in the household for three thousand years, in three palaces, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Santusita</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Santuṭṭha</span>; his chief wife was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rucigattā</span> and their son was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Satthavāha</span>. Koṇāgamana left the world on an elephant and practised austerities only for six months, at the end of which time he was given milk-rice by the daughter of the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggisoma</span> and grass for his seat by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yavapālaka</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tinduka</span>. His Bodhi was an Udumbara tree. His first sermon was preached in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migadāya</span> near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudassananagara</span>, at the foot of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Sāla</span> tree. He held only one assembly of his disciples, who numbered thirty thousand. His body was thirty cubits in height. He died in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pabbatārāma</span> at the age of thirty thousand. His relics were scattered. His chief disciples were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhīyya</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span> among monks, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samuddā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span> among nuns, his constant attendant being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sotthiya</span>. His chief patrons were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Somadeva</span> among laymen, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmā</span> among laywomen. The Bodhisatta was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Khattiya</span> named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pabbata</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span>. He held an almsgiving, heard the Buddha preach and joined the Saṅgha. (DN.i.7; Bv.xxiv; Bv­a.213ff; Ja.i.42f; according to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> his body was twenty cubits high; Vin-a.i.190).

The banker <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span> built for the Buddha a Saṅghārāma half a league in extent (Ja.i.94).

On the day of the Buddha’s birth a shower of gold fell all over <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>, hence he was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇakāgamana</span>, Koṇāgamana being a corrupt form of that word (Bv­a.213-14)

According to the Ceylon Chronicles (Dpv.ii.67; xv.25, 44, 48; xvii.9, 17, 73; Mhv.xv.91-124), Koṇāgamana visited their Island (then known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Varadīpa</span>), with thirty thousand disciples, accepted the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahānoma</span> garden at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaḍḍhamāna</span>, given by King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samiddha</span>, and preached the doctrine. At the conclusion of his sermon, thirty thousand people realised the Truth. At the Buddha’s wish, the nun <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇṭakānandā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t18">v.l.</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kanakadattā</span>) brought to Ceylon a branch of the Bodhi-tree. The Buddha also preached at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratanamāla</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudassanamāla</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgamālaka</span> and gave his girdle for the people’s worship. He left <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sumba</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇṭakānandā</span> to look after the new converts.

In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇāgamana</span>’s time Mount <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepulla</span> was known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅkaka</span>, and the people living on the mountain were called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohitassā</span>, their term of life being thirty thousand years (SN.ii.191). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇāgamana</span> held the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t18">uposatha</span> once a year (Dhp­a.ii.236).

In the Northern books (e.g., Divy.333; Mvu.i.114; Mvu.ii.265f, 300, 302, 304, 430; iii.240-7, 330) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇāgamana</span> is called Kaṇakamuni, Koṇākamuni, and Kaṇakaparvata. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thūpa</span>, erected on the spot where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇāgamana</span> was born, is thought to have existed down to the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>, who rebuilt it to double its original size and worshipped it in his twentieth year (Hultszch: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Inscriptions of Asoka</span>, p.165). Xuanzang (Beal-Xuan, op.cit., ii.19) says he saw <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thūpas</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇāgamana</span>’s birthplace and also at the spot where he met his father after the Awakening. Faxian (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Travels</span>, p.36) saw <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thūpas</span> at the latter place and also at the place of the Buddha’s death.
