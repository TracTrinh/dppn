# Kaṇḍarijātaka

## English source

Kaṇḍarijātaka (Ja 341). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇḍari</span>, a king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, was very handsome; each day he received one thousand boxes of perfume for his use, and his food was cooked with scented wood. His wife, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kiṇṇarā</span>, was very beautiful; his chaplain was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcālacaṇḍa</span>. One day, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kiṇṇarā</span>, on looking out, saw a loathsome cripple in the shade of a *jambu*-tree near her window, and conceived a passion for the man. Thereafter she would wait for the king to fall asleep and would then, nightly, visit the cripple, taking him dainty foods and having her pleasure with him. One day the king, returning from a procession, saw the misshapen creature, and asked the chaplain if such a man could ever win the love of a woman. The cripple, hearing the question, proudly boasted of his intimacy with the queen. At the chaplain’s suggestion the king watched the queen’s movements that same night, and saw the cripple abuse her and strike her for being late in coming. The blow fell on her ear breaking off her ear ornament, which the king picked up.

The next day he ordered the queen to appear before him in all her ornaments, and having proved that he knew of her infidelity, handed her over to the chaplain to be executed. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcālacaṇḍa</span>, pitying the woman, begged that she should be pardoned, because in being unchaste she had but obeyed the instincts common to all women. To prove his contention, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcālacaṇḍa</span> took the king with him and, in disguise, they wandered through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>, testing the virtue of various women, including that of a young bride who was being taken to her husband’s house. Convinced that all women were alike, the king spared <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kiṇṇarā</span>’s life, but drove her out of the palace together with the cripple, and caused the *jambu*-tree to be cut down.

The story was among those related by the bird <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇāla</span> to his friend <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇamukha</span>, testifying to the unfaithfulness of women. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇāla</span> is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcālacaṇḍa</span>. Ja.v.437-40; Ja.iii.132.
