# Kandagalakajātaka

## English source

Kandagalakajātaka (Ja 210). The Bodhisatta was once a woodpecker named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khadiravaniya</span>, and be had a friend named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kandagalaka</span>. One day <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khadiravaniya</span> took <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kandagalaka</span> with him into the acacia wood (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">khadiravana</span>) and gave him insects from the acacia trees. As <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kandagalaka</span> ate them, pride rose in his heart and, feeling he could get food without his friend’s assistance, he told him so. In spite of the warning of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khadiravaniya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kandagalaka</span> pecked at an acacia trunk, broke his beak, and fell down dead.

The story was related in connection with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>’s attempts to imitate the Buddha, these attempts ending in his own ruin. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kandagalaka</span> is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>. Ja.ii.162-4.
