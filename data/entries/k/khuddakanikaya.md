# Khuddakanikāya

## English source

Khuddakanikāya. Sometimes called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khuddakagantha</span> DN-a.i.16.. The fifth and last division of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttapiṭaka</span>.

It consists of fifteen independent treatises, some belonging to the earlier period, while others may be ascribed to the later stratum of the Pāli Canon. This *Nikāya* is composed for the most part in verse, and contains all the most important collections of Pāli poetry.

The fifteen books are:

(1) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khuddakapāṭha</span>

(2) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada</span>

(3) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span>

(4) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Itivuttaka</span>

(5) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span>

(6) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vimānavatthu</span>

(7) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Petavatthu</span>

(8) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span>

(9) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Therīgāthā</span>

(10) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span>

(11) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Niddesa</span>s (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Niddesa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Niddesa</span>)

(12) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭisambhidāmagga</span>

(13) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>

(14) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhavaṁsa</span>

(15) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cariyāpiṭaka</span> (Vin-a.i.18; DN­a.i.17).

According to another classification the whole of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vinayapiṭaka</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhammapiṭaka</span> and all the teachings of the Buddha, not included in the remaining four *Nikāyas*, are regarded as forming the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khuddakanikāya</span> (DN­a.i.23; Vin-a.i.27).

The *Dīghabhāṇakas* refused to accept the authenticity of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khuddakapāṭha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cariyāpiṭaka</span>, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>, and included the other books as part of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhammapiṭaka</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhimabhāṇakas</span> did not accept the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khuddakapāṭha</span> but acknowledged the rest, and included them in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttapiṭaka</span> (DN­a.i.15).

According to Burmese tradition four other later books are added to this list: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttasaṅgaha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Peṭakopadesa</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nettippakaraṇa</span>. Bode, 4.
