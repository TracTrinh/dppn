# Kāḷudāyī Thera

## English source

Kāḷudāyī Thera. Son of one of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span>’s ministers at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>; he was born on the same day as the Bodhisatta and grew up as his playfellow. After <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> left the world, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> made <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷudāyī</span> one of his most trusted counsellors. When the king heard of his son’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> he sent several of his ministers with large retinues to bring the Buddha to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, but they all became *Arahants* as soon as they heard the Buddha’s preaching and then forgot their mission. In the end the king sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷudāyī</span>, on the understanding that he should first be allowed to join the Saṅgha (according to Mhv.iii.233, he was accompanied by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> in this mission). He went to the Buddha and, having listened to him, himself became an *Arahant*. When the rains fell, covering the earth with the glory of leaves and flowers, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷudāyī</span> felt that it was time for the Buddha to visit his kinsmen, and gave him their invitation, singing the season’s beauties in a series of verses. The Buddha took sixty days in covering the sixty leagues from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, and each day <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷudāyī</span> went by air to the king’s palace to tell him of the progress made in the journey and to bring back to the Buddha from the palace a bowl full of excellent food. By the time the Buddha reached his home his kinsmen were already full of faith in him. Because <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷudāyī</span> accomplished this feat, he was declared pre-eminent among those who gladdened the clans (*kulappasādakānaṁ aggo*) (AN.i.25; Thag.527-36; Ja.i.54, 86f; AN­a.i.107, 117; Thag­a.i.497ff; Ud­a.168; DN­a.ii.425).

It is said that he was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span> because he was born on a day on which the citizens were full of joy (*udaggacittadivase jātattā*); and called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷa</span> because of his slightly dark colour. AN­a.i.167; Thag­a.i.498.

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (ii.500f; see also Ap.i.86f, where another set of verses is attributed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷudāyī</span>), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷudāyī</span> had been the son of a minister of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsavatī</span> during the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>, and having heard the Buddha utter the praises of a monk skilled in converting families, had wished for the same eminence.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (AN.iv.449f) records a conversation between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span> (who, according to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (AN­a.ii.815), is to be identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷudāyī</span>) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span> asks <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> to explain in detail a question which is recorded in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (SN.i.48) as having been asked of the Buddha by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcālacaṇḍa</span> *Devaputta* (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcālasutta</span>).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp-a.iv.143) refers to an assembly at which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷudāyī</span> was present, his body of golden hue, sitting near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, at sunset, with the moon rising in the eastern sky. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> looks at them and declares how the Buddha suffuses them all with his glory.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷudāyī</span> is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhisajātaka</span> (Ja.iv.314).
