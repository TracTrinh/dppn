# Kandarakasutta

## English source

Kandarakasutta. Once, when the Buddha was at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Campā</span>, on the banks of Lake <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaggarā</span>, he was visited by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pessa</span>, the elephant trainer’s son, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kandaraka</span>, the Wanderer. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pessa</span> saluted the Buddha and sat down, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kandaraka</span>, after his salutation, remained standing. Surveying the assembly of monks gathered round the Buddha and observing their great silence, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kandaraka</span> expressed his admiration of the Buddha’s training. The Buddha explained that all Buddhas school their disciples in the four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">satipaṭṭhāna</span>s. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pessa</span> stated that it was far more difficult to train men than animals. The Buddha agreed and enumerated the four kinds of people in the world: those who torment themselves, torment others, torment both themselves and others, and, lastly, those who torment neither, dwelling beyond appetites, in bliss and holiness. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pessa</span> stated that he respected only the fourth class, and having given his reasons went away. The Buddha expressed his regret that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pessa</span> could not wait to hear the differences between these four kinds of people; and at the request of the monks the Buddha proceeded to describe them (MN.i.339ff).
