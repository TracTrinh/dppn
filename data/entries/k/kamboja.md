# Kamboja

## English source

Kamboja (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kambojaka</span>). One of the sixteen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janapadas</span> which, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhāra</span>, belonged, not to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhimadesa</span> but, evidently, to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarāpatha</span> (AN.i.213; iv.252, 256, 260). It is often mentioned as the famous birthplace of horses (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">assānaṁ āyatanaṁ</span>) (e.g., DN­a.i.124; AN­a.i.399; Vsm.332; also Ja.iv.464). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇālajātaka</span> (Ja.v.445) we are told that the Kambojas caught their horses by means of moss (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jalajāta</span>), and the scholiast (Ja.v.446) explains at length how this was done. They sprinkled the moss with honey and left it in the horses’ drinking place; from there, by means of honey sprinkled on the grass, the horses were led to an enclosure.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assalāyanasutta</span> (MN.ii.149) it is stated that in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yona</span> and in Kamboja, and also in the neighbouring countries, there were, in the Buddha’s time, only two classes of people, masters and slaves, and that a master could become a slave or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vice versa</span>. The Commentary (MN­a.ii.784) explains that a Brahmin would go there with his wife for purposes of trade and would die there. His wife would then be compelled to work for her living and her children would become slaves.

The Jātakas (e.g., Ja.vi.208, 210; see also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manu</span>.x.44) would lead us to believe that the people of Kamboja had lost their original customs and had become barbarous. Elsewhere’ Kamboja is mentioned as a place not visited by women of other countries. AN.ii.82; on the reading of this passage, however, see GS.ii.92, n.2. The Commentary (AN­a.ii.523) distinctly supports the reading Kamboja.

The country was evidently on one of the great caravan routes, and there was a road direct from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dvāraka</span> to Kamboja (Pv.p.23).

According to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s Rock Edict, No. XIII. (Shābhāzgarhi Text), Kamboja was among the countries visited by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s missionaries. The country referred to is probably on the banks of the Kabul river (Mookerji: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asoka</span>, 168, n.1).

In later literature (e.g., Cv.lxxvi.21, 55) Kamboja is the name given to Western Siam.
