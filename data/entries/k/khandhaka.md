# Khandhakā

## English source

Khandhakā, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khandhaka</span>. The name given to a portion of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vinayapiṭaka</span>. This is generally further divided into two parts, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vagga</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Vagga</span>. It contains an attempt to give a coherent picture of the whole legal life of the Saṅgha, with detailed and connected accounts of the admission thereto, the ceremony of the *uposatha*, the annually recurring observances connected with the rainy season, etc. An account is given, in the case of each regulation, of the occasion on which it was formulated by the Buddha. The separate chapters are arranged in chronological order, and are intended to present a connected account of ecclesiastical history from the time of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> of the Buddha down to that of the Second Council, convened one hundred years after the death of the Buddha (see Oldenberg, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vinayapiṭaka I</span>., Introd., xxii.f.; Law, *Pāli Literature*, i.14f).

In many ways the Khandhakā, resemble the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttavibhaṅga</span> of the Vinaya, but while in the case of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vibhaṅga</span> the stories were added later to an original basis of regulations, the *Pātimokkha*, in that of the Khandhakā the regulations and the stories were contemporary.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khandhakas</span> consist of eighty *bhāṇavāras* (DN­a.i.13), and are divided into twenty-two chapters, ten in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vagga</span> and twelve in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Vagga</span>. Each chapter is called a *khandhaka*. Thus, the first chapter is the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Khandaka</span>; the second, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uposathakhandhaka</span>, and so on.
