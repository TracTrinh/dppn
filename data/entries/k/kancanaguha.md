# Kañcanaguhā

## English source

Kañcanaguhā. A cave in the region of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span>, according to one description (Ja.ii.176; but see Ja.v.357, where it is said to be near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittakūṭa</span>) in the face of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittakūṭa</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pabbata</span>. This cave was the abode of the Bodhisatta when he was born as a lion, as described in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Virocanajātaka</span> (Ja.i.491f), and again in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sigālajātaka</span> (Ja.ii.6). Near by was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rajatapabbata</span>. This cave was also the dwelling-place of the geese mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kacchapajātaka</span> (Ja.ii.176), and in the cave grew the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abbhantaramba</span> (Ja.ii.396), the property of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span>. In the scholiast to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthipālajātaka</span> (Ja.iv.484), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kañcanagūha</span> is mentioned as the abode of the spider <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Unnābhi</span> and the ninety-six thousand geese who took shelter in it, waiting for the rains to clear. Near the cave was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chaddanta</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">daha</span> and the Buddha, when he was born as the elephant <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chaddanta</span>, made the cave his headquarters (Ja.v.37f). In this context the cave is described as being in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suvaṇṇapabbata</span> (probably another name for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kañcanapabbata</span>) to the west of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chaddanta</span> lake, and is said to be twelve leagues in extent. There lived the elephant king with eight thousand companions. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span> tāpasa once lived for seven days at the entrance to the cave, going to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> for his food (Ja.v.316, 392).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pākahaṁsas</span> of great power also lived in the cave (Ja.v.357, 368), once as many in number as ninety thousand (Ja.v.381).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhābhojanajātaka</span> (Ja.v.392), the cave is stated to have been on the top of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manosilātala</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kañcanagūha</span> is mentioned in literature as the dwelling-place of maned lions (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kesarasīhā</span>) (e.g., Ud­a.71, 105).
