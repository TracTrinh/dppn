# Khānu Koṇḍañña

## English source

Khānu Koṇḍañña. A Thera. He became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> while living in the forest. One day, while sitting on a flat stone, he entered into a jhāna. A pack of thieves, mistaking him for the trunk of a tree, piled their sacks on to his head and body and slept around him. In the morning, discovering their mistake, they begged forgiveness and, having listened to his preaching, became monks. It was this incident which gained for him the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khānu</span> (“stump”). The Buddha praised him on account of his achievement (Dhp­a.ii.254f).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khānu Koṇḍañña</span>’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">samādhi</span> is classified under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vipphārā iddhi</span>, inasmuch as even the uproar, caused by the thieves in piling up their sacks, did not disturb it, e.g., Bv­a.24; Paṭis-a.497.
