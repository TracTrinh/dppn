# Kosambī

## English source

Kosambī. The capital of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vatsas</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṁsas</span> (Ja.iv.28; vi.236). In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> its king was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parantapa</span>, and after him reigned his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>. (MN­a.ii.740f; Dhp­a.i.164f). Kosambī was evidently a city of great importance at the time of the Buddha for we find <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> mentioning it as one of the places suitable for the Buddha’s *Parinibbāna* (DN.ii.146,169). It was also the most important halt for traffic coming to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> from the south and the west (see, e.g., Vin.i.277).

The city was thirty leagues by river from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. (Thus we are told that the fish which swallowed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bakkula</span> travelled thirty leagues through the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamunā</span>, from Kosambī to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, AN­a.i.170; Paṭis-a.491). The usual route from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> to Kosambī was up the river (this was the route taken by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> when he went with five hundred others to inflict the higher punishment on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span>, Vin.ii.290), though there seems to have been a land route passing through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anupiya</span> and Kosambī to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> (see Vin.ii.184f). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span> (vv.1010-13) the whole route is given from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahissati</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, passing through Kosambī, the halting-places mentioned being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gonaddha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vedisa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vanasavhya</span>, Kosambī, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setavyā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhoganagara</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>.

Near Kosambī, by the river, was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>’s park, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udakavana</span>, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍola Bhāradvāja</span> preached to the women of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>’s palace on two different occasions (Vin.ii.290f; Snp­a.ii.514; Ja.iv.375). The Buddha is mentioned as having once stayed in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siṁsapāvana</span> in Kosambī (SN.v.437). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span> lived in a woodland near Kosambī after the holding of the First Council (Pv-a.141).

Already in the Buddha’s time there were four establishments of the Saṅgha in Kosambī – the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkuṭārāma</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghositārāma</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvārika-ambavana</span> (these being given by three of the most eminent citizens of Kosambī, named respectively, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkuṭa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosita</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvārika</span>), and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Badarikārāma</span>. The Buddha visited Kosambī on several occasions, stopping at one or other of these residences, and several discourses delivered during these visits are recorded in the books. (Thomas: *Life and Legend of the Buddha*, 115, n.2, doubts the authenticity of the stories connected with the Buddha’s visits to Kosambī, holding that these stories are of later invention).

The Buddha spent his ninth rainy season at Kosambī, and it was on his way there on this occasion that he made a detour to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kammāsadamma</span> and was offered in marriage <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span>, daughter of the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiya</span>. The circumstances are narrated in connection with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyasutta</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span> took the Buddha’s refusal as an insult to herself, and, after her marriage to King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>, tried in various ways to take revenge on the Buddha, and also on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>’s wife <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>, who had been the Buddha’s follower. (Dhp­a.i.199ff; iii.193ff; iv.1ff; Ud.vii.10).

A great schism once arose among the monks in Kosambī. Some monks charged one of their colleagues with having committed an offence, but he refused to acknowledge the charge and, being himself learned in the Vinaya, argued his case and pleaded that the charge be dismissed. The rules were complicated; on the one hand, the monk had broken a rule and was treated as an offender, but on the other, he should not have been so treated if he could not see that he had done wrong. The monk was eventually excommunicated, and this brought about a great dissension. When the matter was reported to the Buddha, he admonished the partisans of both sides and urged them to give up their differences, but they paid no heed, and even blows were exchanged. The people of Kosambī, becoming angry at the monks’ behaviour, the quarrel grew apace. The Buddha once more counselled concord, relating to the monks the story of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghīti</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>, but his efforts at reconciliation were of no avail, one of the monks actually asking him to leave them to settle their differences without his interference. In disgust the Buddha left Kosambī and, journeying through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bālakaloṇakāragāma</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pācīnavaṁsadaya</span>, retired alone to keep retreat in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārileyyaka</span> forest. In the meantime the monks of both parties repented, partly owing to the pressure exerted by their lay followers in Kosambī, and, coming to the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, they asked his pardon and settled their dispute (Vin.i.337-57; Ja.iii.486ff (cp.iii.211ff); Dhp­a.i.44ff; SN­a.ii.222f; the story of the Buddha going into the forest is given in Ud.iv.5. and in SN.iii.94, but the reason given in these texts is that he found Kosambī uncomfortable owing to the vast number of monks, lay people and heretics. But see Ud­a.248f, and SN­a.ii.222f).

The Commentaries give two reasons for the name Kosambī. The more favoured is that the city was so-called because it was founded in or near the site of the hermitage once occupied by the sage <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusumba</span> (*v.l.* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusumbha</span>) (e.g., Ud­a.248; Snp­a.300; MN­a.i.535. Epic tradition ascribes the foundation of Kosambī to a Ceḍi prince, while the origin of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vatsa</span> people is traced to a king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsī</span>, see PHAI.83, 84). Another explanation is (e.g., MN­a i.539; Paṭis-a.413) that large and stately *margosa*-trees (*Kosambarukkhā*) grew in great numbers in and around the city.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bakkula</span> was the son of a banker in Kosambī (MN­a.ii.929; AN­a.i.170). In the Buddha’s time there lived near the ferry at Kosambī a powerful <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>-king, the reincarnation of a former ship’s captain. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> was converted by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgata</span>, who thereby won great fame (AN­a.i.179; but see Ja.i.360, where the incident is given as happening at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddavatikā</span>). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rujā</span> was born in a banker’s family in Kosambī (Ja.vi.237f). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittapaṇḍita</span> was also born there (Ja.iv.392). A king, by name <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambaka</span> (*q.v.*), once ruled there.

During the time of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjī</span> heresy, when the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjī</span> monks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> wished to excommunicate <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa Kākaṇḍakaputta</span>, he went by air to Kosambī, and from there sent messengers to the orthodox monks in the different centres (Vin.ii.298; Mhv.iv.17).

It was at Kosambī that the Buddha promulgated a rule forbidding the use of intoxicants by monks (Vin.ii.307).

Kosambī is mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (SN.iv.179; but see AN­a.i.170; MN­a.ii.929; Paṭis-a.491, all of which indicate that the city was on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamunā</span>) as being “*Gaṅgāya nadiyā tīre*.” This is either an error, or here the name <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgā</span> refers not to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span> but to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamunā</span>. Kosambī is identified with the two villages of Kosam on the Jumna, about ninety miles west of Allahabad (CAGI.448f; Vincent Smith places it further south, JRAS.1898, 503ff).
