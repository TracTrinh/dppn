# Kusaṇālijātaka

## English source

Kusaṇālijātaka (Ja 121). The Bodhisatta was born as a sprite, in a clump of *kusa*-grass. Hard by was a *mukkhaka*-tree in which lived a Tree-sprite. One day the king’s carpenter, looking for a suitable pillar for the king’s one-pillared palace, reluctantly decided to fell the *mukkhaka*. Learning of the Tree-sprite’s imminent danger, the Bodhisatta assumed the shape of a chameleon and deceived the carpenter in such a way that he saw the *mukkhaka* as all rotten and of no use for his purpose.

The story was told in reference to one of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>’s friends. He was of low rank and poor, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>’s other friends protested against such intimacy. But one day the poor friend saved <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>’s house from being burgled. The Buddha related the story to show how each, according to his strength, could help a friend in need (Ja.i.441ff).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> was the Tree-sprite. The story is often quoted (e.g., Ja.iv.77) to show the value of a good friend.
