# Kukkurajātaka

## English source

Kukkurajātaka 01. (Ja 22). Because his carriage straps, left in the rain, are gnawed by his own dogs, the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> orders all dogs except his own to be killed indiscriminately. The Bodhisatta, who is the leader of the pack of dogs in the cemetery, visits the king, points out to him his iniquity, and reveals the truth by causing an emetic to be administered to the king’s dogs. Having convinced the king, the Bodhisatta teaches him the ten stanzas of Righteousness found in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tesakuṇajātaka</span> (*dhammaṁ cara mahārāja*, etc., Ja.v.123). Great are the benefactions made to dogs thereafter. The Bodhisatta’s teaching lasted for ten thousand years under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkurovāda</span>.

The king is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> (Ja.i.175ff). The occasion for the story is given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddasālajātaka</span>.
