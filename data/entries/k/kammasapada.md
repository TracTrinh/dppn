# Kammāsapāda

## English source

Kammāsapāda. The name given to the *porisāda* (cannibal) in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sutasomajātaka</span> (Ja 537). Before becoming man-eater he was the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, and was later weaned from his evil habit by the Bodhisatta <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sutasoma</span>. He is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgulimāla</span> (Ja.v.pp.503f, 511).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (DN­a.ii.483) explains the name (“Spot-foot”) as being given because of a wound he once received, which healed, leaving a scar like a piece of well-grained timber (*cittadārusadiso*). This refers to the flight of the *porisāda* from his pursuers, when he trod on an acacia stake (*khānu*) which pierced his foot (Ja.v.472).

According to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>, the cannibal in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayaddisajātaka</span> (Ja 513) was also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kammāsa</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kammāsapāda</span>. (DN­a.ii.483. See also Watanabe’s article, “*The Story of Kalmāsapāda and its Evolution in Indian Literature*.” JPTS1909, pp.236ff).

The place where the *porisāda* was tamed was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kammāsadamma</span>.
