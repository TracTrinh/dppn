# Kammāsapāda

## English source

Kammāsapāda. The name given to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">porisāda</span> (cannibal) in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sutasomajātaka</span> (Ja 537). Before becoming man-eater he was the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, and was later weaned from his evil habit by the Bodhisatta <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sutasoma</span>. He is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgulimāla</span> (Ja.v.pp.503f, 511).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (DN­a.ii.483) explains the name (“Spot-foot”) as being given because of a wound he once received, which healed, leaving a scar like a piece of well-grained timber (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cittadārusadiso</span>). This refers to the flight of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">porisāda</span> from his pursuers, when he trod on an acacia stake (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">khānu</span>) which pierced his foot (Ja.v.472).

According to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>, the cannibal in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayaddisajātaka</span> (Ja 513) was also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kammāsa</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kammāsapāda</span>. (DN­a.ii.483. See also Watanabe’s article, “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">The Story of Kalmāsapāda and its Evolution in Indian Literature</span>.” JPTS1909, pp.236ff).

The place where the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">porisāda</span> was tamed was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kammāsadamma</span>.
