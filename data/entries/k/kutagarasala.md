# Kūṭāgārasālā

## English source

Kūṭāgārasālā. A hall in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vana</span> near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>. The Buddha stayed there on several occasions, and in the books are found records of various eminent persons who visited him there and of his conversations with them. Among such visitors are mentioned several <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span> chiefs, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāli Oṭṭhatthaddha</span> (DN.i.150ff; SN.i.230f; iii.68f; AN.v.86f; several visits of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāli</span> are mentioned; for details see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāli</span>. Bv­a. p.3 mentions that the Buddha spent his sixth rainy season in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kūṭāgārasālā</span>), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandaka</span> (SN.v.389), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunakkhatta</span> (MN.ii.252), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span> (AN.ii.190f), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāḷha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span> (AN.ii.200), all attended by numerous retinues; their *senāpati*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīha</span>, who went with five hundred chariots, having only decided after much hesitation to see the Buddha (A iii.38f; iv.79, 179ff); the Jaina <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saccaka</span>, whom the Buddha won only after much argumentation, as described in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Saccakasutta</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Saccakasutta</span> (MN.i.227ff; 237ff; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī Dummukha</span> is also mentioned, MN.i.234, as having been present when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saccaka</span> argued with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>); the householder <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, acclaimed by the Buddha for the possession of eight eminent qualities (AN.iii.49; iv.208f; SN.iv.109); the *upāsaka* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāseṭṭha</span> (AN.iv.258f), the two goddesses, daughters of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajjunna</span>, both known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokanadā</span> (SN.i.29f); and the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṅgiyāni</span> (AN.iii.237f).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> waited on the Buddha and ministered to him during his stay in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kūṭāgārasālā</span>, and it is said that they were of various hues: some blue, others yellow, etc. And <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṅgiyānī</span>, seeing the Buddha shining in their midst, surpassing them all, once uttered the Buddha’s praises in verse, winning, as reward from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>, five hundred upper garments, all of which, he, in turn, presented to the Buddha (AN.iii.239f). On one occasion, when the Buddha was preaching to the monks regarding the six spheres of sense contact, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> arranged an earthquake to break the monks’ concentration, but failed to achieve his object (SN.i.112). Several Jātakas were related by the Buddha in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kūṭāgārasālā</span>: the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sigāla</span> (Ja.ii.5), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Telovāda</span> (Ja.ii.262), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhiya</span> (Ja.i.420), and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekapaṇṇa</span> (Ja.i.504). It was here that the Buddha finally agreed to grant the request of the five hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> women, led by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī Gotamī</span>, that they might be ordained as nuns. They had followed the Buddha hither from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> (AN.iv.274f; Vin.ii.253f; Ja.ii.392). The Buddha gave <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī Gotamī</span>, at her special request, a summary of his doctrine (AN.iv.280). It was also at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kūṭāgārasālā</span> that the Buddha uttered his prophecy as to the ultimate downfall of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> (SN.ii.267f).

It was customary for the Buddha, when staying at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kūṭāgārasālā</span>, to spend the noonday siesta in the woods outside the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vana</span>, at the foot of a tree; visitors coming at that time would, if their desire to see him was insistent (see, e.g., DN.i.151; AN.iii.75), seek him there or be conducted to him. Sometimes he would express his desire to see no one during such a retreat, except the monk who brought him his food.

On one occasion the retreat lasted a fortnight, and on his return he found that a large number of monks had committed suicide as a result of a sermon he had preached to them before his retreat on the un-loveliness of the body. He then caused the monks to be assembled, and asked them to concentrate on breathing (SN.v.320f). Sometimes the Buddha would walk from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kūṭāgārasālā</span> to places of interest in the neighbourhood – e.g., the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sārandadacetiya</span> (AN.iii.167) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cāpālacetiya</span> (SN.v.258; AN.iv.308f). It was from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cāpālacetiya</span>, during one of these walks that he gazed for the last time on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>. He then returned to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kūṭāgārasālā</span>, where he announced that his death would take place within three months (DN.ii.119f; SN.v.258ff).

According to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (DN­a.i.310; MN­a.i.450), there was a monastery (*saṅghārāma*) built for the monks in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vana</span>. Part of it consisted of a storeyed house, with a hall below surrounded only by pillars. These pillars held the gabled room which formed the main part of the Buddha’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhakuṭi</span> there. The hall lay from north to south and faced east (DN­a.i.311), and from this hall the whole monastery came to be known as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kūṭāgārasālā</span>. There was a sick ward attached to the monastery, where the Buddha would often visit the patients and talk with them (e.g., SN.iv.210f; AN.iii.142).

The books also contain the names of others who stayed at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kūṭāgārasālā</span> when the Buddha was in residence – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, who was visited there by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍitakumāra</span> (AN.i.220); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span>, who lived there in a forest hut (SN.iii.116; iv.380); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgita</span>, the Buddha’s former attendant, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgita</span>’s nephew the novice <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīha</span> (DN.i.151); also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upacāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakkaṭa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalimbha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nikaṭa</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭissaha</span>, all of whom left the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kūṭāgārasālā</span> and retired to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gosiṅgasālavana</span>, when the visits of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> to the Buddha became disturbing to their solitude (AN.v.133f).

In later times <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa Kākaṇḍakaputta</span> is mentioned as having stayed there (Vin-a.i.34; Mhv.iv.12; Dpv.v.29).

Eighteen thousand monks under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Buddharakkhita</span> went from the monastery in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vana</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> to the foundation ceremony of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> (Mhv.xxix.33).

According to the Northern books (Divy.136, 200; AvS.8; Mvu.i.300), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kūṭāgārasālā</span> was on the banks of the lake Markaṭā (*Markaṭahradatīre*).
