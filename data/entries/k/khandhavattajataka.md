# Khandhavattajātaka

## English source

Khandhavattajātaka (Ja 203). The Bodhisatta was once born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span>, and later became an ascetic. On it being reported to him that many ascetics died of snake-bite, he gathered them together and taught them how, by cultivating love for the four royal races of snakes – the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Virūpakkhas</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Erāpattas</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chabbyāputtas</span>, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇhagotamas</span> – they could prevent themselves from ever being bitten by any creature.

The story was told in reference to a monk who died of snake-bite. Ja.ii.144ff. cf. Vin.ii.109f.

The story is evidently an expansion of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khandaparitta</span>.
