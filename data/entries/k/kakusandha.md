# Kakusandha

## English source

Kakusandha 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakusandha Buddha</span>. The twenty-second of the twenty-four Buddhas and the first of the five Buddhas of the present <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddakappa</span>. He was the son of the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggidatta</span>, chaplain of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemaṅkara</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemavatī</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>. He was born in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khema</span> pleasaunce, and lived for four thousand years in the household in three palaces – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ruci</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suruci</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaḍḍhana</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rativaḍḍhana</span>). His wife was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Virocamānā</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rocanī</span>), and he had a son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span>. He left the world riding in a chariot, and practised austerities for only eight months. Before his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>, he was given a meal of milk-rice by the daughter of the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajirindha</span> of the village <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sucirindha</span>, and grass for his seat by the *yavapālaka* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhadda</span>. His *bodhi* was a *Sirīsa*-tree, and his first sermon was preached to eighty-four thousand monks in the park near the city of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makila</span>. He performed the Twin-Miracle under a *Sāla*-tree at the gates of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇṇakujja</span>. Among his converts was a fierce <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Naradeva</span>. He held only one assembly of his monks. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakusandha</span>’s body was forty cubits in height, and he died at the age of forty thousand years in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khema</span> pleasaunce. The *Thūpa* erected over his relics was one league high. The Bodhisatta was at that time a king named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khema</span>. The Buddha’s chief disciples were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vidhura</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sañjīva</span> among monks, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Campā</span> among nuns. His personal attendant was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhija</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Accuta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samaṇa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunandā</span> were his most eminent lay-supporters (DN.ii.7; Bv.xxiii; Ja.i.42; Bv­a.209ff). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakusandha</span> kept the fast-day (*uposatha*) every year (Dhp­a.iii.236). In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakusandha</span>’s time a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dūsī</span> (a previous birth of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>), gave a great deal of trouble to the Buddha and his followers, trying greatly the Buddha’s patience (MN.i.333ff; Thag.1187). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (SN.ii.190f) mentions that during the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakusandha</span>, the Mount <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepulla</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> was named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pācīnavaṁsa</span> and the inhabitants were called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tivarā</span>.

The monastery built by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Accuta</span> on the site where, in the present age, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span> erected the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavanārāma</span>, was half a league in extent, and the ground was bought by golden *kacchapas* sufficient in number to cover it (Ja.i.94).

According to the Ceylonese chronicles (Dpv.ii.66; xv.25, 34; xvii.9, 16, etc.; Mhv.xv.57-90), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakusandha</span> paid a visit to Ceylon. The island was then known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ojadīpa</span> and its capital was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayanagara</span>, where reigned King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Meghavana</span> was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Tittha</span>. The Buddha came, with forty thousand disciples, to rid the island of a pestilence caused by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> and stood on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devakūṭa</span> mountain from where, by virtue of his own desire, all inhabitants of the country could see him. The Buddha and his disciples were invited to a meal by the king, and after the meal the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Tittha</span> garden was presented to the Saṅgha; there the Buddha sat, in meditation, in order to consecrate various spots connected with the religion. At the Buddha’s wish, the nun <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rucānandā</span> brought to the island a branch of the sacred *bodhi*-tree. The Buddha gave to the people his own drinking-vessel as an object of worship, and returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>, leaving behind his disciples <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Deva</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rucānandā</span> to look after the spiritual welfare of the new converts to the faith.

In Buddhist Sanskrit texts the name of the Buddha is given as Krakucchanda (see especially the Divy.254, 418f; Mvu.iii.247, 330).
