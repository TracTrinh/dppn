# Kappaṭakura Thera

## English source

Kappaṭakura Thera. An <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. He belonged to a poor family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, where he went about in rags, pan in hand, seeking for rice grains (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kura</span>), hence his name (“Rags and Rice”). Later he sold grass for a living. One day, having heard a monk preach, he entered the Saṅgha, leaving his rags in a certain place; seven times disaffection grew within him, and each time he took up his rags and put them on. When the Buddha heard of this he admonished the monk severely, and the latter, greatly disturbed, developed insight and became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span> he was a householder, and offered the Buddha a few <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ketakī</span>-flowers on the banks of the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vinatā</span> (Thag.199f.; Thag­a.i.320ff). He is probably identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ketakapupphiya</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>. Ap.ii.449.
