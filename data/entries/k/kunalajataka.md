# Kuṇālajātaka

## English source

Kuṇālajātaka (Ja 536). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇāla</span>, king of the *Citrakokilas*, though well served by his hen birds, always despised them and found fault with them. The king of the Phussakokilas, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇamukha</span>, on the other hand, always sang the praises of his escort. One day the two kings met, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇamukha</span> asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇāla</span> why he was not more gracious to his ladies. “Because I know too much about women,” was the answer; but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇamukha</span> was not in a mood to discuss the matter any more.

Later, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇamukha</span> fell ill, and his hen birds deserted him and came to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇāla</span>. He drove them away, ministered to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇamukha</span>, and cheered him. Some time after, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇāla</span>, seated on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manosilātala</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span> (according to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>, DN.ii.675, this was on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇāladaha</span>), started to tell his friend of the wickedness of women. Hearing of this, many inhabitants of numerous worlds came to listen to him, among them <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, king of the vultures, and the ascetic <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span>. Many were the instances given by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇāla</span> to illustrate the deceitfulness, ingratitude and immorality of women – among them the stories of:

- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kaṇhā</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Saccatapāvī</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kākātī</span> (Ja 327)
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kuraṅgavī</span>,
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Brahmadatta</span>’s mother who sinned with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Pañcālacaṇḍa</span>
- the queen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kiṇṇarā</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Pañcapāpā</span> and
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Piṅgiyānī</span>

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇāla</span>’s diatribe was followed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>’s, and his by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span>’s, each claiming to speak from facts within their knowledge.

In the stories related by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇāla</span>, the bird-king is identified with one of the characters concerned in each story, so that he was able to speak with authority. Thus he was

- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Ajjuna</span>, one of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kaṇha</span>’s husbands;
- the goldsmith in the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Saccatapāvī</span>;
- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Garuḍa</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kākātī</span>’s tale;
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Chalaṅgakumāra</span>, who misconducted himself with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kuraṅgavī</span>;
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Pañcālacaṇḍa</span>, lover of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Brahmadatta</span>’s mother;
- the chaplain, also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Pañcālacaṇḍa</span>, who saved <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kiṇṇarā</span> from her husband’s wrath;
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Baka</span>, one time husband of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Pañcapāpā</span>;
- and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Brahmadatta</span>, husband of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Piṅgiyānī</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇamukha</span> is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span>, the vulture-king with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>.

The preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇālajātaka</span> was followed by that of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Samayasutta</span>.

This <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> was related in order to destroy the discontent that rose in the hearts of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> youths, kinsmen of the Buddha, who, having entered the Saṅgha, were troubled by the thought of the wives they had left behind. The Buddha therefore took them to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span>, showed them the magnificent beauty of the region, particularly the miraculous splendours of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇāladaha</span>, and there preached to them. At the end of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> they all became *Arahants* (Ja.v.412-56; also DN­a.ii.674ff; AN­a.i.173).

See also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Kuṇālajātaka</span> (Ja 464).
