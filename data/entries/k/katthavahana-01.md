# Kaṭṭhavāhana 01

## English source

Kaṭṭhavāhana 01. A king. A previous birth of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭṭhavāhana</span> had been a very clever carpenter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, having under him sixteen thousand and sixteen assistants. They paid periodical visits to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span> forests, felled trees, and having prepared the timber which was suitable for building purposes, brought it down the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span> and erected houses for the king and for the people.

Growing tired of this work, these carpenters made flying machines of light wood, and going northwards from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span>, established by conquest a kingdom, the chief carpenter becoming the king. He came to be called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭṭhavāhana</span>, the capital was named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭṭhavāhananagara</span> and the country <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭṭhavāhanaraṭṭha</span>. The king was righteous and the people very happy and the country prospered greatly. Later <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭṭhavāhana</span> and the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> became sincere friends, and free trade, exempt from all taxes, was established between the two countries. The kings sent each other very costly and magnificent gifts.

Once <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭṭhavāhana</span> sent to the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> eight priceless rugs in eight caskets of lacquered ivory, each rug being sixteen cubits long and eight cubits wide and of unsurpassed splendour. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> king, wondering how he could adequately return the courtesy, decided to let his friend learn the great news of the appearance in the world of the Buddha (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>), the Dhamma and the Saṅgha. This message was written on a gold leaf and the leaf enclosed in many caskets, one inside the other, the innermost casket being made of the seven kinds of jewels and the outermost of costly wood. The caskets were placed on a splendid palanquin and sent on the back of a royal elephant, accompanied by all the insignia of royalty. All along the route the honours due to a king were paid to the casket, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭṭhavāhana</span> himself escorted the elephant from the frontiers of his kingdom to the capital. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭṭhavāhana</span> discovered the message, he was overjoyed, and sent his nephew with sixteen of his ministers and sixteen thousand followers to investigate the matter and convey his greetings to the Buddha.

The envoys arrived at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> only after the Buddha’s death, but hearing from the Buddha’s disciples of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span> he had proclaimed to the world, the ministers and their followers entered the Saṅgha, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭṭhavāhana</span>’s nephew was sent back to report the news to the king, taking with him the Buddha’s water-pot, a branch of the Bodhi-tree and a monk versed in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span>. The king, having learnt the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span>, engaged in various works of piety till his death, after which he was born among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāmāvacara</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>. Snp-a.ii.675ff.
