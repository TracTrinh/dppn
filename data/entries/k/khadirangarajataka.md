# Khadiraṅgārajātaka

## English source

Khadiraṅgārajātaka (Ja 40). Once the Bodhisatta was Treasurer of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, and a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>, rising from a seven days’ *samāpatti*, came to him at meal time. The Bodhisatta sent him some food, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> created a pit of glowing *khadira*-embers between the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> and the Treasurer’s house. When the Treasurer heard of this, he took the bowl of food himself and stepped into the pit, ready to die rather than to have his alms-giving thwarted. A lotus sprang up to receive his foot, the pit vanished, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>, discomfited, vanished.

The story was related to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>.

A *Devatā*, who lived in the upper storey of his palace, had to come with her children down to the ground floor whenever the Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>. She tried to check the merchant’s munificence by talking to his manager and his eldest son, but all in vain. At last, when as a result of his extreme piety <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>’s wealth was exhausted, the *Devatā* ventured to approach him and warn him of his impending ruin if he did not take heed. He ordered her out of the house, and she had, perforce, to obey. In despair she sought the aid of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, who suggested that she should recover for the merchant all his debts, and reveal to him his hidden treasure which had been lost sight of. She did so, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>, before consenting to pardon her, took her to the Buddha, who then related this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Velāmakasutta</span> was also preached on this occasion (Ja.i.226-34; see also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visayhajātaka</span> (Ja 340)). For a continuation of the story see the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirijātaka</span> (Ja 284).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp­a.i.447) the Khadiraṅgāra Jākata was preached in reference to the two friends <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirigutta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gharadinna</span>. It is said (AN­a.i.57) that at the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> eighty-four thousand beings realised the Truth.
