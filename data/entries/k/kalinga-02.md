# Kāliṅga 02

## English source

Kāliṅga 02. A country: the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāliṅgaraṭṭha</span>. It is one of the seven political divisions mentioned in the time of the mythical king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Reṇu</span> and is given first in the list, its capital being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dantapura</span> and its king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sattabhū</span> (DN.ii.235f; see also Mhv.iii.208; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Vastu</span> also mentions a king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uggata</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dantapura</span>, iii.364f).

It is not, however, included in the list of sixteen Janapadas appearing in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (AN.i.213, etc.), but is found in the extended list of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Niddesa</span> (CNid.ii.37). A later tradition (Bv.xviii.6) states that after the Buddha’s death, a Tooth was taken from among his relics and placed at Kāliṅga, where it was worshipped. From Kāliṅga the Tooth was brought to Ceylon, in the time of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimeghavaṇṇa</span>, by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemamālā</span>, daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Guhasīva</span>, king of Kāliṅga, and her husband <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dantakumāra</span>, a prince of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span> royal family. In Ceylon the Tooth became the “Palladium” of the Sinhalese kings (Cv.xxxvii.92; see also Cv.Trs.i.7, n.4; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dāṭhādhātuvaṁsa</span> gives details, JPTS 1884, pp.108ff).

The Jātakas contain various references to Kāliṅga. There was once a great drought in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dantapura</span>, and the king, acting on the advice of his ministers, sent Brahmins to the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuru</span> to beg the loan of his state elephant, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Añjanavasabha</span>, credited with the power of producing rain. On this occasion, however, the elephant failed and the Kāliṅga king, hearing of the virtues practised by the king and people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dantapura</span>, offered them himself, upon which rain fell. See the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurudhammajātaka</span> (Ja 276), Ja.ii.367ff, also Dhp­a.iv.88f. A similar story is related in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantarajātaka</span>, vi.487, where the Kāliṅga Brahmins ask for and obtain <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span>’s white elephant that he may stay the drought in Kāliṅga.

Another king of Kāliṅga was a contemporary of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aruṇa</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaka</span> king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Potali</span>. The Kāliṅga king, in his eagerness for a fight, picked a quarrel with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aruṇa</span>, but was worsted in battle, and had to surrender his four daughters with their dowries to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aruṇa</span> (Ja.iii.3f).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāliṅgabodhijātaka</span> (Ja 479) relates the story of another ruler of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāliṅga</span> while, according to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhaṅgajātaka</span> (Ja 522), a certain king of Kāliṅga (Ja.v.135f) went with two other kings, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṭṭhaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhīmaratta</span>, to ask <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhaṅga</span> questions referring to the fate of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Daṇḍakī</span>. There they heard the sage preach, and all three kings became ascetics. Another king of Kāliṅga was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāḷikīra</span>, who, having ill-treated a holy man, was swallowed up in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunakhaniraya</span>, while his country was laid waste by the gods and turned into a wilderness (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kāliṅgārañña</span>). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāliṅgārañña</span> is referred to in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upālisutta</span> (MN.i.378); the story is related in Ja.v.144 and, in greater detail, in MN­a.ii.602ff. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumbhakārajātaka</span> (Ja 408, Ja.iii.376) the Kāliṅga king’s name is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Karaṇḍu</span>.

From early times there seems to have been political intercourse between the peoples of Kāliṅga and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅga</span>; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Susīmā</span>, grandmother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijaya</span>, founder of the Sinhalese race, was a Kāliṅga princess, married to the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅga</span> (Mhv.vi.1; Dpv.ix.2ff). Friendly relations between Ceylon and Kāliṅga were evidently of long standing, for we find in the reign of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggabodhi II</span>. (601-11 CE.) the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāliṅga</span>, together with his queen and his minister, coming over to Ceylon intent on leading the life of a recluse and joining the Saṅgha under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotipāla</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggabodhi</span> and his queen treated them with great honour (Cv.xlii.44ff). Later, the queen consort of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda IV</span>. came from Kāliṅga and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijayabāhu I</span>. married a Kāliṅga princess, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tilokasundarī</span> (Cv.lix.30). We are told that scions of the Kāliṅga dynasty had many times attained to the sovereignty of Ceylon and that there were many ties of relationship between the royal families of the two countries (Cv.lxiii.7, 12f). But it was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgha</span>, an offspring of the Kāliṅga kings, who did incomparable damage to Ceylon and to its religion and literature (Cv.lxxx.58ff).

According to the inscriptions, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>, in the thirteenth year of his reign, conquered Kāliṅga and this was the turning-point in his career, causing him to abhor war (Mookerji: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asoka</span>, pp.16, 37, 214). Among the retinue sent by him to accompany the branch of the Sacred Bodhi Tree on its journey to Ceylon, were eight families of Kāliṅga (Vin-a.i.96).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s brother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span>, later known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekavihāriya</span>, spent his retirement in the Kāliṅga country with his instructor <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammarakkhita</span>, and there <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> built for him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhojakagirivihāra</span> (Thag­a.i.506).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantarajātaka</span> (Ja 547, Ja.vi.521), the Brahmin village <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dunniviṭṭha</span>, residence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jūjaka</span>, was in Kāliṅga.

Kāliṅga is generally identified with the modern Orissa. (CAGI.590ff; Law: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Geography of Early Buddhism</span>, 64; see also Bhaṇḍarkar: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ancient History of Deccan</span>, p.12).
