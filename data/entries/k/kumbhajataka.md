# Kumbhajātaka

## English source

Kumbhajātaka (Ja 512). The story of how a forester, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sura</span>, accidentally discovered strong drink, and how, with the help of his accomplice, the ascetic <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Varuṇa</span>, he spread abroad the discovery, thus leading to the destruction of all <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>, had <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> not appeared on earth and by his exposition of the evils of drink induced <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabbamitta</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, to abstain from its use.

The story was told in answer to a question by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> as to the origin of drink. Once during a drinking festival at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> five hundred women, friends of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> in her company. On the way they became drunk, which led to their behaving improperly in the monastery. The Buddha frightened them by his *iddhi*-power and restored them to their senses. (Ja.v.11ff; the Dhp­a.iii.100ff gives a slightly different version of the doings of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>’s friends).

The story of the past is also given in the *Jātakamālā* (Jm 17).
