# Kammāsadamma

## English source

Kammāsadamma (*v.l.* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kammāsadhamma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kammasadamma</span>). A township of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurūs</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>, during the course of his wanderings, stayed there several times; the exact place of his residence is, however, mentioned only once, namely the fire-hut of a Brahmin of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhāradvāja</span> *gotta*, where a grass mat was spread for him by the Brahmin. It was on this occasion, according to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyasutta</span> (MN.i.501), that, after a long discussion, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiya</span> was converted.

Several important discourses were preached at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kammāsadamma</span>, among them being:

- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mahā Nidānasutta</span> (DN.ii.55; SN.ii.92)
- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mahā Satipaṭṭhānasutta</span> (DN.ii.290; MN.i.55)
- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Ānañjasappāyasutta</span> (MN.ii.26)

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (SN.ii.107f) contains a discourse on handling experiences by way of casual relations, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttara</span> (AN.v.29f) a discourse on the ten noble states (*ariyavāsā*), both preached at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kammāsadhamma</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (SN­a.ii.89) says that the people there were full of wisdom and their food was nutritious; it was therefore a compliment to their intellectual calibre that the Buddha should have preached these *Suttas* to them.

Even in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>’s day the name of the township had two different spellings, and two etymologies are suggested for the names (DN­a.ii.483). The place was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kammāsadamma</span> because it was here that the man-eating ogre, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kammāsapāda</span> was tamed and civilized by the Bodhisatta. (*Kammāso ettha damito ti, Kammāsadamaṁ-Kammāso ti Kammāsapādo porisādo vuccati*.)

The spelling <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kammāsadhamma</span> is explained on the ground that the people of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuru</span> country had a code of honour called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuruvattadhamma</span>; it was here that Kammāsa (already referred to) was converted and made to accept this code, hence the name of the township. (*Kururaṭṭhavāsīnaṁ kira kuruvattadhammo, tasmiṁ Kammāso jāto, tasmā taṁ ṭhānaṁ “Kammāso ettha dhamme jāto” ti Kammāsadhamman-ti vuccati*, the people of the Kuru country were said to have had a practice called *Kuruvatta*. In that (region), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t19" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t19">Kammāsa</span> was born. Therefore, that place is called Kammāsa<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span> because <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t19" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t19">Kammāsa</span> was born here in accordance with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span>).

According to the Jātakas, there are two places of the same name, called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Kammāsadamma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kammāsadamma</span> respectively, to distinguish one from the other. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kammāsadamma</span>, which was evidently the original place, was founded on the spot where the *porisāda* of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sutasomajātaka</span> (Ja 537) was tamed (Ja.v.411), while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Kammāsadamma</span> was the name given to the place where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayaddisa</span> (Ja 513) showed his prowess by his spiritual victory over the ogre in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayaddisajātaka</span> (Ja 513, Ja.v.35f).

In the Divy.515f, the place is called Kammāsadamya. It was the residence of the nuns <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanduttarā</span> and Mittākālikā (Thīg­a.87, 89).
