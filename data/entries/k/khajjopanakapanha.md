# Khajjopanakapañha

## English source

Khajjopanakapañha. When, as a result of the conspiracy of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Senaka</span> and the other ministers of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vedeha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> was compelled to flee from the Court and live as a potter outside the city gates, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Devatā</span> of the king’s parasol, wishing to see him reinstated, appeared to the king and put to him several questions. The king consulted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Senaka</span> and his colleagues, but they could find no solution. The deity appeared again to him and said he was like a man who, wanting a fire, blows a firefly, crumbling over it cow-dung and grass, or, wanting milk, milks the cow’s horn. She then threatened the king with death if her questions were not answered. In despair, the king sent for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> (Ja.vi.371f).

It is evidently this story which is mentioned elsewhere (Ja.iii.197) as a separate <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span>, but no details are given, and the reader is referred to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khajjopanakapañha</span>.
