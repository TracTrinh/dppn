# Kusinārā

## English source

As the scene of his death, Kusinārā became one of the four holy places declared by the Buddha to be fit places of pilgrimage for the pious, the other three being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhagayā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> (DN.ii.140). Mention is made of other visits paid to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> by the Buddha, prior to that when his death took place. Thus, once he went there from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āpaṇa</span> and having spent some time at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>, proceeded to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ātumā</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> were always great admirers of the Buddha, even though not all of them were his followers, and on the occasion of this visit they decided that any inhabitant of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> who failed to go and meet the Buddha and escort him to the city, would be fined five hundred. It was on this occasion that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Roja</span> the Mallan was converted and gave to the Buddha and the monks a supply of green vegetables and pastries (Vin.i.247f). During some of these visits the Buddha stayed in a wood called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Baliharaṇa</span>, and there he preached two of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārāsutta</span>s (AN.i.274f; v.79f) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kintisutta</span> (MN.ii.238f). A third <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārāsutta</span> he preached while staying at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upavattana</span>. (AN.ii.79; for another discourse to some noisy monks at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upavattana</span>, see Ud.iv.2).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> was the birthplace of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span> and his wife <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span> (Dhp­a.i.338, 349). It was twenty-five <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojanas</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> (DN­a.ii.609; according to Faxian, p.40, it was twenty-four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojanas</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>) and lay on the high road from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aḷaka</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, the road taken by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span>’s disciples (Snp.v.1012).

This was evidently the road taken also by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span>, when he came to pay his last respects to the Buddha (Vin.ii.284).

According to a late tradition, one-eighth of the Buddha’s relics were deposited in a cairn in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> and honoured by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> (DN.ii.167; Bv.xxviii.3).

In ancient times <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> was the capital of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tālissara</span> and twelve of his descendants (Dpv.iii.32). It was also the scene of the death of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phussa Buddha</span> at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setārāma</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t18">v.l.</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇārāma</span>) (Bv­a.195; Bv.xix.25).

In Xuanzang’s day there still existed towers and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Saṅghārāmas</span> erected to mark the spots connected with the Buddha’s last days and obsequies at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>. According to his account <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> was nineteen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojanas</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> (Beal-Xuan. op.cit.li. lii. n).

To the northern Buddhists the place was also known as Kuśigrāma (Kuśigrāmaka) and Kuśinagarī (e.g., Divy.152f, 208).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> is identified with the village of Kasia at the junction of the river Rapti and the smaller Gondak and to the east of the Gorakhpur district (CAGI.i.493). A copper plate belonging to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thūpa</span> erected at the site of the Buddha’s death has recently been discovered (CAGI.i.714).

The people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> are called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosinārakā</span>, e.g., DN.ii.167.
