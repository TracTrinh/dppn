# Khemā 01

## English source

Khemā 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemā Therī</span>. An *Arahant*, chief of the Buddha’s women disciples. She was born in a ruling family at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgala</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span> country, and her skin was of the colour of gold. She became the chief consort of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>. She would not visit the Buddha who was at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span>, lest he should speak disparagingly of her beauty with which she was infatuated. The king bade poets sing the glories of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span> and persuaded <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemā</span> to go there. She was then brought face to face with the Buddha, and he conjured up, for her to see, a woman like a celestial nymph who stood facing him. Even as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemā</span> gazed on the nymph, whose extraordinary beauty far excelled her own, she saw her pass gradually from youth to extreme old age, and so fall down in the swoon of death. Seeing that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemā</span> was filled with dismay at the sight, the Buddha preached to her on the vanity of lust, and we are told that at that moment she attained *Arahant*-ship. With the consent of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> she entered the Saṅgha, and was ranked by the Buddha foremost among his women disciples for her great insight (*mahāpaññāṇaṁ aggā*) (AN.i.25; Dpv.xviii.9; see also MN­a.iv.168f.; Bv.xxvi.19; Ja.i.15,16).

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span> she was a slave, and having seen the Buddha’s chief disciple, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujāta</span>, gave him three cakes, and that same day she sold her hair and gave him alms.

In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>’s time she became the eldest daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kikī</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, and was named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samaṇī</span>. With her sisters she observed celibacy for twenty thousand years and built a monastery for the Buddha. She learnt the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nidānasutta</span>, having heard the Buddha preach it. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī</span> she became a renowned preacher of the Dhamma, and during the time of both <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakusandha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇāgamana</span> she had great monasteries built for the Buddha and his monks. AN­a.i.187f; Thīg.139-44; Thīg­a.126ff; Ap.ii.543ff; Dhp­a.iv.57ff; cf. the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rūpanandā</span> (Dhp­a.iii.113-9).

Once when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemā</span> was at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Toraṇavatthu</span>, between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, who happened to spend one night there, heard of her presence and went to see her. He questioned her as to whether or not the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tathāgata</span> existed after death. She explained the matter to him in various ways, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, delighted with her exposition, related it to the Buddha (SN.iv.374ff). She is mentioned in several places (e.g., AN.i.88; ii.164; iv.347; SN.ii.236) as the highest ideal of womanhood worthy of imitation, and is described as the nun par excellence.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemā</span> is identified with the mother in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uragajātaka</span> (Ja 354, Ja.iii.168), the queen in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohantamigajātaka</span> (Ja 501, Ja.iv.423) and in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsajātaka</span> (Ja 502, Ja.iv.430), the queen, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemā</span>, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Haṁsajātaka</span> (Ja 534, Ja.v.382), and the princess in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janakajātaka</span> (Ja 539, Ja.vi.68).
