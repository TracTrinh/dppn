# Kaccāyanavyākaraṇa

## English source

Kaccāyanavyākaraṇa. A treatise on Pāli grammar by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāyana</span>. The treatise is in eight divisions, each division comprising *Suttas* or rules, expressed with great brevity; *vutti* or supplements, to render the *Suttas* intelligible; *payoga* or grammatical analyses, with examples, and *nyāsa* or scholia, explanatory notes of the principal grammatical forms in the shape of questions and answers. The *nyāsa* often exists as a separate book, called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mukhamattadīpanī</span>. Orthodox tradition ascribes the whole work to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span>, but another tradition, recorded in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāyanabheda</span>, states that the aphorisms are by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāyana</span>, the *vutti* by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghānandī</span>, the illustrations by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span> and the *nyāsa* by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vimalabuddhi</span> – all perhaps belonging to the same school of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> (see above, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāna</span>).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāyana</span>’s work shows clearly the influence of Sanskrit grammar, chiefly the *Kātantra*. Many later works were written about the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāyana</span> (as it is called) or were based on it, the chief among them being the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rūpasiddhi</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāyanabheda</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāyanasāra</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāyanavaṇṇanā</span> (*q.v.*). For details see PLC.179ff; Bode, 21.
