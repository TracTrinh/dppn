# Kosambījātaka

## English source

Kosambījātaka (Ja 428). The introductory story relates how the monks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> quarrelled and brought about great dissension among themselves because one of their number had left in a vessel the surplus water for rinsing the mouth. When the Buddha found that he could not induce the monks to live in harmony, he related to them the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghīti</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>, and when even that failed to produce the desired effect he uttered ten stanzas, standing poised in mid­air, and went away from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>, leaving the monks to their fate.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambījātaka</span> contains only a small portion of the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghīti</span>, scarcely more than an allusion to it. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghīti Kosalajātaka</span> (*q.v.*) contains further details, but even when taken together, these two do not make the story complete. The full story is related in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vinayapiṭaka</span> (Vin.i.342ff).
