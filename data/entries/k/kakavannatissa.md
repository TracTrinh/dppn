# Kākavaṇṇatissa

## English source

Kākavaṇṇatissa (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kākavaṇṇa</span>). A king of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohaṇa</span> dynasty in Ceylon. He was the great-grandson of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāga</span>, brother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span>, and his father was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Goṭhābhaya</span> (Mhv.xv.170f; Mbv.132). His capital was at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Gāma</span>. He had as wife, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devī</span> (better known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vihāradevī</span>), daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalyāṇi</span>, who had been cast into the sea to expiate her father’s crimes (Mhv.xxii.20ff). Their children were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī Abhaya</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saddhātissa</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kākavaṇṇatissa</span> gathered round him all the foremost Sinhalese warriors of the time so that they should be available for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gāmaṇi</span>, when the time came for his campaign against the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Damiḷas</span> (Mhv.xxiii.2).

But at the start <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kākavaṇṇatissa</span> was very reluctant to allow his son to make preparations for such a campaign (Mhv.xxii.82f), so much so that, in exasperation, the young prince once sent his father some female ornaments to indicate that the king was no man (Mhv.xxiv.4). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kākavaṇṇatissa</span> was very pious, and is said to have built sixty-four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāras</span>, sixty-four years being also the length of his reign (Mhv.xxiv.12; see also AN­a.i.279). Among the religious edifices built by him were the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissamahārāma</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittalapabbata</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> (Mhv.xxii.23) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nuggala</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Cetiya</span>. He was cremated at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissamahārāma</span> (xxiv.8, 13). He evidently received his name on account of his dark colour. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīpavaṁsa</span> (Dpv.xviii.20; were their names <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahilā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samantā</span>?; see also xix.21f) speaks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kākavaṇṇatissa</span>’s daughters as having been proficient in the history of the Religion (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">saddhammavaṁsakovidā</span>).

He was once a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">milakkka</span> in India and looked after a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>. One day he gave the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> a meal of ripe jack-fruit. On another occasion, when the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> visited his house in his absence, his wife tried to tempt him. Having failed, she complained to the husband that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> had assaulted her. The latter sought the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> to kill him, but, seeing him in mid-air putting on his robe, he was filled with wonder and asked the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>’s forgiveness. Later he was born in a hunters’ village near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amaruppalalena</span>, his name being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amaruppala</span>, and did various good deeds.

He was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kākavaṇṇatissa</span> because he knew the speech of crows. Ras.ii.53f; see also p.64, where a crow announces various things to him.
