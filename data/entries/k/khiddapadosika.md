# Khiḍḍāpadosikā

## English source

Khiḍḍāpadosikā *v.l.* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khiḍḍāpadūsikā</span>. A class of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> who live in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cātummahārājika</span>-world. For ages they spend their time in laughter and in sport of sensual lusts. In consequence their self-possession is corrupted and they fall from their state (DN.i.19; Paṭis-a.441; NidA.i.108). They are so-called because they are corrupted and are destroyed by sport (*khiddāya padussanti, vinassanti*) (DN­a.i.113). It is said that while playing about in such pleasances as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandanavana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittalatāvana</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phārusakavana</span>, they forget to eat and drink and fade away like flowers. Their death is due to self-consciousness (*attasañcetanā*) (AN­a.ii.544). They are, as it were, burnt up by their infatuation (*mohassa anudahanatāyamohanavasena hi tesaṁ satisammoso*) (Vibh­a.498). They were present at the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Samayasutta</span> (DN.ii.260).
