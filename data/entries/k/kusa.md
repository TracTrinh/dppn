# Kusa

## English source

Kusa. The Bodhisatta, son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Okkāka</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sammata</span> and of his queen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīlavatī</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Okkāka</span> has no heir, in spite of performing various rites. But at length, by the favour of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīlavatī</span> miraculously gives birth to two sons. The elder, though ill-favoured, is supernaturally wise and is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusa</span>. The younger, very handsome, is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayampati</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusa</span> consents to marry only on condition that a princess can be obtained exactly like an image which he himself has fashioned. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pabhāvatī</span>, daughter of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgala</span>, is found to fulfil this condition, and is married to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusa</span>. The bride is not to look upon her husband’s face until she has conceived, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusa</span> plays various pranks upon her and she accidentally discovers how ugly he is. She leaves him immediately and returns to her father’s court. Thither <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusa</span> follows her, and under a variety of menial disguises, including that of a cook, tries, but in vain, to win her affection. At length <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> intervenes. He sends letters, purporting to come from King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span>, to seven kings, offering <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pabhāvatī</span> to each of them. They arrive in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgala</span> simultaneously and threaten to destroy the city. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span> decides to cut <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pabhāvatī</span> into seven pieces, and she is only saved from immediate death by the despised husband. At his appearance the kings flee, for wherever he looks the earth trembles. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusa</span> returns with his wife to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sammata</span> and they live there happily.

Pleased at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusa</span>’s victory, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> gives him a jewel called the *Verocaṇamaṇi*. It was octagonal, and was evidently handed down in the succession of kings, for we are told that one of the tests, set by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span>, to discover the proficiency of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span>, was for him to break the old thread in this gem, remove it, and insert a new one. (Ja.vi.340; according to SN­a.i.115 and DN­a.iii.266, the jewel was also in the possession of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>; but see the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sārajātaka</span> (Ja 92), where no mention is made of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusa</span>).

Reference is made elsewhere (e.g., Mhv­ṭ.552) to a *tālavanta* (fan?) possessed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusa</span>, in which could be seen the forms of all things in the world. He also possessed the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokanada</span> *vīnā* (*q.v.*) given by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīlavatī</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusa</span> is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīhassara</span>, and his shout, when he appeared before the seven kings, announcing his name, was one of the four shouts heard throughout <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> (Snp­a.i.223; SN­a.i.248).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīpavaṁsa</span> (iii.40) speaks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kusa</span>, both descended from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sammata</span>.
