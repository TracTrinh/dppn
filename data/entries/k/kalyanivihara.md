# Kalyāṇivihāra

## English source

Kalyāṇivihāra (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalyāṇikavihāra</span>). A monastery attached to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalyāṇicetiya</span>. It was from the earliest times the residence of eminent monks, such as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammagutta</span> (Dhamma-protector) and his five hundred colleagues (Mhv.xxxii.51) and of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godattatthera</span> (MN­a.i.100). Here a Thera<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">,</span> called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍapātiya</span>, once recited the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmajālasutta</span>, and the earth trembled as he finished his recital (DN­a.i.131). Near the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> was the village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷadīghavāpigāma</span>, where monks who lived in the monastery went for alms (Snp-a.i.70; AN­a.i.13).

King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaniṭṭhatissa</span> built in this monastery an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">uposatha</span>-hall (Mhv.xxxvi.17). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijayabāhu III</span>. restored the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span>, which had been damaged by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Damiḷas</span>, and reconstructed the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span>, crowning it with a golden finial. He also built a gate-tower on the eastern side (Cv.lxxxi.59f).

In the fourteenth century <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Alagakkonāra</span> seems to have bestowed great patronage on the monastery, and to have done many things for its improvement (See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ceylon Antiquary and Literary Register</span> i.152; ii.149, 182).

Even in the fifteenth century the monastery was evidently considered one of the chief centres of the Saṅgha in Ceylon, for we find that the monks, sent by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammaceti</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāmañña</span> to Ceylon, received their ordination in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sīmā</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalyāṇivihāra</span>, and that on their return they consecrated a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sīmā</span> in Pegu known as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalyāṇisīmā</span> (Bode, 38).
