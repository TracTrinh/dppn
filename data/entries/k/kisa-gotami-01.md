# Kisā Gotamī 01

## English source

Kisā Gotamī 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kisā Gotamī Therī</span>. An <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. She was declared chief among women disciples with respect to the wearing of coarse robes (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">lūkhacīvara-dharānaṁ</span>) (AN.i.25; the Dhp­a.iv.156 contains a story of the Buddha speaking to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> the praises of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kisā Gotamī</span>). She came from a poor family in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> (of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhikula</span>, which had fallen on evil days, says the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> p.565, vs.19). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotamī</span> was her name – she was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kisā</span> because of her thinness. She was married into a rich family, by whom she was disdainfully treated; but as soon as she bore a son she was shown respect.

(Except by her husband says the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">loc.cit.</span>20. The Dhp­a.ii.270ff account, however, makes no mention of her ill treatment; on the contrary, it leads us to expect that she should have been greatly esteemed because, prior to her arrival, her father-in-law’s wealth, forty crores in amount, had all turned into charcoal. When she touched the charcoal it once more became gold. This account is found also in SN­a.i.149).

The boy, however, died when just old enough to run about; his mother, distraught with grief, fearful lest the dead child should be taken from her, went about with him on her hip, seeking medicine to revive his life. People laughed at her, until one wise man, realizing her condition, directed her to the Buddha. The Buddha asked her to bring him a mustard seed from a house where no one had yet died. In the course of her search for the impossible her frenzy left her, and having grasped the truth, she laid the child in the charnel field, and returning to the Master begged admission to the Saṅgha. She became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>, and soon after, when her insight was developed, the Buddha appeared before her in a blaze of radiance and, listening to his words, she became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. (Thīg­a.174ff; Ap.ii.564f; Dhp­a.i.270ff; AN­a.i.205).

In the verses ascribed to her in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Therīgāthā</span> (vv.213-23), she incorporates the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭācārā</span> in her own psalm, as though to utter more fully the pageant and tragedy inherent in woman’s lot, whereof her own sorrow was but a phase.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> she was a householder’s daughter in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsavatī</span>, and having heard the Buddha assign to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">bhikkhunī</span> the foremost rank among wearers of coarse robes, she vowed that one day the same rank should be her’s.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> she was the fifth daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kikī</span> and her name was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammā</span>. Then she entered the Saṅgha and lived a celibate life (Ap.ii.564f; Thīg­a.190f). She is identified with the lizard in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tittirajātaka</span> (Ja.iii.543).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (i.129f) records a visit paid to her by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> as she sat resting in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhavana</span>. He was forced to retire discomfited.
