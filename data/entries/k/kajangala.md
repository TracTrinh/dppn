# Kajaṅgala

## English source

Kajaṅgala (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kajaṅgalā</span>). A township which formed the eastern boundary of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhimadesa</span>. Beyond it was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sālā</span> (Vin.i.197; DN­a.i.173; MN­a.i.316, etc.; AN­a.i.55, etc.; Ja.i.49; Mbv.12). In the Buddha’s time it was a prosperous place where provisions could easily be obtained (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dabbasambhārasulabhā</span>, Ja.iv.310). Once when the Buddha was staying in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kajaṅgala</span>, the lay followers there heard a sermon from the Buddha and went to the nun <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kajaṅgalā</span> to have it explained in detail (AN.v.54f). On another occasion the Buddha stayed in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mukheluvana</span> and was visited there by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span>, the disciple of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārāsariya</span>. Their conversation is recorded in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indriyabhāvanāsutta</span> (MN.iii.298ff). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span> (p.10), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kajaṅgala</span> is described as a Brahmin village and is given as the place of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgasena</span>’s birth. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapotajātaka</span> mention is made of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kajaṅgala</span>, and the scholiast (Ja.iii.226-7) explains that it may be the same as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. According to the scholiast of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhisajātaka</span> (Ja.iv.311), the tree-spirit mentioned in that story was the chief resident monk in an old monastery in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kajaṅgala</span>, which monastery he repaired with difficulty during the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kajaṅgala</span> is identified with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kie-chu-hoh-khi-lo</span> of Xuanzang, which he describes as a district about two thousand <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">li</span> in circumference. (Beal-Xuan.ii.193, and n.; see also Cunningham, AN.G.I.723). It may also be identical with the town Puṇḍavardhana mentioned in the Divy.21f). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Avadānaśataka</span> (ii.41) calls it Kacaṅgalā.
