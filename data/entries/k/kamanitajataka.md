# Kāmanītajātaka

## English source

Kāmanītajātaka (Ja 228). The king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> had two sons; the elder became king, but was full of greed for wealth. The rest of the story resembles that of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāmajātaka</span> (Ja 467); the three cities which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> proposes to win for the king are given as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarapañcāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indapatta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kekaka</span>.

In this case the physician who cures the king is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> himself, who is identified with the Bodhisatta (Ja.ii.212-16). The story was related in the same circumstances as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāmajātaka</span> (Ja 467).
