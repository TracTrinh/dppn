# Kusinārā

## English source

Kusinārā. The capital of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> and the scene of the Buddha’s death. At that time it was a small city, “a branch-township with wattle-and-daub houses in the midst of the jungle,” and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> was, at first, disappointed that the Buddha should have chosen it for his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Parinibbāna</span>. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>, by preaching the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sudassanasutta</span>, pointed out to him that in ancient times it had been <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusāvatī</span>, the royal city of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sudassana</span> (DN.ii.146). Between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span>, three <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gāvutas</span> away (DN­a.ii.573) – from where the Buddha came to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> on his last journey from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, stopping at various places – lay the stream of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakuṭṭha</span> on the banks of which was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambavana</span>; beyond that was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hiraññavatī</span> river, and near the city, in a south-westerly direction, lay the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upavattana</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sāla</span>-grove of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span>, which the Buddha made his last resting-place (Ud­a.238; DN­a.ii.572f).

After the Buddha’s death his body was carried into the city by the northern gate and out of the city by the eastern gate; to the east of the city was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makuṭabandhana</span>, the shrine of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span>, and there the body was cremated. For seven days those assembled at the ceremony held a festival in honour of the relics (DN.ii.160f).
