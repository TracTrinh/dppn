# Kusinārā

## English source

Kusinārā. The capital of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> and the scene of the Buddha’s death. At that time it was a small city, “a branch-township with wattle-and-daub houses in the midst of the jungle,” and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> was, at first, disappointed that the Buddha should have chosen it for his *Parinibbāna*. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>, by preaching the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sudassanasutta</span>, pointed out to him that in ancient times it had been <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusāvatī</span>, the royal city of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sudassana</span> (DN.ii.146). Between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span>, three *gāvutas* away (DN­a.ii.573) – from where the Buddha came to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> on his last journey from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, stopping at various places – lay the stream of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakuṭṭha</span> on the banks of which was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambavana</span>; beyond that was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hiraññavatī</span> river, and near the city, in a south-westerly direction, lay the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upavattana</span>, the *Sāla*-grove of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span>, which the Buddha made his last resting-place (Ud­a.238; DN­a.ii.572f).

After the Buddha’s death his body was carried into the city by the northern gate and out of the city by the eastern gate; to the east of the city was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makuṭabandhana</span>, the shrine of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span>, and there the body was cremated. For seven days those assembled at the ceremony held a festival in honour of the relics (DN.ii.160f).

It is said that the Buddha had three reasons for coming to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> to die:

- (1) Because it was the proper venue for the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mahā Sudassanasutta</span>;
- (2) because <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Subhadda</span> would visit him there and, after listening to his sermon, would develop meditation and become an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Arahant</span> while the Buddha was still alive; and
- (3) because the brahman <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Doṇa</span> would be there, after the Buddha’s death, to solve the problem of the distribution of his relics (Ud­a.402f; DN­a.ii.573f6).

As the scene of his death, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> became one of the four holy places declared by the Buddha to be fit places of pilgrimage for the pious, the other three being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhagayā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> (DN.ii.140). Mention is made of other visits paid to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> by the Buddha, prior to that when his death took place. Thus, once he went there from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āpaṇa</span> and having spent some time at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>, proceeded to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ātumā</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> were always great admirers of the Buddha, even though not all of them were his followers, and on the occasion of this visit they decided that any inhabitant of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> who failed to go and meet the Buddha and escort him to the city, would be fined five hundred. It was on this occasion that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Roja</span> the Mallan was converted and gave to the Buddha and the monks a supply of green vegetables and pastries (Vin.i.247f). During some of these visits the Buddha stayed in a wood called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Baliharaṇa</span>, and there he preached two of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārāsutta</span>s (AN.i.274f; v.79f) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kintisutta</span> (MN.ii.238f). A third <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārāsutta</span> he preached while staying at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upavattana</span>. (AN.ii.79; for another discourse to some noisy monks at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upavattana</span>, see Ud.iv.2).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> was the birthplace of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span> and his wife <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span> (Dhp­a.i.338, 349). It was twenty-five *yojanas* from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> (DN­a.ii.609; according to Faxian, p.40, it was twenty-four *yojanas* from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>) and lay on the high road from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aḷaka</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, the road taken by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span>’s disciples (Snp.v.1012).

This was evidently the road taken also by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span>, when he came to pay his last respects to the Buddha (Vin.ii.284).

According to a late tradition, one-eighth of the Buddha’s relics were deposited in a cairn in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> and honoured by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> (DN.ii.167; Bv.xxviii.3).

In ancient times <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> was the capital of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tālissara</span> and twelve of his descendants (Dpv.iii.32). It was also the scene of the death of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phussa Buddha</span> at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setārāma</span> (*v.l.* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇārāma</span>) (Bv­a.195; Bv.xix.25).

In Xuanzang’s day there still existed towers and *Saṅghārāmas* erected to mark the spots connected with the Buddha’s last days and obsequies at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>. According to his account <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> was nineteen *yojanas* from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> (Beal-Xuan. op.cit.li. lii. n).

To the northern Buddhists the place was also known as Kuśigrāma (Kuśigrāmaka) and Kuśinagarī (e.g., Divy.152f, 208).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> is identified with the village of Kasia at the junction of the river Rapti and the smaller Gondak and to the east of the Gorakhpur district (CAGI.i.493). A copper plate belonging to the *Thūpa* erected at the site of the Buddha’s death has recently been discovered (CAGI.i.714).

The people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> are called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosinārakā</span>, e.g., DN.ii.167.
