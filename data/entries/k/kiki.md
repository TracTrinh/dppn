# Kikī

## English source

Kikī. King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> at the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>. When the Buddha arrived in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, the king, having listened to his sermon, entertained the Buddha and his monks at the palace. When the Buddha was asked to spend the rainy season there he refused, as he had already accepted the invitation of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vehaliṅga</span>. Kikī was at first hurt by the refusal, but when the Buddha described <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span>’s virtues, the king was pleased and sent five hundred cartloads of provisions to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span> who, however, curtly refused the gift (DN.ii.7; MN.ii.49ff).

One of Kikī’s daughters was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uracchadā</span>, who attained *Arahant*-ship at the age of sixteen. He had seven other daughters – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samaṇī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samaṇā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Guttā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhikkhudāsikā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhammā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghadāsī</span> – who, in this Buddha­age became respectively <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uppalavaṇṇā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭācārā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotamā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammadinnā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Māyā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>. Ja.iv.481; in the Ap.ii.561f, the names are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samaṇī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samaṇaguttā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhikkhunī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhikkhadāyikā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammā</span>, etc., and they are mentioned as having lived celibate lives; see also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sattamba</span>; both the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> and the Thīg­a.17, 103f, omit the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Māyā</span> from this list and have, instead, the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā Kuṇḍalakesā</span>, identifying her with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhikkhadāyikā</span>. The Mvu.i.303f mentions another daughter <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mālinī Kisā Gotamī</span>.

He had also a son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭhavindhara</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puthuvindhara</span>), who succeeded him to the throne (Thag­a.i.151). During the life of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> Kikī waited on him with many kinds of gifts (Snp-a.i.281, 283), and at his death built one of the four gates outside the Buddha’s *cetiya*. The gate was a league in width (Snp-a.i.194). According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttara Commentary</span> (AN­a.i.420), Kikī was the *aggupaṭṭhāka* of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>.

In the Sanskrit books he is called Kṛkī, and is mentioned as owning a palace called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokanada</span> (e.g., Mvu.i.325; Divy.22f; Avś.i.338, etc.).
