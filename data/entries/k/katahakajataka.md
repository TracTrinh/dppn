# Kaṭāhakajātaka

## English source

Kaṭāhakajātaka (Ja 125). Once when the Bodhisatta was a rich treasurer in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> a son was born to him. A female slave in the house gave birth to a son on the same day. The boys grew up together, the slave’s son being called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭāhaka</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭāhaka</span> acquired various arts in the company of his master. When he grew up he was appointed as the Treasurer’s private secretary. One day he visited a merchant on the frontier, carrying a letter purporting to be from the Treasurer (in which he was stated to be the son of the latter), asking for the hand of the merchant’s daughter in marriage. The merchant was overjoyed, and the marriage took place. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭāhaka</span> gave himself great airs and spoke contemptuously of everything “provincial.” The Treasurer, discovering what had happened, decided to visit the merchant, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭāhaka</span> went to meet him on the way, and paying him all the honour due from a slave, begged him not to expose him. Meanwhile, he had misled his wife’s relations into the belief that the homage, paid by him to the Treasurer, was but the regard due from a son to his father. He was not like the sons of other parents, but knew what was due to his father. The Bodhisatta, being pleased, did not expose the slave, but on learning from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭāhaka</span>’s wife that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭāhaka</span> always complained of his food, he taught her a stanza which contained the threat – not intelligible to her, though clear to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭāhaka</span> – that if <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭāhaka</span> continued to make a nuisance of himself, the Treasurer would return and expose him. Thenceforth <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭāhaka</span> held his peace.

The story was related in reference to a monk who used to boast of his high lineage and the wealth of his family until his pretensions were exposed (Ja.i.451ff).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp­a.iii.357ff), the story was told in reference to a monk named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> who would complain, no matter what attentions were paid to him.
