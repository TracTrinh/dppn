# Kākajātaka 03

## English source

Kākajātaka 03. (Ja 395). The Bodhisatta was once a pigeon and lived in a net basket in the kitchen of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> merchant. A greedy crow, becoming intimate with him, came to live there. The cook discovered the crow trying to steal some food, and, pulling out his feathers, sprinkled him with flour, hung a chowrie round his neck and flung him into the basket.

The story closely resembles those of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapotajātaka</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lolajātaka</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), and is related in reference to a greedy monk (Ja.iii.314-16; see also Cunningham: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Bharhut Stūpa</span>, xlv. Pl.7).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapotajātaka</span> (Ja.i.241) makes reference to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kākajātaka</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nava-nipāta</span>. There is no such story in the Ninth Book; perhaps it is a wrong reading for the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavākajātaka</span> (Ja 434), where the story is also related with reference to a greedy monk.
