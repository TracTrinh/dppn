# Kasmīra

## English source

Kasmīra. A district in Northern India, the modern Kashmir. In the Pāḷi texts it is always mentioned with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhāra</span> and probably once formed part of that kingdom (see also PHAI., p.93. The Jātakas mention the countries separately as comprising two kingdoms ruled by a single king, e.g., Ja.iii.364, 378). At the end of the Third Council, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggaliputta</span> sent the Thera <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhantika</span> to propagate the religion in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kasmīra-Gandhāra</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhantika</span> quelled the power of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>-king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aravāḷa</span> (*q.v.*), who was a menace to the inhabitants, and converted him to the faith, while the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍaka</span> and his wife <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hāritā</span>, with their five hundred sons, became *Sotāpannas*. The Thera preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āsīvisūpamasutta</span> to the assembled multitude and won eighty thousand converts, while one hundred thousand persons entered the Saṅgha. We are told that from that time onwards the yellow robe was held in great esteem in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kasmīra</span>. (Mhv.xii.3, 9 ff; Dpv.viii.4; Vin-a.i.64ff; see also Beal-Xuan, op.cit., i.134, n.39). There was evidently a large community of monks at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kasmīra</span>, till long after the coming of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhantika</span>, for we are told that two hundred and eighty thousand monks, led by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttiṇṇa</span>, came from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kasmīra</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> on the occasion of the foundation ceremony of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> (Mhv.xxix.37).

In Xuanzang’s time <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kasmīra</span> seems to have been an independent kingdom whose king was given to serpent-worship while his queen was a follower of the Buddha. Near the capital was a *stūpa* which enshrined a tooth of the Buddha. This tooth was soon after taken away by Harṣavardhana of Kanoj. (CAGI.104ff; Beal-Xuan, i.116f, etc.)

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgala</span> is mentioned as being twelve leagues from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kasmīra</span> (Mil.82).
