# Kukkuṭavatī

## English source

Kukkuṭavatī. According to some accounts the city, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kappina</span> ruled before he became a monk, is called, not <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkuṭa</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkuṭavatī</span>. Perhaps the former was the name of the county and the latter that of its capital. From here to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> was a distance of one hundred and twenty leagues, and there was a trade route between the two cities which merchants travelled on foot (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jaṅghavānijā</span>), and also a route from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhimadesa</span>. Three rivers had to be crossed on the way: the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aravacchā</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nīlavāhanā</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candabhāgā</span>. Dhp­a.ii.116ff; a less detailed account is given in SN­a.ii.177f and AN­a.i.175f.
