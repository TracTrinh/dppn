# Kaṁsa 02

## English source

Kaṁsa 02. King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, and called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bārāṇasiggaha</span> because he was ruler of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Seyyajātaka</span> (Ja 282, Ja.ii.403), he was the king who was seized by the monarch of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>, owing to the treachery of a disloyal courtier, and who was later set free on account of his great piety. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekarājajātaka</span>, which purports to relate the same story, and again in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sīlavajātaka</span> (Ja 51), the king is referred to by other names. We probably have here a confusion of legends due to an effort to make three similar stories into one and the same.

It is probably this same <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṁsa</span> *Bārāṇasiggaha* who is referred to in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tesakuṇajātaka</span> (Ja 521), by the owl <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span> (Ja.v.112). There the scholiast explains *Bārāṇasiggaha* as *catūhi saṅgahavatthūhi Bārāṇasiṁ gahetvā vattanto*.
