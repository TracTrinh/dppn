# Koliyā

## English source

Koliyā, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyan</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyans</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyas</span>. One of the republican clans in the time of the Buddha. The Koḷiyā owned two chief settlements – one at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāmagāma</span> and the other at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadaha</span>. The Commentaries contain accounts of the origin of the Koḷiyas (DN­a.i.260f; Snp­a.i.356f; AN.ii.558; Thag­a.i.546; also Ap.i.94). We are told that a king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāma</span> (the Mvu.i.353 calls him Kola and explains from this the name of the Koḷiyas), suffered from leprosy, and being detested by the women of the court, he left the kingdom to his eldest son and retired into the forest. There, living on woodland leaves and fruits, he soon recovered, and, while wandering about, came across <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piyā</span>, the eldest of the five daughters of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Okkāka</span>, she herself being afflicted with leprosy. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāma</span>, having cured her, married her, and they begot thirty-two sons. With the help of the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, they built a town in the forest, removing a big <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kola</span>-tree in doing so. The city thereupon came to be called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kolanagara</span>, and because the site was discovered on a tiger-track (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vyagghapatha</span>) it was also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vyagghapajjā</span>. The descendants of the king were known as Koliyā.

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇālājātaka</span> (Ja.v.413), when the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> wished to abuse the Koliyans, they said that the Koliyans had once “lived like animals in a Kola-tree,” as their name signified. The territories of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> and the Koliyans were adjacent, separated by the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohiṇī</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Khattiyas</span> of both tribes intermarried, and both claimed relationship with the Buddha. (It is said that once the Koliyan youths carried away many <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> maidens while they were bathing, but the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span>, regarding the Koḷiyans as relatives, took no action; DN­a.i.262). A quarrel once arose between the two tribes regarding the right to the waters of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohiṇī</span>, which irrigated the land on both sides, and a bloody feud was averted only by the intervention of the Buddha. In gratitude, each tribe dedicated some of its young men to the membership of the Saṅgha, and during the Buddha’s stay in the neighbourhood, he lived alternately in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> and in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyanagara</span> (for details of this quarrel and its consequences see Ja.v.412ff; DN­a.ii.672ff; Dhp­a.iii.254ff).

Attached probably to the Koliyan central authorities, was a special body of officials, presumably police, who wore a distinguishing headdress with a drooping crest (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Lambacūḷakābhaṭā</span>). They bore a bad reputation for extortion and violence (SN.iv.341).
