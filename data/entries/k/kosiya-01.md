# Kosiya 01

## English source

Kosiya 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosiyagotta</span>. The name of a Brahmin clan. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pācittiya</span> it is given as one of the lower *gottas*. (Vin.iv.8; but it is also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s *gotta*, and is therefore generally regarded as a high one; see e.g., Thag.415; Thag­a.i.452).

Among those mentioned as belonging to this *gotta* are the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kevaṭṭa</span> (Ja.vi.418f), the Brahmin who was the father of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa Kumāra</span> (the Bodhisatta, Ja.v.319ff), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddākāpilāni</span>, born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgala</span> (AN­a.i.99; Thag­a.68; SN­a ii.144), and the banker who came to be known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Macchariya Kosiya</span> (*q.v.*).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kātiyāna</span>’s father was a Kosiyan, but he married a woman of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kātiyāna</span> family (Thag­a.i.452).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhūridattajātaka</span> (Ja 543, Ja.vi.181; Mhv.ii.49) mentions a sage Kosiya, who taught <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Alambāyana</span> the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>-spell. The scholiast says he belonged to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosiyagotta</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sālikedārajātaka</span> (Ja.iv.278f) mentions a Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sālindiya</span>, called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosiyagotta</span>, probably for the same reason.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosiyajātaka</span> (Ja 130, Ja.i.465f) speaks of a Kosiya *brāhmanī*. All these are either addressed or spoken of as Kosiya in their different contexts. The name Kosiya is also used twice in speaking of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> – once by the Buddha (DN.ii.270), once by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Moggallāna</span> (MN.i.252) – and again by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Guttila</span> (Ja.ii.252) and by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> (Ud.iii.7; Ud­a.200; Dhp­a.i.429).

The name means “belonging to the Kusika family.” It is once used of Indra in the Ṛg Veda, in what exact sense is not known. Rhys Davids suggests that perhaps we have here a survival from the time when Indra was only the god of a Kusika clan (Dial.ii.296f; see also Divy.632; Mhv.iii.200, 202, 315, 403).

The word Kosiya (e.g., Ja.ii.208) means “Owl” and is probably one of the several clan names which are also names of animals (cf. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaccha</span>).
