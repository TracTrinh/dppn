# Kalandakanivāpa

## English source

Kalandakanivāpa. A woodland in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span>. Here food (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nivāpa</span>) was regularly placed for the squirrels. It is said that once a certain <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">rājā</span> went there for a picnic and, having over-drunk, fell asleep. His retinue, seeing him sleeping, wandered away, looking for flowers and fruits. A snake, attracted by the smell of liquor, approached the king from a neighbouring tree-trunk, and would have bitten him had not a tree-sprite, assuming the form of a squirrel, awakened him by her chirping. In gratitude the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">rājā</span> gave orders that thenceforth the squirrels in that locality should be fed regularly. Ud­a.60; Snp-a.ii.419. According to some, it was the gift of a merchant named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalandaka</span> (Beal: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Romantic Legend of the Buddha</span>, p.315); Tibetan sources identify the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">rājā</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> and say that the snake was a reincarnation of the owner whose land the king had confiscated. According to these same sources the name is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalantaka</span> and is described as the name of a bird (Rockhill, p.43).

Kalandakanivāpa was evidently a favourite resort of the Buddha and his monks. See Veḷuvana.
