# Kokālika 01

## English source

Kokālika 01. (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokāliya</span>). A monk, one of the chief partisans of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>. Knowing <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>’s might, he was, at first, reluctant to join in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>’s plot against him, but later allowed himself to be persuaded on hearing the scheme explained (Vin.ii.196; iii.171). When the monks blamed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> for his misdeeds, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span> was always ready to defend him (Vin.iii.174). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>’s gains diminished, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span> went about praising him, his birth, accomplishments and holiness, and many believed him (Ja.ii.438f). He was a great friend of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thullanandā</span> (Vin.iv.335). We are told that once he expressed resentment because he had never been asked to recite the texts; so one day the monks gave him his chance. He ate his favourite soup, and at sundown, wearing a blue lower robe and an outer robe of white and carrying an elegantly carved fan, he appeared in the assembly. But when he tried to recite sweat poured from his body and he was utterly confused. Henceforth the monks knew that his claim to learning was but pretence (Ja.ii.65f).

Several Jātakas are related showing how, in previous births also, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span> had come to grief because of his fondness for talk and how he had been the accomplice of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>. He is identified with:

- the jackal in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Daddarajātaka</span> (Ja 172) (ii.65ff) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sīhakotthukajātaka</span> (Ja 188) (ii.108);
- the ass in the lion’s skin in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sīhacammajātaka</span> (Ja 189) (ii.110);
- the talkative tortoise in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kacchapajātaka</span> (Ja 178) (ii.175);
- the crow who praised the jackal (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Devadatta</span>) in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Jambukhādakajātaka</span> (Ja 294) (ii.438);
- the young cuckoo who lost his life because he sang, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kokālikajātaka</span> (Ja 331) (iii.102);
- the tawny-brown Brahmin in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Takkāriyajātaka</span> (Ja 481) (iv.242; but see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kokālika</span> 2);
- and the wicked deity in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Samuddavāṇijajātaka</span> (Ja 466) (iv.166).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (Snp­a.ii.473; AN­a.ii.850; SN­a.i.167) that this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span> was a Brahmin and a pupil of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>, and that he was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kokālika</span> to distinguish him from another <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span> who was similarly called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Kokālika</span> (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span> 2). There seems to be great confusions in the stories of these two men – if they were really two. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka Commentary</span>, for instance, the introductory stories of several of the Jātakas refer to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkāriyajātaka</span> for details of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span>, obviously having in mind <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>’s partisan; but the introductory story of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkāriyajātaka</span> is identical with that related elsewhere of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Kokālika</span>. See also Dhp­a.iv.91f, where the story of the talkative tortoise is related to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālikasutta</span> (Ja 331) which, according to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (Snp­a.ii.473) refers to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Kokālika</span>.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vyagghajātaka</span> (Ja 272, Ja.ii.356) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span> is mentioned as having tried to persuade <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> to go with him to his own country and as having been very angry when they refused. Possibly this story also refers to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Kokālika</span>. See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>.
