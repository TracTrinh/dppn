# Kāḷa Buddharakkhita

## English source

Kāḷa Buddharakkhita. A Thera of Ceylon, an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. He belonged to a minister’s family and was born in a village near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇagirivihāra</span>. When he came of age, he entered the Saṅgha, learned the whole of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Tipiṭaka</span>, and, on going with a large concourse to see his teacher, was asked to give up his following and go into solitude to practise meditation. He went to the Vātakasitapabbatavihāra, practised meditation and became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. While living in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyapabbata</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span>, he was once worshipping at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇṭakacetiya</span> and was seated at the foot of a Kāḷatimbara tree. (Legend says on the night of the new moon, thus completing the circle of Kāḷas). One of the monks asked him a question on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷakārāmasutta</span> and the Elder preached a sermon based on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span>. King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> (probably <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saddhātissa</span>), who was in the vicinity, came to listen to the sermon, which lasted throughout the night; the king remained standing the whole time. Greatly pleased, the king, at the end of the sermon, offered the sovereignty of Ceylon to the monk.

It is said that the Elder had been the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nigaṇṭha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Saccaka</span> in the time of the Buddha, and that the Buddha had preached to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Saccakasutta</span>, not because he could understand it then, but because the Buddha knew that it would help him to rise to eminence in this last life as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷa Buddharakkhita</span>. MN­a.i.469f.
