# Khujjuttarā

## English source

Khujjuttarā. She was born of a nurse in the house of the banker <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosita</span> (AN­a.i.232), and later became a slave of Queen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvati</span>. The queen gave her daily the eight pieces of money allowed to her by the king for the purchase of flowers. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khujjuttarā</span> bought flowers with four pieces from the gardener <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span>, the remaining four pieces she kept. One day the Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khujjuttarā</span>, having heard the Buddha preach to him, became a *Sotāpanna*. That day she spent the whole amount on flowers. The queen asked her how she had obtained so many, and she told her the whole story. From that time <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> showed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khujjuttarā</span> all honour, bathed her in perfumed water, and heard the Dhamma from her. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khujjuttarā</span> became, as it were, a mother to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>, and going regularly to hear the Dhamma, would return and preach it to her and her five hundred attendant women. Under the instruction of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khujjuttarā</span> they all became *Sotāpannas*. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> expressed a desire to see the Buddha, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khujjuttarā</span> suggested that she should pierce holes in the walls of the palace and gaze on the Buddha as he passed along the street. After the death of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khujjuttarā</span> seems to have spent all her time in religious works, listening to the preaching of the Dhamma. The Buddha declared her foremost among lay women by reason of her extensive knowledge (*bahussutānaṁ*). AN.i.26; Dhp­a.i.208ff; AN­a.i.226, 237f; Iti-a.23f.; Paṭis-a.498f.

Once, in the past, she was a serving-woman of the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, and one day, having seen a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> who was slightly hunch-backed, she threw a blanket over her shoulder, and bending down to look like a hunchback, she imitated the Buddha’s manner of walking. Therefore, in this present birth she herself was hunchbacked. On another occasion eight <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>, receiving their bowls filled with rice-porridge from the palace, found the bowls so hot that they were obliged to move them from one hand to the other. Seeing this, Khujjuttarā gave them eight ivory bracelets as stands for their bowls. It is said that these bracelets are still preserved in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandamūlapabbhāra</span>. Because of this act <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khujjuttarā</span> obtained profound wisdom in this birth, and was able to learn the *Tipiṭaka* by heart. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> she was the daughter of a treasurer, and had a friend who was a nun; one day when she was adorning herself at eventide the nun visited her, and as there was no servant-girl at the time Khujjuttarā asked the nun to do various things for her. As a result she was born as a slave.

Her desire to become chief among learned lay-women was formed in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>, on her seeing a similar rank bestowed on a lay-woman (Dhp­a.i.226f, etc.; Divy.339-41).

It is said that the discourses in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Itivuttaka</span> are those which Khujjuttarā learned from the Buddha and later repeated to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> and her attendant women. Because these discourses were all preached at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> and repeated there by her, there was no need to specify the place of their preaching; hence the formula “*Ekaṁ samayaṁ Bhagavā Kosambiyaṁ viharati*, at one time the Fortunate One was residing at Kosambī” is omitted, and instead is found “*vuttam h’etaṁ Bhagavātā arahatā*, this was said by the Bhagavā, the *Arahant*.” (Iti-a.32).

Khujjuttarā is several times mentioned as the paragon among lay-women disciples (e.g., AN.i.88; ii.164; iv.368; SN.ii.236), and in the Commentaries (e.g., DN­a.iii.910) she is given as an example of *kāmabhoginiyo* (women who enjoyed the pleasures of the senses). She possessed the *paṭisambhidā* while yet a householder, but it was the *paṭisambhidā* of the probationer (*sekha*) (Vsm.442; Vibh­a.388).

Khujjuttarā is identified with the slave-girl in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uragajātaka</span> (Ja 154, Ja.iii.168) and in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhisajātaka</span> (Ja 488, Ja.iv.314), the nurse in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Sutasomajātaka</span> (Ja 525, Ja.v.192) and the hunchback in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusajātaka</span> (Ja 531, Ja.v.312). Owing to her personal experience (*abhijānato*) she had the power of recalling her past births (Mil.78).

It is said (Ud­a.384) that when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> and her companions were burnt to death, Khujjuttarā escaped because she had not participated in their previous misdeeds. At the time of the fire she was absent from the palace, some say ten leagues away.
