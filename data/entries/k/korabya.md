# Korabya

## English source

Korabya, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koravya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Korabba</span>. Perhaps the generic name given to the king of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurūs</span> (cf. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span>). Once in the Jātakas <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koravya</span> is given as the name of the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indapatta</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuru</span> country, this king being the father of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sutasoma</span> (Ja.v.457). Elsewhere <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koravya</span> appears as a title of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya</span>, king of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurūs</span> (Ja.ii.368; iii.400, 402; v.59, 61, 65; vi.256, 268, 273). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koravya</span> may also have been used as an adjective, for we find it explained as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kururaṭṭha-vāsika</span> (e.g., Ja.vi.273). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koravya</span> king probably belonged to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yudhiṭṭhila</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gotta</span> (See Ja.iv.361). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (iii.369f) mentions a king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koravya</span> who owned a large banyan tree named Suppatiṭṭha. According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Raṭṭhapālasutta</span>, in the Buddha’s day, too, the ruler of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuru</span> was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koravyarājā</span>, and he owned a park which seems to have been called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migācīra</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) (MN.ii.65; see also Thag.776.ff; Thag­a.ii.34; for details see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Raṭṭhapāla</span>). This king was evidently interested in religious discussion. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thullakoṭṭhika</span> was his capital. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Avadānaśataka</span> speaks of a Kauravya of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thullakoṭṭhika</span> (i.67; ii.118; see also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Cambridge History of India</span>, i.121, which refers to a half-mythical <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcāla</span> king, Kraivya).
