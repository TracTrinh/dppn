# Kokālikasutta 01

## English source

Kokālikasutta 01. (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokāliyasutta</span>) (See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span> 2). The story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span> – according to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (Snp-a.ii.473), to be distinguished as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Kokālika</span>. It contains the verses preached by the Buddha to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span>. The verses describe the evil of back-biting and the terrors that await the back-biter after death. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span> contains twenty-two verses (657-78). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span> Commentary says (p.477f) that the last two stanzas are not explained in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Aṭṭhakathā</span>, and that therefore they did not belong to the original <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span>. Of the remaining twenty the last fourteen (663-76) are called by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Turitavatthugāthā</span>, and he says that they were uttered by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span> lay dying, by way of admonition, and that, according to others, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span> was the speaker. The first three stanzas (658-60) are, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (i.149), attributed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tudu</span>. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span>, also, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tudu</span> speaks them (v.171-4; the verses are also found in AN.ii.3 and in SN.i.149ff; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Netti</span>.132); but according to this version the Buddha repeats them.
