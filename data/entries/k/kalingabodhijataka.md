# Kāliṅgabodhijātaka

## English source

Kāliṅgabodhijātaka (Ja 479). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāliṅga</span> king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dantapura</span> had two sons, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kāliṅga</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Kāliṅga</span>. Soothsayers foretold that the younger would be an ascetic, but that his son would be a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>. Knowing of this prophecy, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Kāliṅga</span> became so arrogant that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kāliṅga</span>, on coming to the throne, ordered his arrest. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Kāliṅga</span> fled to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span> and lived there as an ascetic. Near his hermitage lived the king and queen of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span> who had fled with their daughter from their city of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgala</span>. Soothsayers had predicted that the princess’s son would be a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>, and all the kings of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> sought her hand. Her parents, not wishing to incur the enmity of any of the kings, fled with her from the city. One day a wreath of mango-flowers which the princess dropped into the river was picked up by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Kāliṅga</span>, who thereupon went in search of her. With her parents’ consent he married her, and to these two was born a son whom they called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāliṅga</span>.

When the stars revealed that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kāliṅga</span> had died, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāliṅga</span> was sent to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dantapura</span>, to a courtier who had been an ally of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Kāliṅga</span>. The prince’s identity having been duly established, he was crowned king, and his chaplain, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāliṅga Bhāradvāja</span>, taught him the duties of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>. On the fifteenth day after his coronation, the tokens of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span> king appeared before him. (For details see Ja.iv.232). One day while riding through the air with his retinue, he came to the Bodhi-tree under which Buddhas attain Awakening, and though he prodded his elephant until it died the animal found it impossible to fly over the spot. The royal chaplain investigated matters and reported his finding to the king who, having learnt from the chaplain of a Buddha’s virtues, paid great honour to the tree for seven days. See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samaṇakolañña</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāliṅga</span> is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāliṅga Bhāradvāja</span> with the Bodhisatta.

The story was related in reference to the Bodhi-tree planted, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>’s suggestion, by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>, at the entrance to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, in order that people might worship it while the Buddha was away on tour. As soon as a seedling was planted from the great Bodhi-tree at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā</span>, it grew into a tree fifty cubits high, and the Buddha consecrated it by spending one night under it, wrapt in meditation (Ja.iv.228-36).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāliṅgabodhijātaka</span> is found also in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Bodhivaṁsa</span> (Mbv.62ff); there it is given in much greater detail and differs in minor details from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> version, containing, among other things, a long description of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dibba-cakkhu</span> and the seven gems of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>.
