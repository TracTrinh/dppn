# Kapotakandara

## English source

Kapotakandara. A grotto, probably near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, where, later, a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span>, called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kapotakondaravihāra</span>, was built. The grotto was at one time the residence of a large number of pigeons (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kapotā</span>), hence the name (Ud­a.244). On one occasion <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, having recently shaved his head, was seated there wrapt in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">samādhi</span>, and a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>, passing overhead with his friend to an assembly of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span>, yielded, despite the warning of his friend, to the temptation to give a knock on the monk’s shining head. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> immediately fell down and was swallowed up in the fires of hell. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> had but a slight headache after the blow, which was, it is said, heavy enough to crush an elephant (Ud.39f; Thag.vs.998f; Paṭis-a.494).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visuddhimagga</span> (p.380), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> entered into a jhāna at the very moment the blow was dealt him.
