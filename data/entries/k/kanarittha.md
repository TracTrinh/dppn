# Kāṇāriṭṭha

## English source

Kāṇāriṭṭha. One of the four sons of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>-king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhataraṭṭha</span> and his queen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samuddajā</span>, the others being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudassana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhūridatta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhaga</span>. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāṇāriṭṭha</span> heard that his mother was a human, wishing to test her one day while drinking her milk, he assumed a serpent’s form and struck her foot with his tail. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samuddajā</span> threw him on to the ground with a shriek and accidentally struck his eye with her nail, thereby blinding him. Henceforth he, whose name had been <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariṭṭha</span>, was known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāṇāriṭṭha</span> (Ja.vi.168). When, after <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhūridatta</span>’s disappearance (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhūridattajātaka</span>) from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>-world, his brothers set out to search for him, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāṇāriṭṭha</span> was sent to the world of the gods, for he was so cruel by nature that they knew that if he went to the world of men he would destroy it by fire (Ja.vi.190). When, after his search, he returned to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>-world, he was appointed doorkeeper of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhūridatta</span>’s sick-room; there, seeing <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhaga</span> dragging a Brahmin roughly into the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>-world, he prevented him from ill-treating the man and told him of the greatness of all Brahmins, illustrating his words with various stories (Ja.vi.197; details see pp.200ff). It is said that in his immediately preceding birth, he had been a Brahmin, well-versed in sacrificial lore. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhūridatta</span> heard (from his bed) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariṭṭha</span>’s undue praise of Brahmins and refuted his statements (Ja.vi.205ff).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāṇāriṭṭha</span> is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunakkhatta</span> (Ja.vi.219).
