# Kassapa 19

## English source

Kassapa 19. Son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sena II</span>. The king gave him a special share of his own revenues and a share of the extraordinary revenues of the island (Cv.li.18, 20). Two wives of his are mentioned: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Senā</span> (Cv.li.18, 92). He became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Yuvarāja</span> under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa IV</span>. and ruled over <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇadesa</span> (Cv.lii.1), and, at the death of the king, he became ruler of Ceylon as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa V</span>. (probably 913-23 CE.) (Cv.lii.37ff). He is sometimes referred to as the son of the twice-consecrated queen (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dvayābhisekajāta</span>), his mother being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghā</span>, daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kittaggabodhi</span> (1) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devā</span>. In inscriptions <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> is referred to as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayasilāmeghavaṇṇa</span> (Cv.Trs.i.165, n.3). He was evidently a learned man, and a Sinhalese Commentary to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> is attributed to him (edited by DN. B. Jayatilaka, Colombo 1933). He had one wife, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajirā</span> (Cv.lii.62), a second, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devā</span> (Cv.lii.64), and a third, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājinī</span> (Cv.lii.67). He had a son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siddhattha</span>, who died young, and another, who was given the title of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakkasenāpati</span>. The latter led an expedition to help the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍu</span> king against the King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Coḷa</span>, but he died of plague in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Coḷa</span> (Cv.lii.72-8).
