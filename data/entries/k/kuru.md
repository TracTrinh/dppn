# Kuru

## English source

Kuru, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurū</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurus</span>. A country, one of the sixteen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janapadas</span> (DN.ii.200; AN.i.213 etc.). Frequent references to it are found in the Pāli Canon. It is said that Kuru was originally the name of the chieftains (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">rājakumārā</span>) of the country and that their territory was later named after them. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> records a tradition (DN­a.ii.481f; MN­a.i.184 etc.) which states that, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mandhātā</span> returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> from his sojourn in the four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Dīpas</span> and in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">devalokas</span>, there were in his retinue a large number of the people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span>. They settled down in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>, and their settlement was known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kururaṭṭha</span>. It had many towns and villages.

The country seems to have had very little political influence in the Buddha’s time, though, in the past, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuru</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kekaka</span> were evidently three of the most powerful kingdoms (See, e.g., Ja.ii.214). According to the Jātakas (e.g., Ja.v.57, 484; vi.255. Also Mvu.i.34; ii.419), the kingdom of Kuru was three hundred leagues in extent and its capital, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indapatta</span>, seven leagues in circumference. The ruling dynasty at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indapatta</span> belonged to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yudhiṭṭhila</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gotta</span> (Ja.iii.400; iv.361). Among the kings of the past, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya Koravya</span> is mentioned several times (Ja.ii.366; iii.400; iv.450; vi.260 etc.) and reference is also made to a king called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koravya</span> (Ja.iv.361; v.457) whose son was the Bodhisatta <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sutasoma</span>. During the Buddha’s time, also, the chieftain of Kuru was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koravya</span>, and his discussion with the Elder <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Raṭṭhapāla</span>, who was himself the scion of a noble family of the Kurus, is recounted in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Raṭṭhapālasutta</span> (MN.ii.65ff). Perhaps at one time the Kuru kingdom extended as far as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarapañcāla</span>, for in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Somanassajātaka</span> (Ja.iv.444), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarapañcāla</span> is mentioned as a town in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kururaṭṭha</span>, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Reṇu</span> as its king.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koravya</span> had a park called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migācīra</span> where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Raṭṭhapāla</span> took up his residence when he visited his parents (MN­a.ii.725). The people of Kuru had a reputation for deep wisdom and good health, and this reputation is mentioned (MN­a.i.184f; AN­a.ii.820; they were also probably reputed to be virtuous; see the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurudhammajātaka</span>) as the reason for the Buddha having delivered some of his most profound discourses to the Kurus, for example, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nidānasutta</span>, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Satipaṭṭhānasutta</span>. Among other discourses delivered in the Kuru country are the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyasutta</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anañjasappāyasutta</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sammosasutta</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariyavāsasutta</span>. All these were preached at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kammāsadhamma</span>, which is described as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nigama</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurus</span>, where the Buddha resided from time to time. Another town of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurus</span>, which we find mentioned, is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thullakoṭṭhika</span>, the birthplace of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Raṭṭhapāla</span>, and here the Buddha stayed during a tour (MN.ii.54; Thag­a.ii.30). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>’s queen, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span>, came from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuru</span> (Dhp­a.i.199), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggidatta</span>, chaplain to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> king, lived on the boundary between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuru</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, honoured by the inhabitants of all three kingdoms (Dhp­a.iii.242).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuru</span> country is generally identified as the district around Thānesar, with its capital <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indapatta</span>, near the modern Delhi (CAGI.379f). See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span>.
