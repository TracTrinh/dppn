# Kāḷabāhujātaka

## English source

Kāḷabāhujātaka (Ja 329). Once the Bodhisatta was a parrot named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rādha</span> and his brother was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Poṭṭhapāda</span>. They were captured by a fowler and brought to the court of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, where they were paid great attention. Later, a monkey, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷabāhu</span>, was added to the collection of animals, and the people in the palace lost interest in the parrots, much to the annoyance of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Poṭṭhapāda</span>. Soon, however, the children became frightened of the monkey’s tricks and he was sent away, just as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rādha</span> had prophesied to his brother.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Poṭṭhapāda</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷabāhu</span>.

The story was told in reference to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>’s attempt to kill the Buddha by letting loose <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālāgiri</span>. Ja.iii.97ff.
