# Kapilavatthu

## English source

Kapilavatthu. A city near the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span>, capital of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). It was founded by the sons of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Okkāka</span>, on the site of the hermitage of the sage <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapila</span> – see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapila</span> (3, Ja.i.15, 49, 50, 54, 64, etc.; see also Divy.548, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Buddhacarita</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">I</span>.v.2). Near the city was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Lumbinīvana</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) where the Buddha was born, and which became one of the four places of pilgrimage for the Buddhists. Close to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> flowed the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohiṇī</span>, which formed the boundary between the kingdoms of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyans</span> (Dhp­a.iii.254). In the sixth century B.C. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> was the centre of a republic, at the head of which was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span>. The administration and judicial business of the city and all other matters of importance were discussed and decided in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Santhāgārasālā</span> (DN.i.91; Ja.iv.145). It was here that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Viḍūḍabha</span> was received by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> (Ja.iv.146f). The walls of the city were eighteen cubits high (Ja.i.63; according to Mvu.ii.75 it had seven walls). From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> to the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anomā</span>, along the road taken by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>, when he left his home, was a distance of thirty <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojanas</span> (Ja.i.64). The city was sixty leagues from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, and the Buddha took two months covering this distance when he visited his ancestral home, in the first year after his Awakening. On this journey the Buddha was accompanied by twenty thousand monks, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷudāyī</span> went on ahead as harbinger. The Buddha and his company lived in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span> near the city and, in the midst of his kinsmen, as he did at the foot of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṇḍamba</span>, the Buddha performed the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamakapāṭihāriya</span> to convince them of his powers. (Ja.i.87ff; this journey to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> was one of the scenes depicted in the relic-chamber of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span>, Mhv.xxx.81).

On this occasion he preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantarajātaka</span>. The next day the Buddha went begging in the city to the great horror of his father, who, on being explained that such was the custom of all Buddhas, became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span> and invited the Buddha and his monks to the palace. After the meal the Buddha preached to the women of the palace who, with the exception of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulamātā</span>, had all come to hear him. At the end of the sermon, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sakadāgāmī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Pajāpatī</span> a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>. The Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulamātā</span> in her dwelling and preached to her the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candakinnarajātaka</span>. The next day <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span> was ordained, and seven days later <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span> (also Vin.i.82). As a result of the latter’s ordination, a rule was passed by the Buddha, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span>’s request, that no one should be ordained without the sanction of his parents, if they were alive. On the eighth day was preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Dhammapālajātaka</span>, and the king became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmī</span>. The Buddha returned soon after to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, stopping on the way at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anupiyā</span>, where the conversions of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhagu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbila</span> took place.

During the visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, eighty thousand <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> from eighty thousand families had joined the Buddhist Saṅgha (Vin.ii.180; Dhp­a.i.112; iv.124, etc.). According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhavaṁsa Commentary</span> (Bv­a.4; Bv.p.5f), it was during this visit that, at the request of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, the Buddha preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhavaṁsa</span>. It is not possible to ascertain how many visits in all were paid by the Buddha to his native city, but it may be gathered from various references that he went there several times; two visits, in addition to the first already mentioned, were considered particularly memorable. On one of these he arrived in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> to prevent the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyans</span>, both his kinsmen, from fighting each other over the question of their sharing the water of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohiṇī</span>; he appeared before them as they were preparing to slay each other, and convinced them of the futility of their wrath. On this occasion were preached the following Jātakas: the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phandana</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Daddabha</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Laṭukika</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rukkhadhamma</span>, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṭṭaka</span> – also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Attadaṇḍasutta</span>. Delighted by the intervention of the Buddha, the two tribes each gave him two hundred and fifty youths to enter his Saṅgha and, with these, he went on his alms rounds alternately to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> and to the capital of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyans</span> (Ja.v.412ff; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sammodamānajātaka</span> also seems to have been preached in reference to this quarrel, Ja.i.208). On this occasion he seems to have resided, not at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span>, but in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vana</span>.

The second visit of note was that paid by the Buddha when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Viḍūḍabha</span> (q.v), chagrined by the insult of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span>, invaded <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> in order to take his revenge. Three times <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Viḍūḍabha</span> came with his forces, and three times he found the Buddha seated on the outskirts of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, under a tree which gave him scarcely any shade; near by was a shady banyan-tree, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Viḍūḍabha</span>’s realm; on being invited by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Viḍūḍabha</span> to partake of its shade, the Buddha replied, “Let be, O king; the shade of my kindred keeps me cool.” Thus three times <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Viḍūḍabha</span> had to retire, his purpose unaccomplished; but the fourth time the Buddha, seeing the fate of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span>, did not interfere (Ja.iv.152).

The Buddha certainly paid other visits besides these to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>. On one such visit he preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇhajātaka</span> (Ja.iv.6ff). Various <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> went to see him both at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span> and at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vana</span>, among them being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> (SN.v.369f; AN.iii.284f; iv.220f; v.320f), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandiya</span> (SN.v.403f; 397f; AN.v.334f), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vappa</span> (AN.ii.196; MN.i.91), and perhaps <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sārakāṇi</span> (SN.v.372).

During one visit the Buddha was entrusted with the consecration of a new mote-hall, built by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span>; he preached far into the night in the new building, and, when weary, asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> to carry on while he slept. We are told that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> decorated the town with lights for a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojana</span> round, and stopped all noise while the Buddha was in the mote-hall (MN­a.ii.575). On this occasion was preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sekhasutta</span> (MN.i.353ff).

The books record a visit paid by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahma Sahampati</span> to the Buddha in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vana</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> (this appears, from the context, to have been quite close to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span>).

The Buddha, worried by the noisy behavior of some monks who had recently been admitted into the Saṅgha, was wondering how he could impress on them the nature of their calling. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> visited him and, being thus encouraged, the Buddha returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span> and there performed a miracle before the monks; seeing them impressed, he talked to them on the holy life (SN.iii.91f; Ud.25).

A curious incident is related in connection with a visit paid by the Buddha to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, when he went there after his rounds among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosalans</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> was asked to find a place of lodging for the night; he searched all through the town without success, and at length the Buddha was compelled to spend the night in the hermitage of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bharaṇḍu</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kālāma</span> (AN.i.276f). On another occasion we hear of the Buddha convalescing at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> after an illness (AN.i.219).

Not all the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> believed in their kinsman’s great powers, even after the Buddha’s performance of various miracles. We find, for instance, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Daṇḍapānī</span> meeting the Buddha in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vana</span> and, leaning on his staff, questioning him as to his tenets and his gospel. We are told that in answer to the Buddha’s explanations, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Daṇḍapānī</span> shook his head, waggled his tongue, and went away, still leaning on his staff, his brow puckered into three wrinkles (MN.i.108f.; this was the occasion for the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madhupiṇḍikasutta</span>).

Others were more convinced and patronised the Saṅgha – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷa Khemaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghatāya</span>, who built cells for monks in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span> (MN.iii.109. As a result of noticing these cells, the Buddha preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Suññātasutta</span>).

It is said that the Buddha ordained ten thousand householders of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> with the “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ehi-bhikkhu pabbajā</span>.” (Vin-a.i.241).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="119" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="120" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) was the Buddha’s most frequent visitor; to him was preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="121" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Dukkhakkhandhasutta</span> (MN.i.91f).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="122" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇāvibhaṅgasutta</span> was preached as the result of a visit to the Buddha by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="123" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Pajāpatī Gotamī</span>. Apart from those already mentioned, another <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="124" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> lady lived in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="125" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="126" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷigodhā</span> by name, and she was the only kinsman, with the exception of the Buddha’s father and wife, to be specially visited by the Buddha (SN.v.396).

The inhabitants of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="127" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> are called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="128" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kapilavatthavā</span> (e.g., SN.iv.182).

From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="129" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> lay a direct road to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="130" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> (Vin.ii.253), and through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="131" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> passed the road taken by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="132" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span>’s disciples from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="133" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aḷaka</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="134" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> (Sn.p.194).

From the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="135" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vana</span>, outside <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="136" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, the forest extended up to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="137" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span>, and on the other side of the city it reached as far as the sea (MN­a.i.449, Ud­a.184; Vin-a.ii.393).

It is significant that, in spite of the accounts given of the greatness of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="138" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, it was not mentioned by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="139" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> among the great cities, in one of which, in his opinion, the Buddha could more fittingly have died than in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="140" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> (DN.ii.146). After the Buddha’s death, a portion of the relics was claimed by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="141" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="142" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, and a shrine to hold them was erected in the city (DN.ii.167; Bv.xxviii.2). Here was deposited the rug (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="143" data-dppn-term-class="t18">paccattharaṇa</span>) used by the Buddha (Bv.xxviii.8).

In the northern books the city was called Kapilavastu, Kapilapura, and Kapilāvhayapura (E.g. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="144" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Lalitavistara</span> p.243, 28; The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="145" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Buddhacarita</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="146" data-dppn-term-class="t14">I</span>.v.2 calls it Kapilasyavastu). According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="147" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Dulva</span> (Rockhill, p.11), the city was on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="148" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhagīrathī</span>.

The identification of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="149" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> is not yet beyond the realm of conjecture. Xuanzang (Beal-Xuan.ii.,p.13f) visited the city and found it like a wilderness. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="150" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> inscriptions of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="151" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lumbinī</span> pillar and the Niglīva pillar are helpful in determining the site. Some identify the modern village of Piprāwā – famous for the vases found there – with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="152" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> (e.g., Fleet, JRAS.1906, p.180; CAGI.711f). Others, including Rhys Davids, say there were two cities, one ancient and the other modern, founded after <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="153" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Viḍūḍabha</span>’s conquest, and the ancient one they call Tilaura Kot. But the theory of two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="154" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> is rejected by some scholars. JRAS.1906, pp.453, 563. See also the article by Mukherji on Kapilavastu in ERE.
