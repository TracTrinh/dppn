# Kaṇḍula 01

## English source

Kaṇḍula 01. The state-elephant of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmiṇī</span>. He was of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chaddanta</span> race, and was left by his mother and discovered by a fisherman, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇḍula</span>, after whom he was named. Mhv.xxii.62f.

He grew up to be of great strength. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmiṇī</span>’s father died, his younger son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span>, took possession of the queen-mother and of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇḍula</span>, the state-elephant, and fled, but in the battle between the brothers, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇḍula</span> shook himself free from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> and went over to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmiṇī</span>, whom he served to the end of his life. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇḍula</span> took a prominent part in the campaign against the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Damiḷas</span>, distinguishing himself particularly in the siege of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijitapura</span> (Mhv.xxiv.15, 89). In the single combat between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Eḷāra</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmiṇī</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇḍula</span> attacked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Eḷāra</span>’s elephant, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Pabbata</span>, and disabled him (Mhv.xxv.5-83). It is said that once the warrior <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandhimitta</span> seized <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇḍula</span> by his tusks and forced him on to his haunches, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇḍula</span> nursed a grudge against him until <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandhimitta</span> rescued him from being crushed under a gate-tower which fell on him during his attack on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijitapura</span>. Mhv.xxv.22, 39f.; see also Dpv.xviii.53; Mbv.133.
