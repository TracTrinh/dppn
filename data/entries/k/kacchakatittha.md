# Kacchakatittha

## English source

Kacchakatittha (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kacchatittha</span>). A ford in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vālukagaṅgā</span>, near the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhūmarakkha</span> mountain. It was here that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍukābhaya</span> captured the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Yakkhinī</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyā</span> (Mhv.x.59). This was a strategic point in the wars with the Tamils, and we find <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kākavaṇṇatissa</span> entrusting its protection to his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghābhaya</span> (Mhv.xxiii.17). It is probable that, some time afterwards, the place fell into the hands of the Tamils, for we find <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> mentioned as having captured it from the Tamil general <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapisīsa</span> (Mhv.xxv.12). According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsaṭīkā</span> (322, 366) the place was nine leagues from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nimila</span> journeyed there and back in one day.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttara Commentary</span> (i.367) mentions that a man named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vācakāla</span> was once born there as a crocodile, a fathom in length, for having cast doubts on the efficacy of the Buddha’s religion. Once he swallowed sixty carts with the bulls attached to them, the carts being filled with stone.

The ford is now identified with Mahā Gantoṭa, the spot where the Ambaṅgaṅga and the Maha Veligaṅga meet (Geiger, Mhv.Trs., 72, n.2). The Ambaṅgaṅga was probably called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kacchakanadī</span>, and at the spot where it met the Maha Veligaṅga, King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subha</span> built the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandigāmaka</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span>. See Mhv.xxxv.58, and Mhv­ṭ.472; on this passage see also Geiger’s Trs., p.250, n.2; Mhv­ṭ.472.

See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assamaṇḍala</span>.
