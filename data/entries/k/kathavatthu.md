# Kathāvatthu

## English source

Kathāvatthu. The fifth of the seven books of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhammapiṭaka</span> (sometimes called the third, e.g., in Mbv.94). It seems to have been compiled when the contents of at least the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammasaṅgaṇī</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vibhaṅga</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭṭhāna</span> were already accepted as orthodox. Tradition ascribes its compilation to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggaliputtatissa</span> at the end of the Third Council, held under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s patronage; at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span> (Mhv.v.278; Dpv.vii.41, 56-8). It was rejected by some on the ground that it was set forth two hundred and eighteen years after the Buddha’s death, and was hence only a disciple’s utterance; but the Commentaries take the view that the *mātikā*, the principles taught therein, were laid down by the Teacher himself, and that the whole work should be regarded as the utterance of the Buddha, just as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madhupiṇḍikasutta</span>, preached really by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span>, is considered as the Buddha’s teaching. The book consists of twenty-three chapters, and is a collection of discussions (*kathā*) and refutations of the heretical views of various sects on matters connected with theology and philosophy. The Buddha’s authority is accepted as final. See the very valuable Preface to the *Points of Controversy*, by Mrs. Rhys Davids, vii ff See also Rhys Davids on “Questions discussed in the Kathāvatthu,” JRAS.1892.

It has sometimes been suggested (e.g., JRAS.1915, 805ff) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s Rock Edict <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">IX</span>. has been influenced by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kathāvatthu</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemā Therī</span>, chief of the Buddha’s women disciples, describes herself as being “*Kathāvatthuvisāradā*,” (Thīg­a.135) thus strengthening the theory that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kathāvatthu</span> was known already in the Buddha’s time.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> Commentary (Ud­a.94) refers to a *Kathāvatthūpakaraṇaṭīkā* for details of certain points raised.
