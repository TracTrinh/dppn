# Kanthaka

## English source

Kanthaka (*v.l.* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇṭhaka</span>). The horse on which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> left his father’s palace, accompanied by his attendant <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span>. It is said that when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kanthaka</span> was saddled for the journey, he realised the importance of the hour and neighed loudly for joy, but the gods muffled the sound of his neighing as also that of his footsteps as he galloped through the streets; ordinarily the sound of his neighing and galloping could be heard throughout <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>. He was eighteen cubits long from neck to tail and proportionately broad, quite white in colour, like a clean conch-shell.

In this journey of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> held on to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kanthaka</span>’s tail. The horse had the strength, had it been necessary, to clear the ramparts of the city, eighteen hands high, at one bound, with the prince and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> on his back. Just outside <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> the prince stopped the horse, in order to take a last look at the city. A *cetiya* was later erected on this spot and called *Kanthakanivattacetiya*. The horse travelled thirty leagues between midnight and the following morning, as far as the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anomā</span>. It is said that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kanthaka</span> could travel round the whole *cakkavāla* in one night. With one leap the horse cleared the river, which was eight fathoms wide. On arriving on the opposite bank, the Bodhisatta gave orders that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kanthaka</span> should be taken back to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kanthaka</span> kept looking back at his master, and when the Bodhisatta disappeared from view the horse died of a broken heart, and was reborn in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kanthaka</span> *Devaputta*. (Ja.i.62-5; Mvu.ii.159f., 165, 189, 190; Vibh­a.34, etc.; *Buddhacarita*, v.3, 68; vi.53ff).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kanthaka</span> was born on the same day as the Bodhisatta (Ja.i.54; Bv­a.106, 234, etc.). In heaven he had a magnificent palace of *veluriya* gems, which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> visited on one of his tours in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>. (Vv.73f;-Vv-a.311-18; see also Dhp­a.i.70; iii.195).
