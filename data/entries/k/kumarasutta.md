# Kumārasutta

## English source

Kumārasutta. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī Mahā Nāma</span> sees a number of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span> youths sitting by the Buddha, at the foot of a tree in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vana</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>; be expresses his joy at the sight for, he says, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> are usually so mischievous. The Buddha thereupon tells <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> of five things the practice of which will tend to progress: the tending of father and mother, the support of wife and children with well-gotten wealth, devotion to one’s own business, honouring one’s own ancestral gods, showing respect to good and holy men. AN.iii.75ff.
