# Kāraṇapālī

## English source

Kāraṇapālī. A Brahmin employed as superintendent of works by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>. One morning early he saw <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṅgiyānī</span> evidently returning from somewhere, and on inquiry learnt that he had been to see the Buddha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāraṇapālī</span> asked him what he thought of the Buddha, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṅgiyānī</span> sang his praises, illustrating his meaning with various figures of speech. Convinced by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṅgiyānī</span>’s earnestness, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāraṇapālī</span> knelt on the ground and expressed his homage to the Buddha (AN.iii.236ff).

The Commentary (AN­a.ii.636) says that the man’s name was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāla</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pālī</span>), and he was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāraṇapālī</span> because he supervised the business of various chieftains’ families.
