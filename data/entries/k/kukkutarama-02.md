# Kukkuṭārāma 02

## English source

Kukkuṭārāma 02. A pleasaunce in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span>. It was evidently the residence of monks from very early times, probably, for some time, of the Buddha himself. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vagga</span> (Vin.i.300) mentions the names of several Theras who lived there: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nilavāsi</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāṇavāsi</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gopaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhagu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phalikasandana</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (SN.v.15f; 171f) records several discussions which took place there between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhadda</span>. It may have been a favourite resort of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, for we find the householder <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dasama</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṭṭhakanagara</span> going there to enquire as to his whereabouts (AN.v.342; MN.i.349). It was also (probably at a later date) the residence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span> who converted King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Muṇḍa</span> (AN.iii.57f), and afterwards of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇaka</span>, the *upajjhāya* of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siggava</span>, and of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍavajji</span>, the teacher of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggaliputtatissa</span> (Mhv.v.122). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> mentions (MN­a.ii.571; AN­a.ii.866) that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkuṭārāma</span> was made by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkuṭa</span> *seṭṭhi*, but gives no further particulars. Here there is probably some confusion with the *ārāma* of the same name at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>. Xuanzang (Beal-Xuan: op.cit.ii, 95) says that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkuṭārāma</span> was to the southeast of the old city of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span> and was built by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> when he first became a convert to the Buddha’s religion. “It was a sort of first-fruit and a pattern of majestic construction.” Only the foundation of the building was left at the time of Xuanzang’s visit. It is probable that this account refers to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asokārāma</span> which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> built as the first of his Buddhist structures, and that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asokārāma</span> was constructed on the site of the old <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkuṭārāma</span>. It is significant that the Pāli books, in recording <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s doings, make no mention of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkuṭārāma</span> existing in his time, though the Sanskrit texts, the *Divyāvadāna*, for instance, makes frequent reference to it (e.g., pp.381f, 430ff; see also Smith: *Asoka*, 183, 193f). If the conjecture made above, namely that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asokārāma</span> replaced the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkuṭārāma</span>, be correct, it may have been that the place was known by both names in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s time.
