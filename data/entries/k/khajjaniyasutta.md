# Khajjanīyasutta

## English source

Khajjanīyasutta. The well-taught Ariyan disciple, remembering his past lives, realises how, in the past, he has been a prey to the body, feelings, perception, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t20">volitions</span> and consciousness; how he is still their prey, and will be so in the future, too, if he be enamoured of them. Thus realising, he conceives disgust for the body, etc., is repelled by them, and obtains release from them. He thereby attains freedom and becomes aware that he is free. SN.iii.86-91; this *Sutta* is wrongly titled <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīha</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta</span> text; see KS.iii.72, n.3.

The *Sutta* was also preached by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandana</span> grove on the fifth day of his visit to Ceylon (Mhv.xv.195).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vibhaṅga</span> Commentary (Vibh­a.32) the *Sutta* is referred to as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khajjanīyapariyāya</span>.
