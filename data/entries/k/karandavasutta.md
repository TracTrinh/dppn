# Kāraṇḍavasutta

## English source

Kāraṇḍavasutta. Once, while the Buddha was at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Campā</span> on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaggarā</span> Lake, a monk, charged by his colleagues with an offence, reviled them; the Buddha, hearing of it, insisted that the man should be expelled, lest the rest of the community should suffer by his presence. He illustrated his argument with various similes, among them that of the owner of a barley-field who, seeing among his corn a diseased plant (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yava-kāraṇḍava</span>) which failed to ripen, would uproot it and throw it away lest the other plants should be affected. AN.iv.168-72.
