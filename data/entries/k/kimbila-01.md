# Kimbila 01

## English source

Kimbila 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimila</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thera</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimmila</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thera</span>. Thera. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>. He was converted with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span> and four other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> nobles at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anupiyā</span>, shortly after the Buddha’s visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> (Vin.ii.182; Dhp­a.i.112f). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> Commentary says (Thag­a.i.235f; Thag.118, 155f. According to Dhp­a.i.117, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbila</span> became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> soon after ordination together with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhagu</span>; see also Ja.i.140 and AN­a.i.108) that while at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anupiyā</span> the Buddha, in order to arouse <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbila</span>, conjured up a beautiful woman in her prime and then showed her to him passing into old age. Greatly inspired, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbila</span> sought the Buddha, heard the Dhamma and, having entered the Saṅgha, in due course won <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbila</span> seems to have maintained throughout his early friendship with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span>, dwelling with him and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandiya</span>, now in this wood or park, now in that. The Buddha visited them at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pācīnavaṁsadāya</span> when he was going away, disgusted with the recalcitrant monks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> (Vin.i.350; Ja.iii.489; see also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upakkilesasutta</span>, MN.iii.155ff and Thag­a.i.275f). They were in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gosiṅgasālavana</span> when the Buddha preached to them the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Gosiṅgasutta</span>, at the conclusion of which, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Dīgha Parajana</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> sang the praises of all three (MN.i.205ff). Their number was increased by the presence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhagu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇḍadhāna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, on the occasion when the Buddha preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Naḷakapānasutta</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Palāsavana</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Naḷakapāna</span> (MN.i.462ff).

In three different places in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (AN.iii.247; 339; iv.84) record is made of a conversation between Kimbila and the Buddha, when Kimbila asks how the Dhamma could be made to endure long after the Buddha’s death and what were the causes which might bring about its early disappearance. The conversation took place in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Niceḷuvana</span>?) in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbilā</span>. According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttara Commentary</span> (AN­a.ii.642), however, it would appear that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbila</span> mentioned here was not Kimbila the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> but another. We are told that this Kimbila was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhiputta</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbilā</span>. He joined the Saṅgha and acquired the power of knowing his previous births. He recollected how he had been a monk at the time when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>’s religion was falling into decay, and seeing how the Faith was neglected by its followers, he made a stairway up a cliff and lived there as a recluse. It was this memory of his previous life which prompted Kimbila’s question.

Elsewhere (SN.v.322f) the Buddha is reported as questioning Kimbila at the same spot on the question of breathing. Kimbila remains silent though the question is put three times. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> intervenes and suggests that the Buddha should himself furnish the answer so that the monks may learn it and profit thereby.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakusandha Buddha</span>, Kimbila had been a householder; after the Buddha’s death he erected a pavilion of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t18">salala</span>-garlands round his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span> (Thag­a.i.235). He is probably to be identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Salaḷamaṇḍapiya Thera</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (Ap.i.333).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp­a.i.115; iv.126) mentions a story which shows how delicately nurtured <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbila</span> was. One day, in a discussion with his friends, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span>, as to where rice came from, Kimbila remarked that it came from the granary (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t18">koṭṭhe</span>).
