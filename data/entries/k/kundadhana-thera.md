# Kuṇḍadhāna Thera

## English source

Kuṇḍadhāna Thera (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">v.l.</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇḍadhāna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇṭhadhāna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuḍḍadhāna</span>). An <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. He was proclaimed the first among those who received food tickets (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">salāka</span>) (AN.i.24). He came of a Brahmin family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> and his name was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhāna</span>. He knew the Vedas by heart, and when advanced in years, heard the Buddha preach and joined the Saṅgha. From that day, however, in all his movements the form of a young woman followed him wherever he went, though he himself could not see the figure. This caused great merriment and evoked many sarcastic remarks, which he could not understand. When he went for alms, women would put into his bowl two portions of food, saying, “One is for your Reverence and the other for your friend, your companion, the young lady.” In the monastery the novices and young monks would point at him and say: “Look, our venerable one has become a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">koṇḍa</span>” (gallant?). From this he became known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇḍadhāna</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇḍadhāna</span>. Driven to distraction by this teasing, he became abusive and was reported to the Buddha, who bade him be patient as he was only being pursued by the remnant of an evil <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kamma</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>, hearing of Kuṇḍadhāna, was interested, and being satisfied by personal investigation that the Elder was blameless, provided him with all necessaries, so that he need no longer go round for alms. This enabled him to concentrate his mind, and he became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. Thereupon the figure of the woman disappeared.

Kuṇḍadhāna’s claim to be the first among receivers of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">salāka</span> was due to the fact that he it was who received the first food-ticket when the Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Subhaddā</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugganagara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Subhaddā</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span>, and also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunāparanta</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">janapada</span>. Only <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">khīṇāsavas</span> were allowed to accompany the Buddha on these visits.

Kuṇḍadhāna’s determination to attain this special eminence was formed in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>. Once he gave <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span> a well-ripened “comb” of bananas when the Buddha arose from a long jhāna. As a result he became king of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> eleven times and king of men twenty-four times.

He was an earthbound sprite in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>. Seeing two monks, firm friends, on their way to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">uposatha</span> held by the Buddha, he had a mischievous desire to test their friendship, and when one of the monks retired into the forest leaving the other on the road, he followed the former, unseen by him, assuming the form of a woman arranging her hair, adjusting her garments, and so on. The second monk, seeing his friend return and shocked by his apparent misdemeanour, left him in disgust, refusing to perform the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">uposatha</span> with him. Realising the effect of his practical joke, the sprite did all he could to make amends, but the friendship of the two monks was for ever spoilt. The sprite suffered the fears of hell for a whole Buddha-era, and even in his last birth as Kuṇḍadhāna his evil <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kamma</span> pursued him, as seen above. AN­a.i.146ff; Thag.A.i.62ff; also Ap.i.81f; Thag.15. The version given in Dhp-a.iii.52f differs very slightly in certain details.

Kuṇḍadhāna was among those to whom the Buddha preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Naḷakapānasutta</span>, and was therefore probably a friend of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> and the other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> nobles present on that occasion (MN.i.462).
