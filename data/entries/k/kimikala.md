# Kimikālā

## English source

Kimikālā <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">v.l.</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kipillikā</span>. A river near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jantugāma</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meghiya</span> Thera was attracted by a mango-grove on its banks and asked the Buddha’s permission to meditate there. Twice the Buddha refused; the third time he gave his permission. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meghiya</span> went, but soon returned, finding that he could not concentrate his mind there. The river was evidently in the neighbourhood of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cālikā</span> (AN.iv.354f; Thag­a.i.149; Ud.iv.1).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> Commentary (Ud­a.217), it was so-called because it abounded in black worms (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kālakimīnaṁ bahulatāya</span>).
