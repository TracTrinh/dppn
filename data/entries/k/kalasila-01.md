# Kāḷasilā 01

## English source

Kāḷasilā 01. The black rock by the side of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isigili</span> (Isigilipasse).

It was there that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> was murdered (Ja.v.125f; Dhp­a.iii.65f; Ap­a.i.206) and that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godhika</span> (SN.i.120f; Dhp­a.i.431f) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vakkali</span> (SN.iii.124) committed suicide.

It was a lonely spot, and we are told that when monks came from afar to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> they would ask <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba Mallaputta</span> to find them lodgings there because they wished to see evidence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba</span>’s *iddhi*-powers (Vin.ii.76; iii.159).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> is mentioned as having stayed at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷasilā</span> with a great company of monks (SN.i.194). On one such occasion the Buddha is said to have given <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> an opportunity of asking him to continue to live for a whole aeon. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> failed to do so (DN.ii.116).

The Buddha is elsewhere (Thag­a.ii.209) described as residing at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷasilā</span> with five hundred monks, all of whom were *Arahants*. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> discovered their powers, and thus earned the praise of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅgīsa</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta Commentary</span> (SN­a.ii.229) speaks of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷasilāvihāra</span>.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Dukkhakkhandhasutta</span> (MN.i.92), it is said that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷasilā</span> was also the residence of some <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span>, followers of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāthaputta</span>.
