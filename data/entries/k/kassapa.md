# Kassapa

## English source

Kassapa 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>. Also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Dasabala</span> to distinguish him from other Kassapas. The twenty-fourth Buddha, the third of the present aeon (the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddakappa</span>) and one of the seven Buddhas mentioned in the Canon (DN.ii.7). He was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, in the Deer Park at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>, of Brahmin parents, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanavatī</span>, belonging to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapagotta</span>. For two thousand years he lived in the household, in three different palaces, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirinanda</span> (the Bv­a.217 calls the first two palaces <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsavā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasavā</span>). He had as chief wife <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunandā</span>, by whom he begot a son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijitasena</span>. Kassapa left the world, traveling in his palace (*pāsāda*), and practiced austerities for only seven days. Just before his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> his wife gave him a meal of milk-rice, and a *yavapāla* named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soma</span> gave him grass for his seat. His *bodhi* was a banyan-tree, and he preached his first sermon at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> to a crore of monks who had renounced the world in his company. He performed the Twin-Miracle at the foot of an *asana*-tree outside <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sundaranagara</span>. He held only one assembly of his disciples; among his most famous conversions was that of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Naradeva</span> (*q.v.*). His chief disciples were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhāradvāja</span> among monks, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anulā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span> among nuns, his constant attendant being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabbamitta</span>. Among his patrons, the most eminent were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumaṅgala</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijitasenā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā</span>. His body was twenty cubits high, and, after having lived for twenty thousand years, he died in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setavya</span> pleasance at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setavyā</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsī</span>. Over his relics was raised a *Thūpa* one league in height, each brick of which was worth one crore. It is said (MN­a.i.336ff) that there was a great difference of opinion as to what should be the size of the *Thūpa* and of what material it should be constructed; when these points were finally settled and the work of building had started, the citizens found they had not enough money to complete it. Then an *Anāgāmī* devotee, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sorata</span>, went all over <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>, enlisting the help of the people for the building of the *Thūpa*. He sent the money as he received it, and on hearing that the work was completed, he set out to go and worship the *Thūpa*; but he was seized by robbers and killed in the forest, which later came to be known as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhavana</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upavāṇa</span>, in a previous birth, became the guardian deity of the *cetiya*, hence his great majesty in his last life (DN­a.ii.580; for another story of the building of the shrine see Dhp­a.iii.29).

Among the thirty-seven goddesses noticed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Guttila</span>, when he visited heaven, was one who had offered a scented five-spray at the *cetiya* (Ja.ii.256). So did <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Alāta</span> offer *āneja*-flowers and obtain a happy rebirth (Ja.vi.227).

The cause of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span>’s golden complexion was his gift of a golden brick to the building of Kassapa’s shrine (AN­a.i.116).

At the same *cetiya*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span>, who was then a householder in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, offered butter and molasses in bowls of brass, which were placed without any interval around the *cetiya* (AN­a.i.105).

Among those who attained *Arahant*-ship under Kassapa is mentioned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gavesī</span>, who, with his five hundred followers, strove always to excel themselves until they attained their goal (AN.iii.214ff).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kappina</span>, then a clansman, built, for Kassapa’s monks, a *pariveṇa* with one thousand cells (AN­a.i.175).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bakkula</span>’s admirable health and great longevity were due to the fact that he had given the first fruits of his harvest to Kassapa’s monks (MN­a.iii.932).

During the time of Kassapa Buddha, the Bodhisatta was a Brahmin youth named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotipāla</span> who, afterwards, coming under the influence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span>, became a monk. (Bv.xxv.; Bv­a.217ff; DN.ii.7; Ja.i.43, 94; DN.iii.196; Mvu.i.303ff, 319). This <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span> was later born in the *Brahma*-world and visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>, after his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> then reminded him of this past friendship, which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span> seemed too modest to mention (SN.i.34f).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhimanikāya</span> (MN.ii.45f) gives details of the earnestness with which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span> worked for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotipāla</span>’s conversion when Kassapa was living at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vehaliṅga</span>. The same *Sutta* bears evidence of the great regard Kassapa had for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span>.

The king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> at the time of Kassapa was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kikī</span>, and the four gateways of Kassapa’s *cetiya* were built, one by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kikī</span>, one by his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭhavindhara</span>, one by his ministers led by his general, and the last by his subjects with the treasurer at their head (Snp-a.i.194).

It is said that the Buddha’s chief disciple, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span>, was born on the same day as Kassapa and that they were friends from birth. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> left the world earlier and became an ascetic. When he visited the Buddha after his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>, he was greatly grieved to learn that the Buddha ate meat (*āmagandha*), and the Buddha preached to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āmagandhasutta</span>, by which he was converted (Snp-a.i.280ff).

The Ceylon Chronicles mention a visit paid by Kassapa to Ceylon in order to stop a war between King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayanta</span> and his younger brother (Mhv.xv.128ff; Vin-a.i.87; Dpv.xv.55ff; Mbv.129). The island was then known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇḍadīpa</span>, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visāla</span> as capital. The Buddha came with twenty thousand disciples and stood on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhakūṭa</span>, and the armies seeing him stopped the fight. In gratitude, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayanta</span> presented to the Buddha with the Mahā Sāgara garden, in which was afterwards planted a branch of the Bodhi-tree brought over by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhammā</span>, in accordance with the Buddha’s wish. The Buddha preached at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asokamālaka</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudassanamālaka</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Somanassamālaka</span>, and gave his rain-cloak as a relic to the new converts, for whose spiritual guidance he left behind his disciples <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabbananda</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhammā</span> and their followers. In Kassapa’s time Mt. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepulla</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> was known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Supassa</span> and its inhabitants as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppiyas</span> (SN.ii.192).

But many other places had the same names in the time of Kassapa as they had in the present age – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span> (Ja.vi.122), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> (Ja.vi.123), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbila</span> (Ja.vi.121) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bārāṇasī</span>. (Ja.vi.120).

Besides the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āmagandhasutta</span> mentioned above, various other teachings are mentioned as having been first promulgated by Kassapa and handed on down to the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> and re-taught by him. Such, for instance, are the questions (*pucchā*) of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiya</span> and the stanzas taught to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sutasoma</span> by the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> (Ja.v.476f; 453). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mittavindajātaka</span> (Ja 104) is mentioned as belonging to the days of Kassapa Buddha (Ja.i.413).

Mention is also made of doctrines which had been taught by Kassapa but forgotten later, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> is asked by those who had heard faint echoes of them to revive them (e.g., MN­a, i.107, 528; AN­a.i.423). A sermon attributed to Kassapa, when he once visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> with twenty thousand monks, is included in the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍita</span> *Sāmaṇera* (Dhp­a.ii.127ff). It was on this occasion that Kassapa accepted alms from the beggar <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Duggata</span> in preference to those offered by the king and the nobles.

Kassapa held the *uposatha* only once in six months (Dhp­a.iii.236).

Between the times of Kassapa and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> the surface of the earth grew enough to cover <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sūkarakatalena</span> (MN­a.ii.677).

The records of Chinese pilgrims contain numerous references to places connected with Kassapa. Xuanzang speaks of a *stūpa* containing the relics of the whole body of the Buddha, to the north of the town, near Śrāvasti, where, according to him, Kassapa was born (Beal-Xuan, op.cit., ii.13). Mention is also made of a footprint of Kassapa (ibid.i., Introd. ciii). Stories of Kassapa are also found in the *Divyāvadāna* (e.g., pp.22f; 344f; 346f; see also *Mahāvastu*, e.g., Mvu.i.59, Mvu.303f).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp-a.iii.250f) contains a story, which seems to indicate that, near the village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Todeyya</span>, there was a shrine thought to be that of Kassapa and held in high honour by the inhabitants of the village. After the disappearance of Kassapa’s *Sāsana*, a class of monks called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setavatthasamaṇavaṁsa</span> (“white-robed recluses”) tried to resuscitate it, but without success (Vibh­a.432).

## Vietnamese translation

Chưa dịch.

## Notes
