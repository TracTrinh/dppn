# Kumāra Kassapa 01

## English source

Kumāra Kassapa 01. A *Thera* He was foremost among those who had the gift of varied and versatile discourse (*cittakathikānaṁ*) (AN.i.24). His mother was the daughter of a banker of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, and she, having failed to obtain her parent’s consent to become a nun, married and, with her husband’s consent, joined the Saṅgha, not knowing that she was with child. When her condition was discovered her colleagues consulted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>, who declared that she was no true nun. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>, on being consulted, entrusted the matter to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli</span>, who had it fully investigated by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> and other residents of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, and he gave his finding in the assembly, in the presence of the king, that the nun was innocent. (For details see Ja.i.148; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli</span>’s handling of the case won the Buddha’s special commendation, see, e.g., AN­a.i.172). When the boy was born the king reared him, and the boy was ordained at the age of seven. The boy came to be called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumāra</span>, because he joined the Saṅgha so young and was of royal upbringing, and also because the Buddha, when sending him little delicacies such as fruit, referred to him as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumāra Kassapa</span>. Once when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumāra Kassapa</span> was meditating in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhavana</span>, an *Anāgāmī Brahma*, who had been his companion in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>, appeared before him, and asked him fifteen questions which only the Buddha could answer. This led to the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vammikasutta</span> (MN.i.143ff), and after dwelling on its teachings <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> became an *Arahant*. (For <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumāra Kassapa</span>’s story see Ja.i.147ff; AN­a.i.158f; Thag­a.i.322f; MN­a.i.335f). His mother, too, developed insight and attained to *Arahant*-ship. It is said that she wept for twelve years because she could not be with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>, and one day, seeing him in the street, as she ran towards him and fell, milk flowed from her breasts and wet her robe. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>, realising that her great love was standing in the way of her attainments, spoke harshly to her that she might love him the less. The ruse succeeded and she became an *Arahant* that very day (Dhp­a.iii.147).

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha Kassapa</span> was a learned Brahmin, and having heard a monk ranked foremost in eloquence, he wished for a similar distinction and did many acts of piety towards that end. When the teachings of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> were being forgotten, he, together with six others, entered the Saṅgha and lived a life of rigorous asceticism on the summit of a mountain. (Ap.ii.473f; the details of this story are given in Dhp­a.ii.210-12; among <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>’s companions were also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dārucīriya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba Mallaputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiya</span>; see also Ud­a.80f).

Two verses of deep significance ascribed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumāra Kassapa</span> are found in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> (vv.201.202). Although it is said that he was a very eloquent speaker, the examples given of his preaching are extremely scanty. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttara Commentary</span> (i.159) states that the Buddha gave him his title from the skilful way in which he argued with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāyāsi</span>, as related in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāyāsisutta</span>; but this cannot be correct for, according to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapāla</span> (Vv-a.297), the events of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāyāsisutta</span> took place after the Buddha’s death. The *Sutta*, however, does justify <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>’s reputation. (For his praises see also MN­a.i.500f).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>’s *upasampadā* took place in his twentieth year. A doubt arose as to whether this was valid because, according to the rule, twenty years must be completed for *upasampadā*. The Buddha held that in reckoning the age the time spent in the mother’s womb could also be included. Vin.i.93; Vin-a.iv.867.
