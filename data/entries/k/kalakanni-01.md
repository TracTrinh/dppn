# Kāḷakaṇṇī 01

## English source

Kāḷakaṇṇī 01. A friend of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>. They had made mud-pies together and had gone to the same school. Later, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷakaṇṇī</span> fell on evil days and sought the protection of his friend, who appointed him to look after his business. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>’s friends and acquaintances remonstrated against the employment of a man with so inauspicious a name, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span> heeded them not. One day, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span> was away, a gang of robbers tried to enter his house, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷakaṇṇī</span>, with great presence of mind, asked the few remaining servants to beat drums all over the house, thereby giving the impression that the house was fully occupied. The robbers fled leaving their weapons, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷakaṇṇī</span> was greatly praised. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span> reported the matter to the Buddha, the Buddha related the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷakaṇṇījātaka</span>, containing a similar story of the past. Ja.i.364f.
