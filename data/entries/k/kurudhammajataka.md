# Kurudhammajātaka

## English source

Kurudhammajātaka (Ja 276). The Bodhisatta was once born as the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya</span>, king of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurūs</span>, and, after his father’s death, reigned in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indapatta</span>. He observed the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurudhamma</span> – that is to say, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pañcasīla</span> – as did the queen-mother, his queen-consort, the viceroy, the chaplain, the king’s driver, his charioteer, the treasurer, the keeper of the royal granaries, the palace porter and the courtesan of the city. The country thus became very prosperous and its people happy. In the kingdom of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaliṅga</span> there was a drought and consequent scarcity of food. The king, acting on the advice of his ministers, sent Brahmins to beg from the Bodhisatta the loan of his state elephant, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Añjanavasabha</span>, who was reported to bring rain. The elephant was lent willingly but no rain fell. It was thereupon decided that the prosperity of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurus</span> was due to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurudhamma</span> observed by the king and the others, and messengers were despatched to find out what these Kurudhammas were. From the king down to the courtesan, all had rigorously kept them, but each had unwittingly done something which he or she considered a violation of the Dhamma. The messengers, therefore, had to visit each one and take down a list of the Dhamma. The incidents related by each to the messengers, explaining wherein they had transgressed the dhammas, only served to emphasise how scrupulously they had conducted themselves.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaliṅga</span> king practised the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurudhamma</span> and rain fell in his country.

The story was told in reference to a monk who had killed a wild goose. Two monks bathed in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aciravatī</span>, and while standing on the bank, drying, they saw two geese appear. The monks took a bet as to which should hit the goose in the eye, and one of them threw a stone which pierced one eye and came out of the other. The monk was reported to the Buddha. Ja.ii.365ff; Dhp­a.iv.86ff; cp. Cariyāpiṭaka i.3.

With the introductory story compare that of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sālittakajātaka</span> (Ja 107, Ja.i.418).
