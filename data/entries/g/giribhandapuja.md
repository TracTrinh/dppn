# Giribhaṇḍapūjā

## English source

Giribhaṇḍapūjā, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Giribhaṇḍavāhanapūjā</span>. A great festival, instituted by King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Dāṭhika Mahā Nāga</span> on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyagiri</span> mountain. Carpets were laid from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kadambanadī</span> to the mountain, in order that people might approach the mountain with clean feet; the road was decorated and illuminated, shops were erected and largesse distributed. There were mimes, songs and music. Lamps were lit throughout the island and even on the sea for one <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojana</span> round (Mhv.xxxiv.75ff; AN­a.i.13). It is said that on the day of the festival <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>, wishing to spoil it, rained down a shower of coal, but an Elder created earth in the sky, thus preventing the coal from falling (Vsm.376; Vsm.Trs.ii.436, n.4). The most costly offerings given during this feast to any monk – namely, a pair of garments – fell to the lot of a young novice, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Loṇagirivāsītissa</span>, in spite of the efforts of the king’s ministers to get them into the hands of the older monks. It was because the novice had practised the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sārāṇīyadhammā</span> (DN­a.ii.535; AN­a.ii.653f; MN­a.i.545f).

The festival was probably connected with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Giribhaṇḍavihāra</span>, in which case that was the reason for the name.
