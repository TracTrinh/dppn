# Ghosakaseṭṭhi

## English source

Ghosakaseṭṭhi (*v.l.* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghositaseṭṭhi</span>). A *seṭṭhi* of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>. Being born as the son of a courtesan, he is cast away on a refuse heap. A passer-by takes him home, but the Treasurer of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>, knowing from an astrologer that the stars showed the birth of a very lucky boy, seeks him out and adopts him. A few days after, the Treasurer’s wife bears him a son, and he therefore plans to kill <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span> with the help of a slave woman, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷī</span>. All his attempts having failed, he promises a potter one thousand pieces if he will kill the boy. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosita</span> is sent to the potter with a message; on the way he meets his foster-brother, and gives him the message, promising to win for him a game of marbles. The foster-brother goes to the potter and is killed. The Treasurer then sends <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span> to the superintendent of his hundred villages with a letter ordering that he be killed. The letter is fastened to the boy’s garment. On the way he stops for a meal at the house of a country-treasurer whose beautiful daughter falls in love with him. Discovering the letter, she substitutes another to the effect that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span> should be married to her with great festivity and that a two-storied house should be built for them. The superintendent carries out these orders and the Treasurer falls ill on receiving the news. He is visited on his death-bed by Ghosaka and his wife, and while trying with his dying breath to say “I do not give him my wealth,” by a slip of the tongue he says “I do.” Ghosaka becomes a very pious man and is made the Treasurer of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>. Later he meets <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>, daughter of his friend <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddavatiya</span>, adopts her as his daughter and, when the time comes, gives her in marriage to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>.

In a past life <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span> had been <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kotūhalaka</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Addilaraṭṭha</span>, but left there with his wife and child on account of great poverty. On the way he cast off the child on account of its being too heavy, but rescued it later in answer to his wife’s importunities. It was as a result of that act that he was cast away in this birth. Later he was born as a dog and then as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span> *Devaputta* (Dhp­a.i.169ff; Paṭis-a.504ff) (*q.v.*).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span> had two colleagues in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkuṭa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pavāriya</span>. For a number of years they entertained five hundred ascetics from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span>, during the rainy season, until one year the ascetics, hearing from a tree-sprite, who had been one of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>’s labourers, of the arising of the Buddha, informed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span> and his friends of their determination to see the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>. The ascetics went on ahead, followed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span> and the others, bearing all kinds of gifts. They all heard the Buddha preach, became *Sotāpannas*, and invited the Buddha to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>. On the invitation being accepted, they built residences for the Buddha and the monks at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>, that built by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span> being called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghositārāma</span> (Dhp­a.i.203ff; AN­a.i.234f.; MN­a.i.539f; Paṭis-a.414, etc.).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mitta</span> (Dhp­a.i.189) was the householder in charge of the refectory from which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span> had food daily distributed to the needy, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span>’s gardener (Dhp­a.i.208).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span> is mentioned as an example of a man possessing *puññiddhi*. He could not have been killed even if stabbed in seven places (Bv­a.24).

See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghositasutta</span>.
