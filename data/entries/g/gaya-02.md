# Gayā 02

## English source

Gayā 02. A town in India. It lay on the road between the Bodhi-tree and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, and was three *gāvutas* from the Bodhi-tree and fifteen *yojanas* from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. (MN­a.i.387f; Faxian says the distance from the Bodhi-tree to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā</span>, was twenty *li*, or about 3-1/3 miles). It was between the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bodhimaṇḍa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā</span> that the Buddha, on his way to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>, met <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upaka</span> (Vin.i.8).

The Buddha stayed at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā</span> on several occasions: once at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayāsīsa</span> (Vin.i.34; SN.iv.19; AN.iv.302), and also near the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ṭaṅkitamañca</span> (Sn. p.47; SN.i.207, etc.), the residence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suciloma</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā</span> was the name given both to the village and a bathing ghat near to it (also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayāpokkharaṇī</span>). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapāla</span> (Ud­a.74, 75; cp. Snp­a.i.301), on the other hand, speaks of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayānadī</span> and a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayāpokkharaṇī</span> as being two distinct bathing ghats, both commonly called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayātittha</span>, and both considered to possess the power of washing away sins. People went there, offered sacrifices to the gods, recited the Vedas, and immersed themselves in the water.

Elsewhere it is stated that every year, in the earlier half of the month of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phagguna</span> (March), people held a bathing festival at the bathing ghat at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā</span>, the festival being called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayāphaggunī</span> (Thag­a.i.388f, 418; Thag.v.287). It was at one of these festivals that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Senaka</span> Thera was converted by the Buddha. This explanation of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayāphaggu</span> is, perhaps, not quite correct, for, according to some, the river (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nerañjarā</span>) which ran by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā</span> was itself called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phaggu</span> (Skt. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phalgu</span>), e.g., Cunningham: *Ancient Geography of India*.524; Böthlinck and Roth’s *Dict*. *s.v.* Phalgu; Neumann (MN.Trs.i.271) says that the village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā</span> itself was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phaggu</span>.

The town of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā</span> is often called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmagayā</span> to distinguish it from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhagayā</span> (*q.v.*).
