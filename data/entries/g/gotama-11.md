# Gotama 11

## English source

Gotama 11. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>. The last of the twenty-five Buddhas.

No comprehensive account of Gotama Buddha is as yet possible. The details given in this article are those generally accepted by orthodox <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theravādins</span> and contained in their books, chiefly the Pāli Commentaries, more especially the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nidānakathā</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhavaṁsa</span> Commentary

Biographical details are also found in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vagga</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Vagga</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vinayapiṭaka</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhavaṁsa</span> and in various scattered passages of the *Nikāyas* of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttapiṭaka</span>. References to these are given where considered useful. Controversy exists with regard to many of the matters mentioned; for discussion of the varying views regarding these, reference should be made to the works of Oldenberg, Rhys Davids (both Professor and Mrs. Rhys Davids), Kern, E. J. Thomas and other scholars. Further particulars of persons and places mentioned can be obtained by reference to the articles under the respective names.

He was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> (the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> were evidently subjects of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> king; the Buddha calls himself a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosalan</span>, MN.ii.124), son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> (all Pāli Commentaries and Sanskrit works represent the Buddha as the son of a king, descendant of a long line of famous ancestors), chief ruler of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, and of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Māyā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span>’s chief consort, and he belonged to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotamagotta</span>.

Before his conception he was in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> heaven, waiting for the due time for his birth in his last existence. Then, having made the “five investigations” (*pañcavilokanāni*) (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>), he took leave of his companions and descended to earth. (According to the *Lalitavistara* he appointed the Bodhisatta Maitreya as king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> in his place). Many wondrous and marvellous events attended his conception and birth. (Given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Acchariyabbhutadhammasutta</span>, MN.iii.118f; also DN.ii.12f. A more detailed account is found in Ja.i.47ff; both the *Lalitavistara* and the Mvu.ii.14ff differ as to the details given here of the conception and the birth).

The conception takes place on the full-moon day of *Āsāḷha*, with the moon in *Uttarāsāḷha*, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māyā</span> has no relations with her husband. She has a marvellous dream in which the Bodhisatta, as a white elephant, enters her womb through her side. When the dream is mentioned to the Brahmins, they foretell the birth of a son who will be either a universal monarch or a Buddha. An earthquake takes place and thirty-two signs appear, presaging the birth of a great being. The first of these signs is a boundless, great light, flooding every corner of the ten thousand worlds; everyone beholds its glory, even the fires in all hells being extinguished. Ten months after the conception, in the month of Visākha, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māyā</span> wishes to visit her parents in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadaha</span>. On the way thither from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> she passes the beautiful <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lumbini</span> grove, in which she desires to wander; she goes to a great *sāla*-tree and seizes a branch in her hand; labour pains start immediately, and, when the courtiers retire, having drawn a curtain round her, even while standing, she is delivered of the child. It is the day of the full moon of Visākha; four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahmas</span> receive the babe in a golden net, and streams of water descend from the sky to wash him. The boy stands on the earth, takes seven steps northwards and utters his lion-roar, “I am the chief in the world.” On the same day seven other beings were born: the Bodhi-tree, Rāhula’s mother (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulamātā</span>, his future wife), the four Treasure-Troves (described at DN­a.i.284), his elephant, his horse <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kanthaka</span>, his charioteer <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷudāyī</span>. The babe is escorted back to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> on the day of his birth and his mother dies seven days later.

The *Isi* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asita</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷadevala</span>), meditating in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span>, learns from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> gods of the birth of the Buddha, visits <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> the same day and sees the boy, whom they both worship. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asita</span> weeps for sorrow that he will not live to see the boy’s Buddhahood, but he instructs his nephew <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālaka</span> (*v.l.* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Naradatta</span>) to prepare himself for that great day. On the fifth day after the birth is the ceremony of name-giving. One hundred and eight Brahmins are invited to the festival at the palace; eight of them – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhaja</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lakkhaṇa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manti</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇḍañña</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhoja</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suyāma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudatta</span> – are interpreters of bodily marks, and all except <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇḍañña</span> prophesy two possibilities for the boy; but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇḍañña</span>, the youngest, says, quite decisively, that he will be a Buddha. The name given to the boy at this ceremony is not actually mentioned, but from other passages it is inferred that it was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siddhattha</span> (*q.v.*).

Among other incidents recounted of the Buddha’s boyhood is that of his attaining the first *jhāna* under a *jambu*-tree. One day he is taken to the state ploughing of the king where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> himself, with his golden plough, ploughs with the farmers. The nurses, attracted by the festivities, leave the child under a *jambu*-tree. They return to find him seated, cross-legged, in a *jhāna*, the shadow of the tree remaining still, in order to protect him. The king is informed and, for the second time, does reverence to his son. Ja.i.57f; MN­a.i.466f; the incident is alluded to in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Saccakasutta</span> (MN.i.246); the corresponding incident recounted in *Mahā Vastu* (Mvu.ii.45f.) takes place in a park, and the details differ completely. The *Lalitavistara* has two versions, one in prose and one in verse and both resemble the *Mahā Vastu*; but in these the Buddha is represented as being much older. The *Divyāvadāna* (391) and the Tibetan versions (e.g., Rockhill, p.22) put the incident very much later in the Buddha’s life. Other incidents are given in *Lalitavistara* and *Mahā Vastu*.

The Bodhisatta is reported to have lived in the household for twenty-nine years a life of great luxury and excessive ease, surrounded by all imaginable comforts. He owns three palaces – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ramma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suramma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subha</span> – for the three seasons. Mention is made of his luxurious life in AN.i.145; also in MN.i.504; further details are given in AN­a.i.378f.; Ja.i.58. See also Mvu.ii.115; cf. Vin.i.15; DN.ii.21.

When the Bodhisatta is sixteen years old, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> sends messengers to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> asking that his son be allowed to seek a wife from among their daughters; but the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> are reluctant to send them, for, they say, though the young man is handsome, he knows no art; how, then, can he support a wife? When this is reported to the prince, he summons an assembly of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> and performs various feats, chief of these being twelve feats with a bow which needs the strength of one thousand men. (The feats with the bow are described in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhaṅgajātaka</span>, Ja.v.129f). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> are so impressed that each sends him a daughter, the total number so sent being forty thousand. The Bodhisatta appoints as his chief wife the daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppabuddha</span>, who, later, comes to be called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulamātā</span>. She is known under various names: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddakaccā</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddakaccānā</span>), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasodharā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbasundarī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gopā</span>. For a discussion see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulamātā</span>.

According to the generally accepted account, Gotama is twenty-nine when the incidents occur which lead to final renunciation. Following the prophecy of the eight Brahmins, his father had taken every precaution that his son should see no sign of old age, sickness or death. But the gods decide that the time is come for the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>, and instil into Gotama’s heart a desire to go into the park. On the way, the gods put before him a man showing signs of extreme age, and the Bodhisatta returns, filled with desire for renunciation. The king, learning this, surrounds him with even greater attractions, but on two other days Gotama goes to the park and the gods put before him a sick man and a corpse. (According to some accounts, e.g. that of the *Dīghabhāṇakas*, the four omens were all seen on the same day, Ja.i.59)

On the full-moon day of *Āsāḷha*, the day appointed for the Great Renunciation, Gotama sees a monk and hears from his charioteer praise of the ascetic life. Feeling very happy, he goes to the park to enjoy himself. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> sends <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span> himself to bathe and adorn him, and as Gotama returns to the city in all his majesty, he receives news of the birth of his son. Foreseeing in this news a bond, he decides to call the babe <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span> (*q.v.*) (In some versions the Renunciation takes place seven days after the birth of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span>, Ja.i.62). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kisā Gotamī</span> (*q.v.*) sees Gotama on the way to the palace and, filled with longing for him, sings to him a song containing the word *nibbuta*. The significance of the word (= extinguished, at peace) thrills him, and he sends to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kisā</span> his priceless gold necklace which she, however, accepts as a token of love. Gotama enters the palace and sleeps. He wakes in the middle of the night to find his female musicians sleeping in attitudes which fill him with disgust and with loathing for the worldly life, and he decides to leave it. He orders <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> to saddle <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kanthaka</span>, and enters his wife’s room for a last look at her and their son.

He leaves the city on his horse <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kanthaka</span>, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> clinging to its tail. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> muffle the sound of the horse’s hoofs and of his neighing and open the city gates for Gotama to pass. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> appears before Gotama and seeks to stay him with a promise that he shall be universal monarch within seven days. On his offer being refused, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> threatens to shadow him always. Outside the city, at the spot where later was erected the *Kanthakanivattanacetiya*, Gotama turns his horse round to take a last look at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>. It is said that the earth actually turned, to make it easy for him to do so. Then, accompanied by the gods, he rides thirty leagues through three kingdoms – those of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyans</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> – and his horse crosses the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anomā</span> in one leap. On the other side, he gives all his ornaments to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span>, and with his sword cuts off hair and beard, throwing them up into the air, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> takes them and enshrines them in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷāmaṇicetiya</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>. The *Brahma* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span> offers Gotama the eight requisites of a monk, which he accepts and adopts. He then sends <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kanthaka</span> back to his father, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kanthaka</span>, broken-hearted, dies on the spot and is reborn as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kanthaka</span> *Devaputta*.

The account given here is taken mainly from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nidānakathā</span> (Ja.i.59ff) and evidently embodies later tradition; cp. DN.ii.21ff. From passages found in the *Piṭakas* (e.g., AN.i.145; MN.i.163, 240; MN.ii.212f.) it would appear that the events leading up to the Renunciation were not so dramatic as given here, the process being more gradual. I do not, however, agree with Thomas (op.cit., 58) that, according to these accounts, the Bodhisatta left the world when “quite a boy.” I think the word *dahara* is used merely to indicate “the prime of youth,” and not necessarily “boyhood.” The description of the Renunciation in the *Lalitavistara* is very much more elaborate and adds numerous incidents, no account of which is found in the Pāli.

From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anomā</span> the Bodhisatta goes to the mango-grove of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anupiya</span>, and after spending seven days there walks to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> (a distance of thirty leagues) in one day, and there starts his alms rounds. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>’s men, noticing him, report the matter to the king, who sends messengers to enquire who this ascetic is. The men follow Gotama to the foot of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍava</span> *pabbata*, where he eats his meal, and they then go and report to the king. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> visits Gotama, and, pleased with his hearing, offers him the sovereignty. On learning the nature of Gotama’s quest, he wins from him a promise to visit <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> first after the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>.

This incident is also mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pabbajjāsutta</span> (Snp.vv.405-24), but there it is the king who first sees Gotama. It is significant that, when asked his identity, Gotama does not say he is a king’s son. The Pāḷi version of the *Sutta* contains nothing of Gotama’s promise to visit <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, but the *Mahā Vastu* version (Mvu.ii.198-200), which places the visit later, has two verses, one of which contains the request and the other the acceptance; and the Snp­a. (ii.385f.), too, mentions the promise and tells that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> was informed of the prophecy concerning Gotama. There is another version of the *Mahā Vastu* (ii.117-20) which says that Gotama went straight to Vaiśāli after leaving home, joining <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span>, and later visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uddaka</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. Here no mention is made of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>. We are told in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsa</span> (ii.25ff) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="119" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> and Gotama (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="120" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siddhattha</span>) had been playmates, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="121" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> being the younger by five years. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="122" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>’s father (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="123" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhātī</span>) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="124" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> were friends.

Journeying from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="125" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, Gotama in due course becomes a disciple of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="126" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra Kāḷāma</span>. Having learnt and practised all that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="127" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span> has to teach, he finds it unsatisfying and joins <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="128" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uddaka Rāmaputta</span>; but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="129" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uddaka</span>’s doctrine leaves him still unconvinced and he abandons it. He then goes to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="130" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Senānīnigama</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="131" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span> and there, during six years, practises all manner of severe austerities, such as no man had previously undertaken. Once he falls fainting and a *Deva* informs <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="132" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> that Gotama is dead. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="133" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span>, relying on the prophecy of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="134" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷadevala</span>, refuses to believe the news. Gotama’s mother, now born as a *Devaputta* in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="135" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, comes to him to encourage him. At <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="136" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="137" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcavaggiya</span> monks are his companions, but now, having realised the folly of extreme asceticism, he decides to abandon it, and starts again to take normal food; thereupon the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="138" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcavaggiya</span>s, disappointed, leave him and go to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="139" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>.

Ja.i.66f. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="140" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Therīgāthā</span> Commentary (p.2) mentions another teacher of Gotama, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="141" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaggava</span>, whom Gotama visited before <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="142" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span>. *Lalitavistara* (330 264) contains a very elaborate account of Gotama’s visits to teachers; he goes first to two Brahmin women, Śākī and Padmā, then to Raivata and Rajaka, son of Trimaṇḍika, and finally (as far as this chapter is concerned) to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="143" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷāra</span> at Vaiśāli. A poem containing an account of the meeting of Gotama with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="144" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> is inserted into this account. The next chapter tells of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="145" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uddaka</span>. An account of Gotama’s visits to teachers and of the details of his austerities is also given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="146" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Saccakasutta</span>, already referred to (MN.i.240ff); the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="147" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sīhanādasutta</span> (MN.i.77ff) contains a long and detailed account of his extreme asceticisms. See also MN.i.163ff; ii.93f.

Gotama’s desire for normal food is satisfied by an offering brought by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="148" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujātā</span> to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="149" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajapāla</span> banyan tree under which he is seated. She had made a vow to the tree, and her wish having been granted, she takes her slave-girl, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="150" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇā</span>, and goes to the tree prepared to fulfil her promise. They take Gotama to be the Tree-god, come in person to accept her offering of milk-rice; the offering is made in a golden bowl and he takes it joyfully. Five dreams he had the night before convince Gotama that he will that day become the Buddha. (The dreams are recounted in AN.iii.240 and in Mvu.ii.136f). It is the full-moon day of Visākha; he bathes at Suppatiṭṭha in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="151" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nerañjarā</span>, eats the food and launches the bowl upstream, where it sinks to the abode of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="152" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> king, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="153" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷa</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="154" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kāḷa</span>).

Gotama spends the rest of the day in a *sāla*-grove and, in the evening, goes to the foot of the Bodhi-tree, accompanied by various divinities; there the grass-cutter <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="155" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sotthiya</span> gives him eight handfuls of grass; these, after investigation, Gotama spreads on the eastern side of the tree, where it becomes a seat fourteen hands long, on which he sits cross-legged, determined not to rise before attaining <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="156" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>, Ja.i.69.

The *Piṭakas* know nothing of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="157" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujātā</span>’s offering or of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="158" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sotthiya</span>’s gift. *Lalitavistara* (334-7 267-70) mentions ten girls in all who provide him with food during his austerities. *Divyāvadāna* (392) mentions two, Nandā and Nandabalā.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="159" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>, lord of the world of passion, is determined to prevent this fulfilment, and attacks Gotama with all the strength at his command. His army extends twelve leagues to the front, right, and left of him, to the end of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="160" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavāḷa</span> behind him, and nine leagues into the sky above him. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="161" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> himself carries numerous weapons and rides the elephant <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="162" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Girimekhala</span>, one hundred and fifty leagues in height. At the sight of him all the divinities gathered at the Bodhi-tree to do honour to Gotama – the great <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="163" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="164" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="165" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>-king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="166" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kāḷa</span> – disappear in a flash, and Gotama is left alone with the ten *pāramī*, long practised by him, as his sole protection. All <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="167" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>’s attempts to frighten him by means of storms and terrifying apparitions fail, and, in the end, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="168" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> hurls at him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="169" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkāvudha</span>. It remains as a canopy poised over Gotama. The very earth bears witness to Gotama’s fitness to be the Awakened One, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="170" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Girimekhala</span> kneels before him. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="171" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> is vanquished and flees headlong with his vast army. The various divinities who had fled at the approach of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="172" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> now return to Gotama and exult in his triumph.

The whole story of the contest with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="173" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> is, obviously, a mythological development. It is significant that in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="174" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhima</span> passages referred to earlier there is no mention of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="175" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>, of a temptation, or even of a Bodhi-tree; but see DN.ii.4 and Thomas (op.cit., n.1). According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="176" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāliṅgabodhijātaka</span>, which, very probably, embodies an old tradition, the Bodhi-tree was worshipped even in the Buddha’s life-time. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="177" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> legend is, however, to be found in the Canonical <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="178" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padhānasutta</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="179" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span>. This perhaps contains the first suggestion of the legend. For a discussion see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="180" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>.

Gotama spends that night in deep meditation. In the first watch he gains remembrance of his former existences; in the middle watch he attains the divine eye (*dibbacakkhu*); in the last watch he revolves in his mind the Chain of Causation (*paṭiccasamuppāda*). As he masters this, the earth trembles and, with the dawn, comes <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="181" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>. He is now the supreme Buddha, and he breaks forth into a paean of joy (*udāna*).

There is great doubt as to which were these <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="182" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> verses. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="183" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nidānakathā</span> and the Commentaries generally quote two verses (153, 154) included in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="184" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada</span> collection (*anekajāti Saṁsāraṁ*, etc.). The Vinaya (i.2) quotes three different verses (as does also Dhs­a.17), and says that one verse was repeated at the end of each watch, all the watches being occupied with meditation on the *paṭiccasamuppāda*. *Mahā Vastu* (Mvu.ii.286) gives a completely different <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="185" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span>, and in another place (ii.416) mentions a different verse as the first <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="186" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span>. The Tibetan Vinaya is, again, quite different (Rockhill, p.33). For a discussion see Thomas: *Life and Legend of the Buddha*, 75ff.

For the first week the Buddha remains under the Bodhi-tree, meditating on the *paṭiccasamuppāda*; the second week he spends at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="187" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajapālanigrodha</span>, where the “*Huhuṅka*” Brahmin accosts him (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="188" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> now comes again and asks the Buddha to die at once; DN.ii.112) and where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="189" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>’s daughters, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="190" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Taṇhā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="191" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aratī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="192" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāgā</span>, appear before the Buddha and make a last attempt to shake his resolution (Ja.i.78; SN.i.124; *Lal*.490 (378); the third week he spends under the hood of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="193" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>-king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="194" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mucalinda</span> (Vin.i.3); the fourth week is spent in meditation under the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="195" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājāyatana</span> tree; at the end of this period takes place the conversion of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="196" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tapussa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="197" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhallika</span>. They take refuge in the Buddha and the Dhamma, though the Buddha does not give them any instruction. This is the Vinaya account (Vin.i.1ff); but the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="198" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> (Ja.i.77ff), extends this period to seven weeks, the additional weeks being inserted between the first and second. The Buddha spends one week each at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="199" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Animisacetiya</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="200" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratanacaṅkama</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="201" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratanaghara</span>, and this last is where he thinks out the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="202" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhammapiṭaka</span>.

Doubts now assail the Buddha as to whether he shall proclaim to the world his doctrine, so recondite, so hard to understand. The *Brahma* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="203" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> (according to Ja.i.81, with the gods of the thousand worlds, including <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="204" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="205" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suyāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="206" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Santusita</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="207" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunimmita</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="208" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vasavatti</span>, etc.) appears before him and assures him there are many prepared to listen to him and to profit by his teaching, and so entreats him to teach the Dhamma. The Buddha accedes to his request and, after consideration, decides to teach the Dhamma first to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="209" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcavaggiyas</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="210" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>. On the way to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="211" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> he meets the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="212" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ājīvaka</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="213" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upaka</span> and tells him that he (the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="214" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>) is a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="215" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jina</span>. On his arrival at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="216" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="217" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcavaggiyas</span> are, at first, reluctant to acknowledge his claim to be the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="218" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tathāgata</span>, but they let themselves be won over and, on the full-moon day of *Āsāḷha*, the Buddha preaches to them the sermon which came to be known as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="219" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammacakkappavattanasutta</span> (Vin.i.4ff; MN.i.118ff; cp. DN.ii.36ff. Regarding the claim of this *Sutta* to be the Buddha’s first sermon, see Thomas: *Life and Legend of the Buddha*, p.86; see also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="220" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcavaggiyā</span>). At the end of the sermon <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="221" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇḍañña</span> becomes a *Sotāpanna* and they all become monks.

This sermon is followed five days later by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="222" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anattalakkhaṇasutta</span>, at the conclusion of which all five become *Arahants*. The following day the Buddha meets <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="223" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span>, whom he converts. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="224" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span>’s father, who comes seeking him, is the first to take the threefold formula of Refuge.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="225" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span> becomes an *Arahant* and is ordained. The Buddha accepts a meal at his house, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="226" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span>’s mother and one of his former wives are the first two lay-women to become the Buddha’s disciples. Then four friends of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="227" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span> and, afterwards, fifty more, enter the Saṅgha and become *Arahants*. There are now sixty *Arahants* besides the Buddha, and they are sent in different directions to preach the Dhamma. They return with many candidates for admission to the Saṅgha, and the Buddha, who up till now had ordained men with the “*ehi bhikkhu*” formula, now allows the monks themselves to perform the ceremony of ordination (Vin.i.15ff; Ja.i.81f).

After spending the rainy season at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="228" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, the Buddha returns to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="229" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Senānīgāma</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="230" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvela</span>, on the way converting and ordaining the thirty <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="231" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddavaggiyā</span> (about this time <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="232" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> twice tries to tempt the Buddha, once after he had sent the disciples out to preach and once after the Retreat, SN.i.105, 111; Vin.i.21, 22).

At <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="233" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvela</span>, after a long and protracted exercise of magical powers, consisting in all of three thousand five hundred miracles, the Buddha wins over the three <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="234" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> brothers, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="235" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tebhātikajaṭilā</span>, with their thousand followers, and ordains them. They become *Arahants* after listening to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="236" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ādittapariyāyasutta</span> preached at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="237" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayāsīsa</span>; with these followers he visits <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="238" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, where King Seniya <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="239" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> comes to see him at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="240" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Laṭṭhivanuyyāna</span>. The following day the Buddha and the monks visit the palace, preceded by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="241" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> disguised as a youth and singing the praises of the Buddha. After the meal, the king gifts <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="242" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span> to the Buddha and the Saṅgha. The Buddha stays for two months at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="243" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> (Bv­a.4), and it is during this time that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="244" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="245" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> join the Saṅgha, through the instrumentality of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="246" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji</span> (Vin.i.23ff). It was probably during this year, at the beginning of the rainy season, that the Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="247" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> at the request of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="248" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>, conveyed through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="249" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāli</span>. The city was suffering from pestilence and famine. The Buddha went, preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="250" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratanasutta</span> and dispelled all dangers (Dhp­a.iii.436ff).

The number of converts now rapidly increases and the people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="251" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, alarmed by the prospect of childlessness, widowhood, etc., blame the Buddha and his monks. The Buddha, however, refutes their charges (Vin.i.42f).

The account of the first twenty years of the Buddha’s ministry is summarised from various sources, chiefly from Thomas’s admirable account in his *Life and Legend of the Buddha* (pp.97ff). The necessary references are to be found under the names mentioned.

On the full-moon day of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="252" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phagguna</span> (February-March) the Buddha, accompanied by twenty thousand monks, sets out for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="253" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> at the express request of his father, conveyed through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="254" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷudāyī</span> (this visit is not mentioned in the Canon; but see Thag.527-36; AN­a.i.107, 167; Ja.i.87; Dhp­a.i.96f; Thag­a.i.997ff).

By slow stages he arrives at the city, where he stays at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="255" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span>, and, in order to convince his proud kinsmen of his power, performs the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="256" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamakapāṭihāriya</span> and then relates the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="257" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantarajātaka</span>. The next day, receiving no invitation to a meal, the Buddha begs in the streets of the city; this deeply grieves <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="258" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span>, but later, learning that it is the custom of all Buddhas, he becomes a *Sotāpanna* and conducts the Buddha and his monks to a meal at the palace. There all the women of the palace, excepting only <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="259" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulamātā</span>, come and do reverence to the Buddha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="260" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Pajāpatī</span> becomes a *Sotāpanna* and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="261" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> a *sakadāgāmi*. The Buddha visits <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="262" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulamātā</span> in her own apartments and utters her praises in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="263" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candakinnarajātaka</span>. The following day the Buddha persuades his half-brother, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="264" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span>, to come to the monastery, where he ordains him and, on the seventh day, he does the same with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="265" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span>. This is too great a blow for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="266" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span>, and at his request the Buddha rules that no person shall be ordained without the consent of his parents. The next day the Buddha preaches to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="267" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span>, who becomes an *Anāgāmī*. During the Buddha’s visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="268" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, eighty thousand <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="269" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> join the Saṅgha, one from each family. With these he returns to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="270" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, stopping on the way at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="271" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anupiya</span>, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="272" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="273" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="274" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="275" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhagu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="276" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbila</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="277" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>, together with their barber, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="278" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli</span>, visit him and seek ordination.

On his return to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="279" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> the Buddha resides in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="280" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sītavana</span> (Ja.i.92, the story is also told in the Vinaya ii.154, but no date is indicated). There <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="281" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudatta</span>, later known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="282" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>, visits him, is converted, and invites him to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="283" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>. The Buddha accepts the invitation and journeys through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="284" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="285" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, there to pass the rainy season (Vin.ii.158; but see Bv­a.3, where the Buddha is mentioned as having spent the *vassa* in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="286" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="287" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span> gifts <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="288" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, provided with every necessity, for the residence of the Buddha and his monks. Probably to this period belongs the conversion of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="289" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span>, father-in-law of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="290" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>, and the construction, by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="291" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>, of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="292" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pubbārāma</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="293" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>. The *vassa* of the fourth year the Buddha spends at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="294" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span>, where he converts <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="295" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uggasena</span> (Dhp­a.iv.59f). In the fifth year <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="296" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> dies, having realised *Arahant*-ship, and the Buddha flies through the air, from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="297" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kūṭāgārasālā</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="298" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> where he was staying, to preach to his father on his death-bed. According to one account it is at this time that the quarrel breaks out between the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="299" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="300" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyans</span> regarding the irrigation of the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="301" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohiṇī</span> (AN­a.i.186; Snp­a.i.357; Thīg­a.141; details of the quarrel are given in Ja.v.412ff). The Buddha persuades them to make peace, and takes up his abode in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="302" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="303" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Pajāpatī Gotamī</span>, with other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="304" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> women, visits him there and asks that women may be allowed to join the Saṅgha. Three times the request is made, three times refused, the Buddha then returning to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="305" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>. The women cut off their hair, don yellow robes and follow him thither. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="306" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> intercedes on their behalf and their request is granted (Vin.ii.253ff; AN.iv.274f.; for details see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="307" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Pajāpatī</span>).

In the sixth year the Buddha again performs the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="308" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamakapāṭihāriya</span>, this time at the foot of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="309" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṇḍamba</span> tree in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="310" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>. Prior to this, the Buddha had forbidden any display of magic powers, but makes an exception in his own case (Dhp­a.iii.199f.; Ja.iv.265, etc.).

He spends the *vassa* at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="311" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṅkulapabbata</span>. After the performance of the miracle he follows the custom of all Buddhas and ascends to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="312" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> in three strides to preach the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="313" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span> to his mother who is born there as a *Deva*, and there he keeps the seventh *vassa*. The multitude, gathered at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="314" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="315" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamakapāṭihāriya</span>, refuse to go away until they have seen him. For three months, therefore, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="316" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> expounds to them the Dhamma, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="317" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Anāthapiṇḍika</span> provides them with food. During the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="318" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="319" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> visits the Buddha daily and learns from him all that has been recited the previous day. At the end of the *vassa*, the Buddha descends a jewelled staircase and comes to earth at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="320" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span>, thirty leagues from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="321" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> (for details see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="322" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devorohaṇa</span>). It was about this time, when the Buddha’s fame was at its height, that the notorious <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="323" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ciñcāmāṇavikā</span> was persuaded by members of some hostile sect to bring a vile accusation against the Buddha. A similar story, told in connection with a *paribbājikā* named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="324" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sundarī</span>, probably refers to a later date.

The eighth year the Buddha spends in the country of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="325" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaggas</span> and there, while residing in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="326" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhesakalāvana</span> near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="327" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suṁsumāragiri</span>, he meets <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="328" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nakulapitā</span> and his wife, who had been his parents in five hundred former births (AN.A.i.217).

The same is told of another old couple in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="329" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span>. See the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="330" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketajātaka</span>. The Buddha evidently stayed again at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="331" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suṁsumāragiri</span> many years later. It was during his second visit that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="332" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bodhirājakumāra</span> (*q.v.*) invited him to a meal at his new palace in order that the Buddha might consecrate the building by his presence.

In the ninth year the Buddha is at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="333" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>. While on a visit to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="334" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuru</span> country he is offered in marriage <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="335" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span>, the beautiful daughter of the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="336" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiya</span>. The refusal of the offer, accompanied by insulting remarks about physical beauty, arouses the enmity of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="337" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span> who, thenceforward, cherishes hatred against the Buddha. Snp., pp.163ff; Snp­a.ii.542ff; Dhp­a.i.199ff. Thomas (op.cit., 109) assigns the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="338" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span> incident to the ninth year. I am not sure if this is correct, for the Commentaries say the Buddha was then living at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="339" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>.

In the tenth year there arises among the monks at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="340" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> a schism which threatens the very existence of the Saṅgha. The Buddha, failing in his attempts to reconcile the disputants, retires in disgust to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="341" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārileyyaka</span> forest, passing on his way through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="342" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bālakaloṇakāragāma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="343" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pācīnavaṁsadāya</span>. In the forest he is protected and waited upon by a friendly elephant who has left the herd. The Buddha spends the rainy season there and returns to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="344" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>. By this time the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="345" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> monks have recovered their senses and ask the Buddha’s pardon. This is granted and the dispute settled. (Vin.i.337ff; Ja.iii.486f; Dhp­a.i.44ff; but see Ud.iv.5; *s.v.* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="346" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārileyyaka</span>).

In the eleventh year the Buddha resides at the Brahmin village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="347" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekanāḷā</span> and converts <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="348" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kasi Bhāradvāja</span> (Snp., p.12f.; SN.i.172f). The twelfth year he spends at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="349" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verañjā</span>, keeping the *vassa* there at the request of the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="350" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verañja</span>. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="351" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verañja</span> forgets his obligations; there is a famine, and five hundred horse-merchants supply the monks with food. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="352" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>’s offer to obtain food by means of magic power is discouraged (Vin.iii.1ff; Ja.iii.494f; Dhp­a.ii.153). The thirteenth Retreat is kept at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="353" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cālikapabbata</span>, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="354" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meghiya</span> is the Buddha’s personal attendant (AN.iv.354; Ud.iv.1). The fourteenth year is spent at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="355" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, and there <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="356" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span> receives the *upasampadā* ordination.

In the fifteenth year the Buddha revisits <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="357" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, and there his father-in-law, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="358" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppabuddha</span>, in a drunken fit, refuses to let the Buddha pass through the streets. Seven days later he is swallowed up by the earth at the foot of his palace (Dhp­a.iii.44).

The chief event of the sixteenth year, which the Buddha spent at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="359" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavī</span>, is the conversion of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="360" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="361" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span>. In the seventeenth year the Buddha is back at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="362" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, but he visits <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="363" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavī</span> again out of compassion for a poor farmer who becomes a *Sotāpanna* after hearing him preach (Dhp­a.iii.262ff). He spends the rainy season at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="364" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. In the next year he again comes to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="365" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavī</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="366" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> for the sake of a poor weaver’s daughter. She had heard him preach, three years earlier, on the desirability of meditating upon death. She alone gave heed to his admonition and, when the Buddha knows of her imminent death, he journeys thirty leagues to preach to her and establish her in the *Sotāpattiphala* (Dhp­a.iii.170ff).

The Retreat of this year and also that of the nineteenth are spent at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="367" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cālikapabbata</span>. In the twentieth year takes place the miraculous conversion of the robber <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="368" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgulimāla</span>. He becomes an *Arahant* and dies shortly after. It is in the same year that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="369" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> is appointed permanent attendant on the Buddha, a position which he holds to the end of the Buddha’s life, twenty-five years later (for details see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="370" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>). The twentieth Retreat is spent at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="371" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>.

There is a more or less a continuous account of the last year of the Buddha’s life. This is contained in three *Suttas*: the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="492" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Parinibbāna</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="493" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sudassana</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="494" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janavasabha</span>. These are not separate discourses but are intimately connected with each other. The only event prior to the incidents recounted in these *Suttas*, which can be fixed with any certainty, is the death of the Buddha’s pious patron and supporter, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="495" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, which took place eight years before the Buddha’s *Parinibbāna* (Mhv.ii.32). It was at this time that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="496" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> tried to obtain for himself a post of supremacy in the Saṅgha, and, failing in this effort, became the open enemy of the Buddha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="497" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>’s desire to deprive the Buddha of the leadership of the Saṅgha seems to have been conceived by him, according to the Vinaya account (Vin.ii.184), almost immediately after he joined the Saṅgha, and the Buddha was warned of this by the *Devaputta* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="498" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakudha</span>. This account lends point to the statement contained especially in the Northern books, that even in their lay life <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="499" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> had always been Gotama’s rival.

Enlisting the support of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="500" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, he tried in many ways to kill the Buddha. Royal archers were bribed to shoot the Buddha, but they were won over by his personality and confessed their intentions. Then <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="501" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> hurled a great rock down <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="502" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span> on to the Buddha as he was walking in the shade of the hill; the hurtling rock was stopped by two peaks, but splinters struck the Buddha’s foot and caused blood to flow; he suffered great pain and had to be taken to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="503" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maddakucchi</span> garden, where his injuries were dressed by the physician <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="504" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span> (SN.i.27). The monks wished to provide a guard, but the Buddha reminded them that no man had the power to deprive a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="505" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tathāgata</span> of his life.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="506" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> next bribed the royal elephant keepers to let loose a fierce elephant, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="507" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālāgiri</span>, intoxicated with toddy, on the road along which the Buddha would go, begging for alms. The Buddha was warned of this but disregarded the warning, and when the elephant appeared, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="508" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, against the strict orders of the Buddha, threw himself in its path, and only by an exercise of *iddhi*-power, including the folding up of the earth, could the Buddha come ahead of him. As the elephant approached, the Buddha addressed it, pervading it with his boundless love, until it became quite gentle (this incident, with great wealth of detail, is related in several places – e.g., in Ja.v.333ff).

These attempts to encompass the Buddha’s death having failed, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="509" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>, with three others, decides to create a schism in the Saṅgha and asks the Buddha that five rules should be laid down, whereby the monks would be compelled to lead a far more austere life than hitherto. When this request is refused, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="510" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> persuades five hundred recently ordained monks to leave <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="511" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> with him and take up their residence at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="512" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayāsīsa</span>, where he would set up an organisation similar to that of the Buddha. But, at the Buddha’s request, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="513" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="514" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> visit the renegade monks; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="515" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> preaches to them and they are persuaded to return. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="516" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> discovers this, he vomits hot blood and lies ill for nine months. When his end approaches, he wishes to see the Buddha, but he dies on the way to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="517" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> – whither he is being conveyed in a litter – and is born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="518" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avīci</span>.

From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="519" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span>, near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="520" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, the Buddha starts on his last journey. Just before his departure he is visited by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="521" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span>, and the talk is of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="522" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjians</span>; the Buddha preaches to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="523" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> and the monks on the conditions that lead to prosperity. The Buddha proceeds with a large concourse of monks to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="524" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambalaṭṭhikā</span> and thence to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="525" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālandā</span>, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="526" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> utters his lion-roar (*sīhanāda*) regarding his faith in the Buddha. The Buddha then goes to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="527" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaligāma</span>, where he talks to the villagers on the evil consequences of immorality and the advantages of morality. He utters a prophecy regarding the future greatness of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="528" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span> and then, leaving by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="529" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotamadvāra</span>, he crosses the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="530" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="531" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotamatittha</span>. He proceeds to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="532" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṭigāma</span> and thence to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="533" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ñātika</span>, where he gives to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="534" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> the formula of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="535" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammādāsa</span>, whereby the rebirth of disciples could be ascertained. From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="536" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ñātika</span> he goes to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="537" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, staying in the park of the courtesan <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="538" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambapālī</span>. The following day he accepts a meal from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="539" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambapālī</span>, refusing a similar offer from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="540" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="541" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambapālī</span> makes a gift of her park to the Buddha and his monks. The Buddha journeys on to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="542" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Beluva</span>, where he spends the rainy season, his monks remaining in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="543" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>. At <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="544" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Beluva</span> he falls dangerously ill but, with great determination, fights against his sickness. He tells <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="545" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> that his mission is finished, that when he is dead the Saṅgha must maintain itself, taking the Dhamma alone as its refuge, and he concludes by propounding the four subjects of mindfulness (DN.ii.100). The next day he begs in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="546" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> and, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="547" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, visits the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="548" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cāpālacetiya</span>. There he gives to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="549" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> the opportunity of asking him to live until the end of the *kappa*, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="550" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> fails to take the hint. Soon afterwards <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="551" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> visits the Buddha and obtains the assurance that the Buddha’s *Nibbāna* will take place in three months. There is an earthquake, and, in answer to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="552" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>’s questions, the Buddha explains to him the eight causes of earthquakes. This is followed by lists of the eight assemblies, the eight stages of mastery and the eight stages of release. The Buddha then repeats to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="553" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> his conversation with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="554" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="555" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> now makes his request to the Buddha to prolong his life, but is told that it is now too late; several opportunities he has had, of which he has failed to avail himself. The monks are assembled in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="556" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, in the Service Hall, and the Buddha exhorts them to practise the doctrines he has taught, in order that the religious life may last long. He then announces his impending death.

According to the Commentaries (e.g., DN­a.ii.549), after the rainy season spent at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="557" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Beluva</span>, the Buddha goes back to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="558" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, where he is visited by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="559" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, who is preparing for his own *Parinibbāna* at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="560" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālakagāma</span>. From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="561" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> the Buddha went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="562" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="563" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Moggallāna</span> died. Thence he proceeded to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="564" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkācelā</span>, where he spoke in praise of the two chief disciples. From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="565" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkācelā</span> he proceeded to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="566" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> and thence to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="567" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaṇḍagāma</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="568" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span>, too, predeceased the Buddha (DN­a.ii.549).

The next day, returning from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="569" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, he looks round at the city for the last time and goes on to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="570" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaṇḍagāma</span>; there he preaches on the four things the comprehension of which destroys rebirth – noble conduct, earnestness in meditation, wisdom and freedom.

He then passes through the villages of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="571" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthigāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="572" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambagāma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="573" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambugāma</span>, and stays at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="574" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhoganagara</span> at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="575" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānandacetiya</span>. There he addresses the monks on the Four Great Authorities (*Mahā Padesā*), by reference to which the true doctrine may be determined (Cf. AN.ii.167ff). From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="576" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhoganagara</span> the Buddha goes to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="577" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span> and stays in the mango-grove of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="578" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span>, the smith. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="579" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span> serves him with a meal which includes *sūkaramaddava*. (There is much dispute concerning this word. See Thomas: *Life and Legend of the Buddha*, 149, n.3). The Buddha alone partakes of the *sūkaramaddava*, the remains being buried. This is the Buddha’s last meal; sharp sickness arises in him, with flow of blood and violent, deadly pains, but the Buddha controls them and sets out for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="580" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>. On the way he has to sit down at the foot of a tree. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="581" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> fetches him water to drink from the stream <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="582" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakuṭṭhā</span>, over which five hundred carts had just passed; but, through the power of the Buddha, the water is quite clear. Here the Buddha is visited by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="583" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusa</span>, the Mallan, who is converted and presents the Buddha with a pair of gold-coloured robes. The Buddha puts them on and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="584" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> notices the marvellous brightness and clearness of the Buddha’s body. The Buddha tells him that the body of a Buddha takes on this hue on the night before his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="585" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> and on the night of his passing away, and that he will die that night at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="586" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>. He goes to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="587" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakuṭṭhā</span>, bathes and drinks there and rests in a mango-grove. There he instructs <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="588" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> that steps must be taken to dispel any remorse that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="589" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span> may feel regarding the meal he gave to the Buddha.

From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="590" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakuṭṭhā</span> the Buddha crosses the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="591" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hiraññavatī</span> to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="592" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upavattana</span> *sāla*-grove in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="593" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>. There <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="594" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> prepares for him a bed with the head to the north. All the trees break forth into blossom and flowers cover the body of the Buddha. Divine *mandārava*-flowers and sandalwood powder fall from the sky, and divine music and singing sound through the air. But the Buddha says that the greater honour to him would be to follow his teachings.

The gods of the ten thousand world systems assemble to pay their last homage to the Buddha, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="595" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upavāṇa</span>, who stands fanning him, is asked to move away as he obstructs their view.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="596" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> asks for instruction on several points, including how the funeral rites should be performed; he then goes out and abandons himself to a fit of weeping; the Buddha sends for him, consoles him and speaks his praises. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="597" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> tries to persuade the Buddha not to die in a mud-and-wattle village, such as is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="598" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>, but the Buddha tells him how it was once the mighty <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="599" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sammata</span>, capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="600" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sudassana</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="601" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="602" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusināra</span> are informed that the Buddha will pass away in the third watch of the night, and they come with their families to pay their respects. The ascetic <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="603" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhadda</span> comes to see the Buddha and is refused admission by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="604" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, but the Buddha, overhearing, calls him in and converts him. Several minor rules of discipline are delivered, including the order for the excommunication of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="605" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span>. The Buddha finally asks the assembled monks to speak out any doubts they may have. All are silent and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="606" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> expresses his astonishment, but the Buddha tells him it is natural that the monks should have no doubts. Then, addressing the monks for the last time, he admonishes them in these words: “Decay is inherent in all component things; work out your salvation with diligence.” These were the Buddha’s last words. Passing backwards and forwards through various stages of *jhāna*, he attains *Parinibbāna*. There is a great earthquake and terrifying thunder, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="607" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahma Sahampati</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="608" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, king of the gods, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="609" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="610" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> utter stanzas, each proclaiming the feeling uppermost in his mind. It is the full-moon day of the month of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="611" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākha</span> and the Buddha is in his eightieth year.

The next day <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="612" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> informs the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="613" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="614" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> of the Buddha’s death, and for seven days they hold a great celebration. On the seventh day, following <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="615" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>’s instructions, they prepare the body for cremation, taking it in procession by the eastern gate to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="616" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makuṭabandhana</span> shrine, thus altering their proposed route, in order to satisfy the wishes of the gods, as communicated to them by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="617" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span>. The whole town is covered knee-deep with *mandārava*-flowers, which fall from the sky. When, however, four of the chief <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="618" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> try to light the pyre, their attempt is unsuccessful and they must wait until <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="619" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span>, coming with a company of five hundred monks, has saluted it. The Commentaries (e.g., DN­a.ii.603) add that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="620" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> greatly desired that the Buddha’s feet should rest on his head when he worshipped the pyre. The wish was granted: the feet appeared through the pyre, and when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="621" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> had worshipped them, the pyre closed together. The pyre burns completely away, leaving no cinders nor soot. Streams of water fall from the sky to extinguish it and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="622" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> pour on it scented water. They then place a fence of spears around it and continue their celebrations for seven days. At the end of that period there appear several claimants for the Buddha’s relics: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="623" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="624" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="625" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="626" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="627" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="628" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bulis</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="629" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Allakappa</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="630" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyas</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="631" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāmagāma</span>, a Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="632" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veṭhadīpa</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="633" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="634" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span>. But the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="635" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="636" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> refusing to share the relics with the others, there is danger of war. Then the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="637" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Doṇa</span> counsels concord and divides the relics into eight equal parts for the eight claimants. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="638" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Doṇa</span> takes for himself the measuring vessel and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="639" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moriyas</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="640" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pipphalivana</span>, who arrive late, carry off the ashes. *Thūpas* were built over these remains and feasts held in honour of the Buddha.

The concluding passage of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="641" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Parinibbānasutta</span> (DN.ii.167) states that the Buddha’s relics were eight measures, seven of which were honoured in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="642" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> and the remaining one in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="643" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> realm in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="644" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāmagāma</span>. One tooth was in heaven, one in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="645" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhāra</span>, a third in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="646" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāliṅga</span> (later taken to Ceylon), and a fourth in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="647" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> world. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="648" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>’s share was deposited in a *Thūpa* and forgotten. It was later discovered by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="649" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> (with the help of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="650" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>) and distributed among his eighty-four thousand monasteries. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="651" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> also recorded the finding of all the other relics except those deposited in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="652" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāmagāma</span>. These were later deposited in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="653" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Cetiya</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="654" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> (Mhv.xxxi.17ff). Other relics are also mentioned, such as the Buddha’s collar-bone, his alms bowl, etc. (Mhv.xvii.9ff; Mhv.i.37, etc.).

It is said (e.g., DN­a.iii.899) that just before the Buddha’s *Sāsana* disappears completely from the world, all the relics will gather together at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="655" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Cetiya</span>, and travelling from there to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="656" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadīpa</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="657" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratanacetiya</span>, assemble at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="658" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Bodhi</span>, together with the relics from other parts. There they will reform the Buddha’s golden hued body, emitting the six-coloured aura. The body will then catch fire and completely disappear, amid the lamentations of the ten thousand world-systems.

The Burmese claim that the Buddha visited their land and went to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="659" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohitacandanavihāra</span>, presented by the brothers <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="660" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Puṇṇa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="661" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Puṇṇa</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="662" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vānijagāma</span> (Ind.An.xxii., and Sās.36f.).

The Ceylon Chronicles record that the Buddha visited the Island on three separate occasions (Mhv.i.12ff; Dpv.i.45ff; ii.1ff etc.).

The first was while he was dwelling at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="663" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span>, awaiting the moment for the conversion of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="664" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tebhātikajaṭilas</span>, in the ninth month after the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="665" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>, on the full-moon day of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="666" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phussa</span> (Dec–Jan). He came to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="667" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāga</span> garden, and stood in the air over an assembly of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="668" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> then being held. He struck terror into their hearts and, at his suggestion, they left Ceylon and went in a body to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="669" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Giridīpa</span>, hard by. The Buddha gave a handful of his hair to the *Deva* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="670" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sumana</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="671" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanakūṭa</span> mountain, who built a *Thūpa* which was later enlarged into the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="672" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahiyaṅgaṇa Thūpa</span>. The Buddha again visited Ceylon in the fifth year, on the new-moon day of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="673" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta</span> (March-April), to check an imminent battle between two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="674" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> chiefs in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="675" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadīpa</span>; the combatants were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="676" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahodara</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="677" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷodara</span>, uncle and nephew, and the object of the quarrel was a gem-set throne. The Buddha appeared before them, accompanied by the *Deva* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="678" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samiddhi Sumana</span>, carrying a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="679" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājāyatana</span> tree from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="680" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, settled their quarrel and received, as a gift, the throne, the cause of the trouble. He left behind him both the throne and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="681" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājāyatana</span> tree for the worship of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="682" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> and accepted an invitation from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="683" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> king, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="684" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇi­akkhika</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="685" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalyāṇi</span>, to pay another visit to Ceylon. Three years later <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="686" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇi­akkhika</span> repeated the invitation and the Buddha came to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="687" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalyāṇi</span> with five hundred monks, on the second day of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="688" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesākha</span>. Having preached to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="689" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span>, he went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="690" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanakūṭa</span>, on the summit of which mountain he left the imprint of his foot (legend has it that other footprints were left by the Buddha, on the bank of the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="691" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nammadā</span>, on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="692" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saccabaddha</span> mountain and in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="693" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yoṇakapura</span>). He then stayed at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="694" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghavāpī</span> and from there visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="695" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Meghavana</span>, where he consecrated various spots by virtue of his presence, and proceeded to the site of the later <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="696" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Silācetiya</span>. From there he returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="697" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>.

Very little information as to the personality of the Buddha is available. We are told that he was golden-hued (e.g., Vin-a.iii.689), that his voice had the eight qualities of the *Brahmassāra* (e.g., DN.ii.211; MN.ii.166f. It is said that while an ordinary person spoke one word, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="698" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> could speak eight; but the Buddha could speak sixteen to the eight of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="699" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, MN­a.i.283) – fluency, intelligibility, sweetness, audibility, continuity, distinctness, depth and resonance – that he had a fascinating personality – he was described by his opponents as seductive (e.g., MN.i.269, 275) – that he was handsome, perfect alike in complexion and stature and noble of presence (e.g., MN.ii.167). He had a unique reputation as a teacher and trainer of the human heart. He was endowed with the thirty-two marks of the *Mahā Purisa* (for details of these, see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="700" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>). There is a legend that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="701" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span>, though slightly shorter, resembled the Buddha in appearance.

Attempts made, however, to measure the Buddha always failed; two such attempts are generally mentioned – one by a Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="702" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> and the other by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="703" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhu</span>, chief of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="704" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span> (DN­a.i.284f). The Buddha had the physical strength of many millions of elephants (e.g., Vibh­a.397), but his strength quickly ebbed away after his last meal and he had to stop at twenty-five places while travelling three *gāvutas* from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="705" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="706" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusināra</span> (DN­a.ii.573).

Mention is often made of the Buddha’s love of quiet and peace, and even the heretics respected his wishes in this matter, silencing their discussions at his approach (e.g., DN.i.178f; iii.39; even his disciples had a similar reputation, e.g., DN.iii.37). Examples are given of the Buddha refusing to allow noisy monks to live near him. (e.g., MN.i.456; see also MN.ii.122, where a monk was jogged by his neighbour because he coughed when the Buddha was speaking). He loved solitude and often spent long periods away from the haunts of men, allowing only one monk to bring him his meals, e.g., SN.v.12, 320; but this very love of solitude was sometimes brought against him. By intercourse with whom does he attain to lucidity in wisdom? they asked. His insight, they said, was ruined by his habit of seclusion (DN.iii.38).

According to one account (AN.i.181), it was his practice to spend part of the day in seclusion, but he was always ready to see anyone who urgently desired his spiritual counsel (e.g., AN.iv.438).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="707" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Govindasutta</span> (DN.ii.222f) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="708" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> is represented as having uttered “eight true praises” of the Buddha. Perhaps the most predominant characteristics of the Buddha were his boundless love and his eagerness to help all who sought him. His fondness for children is seen in such stories as those of the two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="709" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sopāka</span>s, of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="710" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumāra Kassapa</span>, of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="711" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Panthaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="712" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba Mallaputta</span> and also of the novices <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="713" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍita</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="714" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sukha</span>. His kindness to animals appears, for instance, in the introductory story of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="715" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Macchajātaka</span> and his interference on behalf of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="716" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>’s aged elephant, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="717" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddavatikā</span> (*q.v.*). The Buddha was extremely devoted to his disciples and encouraged them in every way in their difficult life. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="718" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="719" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Therīgāthā</span> are full of stories indicating that he watched, with great care, the spiritual growth and development of his disciples, understood their problems and was ready with timely interference to help them to win their aims. Such incidents as those mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="720" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddālisutta</span> (MN.i.445), the introduction to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="721" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Titthajātaka</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="722" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kañcakkhandhajātaka</span>, seem to indicate that he took a personal and abiding interest in all who came under him. It was his unvarying custom to greet with a smile all those who visited him, inquiring after their welfare and thus putting them at their ease (Vin.i.313). When anyone sought permission to question him, he made no conditions as to the topic of discussion. This is called *sabbaññupavāraṇa*, e.g., MN.i.230. When the Buddha himself asked a question of any of his interrogators, they could not remain silent, but were bound to answer; a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="723" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="724" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajirapāṇī</span> was always present to frighten those who did not wish to do so (e.g., MN.i.231).

The Buddha was not over­anxious to get converts, and when his visitors declared themselves his followers he would urge them to take time to consider the matter – e.g., in the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="725" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Acela Kassapa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="726" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli</span> *gahapati*.

When he was staying in a monastery, he paid daily visits to the sick ward to talk to the inmates and to comfort them (see, e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="727" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṭāgārasālā</span>). The charming story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="728" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūtigatatissa</span> shows that he sometimes attended on the sick himself, thus setting an example to his followers. In return for his devotion, his disciples adored him, but even among those who immediately surrounded him there were a few who refused to obey him implicitly – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="729" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lāḷudāyī</span>, the companions of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="730" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="731" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Punabbasuka</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="732" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chabbaggiyas</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="733" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sattarasavaggiyas</span> and others, not to mention <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="734" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> and his associates.

The Buddha seems to have shown a special regard for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="735" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="736" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="737" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> among the monks, and for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="738" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="739" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="740" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="741" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="742" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> among the laity. He seems to have been secretly amused by the very human qualities of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="743" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> and by his failure to appreciate the real superiority of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="744" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span>, his wife.

The Buddha always declared that he was among the happy ones of this earth, that he was far happier, for instance, than <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="745" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> (e.g., MN.i.94), and he remained unmoved by opposition or abuse, e.g., in the case of the organised conspiracy of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="746" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span> (Dhp­a.iv.1f.).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="747" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span> (p.134) mentions several illnesses of the Buddha: the injury to his foot has already been referred to; once when the humours of his body were disturbed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="748" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span> administered a purge (Vin.i.279); on another occasion he suffered from some stomach trouble which was cured by hot water, or, according to some, by hot gruel (Vin.i.210f.; Thag.185). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="749" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp­a.iv.232; Thag­a.i.311f) mentions another disorder of the humours cured by hot water obtained from the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="750" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devahita</span>, through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="751" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upavāṇa</span>. The Commentaries mention that he suffered, in his old age, from constant backache, owing to the severe austerities practised by him during the six years preceding his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="752" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>, and the unsuitable meals taken during that period were responsible for a dyspepsia which persisted throughout the rest of his life (SN­a.i.200), culminating in his last serious illness of dysentery. MN­a.i.465; DN­a.iii.974; see also DN.iii.209, when he was preaching to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="753" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="754" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="755" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (Ap.i.299f) contains a set of verses called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="756" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pubbakammapiloti</span>; these verses mention certain acts done by the Buddha in the past, which resulted in his having to suffer in various ways in his last birth. He was once a drunkard named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="757" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Munāli</span> and he abused the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="758" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="759" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Surabhi</span>. On another occasion he was a learned Brahmin, teacher of five hundred pupils. One day, seeing the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="760" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="761" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isigaṇa</span>, he spoke ill of him to his pupils, calling him “sensualist.” The result of this act was the calumny against him by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="762" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sundarikā</span> in this life.

In another life he reviled a disciple of a Buddha, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="763" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span>; for this he suffered in hell for twelve thousand years and, in his last life, was disgraced by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="764" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ciñcā</span>. Once, greedy for wealth, he killed his step-brothers, hurling them down a precipice; as a result, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="765" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> attempted to kill him by hurling down a rock. Once, as a boy, while playing on the highway, he saw a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="766" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> and threw a stone at him, and as a result, was shot at by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="767" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>’s hired archers. In another life he was a mahout, and seeing a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="768" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> on the road, drove his elephant against him; hence the attack by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="769" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālāgiri</span>. Once, as a king, he sentenced seventy persons to death, the reward for which he reaped when a splinter pierced his foot. Because once, as a fisherman’s son, he took delight in watching fish being caught, he suffered from a grievous headache when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="770" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Viḍūḍabha</span> slaughtered the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="771" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span>. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="772" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phussa Buddha</span> he asked the monks to eat barley instead of rice and, as a result, had to eat barley for three months at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="773" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verañja</span> (according to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="774" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> Dhp-a.iii.257, the Buddha actually had to starve one day at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="775" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasālā</span>, because none of the inhabitants were willing to give him alms.) Because he once killed a wrestler, he suffered from cramp in the back. Once, when a physician, he caused discomfort to a merchant by purging him, hence his last illness of dysentery. As <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="776" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotipāla</span>, he spoke disparagingly of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="777" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="778" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>, and in consequence had to spend six years following various paths before becoming the Buddha. He was one of the most short-lived Buddhas, but because of those six years his *Sāsana* will last longer (Vin-a.i.190f).

The Buddha was generally addressed by his own disciples as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="779" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhagavā</span>. He spoke of himself as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="780" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tathāgata</span>, while non-Buddhists referred to him as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="781" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="782" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Samaṇa</span>. Other names used are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="783" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Muni</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="784" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyamuni</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="785" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jina</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="786" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> (e.g., Sn.vs.345) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="787" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahma</span> (Sn.vs.91; Snp-a.ii.418), also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="788" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> (*q.v.*).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="789" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (AN.i.23ff) gives a list of the Buddha’s most eminent disciples, both among members of the Saṅgha and among the laity. Each one in the list is mentioned as having possessed pre-eminence in some particular respect.
