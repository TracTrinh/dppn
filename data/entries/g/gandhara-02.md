# Gandhāra 02

## English source

Gandhāra 02. One of the sixteen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janapadas</span> (countries) (AN.i.213; iv.252, etc.; in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Niddesa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Vastu</span> lists Gandhāra is omitted and others substituted). Its capital was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span>, famous for its university; its king in the time of the Buddha was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span>. There was friendly intercourse between him and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>. Merchants and visitors from one country to another were lodged and fed at the expense of the country’s king, and no tariffs were levied on their merchandise. There was constant exchange of goods and valuables, and on one occasion <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, wishing to send his friend a gift of particular value, despatched to him a letter containing news of the appearance in the world of the Buddha, the Dhamma and the Saṅgha. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span> read the letter he decided to become a follower of the Buddha, and ordained himself as a monk; then, leaving his kingdom, he travelled all the way to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> to see the Buddha (MN­a.ii.979ff). This conversion of Gandhāra’s king, however, does not seem to have had the effect of converting the rest of its people to the Buddha’s faith. The memory of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span> was evidently soon forgotten, for we find <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggaliputtatissa</span>, at the conclusion of the Third Council, sending the Thera <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhantika</span> to convert Gandhāra (Mhv.xii.3ff).

According to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>’s account, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span>’s kingdom was over one hundred leagues in extent (MN­a.ii.988), and the distance from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> was one hundred and ninety-two leagues (MN­a.ii.987; from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> it was one hundred and twenty leagues, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vīsaṁyojanasata</span>; Ja.i.395; ii.47). There was evidently a well-known caravan route linking the two countries, although Gandhāra was regarded as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">paccantimajanapada</span> a border country. (MN­a.ii.982; there was also constant trade between Gandhāra and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span>, Ja.iii.365ff. It would appear from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Niddesa</span> i.154 that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> was a regular centre of trade).

At the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhantika</span>’s visit, the people of Gandhāra were being harassed by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>-king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aravāḷa</span>, and the chronicles contain details of his conversion by the monk. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>-king, together with his retinue, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍaka</span> and his wife <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hāritā</span>, became devout followers of the Buddha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhantika</span> preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āsīvisūpamasutta</span>, and many thousands joined the Saṅgha (Mhv.xii.9ff; Smp.i.64f; Dpv.viii.4).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhāra</span> appears to have included <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kasmīra</span>, the two countries being always mentioned together as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kasmīra</span>-<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhāra</span>. They occupied the sites of the modern districts of Peshawar and Rawalpindi in the northern Punjab (PHAI. p.93). In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> the country formed part of his empire, and is mentioned as such in Rock Edict <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">V</span>. Before that it was subject to the Achaemenid kings. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhāra</span> was always famous for its red woollen shawls (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kambala</span>) (Snp­a.ii.487; Ja.vi.501).

Another king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> besides <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span> is mentioned – namely, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Naggaji</span>, who was a contemporary of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nimi</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span> (Ja.iii.377; cf. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ait. Brāhmaṇa</span> vii.34; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sat. Brāhmaṇa</span> viii.1, 4, 10; see also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhārarājā</span>).

One of the eye teeth of the Buddha was deposited in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhāra</span> (Bv.xxviii.6; DN.ii.167).
