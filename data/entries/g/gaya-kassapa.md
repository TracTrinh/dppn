# Gayā Kassapa

## English source

Gayā Kassapa. One of the three <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> brothers, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tebhātikajaṭilā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). On leaving the world with his brothers and becoming an ascetic, he gathered round him a company of two hundred other ascetics. They all lived at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayāsīsa</span>, hence his name (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Gayāsīse pabbajito ti Gayā Kassapo nāma jāto</span>). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā Kassapa</span> was converted, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā Kassapa</span>, with his followers, joined the Saṅgha, and at the conclusion of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ādittapariyāyasutta</span> they all became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span> (Vin.i.33f.; AN­a.i.165). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā Kassapa</span> is reported (Thag.v.345f) to have said that he used to bathe three times a day at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayātittha</span>, in order to wash away his sins during the festival of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayāphaggu</span>.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sikhī Buddha</span> he was a householder, and later became a forest-dwelling hermit. One day he saw the Buddha walking alone in the forest and offered him a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kola</span>-fruit (Thag­a.i.417f).

He is evidently identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koladāyaka</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>. Ap.ii.379; see also ii.483.
