# Gajabāhu

## English source

Gajabāhu, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabhuja</span>. King of Ceylon (1137-1153). He was the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vikkamabāhu II</span>. and succeeded his father to the throne (Cv.lx.88, according to the *Dimbulāgala Inscription*, his mother was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sundarī</span>). Thus he was the grandson of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijayabāhu I</span>. and of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tilokasundarī</span>, and came, therefore, of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāliṅga</span> stock. When he saw the increasing power of the Prince <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu</span> (afterwards <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu I</span>.), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhu</span> sent for him with many marks of favour and welcomed him at his court. In order to win the king’s confidence <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkama</span> gave his sister <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddavatī</span> to be his queen, but when he saw that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhu</span> was becoming suspicious of his power he left <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulatthipura</span> and made preparations to wage war against him. In the campaign that followed, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhu</span> suffered many reverses and, in the end, fell into the hands of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkama</span>’s forces. With great difficulty <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkama</span> saved him from death, but in the meantime <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māṇābharaṇa</span> managed to get <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhu</span> into his power and cast him into a dungeon. From there he was rescued by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu</span> and fled to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṭṭhasāra</span>. Meanwhile, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu</span> had consolidated his power, and his officers captured <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulatthipura</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhu</span>, being able to see no other help, implored the monks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulatthipura</span> to intercede on his behalf, and, at their request, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu</span> left to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhu</span> the enjoyment of his possessions (this is rather odd, especially in view of the fact that he invited heretical nobles to come to Ceylon, Cv.lxx.53). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhu</span> took up his abode at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgātaḷāka</span> and spent his last days there in comparative peace. As he had no heir and no brothers, he bequeathed his kingdom to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu</span>, and engraved his will on a stone tablet at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇḍalagirivihāra</span>. He was cremated at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṭṭhasāra</span> (details of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhu</span>’s reign and his fights with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu</span> are contained in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Vaṁsa</span>, particularly in chapters 63, 66, 67, 70, 71). See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhukagāmaṇi</span>.
