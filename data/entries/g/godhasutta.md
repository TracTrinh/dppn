# Godhasutta

## English source

Godhasutta or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāmasutta</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> visits <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godha</span> the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> and asks him what qualities, possessed by a *Sotāpanna*, will secure for him ultimate enlightenment. There are three, answers <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godha</span> – unwavering loyalty to the Buddha, the Dhamma and the Saṅgha. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godha</span>, in his turn, asks the same question of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span>, the latter answers that there are four, and adds the possession of Āriyan virtues.

The two seek the Buddha to ask his opinion. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> declares to the Buddha that he would accept the Buddha’s decision in face of the opinion of everyone else in the universe. In answer to a question of the Buddha, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godha</span> answers that of a person who holds such a view he has nothing but good to say, the conclusion evidently being that he accepts <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span>’s opinion in preference to his own. SN.v.371f.
