# Goṭhakābhaya 02

## English source

Goṭhakābhaya 02. King of Ceylon (302-15 CE.). He was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lambakaṇṇa</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahiyaṅgaṇa</span>, and having gone to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghatissa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghabodhi</span>, he, with their help, slew the reigning king, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijaya</span>, and they reigned in succession (Mhv.xxxvi.58). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Goṭhakābhaya</span> became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghabodhi</span>’s treasurer but, seeing the king’s weakness, he led a rebellion against him and captured the throne (vv.91, 98-117; for details see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghabodhi</span>). In addition to other religious works, he built the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meghavaṇṇābhaya</span> *vihāra*. He banished from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> sixty monks who had accepted the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vetulla</span> heresy, but a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Coḷa</span> monk, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghamitta</span>, defeated in discussion the Thera <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Goṭhābhaya</span>, the king’s uncle, and became the king’s favourite, obtaining the position of tutor to the king’s two sons, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jeṭṭhatissa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sena</span>. The king was also known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meghavaṇṇābhaya</span> (v.98).
