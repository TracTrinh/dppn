# Gandhamādana

## English source

Gandhamādana. A mountain range beyond the seven ranges of:

- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Cūḷa Kāḷa</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mahā Kāḷa</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Nāgapaliveṭhana</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Candagabbha</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Suriyagabbha</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Suvaṇṇapassa</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Himavā</span>

It is one of the five mountain ranges that encircle <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anotatta</span>. It is crowned with a tableland, is green in colour (*muggavaṇṇa*), and covered with various medicinal plants. It shines from afar “like a glowing fire on a new-moon night.”

In the range is an inclined slope (*pabbhāra*) named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandamūlaka</span> containing three caves, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suvaṇṇaguhā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇiguhā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rajataguhā</span>, which are the abodes of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>.

At the entrance to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇiguhā</span> is a tree named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mañjūsaka</span>, one league in height and in girth; on this tree bloom all the flowers that grow both on land and in water, and especially do they bloom on the occasions of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>’ visits; round the tree is the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabbaratanamāla</span>.

There the *Sammajjanakavāta* sweeps the ground, the *Samakaranavāta* levels the sand, and the *Siñcanakavāta* sprinkles water from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anotatta</span>.

The *Sugandhakaraṇavāta* brings all the perfumes of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span>, the *Ocinakavāta* plucks flowers, and the *Santharaṇakavāta* spreads them.

In the *māla* seats are always ready for the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>, who on fast days and on their own birthdays assemble there. When a new <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> arises in the world, he goes first to Gandhamādana and other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>, who may be in the world, assemble there to greet him, and they all sit rapt in *samādhi*. Then the senior among them asks the newcomer to describe how he came to be a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> (Snp­a.i.52, 66f; ii.437; AN­a.ii.759; Ud­a.300, etc.; MN­a.ii.585).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> who live on Gandhamādana will often enter into *samādhi* for seven days, and at the end of that period seek alms from someone on whom they wish to bestow a special favour, that he may thereby obtain merit (e.g., Dhp­a.iii.368f; iv.121, 199f; Ja.iv.16). These Buddhas will sometimes leave the mountain, and, having admonished those whom they wish to help, return again (e.g., Ja.iii.453).

Besides <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>, others are also mentioned as having resided in Gandhamādana – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span> (Ja.iv.393), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Naḷinikā</span> (Ja.v.186), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bahusodarī</span> (Ja.vi.83); also the *Deva* king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadatta</span> (Thag­a.i.138), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span>, with his family, after he renounced his kingdom (Ja.vi.528f.). It is also said that *Kinnaras* (Ja.iv.438) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> (Rockhill, 169) lived on the slopes of Gandhamādana. It was among the places visited by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khadiravaniya Revata</span> (AN­a.i.139).

It is not explicitly mentioned that all <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> die in Gandhamādana, but the inference seems to be such. Thus, once, five hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> led by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Paduma</span> died there, and their bodies were cremated (Thag­a.ii.141).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka Commentary</span> (vi.79) explains Gandhamādana as *gandhena madakaro pabbato*.

The fragrant tree <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhujaka</span> grows only in heaven and in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhamādana</span> (Vv-a.162).

It is said that the Buddha <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Metteya</span> will retire for a while to Gandhamādana, after spending his first rainy season (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāgatavaṁsa</span> v.81).
