# Ghaṭīkāra

## English source

Ghaṭīkāra. One of the great <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> he was a potter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vehaliṅga</span>, looking after his blind parents. He was a very pious and devoted follower of the Buddha, ministering to him better than anyone else, and the Buddha accepted his invitation in preference to that of the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. It is said that when the Buddha was in need of anything he would go to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span>’s house and take it, whether he were at home or not, so great was his confidence in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span>’s piety. Once, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span> was absent, the people, at the Buddha’s suggestion, took away the thatch from his house to roof the hut of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tathāgata</span>. For three months <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span>’s house remained open to the sky, but no rain fell on it, so great was his faith (Mil.223f). According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Naḷapānajātaka</span> (Ja.i.172), no rain will ever fall on the site of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span>’s house as long as this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappa</span> lasts.

The Bodhisatta, who at the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span> was a young Brahmin named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotipāla</span>, was the friend of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span> but had no faith in the Buddha, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span>, having failed to persuade him to visit the Buddha, in the end took him by force. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotipāla</span> was converted and joined the Saṅgha, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span>, as the only support of his parents, could not renounce the world. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kikī</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, having heard of his virtues from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>, sent him five hundred cartloads of the choicest rice, etc., but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span> returned the gifts, saying that he had plenty for himself (MN.ii.46ff; SN.i.35f; Bv.xxv.41; Snp-a.i.152).

After death, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span> was born as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Brahmā</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avihā</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>-world, and an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmī</span>. He was evidently already a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sakadāgāmi</span> before his death (see Dhp­a.i.380), but he did not wish his attainments to be known (AN­a.i.44).

He provided the Buddha with the eight requisites of a monk when the Buddha, having left the world, decided to lead the life of a bhikkhu (Ja.i.65; Snp-a.ii.382; Bv­a.236; Vv-a.314). The begging bowl, then provided by him, vanished when the Buddha was given a bowl of milk-rice by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujātā</span> (Ja.i.69).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (SN.i.35f; 60), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span> visited the Buddha some time after the Awakening and the Buddha reminded him of their former friendship. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭīkāra</span>, on that occasion, speaks of several others (besides <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotipāla</span>) who had been his friends in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vehaliṅga</span> – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phalagaṇḍa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khaṇḍadeva</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhuraggi</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṅgiya</span>. They had listened to the Buddha’s teaching and, after death, were born in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Avihā</span>-world, where he himself was. In this context the Buddha addresses him as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaggava</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>).
