# Giñjakāvasatha

## English source

Giñjakāvasatha. A brick hall at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nādikā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ñātikā</span>). The Buddha stayed there on various occasions during his visits to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nādikā</span>. It was during one of these visits that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambapālī</span> presented her park to the Buddha and the Saṅgha (Vin.i.232).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Giñjakāvasatha</span> the Buddha preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Gosiṅgasutta</span> (MN.i.205), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janavasabhasutta</span> (DN.ii.200) and several discourses on *maraṇassati* (e.g., AN.iii.303f; 306f; 391f; iv.320f); also the sermons to the Elder <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sandha</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccānagotta</span> (AN.v.322f) and the Elder <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāyana</span> (SN.ii.153f; see also SN.ii.74; iv.90).

Both in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janavasabhasutta</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Giñjakāvasathasutta</span> (SN.v.356f), which was preached at the same place, the Buddha is represented as having answered questions regarding the destiny and the rebirth of several residents of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nādikā</span>. Does this perhaps mean that the people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nādikā</span> were more interested in this problem than the people of other places? It was by way of finding a permanent solution to these questions that the Buddha preached to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Giñjakāvasatha</span> the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammādāsa</span> (Mirror of Truth) on his last visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nādikā</span>, as described in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Parinibbānasutta</span> (DN 16) (DN.ii.91ff; see also SN.v.357).

The Commentaries (e.g., MN­a.i.424) state that once the Buddha arrived at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nādikā</span> while travelling in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjī</span> country, and the people there built for him a residence entirely made of bricks (*giñjakā*), hence its name. Later, residences were also built for the monks, complete with all requirements. The bricks were evidently a special architectural feature, and this confirms the belief that buildings were generally of wood. The “Brick Hall” was, however, not designed for the Buddha and his monks alone, for we find mention of members of other sects staying there – e.g., the *Paribbājaka* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiya</span> Kaccāna (SN.iv.401). The building was probably a resting place for all travellers.
