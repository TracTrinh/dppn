# Gandhabbā

## English source

Gandhabbā, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhabba</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhabbas</span>. A class of semi-divine beings who inhabit the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cātummahārājika</span>-realm and are the lowest among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> (DN.ii.212). They are generally classed together with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> (e.g., AN.iv.200, 204, 207). Beings are born among them as a result of having practised the lowest form of *sīla* (DN.ii.212, 271).

It is a disgrace for a monk to be born in the Gandhabba-world (DN.ii.221, 251, 273f). The Gandhabbas are regarded as the heavenly musicians, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suriyavaccasā</span> and her father <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Timbarū</span> are among their number (DN.ii.264).

They wait on such <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, and the males among them form the masculine counterpart of the *accharā*, the nymphs. Their king is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhataraṭṭha</span>, ruler of the eastern quarter (DN.ii.257). Other chieftains are also mentioned (DN.ii.258): <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Panāda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Opamañña</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s charioteer <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātali</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittasena</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nala</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janesabha</span>.

The Gandhabbas are sometimes described as *vihaṅgamā* (going through the air) (AN.ii.39; AN­a.ii.506). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āṭānāṭiyasutta</span> (DN.iii.203, 204) the Gandhabbas are mentioned among those likely to trouble monks and nuns in their meditations in solitude. The Buddha says that beings are born among the *Gandhabbakāyikādevā* because they wish to be so; they are described as dwelling in the fragrance of root-wood, of bark and sap, and in that of flowers and scents (SN.iii.250f).

It is often stated that the Gandhabbas preside over conception; this is due to an erroneous translation of the word Gandhabba in passages (e.g., MN.i.157, 265f) dealing with the circumstances necessary for conception (*mātāpitaro ca sannipatitā honti, mātā ca utunī hoti, gandhabbo ca paccupaṭṭhito hoti*).

The Commentaries (e.g., MN­a.i.481f) explain that here Gandhabba means *tatrūpakasatta – tasmiṁ okāse nibbattanako satto* – meaning a being fit and ready to be born to the parents concerned. The *Ṭīkā* says that the word stands for *gantabba*. See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhabbarājā</span>.
