# Ghositārāma

## English source

Ghositārāma. A monastery in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>, built by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosita</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span>, *q.v.*) for the use of the Buddha and the monks. The Buddha often stayed there during his visits to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> and numerous incidents are mentioned in the books in connection with the monastery. It was because of a dispute between two monks of the Ghositārāma, one expert in the Vinaya and one in the Dhamma, that the first schism arose in the Saṅgha, driving the Buddha himself to seek quiet in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārileyyaka</span> forest. Vin.i.337f; MN.i.320; Dhp­a.i.44ff; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> monks were evidently somewhat peculiar (see Vin.iv.197).

Even at other times the Buddha seems to have sought solitude in this forest during his sojourns at the Ghositārāma (See, e.g., SN.iii.96f). It was here that the Buddha decreed the *ukkhepaniyakamma* for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span>, who refused to acknowledge and atone for his offences (Vin.ii.21f), and here that he laid down the procedure in that connection to be followed. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> was at Ghositārāma when he first conceived the idea of using <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> for his own ends (Vin.ii.184f). The Buddha was there at the time and it is said that the *Devaputta* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakudha</span> appeared before <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Moggallāna</span> to warn him of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>’s schemes. The information was reported to the Buddha, who warned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> not to pass it on to others. The Buddha then proceeded to tell <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> of the five kinds of teachers which appear in the world (AN.iii.122f). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> is several times spoken of as staying in the Ghositārāma, sometimes with the Buddha, sometimes alone. On one such occasion he asks the Buddha why women should suffer from certain disabilities as compared with men (AN.ii.82). And again (AN.iii.132f), what are the circumstances which conduce to ease (*phāsuvihāra*) in the case of monks? Could it be said of a follower of the Buddha that his attainments depend on the length of time during which he has observed the Buddha’s teachings? Once <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> visits (AN.iv.37f), at her request, a nun living nearby reported to be ill and enamoured of him. The mere sight of him causes her recovery, but he preaches to her on the impermanent nature of the body and makes her realise the truth (AN.ii.144f). Among those who visit <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> at the Ghositārāma and discuss various matters with him are mentioned: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosita</span> (SN.iv.113), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uṇṇābha</span> (SN.v.271f.), a householder, follower of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t28" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t28">Ājīvakas</span> (AN.i.217f.), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddaji</span> (AN.iii.202). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span> twice visits him there, once to ask for a description of consciousness (SN.iv.169f.), and again to quote a verse uttered by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcālacaṇḍa</span> *Devaputta* and to ask <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> to explain it (AN.iv.449). We find him also joining in a discussion which ensued on a sermon to the monks by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> (AN.iv.426f.). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span> preached to large audiences at the Ghositārāma and was evidently appreciated, for we find <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> reporting it to the Buddha and being told that it is no easy matter to preach to a large assembly with acceptance (AN.iii.184f.). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yuganaddhasutta</span> is a discourse preached by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> to the monks at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghositārāma</span> of his own accord (AN.ii.156f.). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> is several times mentioned in connection with incidents taking place at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghositārāma</span>. Mention has already been made of the *ukkhepaniyakamma* declared on him. A devoted householder, wishing to build a *vihāra* for him, asked him for a site. Vin.iii.155f; mention is made of other misdemeanours which he committed in order to have a fine *vihāra* (Vin.iv.47).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> started cutting down trees and other things, in order to clear the site, and this led to great uproar. On another occasion he is reported to the Buddha for refusing to listen to his colleagues and the Buddha chides him (Vin.iii.77, also iv.35f, 113. See also SN.iii.132f). It was when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> was at the Ghositārāma that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> came, at the bidding of the monks, to inflict on him the *brahmadaṇḍa* (Vin.ii.292).

Among others mentioned as staying at the Ghositārāma are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mūsila</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saviṭṭha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span>, in the company of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> (SN.ii.115), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍola Bhāradvāja</span> – who converts <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> when the latter comes to see him (SN.iv.110f.) and earns the Buddha’s praises for his attainments (SN.v.224) – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upavāṇa</span> (SN.v.76), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhiya</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> (AN.ii.239). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> is there at the time of the schism of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> monks but refuses to intervene. He indulges, instead, in his powers of clairvoyance and mention is made of a visit paid to him by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manāpakāyikā</span> *Devā* (AN.iv.262f).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dāsaka</span> and a number of other monks were once staying in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghositārāma</span>; on learning that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemaka</span> lies ill in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Badarikārāma</span>, one *gāvuta* away, the others send <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dāsaka</span> several times to and fro to ask various questions of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemaka</span>. In the end, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemaka</span> himself comes to them to solve their difficulties (SN.iii.126f).

See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambiyasutta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jāliyasutta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sandakasutta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upakkilesasutta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sekhasutta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Daḷhadhammajātaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambījātaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suripānajātaka</span>, all preached while the Buddha was staying at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghositārāma</span>.

Thirty thousand monks from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghositārāma</span>, under the leadership of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Urudhammarakkhita</span>, were present at the foundation of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>. Mhv.xxix.34.
