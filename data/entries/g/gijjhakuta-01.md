# Gijjhakūṭa 01

## English source

Gijjhakūṭa 01. One of the five hills encircling <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. It was evidently a favourite resort of those who followed the religious life (it was so even in times gone by, see, e.g., Ja.ii.55).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> seems to have been attracted by its solitude, and is mentioned as having visited it on several occasions, sometimes even in the dark, in drizzling rain, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> made unsuccessful attempts to frighten him (SN.i.109).

It was on the slopes of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span>, where the Buddha was wandering about, that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> hurled at him a mighty stone to kill him, but only a splinter injured his foot (Vin.ii.193, etc.).

It was there also that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span> Komārabhacca administered a purgative to the Buddha (AN­a.i.216).

Among those who visited the Buddha on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span> are mentioned:

- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sahampati</span> (SN.i.153),
- the youth <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Māgha</span> (Sn., p.86),
- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Yakkha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Inda</span> (SN.i.206),
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sakka</span> (SN.i.233; iv.102),
- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Paribbājaka</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sajjha</span> (AN.iv.371),
- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kassapagotta</span> monk (AN.i.237),
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Pañcasikha</span> (SN.iv.103; DN.ii.220),
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sutavā</span> (AN.iv.369),
- the four kings of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Cātummahārājika</span> world and their followers (DN.iii.195),
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Abhayarājakumāra</span> (SN.v.126),
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Upaka</span> Maṇḍikāputta (AN.ii.181),
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Dhammika</span> (AN.iii.368), and
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Vassakāra</span> (AN.iv.18; DN.ii.72).

Several well-known *Suttas* were preached on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span> – e.g., the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammika</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chaḷabhijāti</span> *Suttas*, the discourse on the seven <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparihānīyadhammā</span> (AN.iv.21f.), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sāropamasutta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āṭānāṭiyasutta</span> (see also SN.ii.155, 185, 190, 241; iii.121; AN.ii.73; iii.21; iv.160).

It is said (AN­a.i.412) that in due course a *vihāra* was erected on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span> for the Buddha and his monks; here cells were erected for the use of monks who came from afar, but these cells were so difficult of access that monks arriving late at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> would ask <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba Mallaputtatissa</span> to find accommodation for them in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span>, in order to test his capabilities (Vin.ii.76; Dhp­a.iii.321f).

Mention is made of several eminent monks who stayed at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span> from time to time – e.g.,

- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sāriputta</span> (MN.iii.263; AN.iii.300; SN.ii.155),
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Ānanda</span> (AN.iii.383),
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mahā Kassapa</span>,
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Anuruddha</span>,
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Puṇṇa Mantāṇiputta</span>,
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Upāli</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Devadatta</span> (SN.ii.155),
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Cunda</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Channa</span> (SN.iv.55).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> fell ill there, and ultimately committed suicide (another monk is mentioned as having thrown himself down from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span> because he was discontented with his life, Vin.iii.82. According to one account, AN­a.i.146f, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vakkali</span>, too, committed suicide by throwing himself from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span>; but see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vakkali</span>).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lakkhaṇa</span> are reported to have stayed there, and to have seen many inhabitants of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> reborn in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span> as *Petas* (SN.ii.254; Vin.iii.104; for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> see also AN.iv.75).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mettiyabhummajakas</span> (Vin.iii.167) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chabbaggiyas</span> (ibid., 82) were also in the habit of visiting the hill.

Several places are mentioned as having been visited by the Buddha during his sojourns on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span>, and it may be inferred from accounts given of these visits that these places were within easy reach of the hill. Such, for example, are:

- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Paṭibhāṇakūṭa</span> (SN.v.448),
- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sītavana</span>, where the Buddha went to visit <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Soṇa</span> (AN.iii.374),
- the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sappinī</span>, on the banks of which lived various <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Paribbājakas</span>, including <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sarabha</span> (AN.i.185; ii.29, 176),
- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Paribbājakārama</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Udumbarikā</span>,
- the residence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Nigrodha</span>, near the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Moranivāpa</span> on the bank of the lake <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sumāgadhā</span> (DN.iii.39), and
- the park <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Maddakucchi</span>, where the Buddha was removed after the injury to his foot (Dhp­a.ii.164).
- The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sūkarakhatalena</span> was on the slope of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Gijjhakūṭa</span>, and there the Buddha was once visited by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Dīghanakha</span> (SN.v.233; MN.i.497).
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Jīvaka</span>’s mango-grove lay between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Gijjhakūṭa</span> and the walls of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Rājagaha</span> (DN­a.i.150).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span> was so-called, either because its peak was like a vulture’s beak, or because it was the resort of many vultures (Snp­a.ii.417; AN­a.i.412; MN­a.i.291, etc).

Cunningham (CAGI.534), on the authority of both Faxian and Xuanzang, identifies <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span> with the modern <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sailagiri</span>, about two and a half miles to the north-east of the old town. It is also called Giriyek Hill. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span> is sometimes referred to as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhapabbata</span> (Ja.ii.50; iii.255, 484) and as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjha</span>. Ja.vi.204, 212.
