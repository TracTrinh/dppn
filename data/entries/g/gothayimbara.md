# Goṭhayimbara

## English source

Goṭhayimbara. One of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span>’s chief warriors. He was the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāga</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Niṭṭhulaviṭṭhika</span> and owed his name to his dwarfish stature. He pulled up <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">imbara</span>-trees from a forest clearing, thus demonstrating his enormous strength. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kākavaṇṇatissa</span>, hearing of this, sent for him to the court (Mhv.xxiii.49ff; the Rasavāhini ii.28, says he subdued a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayasena</span>). He took a prominent part in the attack on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijitapura</span>, using a coconut-palm as weapon (Mhv.xxv.27, 32, 44f).

It is said (Mhv­ṭ.452) that in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> he gave daily milk-rice to the monks.

See also Ras.ii.87f, which says that he was so-called because he was short, but that according to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttaravihāra</span> monks he was given the name because, when he went hunting, he killed animals by dashing them on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">koṭṭhayimbara</span>-trees. Once when he was holding a feast in his house, after defeating the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Damiḷas</span>, a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayasena</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariṭṭhapabbata</span>, happened to pass by, and, entering the house, fell in love with Goṭhayimbara’s wife. Goṭhayimbara challenged him to a fight and defeated him. He celebrated the victory for seven days and went to the palace drunk. Being refused admission, he swam across to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāvīrapaṭṭana</span>, and, having gone as far as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span> in search of holy monks, was about to kill himself in despair when an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> Thera appeared before him and ordained him. He later attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship.
