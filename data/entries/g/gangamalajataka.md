# Gaṅgamālajātaka

## English source

Gaṅgamālajātaka (Ja 421). The Bodhisatta once took service under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suciparivāra</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, in whose household everyone kept the fast on *uposatha*-days. The Bodhisatta, not knowing this, went to work as usual on the fast day, but, on discovering that no one else was working and the reason for their abstention, he refused to take any food, and as a result of his fasting died in the night. He was reborn as son of the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, and later became king under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udaya</span>. On meeting <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aḍḍhamāsaka</span> (*q.v.*), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udaya</span> shared the kingdom with him, but one day <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aḍḍhamāsaka</span>, discovering that he harboured a desire to kill <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udaya</span>, renounced his kingdom and became an ascetic. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udaya</span> heard of this he uttered a stanza, referring to his own past life, but no one could understand the meaning of it. The queen, anxious to learn the meaning, told the king’s barber <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgamāla</span> how he might win the king’s favour, and when the king offered him a boon, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgamāla</span> chose to have the stanza explained to him. When he learnt how <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udaya</span> had won a kingdom as a result of having kept the fast for half a day, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgamāla</span> renounced the world and, developing asceticism, became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>. Later he visited King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udaya</span> and preached to him and his retinue, addressing the king by name. The queen-mother took offence at this and abused <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgamāla</span>, but the king begged him to forgive her. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgamāla</span> returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhamādana</span>, though urged by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udaya</span> to stay in the royal park.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aḍḍhamāsaka</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulamātā</span> was the queen.

The story was related by the Buddha to some lay-followers to encourage them in their observance of the *Uposatha* (Ja.iii.444ff). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgamāla</span> is mentioned as an example of a man who realised the evils of *taṇhā* and renounced desire, e.g., Ja.iv.174.
