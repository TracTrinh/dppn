# Goṇa

## English source

Goṇa (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Goṇaka</span>). A river to the south of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>. On its banks, King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷābhaya</span> built the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Gallakavihāra</span> (Mhv.xxxv.13), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅkanāsikatissa</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Maṅgalavihāra</span> (Mhv.v.113). Once, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhātusena</span> was fleeing from his enemies with a Thera*,* his uncle, the river was found to be in flood, but a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> king took them across (Cv.xxxviii.24). Later, when building the *Kālavāpi*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhātusena</span> dammed up the river. Cv.v.42.

It is identified with the modern Kalā-oya (Cv.Trs.i.30, n.3).3.
