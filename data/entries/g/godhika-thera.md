# Godhika Thera

## English source

Godhika Thera. Son of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malla</span> chief in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span>. He went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> with his friends, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subāhu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Valliya</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttiya</span>, and there he saw the Twin Miracle and joined the Saṅgha, later attaining <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship (but see below.) At <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> built a hut for him but forgot the roof. The gods prevented rain from falling till this error was rectified. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godhika</span> and his friends had been companions in good deeds in the past, especially in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siddhattha Buddha</span> and of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>. Eighty-seven <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> ago <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godhika</span> was seven times king, under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sena</span> (Thag.vs.51; Thag­a.i.123f; Ap.i.140).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta</span> (SN.i.120f; SN­a.i.144f; also Dhp­a.i.431f) account, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godhika</span> lived on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷasilā</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isigilipassa</span>. There he made various vain attempts to win <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship, achieving only temporary emancipation of mind, from which he then fell away (the Dhp­a.i.431f adds that he suffered from a disease brought about by hard work). Six times this happened and then he decided to commit suicide by cutting his throat. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> saw this and reported it to the Buddha, but when the Buddha arrived it was too late and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godhika</span> lay “supine on his couch with his shoulders twisted around.” The Buddha, however, declared that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godhika</span> had attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nibbāna</span>. The Commentary states that, after cutting his throat, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godhika</span> so checked his final agony that he won <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship.
