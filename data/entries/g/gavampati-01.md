# Gavampati 01

## English source

Gavampati 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gavampati Thera</span>. An <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. He was a son of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, and one of the four lay companions of the Thera <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span>, who, when they heard of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span>’s renunciation, imitated him and won <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship. Later, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gavampati</span> lived in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Añjanavana</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span>. One day, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> visited the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Añjanavana</span>, some of the monks accompanying him slept on the sandbanks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhū</span>. The river rose in the night and there was great dismay. The Buddha sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gavampati</span> to stem the flood, which he did by his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span>-power. The water stopped afar off, looking like a mountain peak.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sikhī Buddha</span> he was a huntsman and seeing the Buddha offered him flowers. Later he built a parasol and a railing for the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thūpa</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇāgamana</span>. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> he was a rich householder possessed of many cattle. One day he saw an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> eating his meal in the sun for lack of shade, and built for him a shelter and planted in front of it a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sirīsa</span>-tree. As a result he was born in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cātummahārājika</span> world, and his palace was known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Serissaka</span> (Vin.i.18f.; Thag.v.38; Thag­a.i.103f; Vv-a.331f; DN­a.iii.814 gives a slightly different version of the origin of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Serissakavimāna</span>).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gavampati</span> was the teacher of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāga</span>, son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madhu Vāseṭṭha</span> (Thag­a.i.443). It is said that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Serissakavimāna</span>, occupied by Gavampati, remained in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cātummahārajika</span> world even after he had left it (DN.ii.356f; DN­a.iii.814 says he went there because he found the “climate” (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">utu</span>) more agreeable. Snp­a.i.347 says it was because he, like <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍola Bhāradvāja</span>, loved his old haunts).

There <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gavampati</span> often spent his siesta and held conversations with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāyāsi</span>, who sent through him a message to the inhabitants of the earth, that they should profit by the example of him (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāyāsi</span>) and discriminate in the bestowal of their gifts.

The Dulvā mentions that after the Buddha’s death, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> wished to hold a Convocation of the chief monks, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span> was sent as a special messenger to summon Gavampati, who was then in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Serissakavimāna</span> (Rockhill, p.149f). But Gavampati did not attend, his death being imminent. Instead he sent his bowl and three robes as a gift to the Saṅgha.

Immediately afterwards he died, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span> carried out his funeral rites.

Gavampati is evidently identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Girinelapūjaka</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (ii.457).

See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gavampatisutta</span>.
