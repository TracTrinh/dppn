# Gahvaratīriya Thera

## English source

Gahvaratīriya Thera (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gavharatīriya Thera</span>). He was a Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggidatta</span>. Having seen the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamakapāṭihāriya</span>, he entered the Saṅgha and lived in a spot called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gahvaratīra</span> – hence his name – and there in due course became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. On his return to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, his relations held a great almsgiving in his honour and requested him to live near them. But be refused this request and returned to the forest.

He was a hunter in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sikhī Buddha</span>, and was delighted by the sound of the Buddha’s voice as he preached (Thag.v.31; Thag­a.i.91).

He is probably identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosasaññaka</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (Ap.ii.451), but the same verses are also attributed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammika</span>. Thag­a.i.398.
