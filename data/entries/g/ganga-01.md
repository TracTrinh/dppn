# Gaṅgā 01

## English source

Gaṅgā 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅga</span>,<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14"> Ganges</span>. One of the five great rivers (*Mahā Nadī*) that water <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>, the others being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamunā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aciravatī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhū</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahī</span> (e.g., Vin.ii.237; SN.ii.135; v.401; AN.iv.101; v.22; Mil.114 mentions ten).

The Commentaries give a long description of their origin (e.g., Snp­a.ii.438f; AN­a.ii.761ff; MN­a.ii.586; Ud­a.301). From the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anotatta</span> lake flow four rivers: that from the south circles the lake three times under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avaṭṭagaṅgā</span>, then as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇhagaṅgā</span> flows straight for sixty leagues along the surface of a rock, comes into violent contact with a vertical rock, and is thrown upwards as a column of water three *gāvutas* in circumference; this column, known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Akāsagaṅgā</span>, flows through the air for sixty leagues, falls on to the rock <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tiyaggala</span>, excavating it to a depth of fifty leagues, thus forming a lake which is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tiyaggala</span> *pokkharaṇī*; then the river, under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bahalagaṅgā</span>, flows through a chasm in the rock for sixty leagues, then, under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ummaggagaṅgā</span>, through a tunnel for a further sixty leagues, and finally coming upon the oblique rock <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijjha</span>, divides into five streams, forming the five rivers above mentioned.

Among places mentioned as being on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgā</span> are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Campā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ayojjha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbhilā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkāvelā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Payāga</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgā</span> formed one of the most important means of communication and trade for the districts through which it flowed – e.g., from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>. The district to the north of the river and bordering on the kingdom of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span> was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttarāpa</span> (Snp­a.ii.439). The river was five hundred leagues in length (SN­a.ii.119).

The name of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgā</span> appears again and again in similes and metaphors in the Pāli books:

- its sands are immeasurable (SN.iv.376);
- its waters cannot be made bracken by adding to them a grain of salt (AN.i.250);
- it is full of foam, and yet its foam is empty (SN.iii.140);
- it were folly to wish to hold up the course of its waters with one’s fist (SN.iv.298);
- as the river finds repose only in the ocean, so do the followers of the Buddha find repose only in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Nibbāna</span> (MN.i.493);
- some things are as inevitable as that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Gaṅgā</span> should flow into the sea (SN.iv.179);
- there is no such thing as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Gaṅgā</span> apart from its sand, its water, and its banks;
- to be cast on the other side of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Gaṅgā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t23">pāragaṅgāya</span>) is great misfortune (see, e.g., SN.i.207, Snp-a.i.228).
- The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Gaṅgā</span> flows from west to east (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t23">pācīnaninnā</span>, SN.iv.191);
- during the rains it is so full of water that even a crow could drink water from its bank (Vin.i.230);
- sometimes the banks would be flooded and the buildings on them destroyed (SN­a.i.164), and people would find difficulty in crossing;
- at others it was shallow and could be crossed by means of a reed bridge (Snp-a.i.18);
- cattle could easily be driven from one bank to another (MN.i.225).
- At various spots were ferries where boatmen plied for hire (e.g., Ja.iii.230).
- On its banks, on the higher reaches, were numerous snakes and parrots (Ja.ii.145, iii.491),
- and all along the banks were hermitages (e.g., Ja.iii.476, v.191, etc.).
- Men always bathed in the river, and on festival days even women of very good family came for water-sports, sometimes spending the whole day in the river; kings also came with their retinues (e.g., Ja.i.295; MN­a.ii.604; Dhp­a.iii.199).

Reference is also made to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgāmahīkīlā</span> (Smp. on Vin.i.191, and again, ii.276). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahī</span> here refers to the earth, but Rhys Davids (VT.ii.25, n.3) thinks it refers to the river of that name.

The junction of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgā</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamunā</span> is frequently referred to, and is used as a simile for perfect union (e.g., Ja.vi.412, 415). A tributary of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgā</span> is mentioned which flows from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span>, its name being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migasammatā</span> (Ja.vi.72). The ford at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span>, where the Buddha crossed on his way from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotamatittha</span> (Vin.i.230); its distance from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> was five leagues, and from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> three (Khp-a.162-3). When the Buddha, after curing the plague at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, great festivities marked the event, and the celebration was known as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgārohaṇa</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> vied with each other to do honour to the Teacher, and there was a great assembly of all classes of beings, comparable to those on the occasions of the Twin Miracle and the Descent from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> (Dhp­a.iii.444). Among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> who dwelt in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgā</span> is mentioned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Eraka</span> (Dhp­a.iii.231).

The water of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgā</span> was considered holy and was used for the consecration of kings, not only of India but also of Ceylon (Mhv.xi.30; Mhv­ṭ.305).

The people on the northern bank were rough and coarse, while those on the south were pious and generous, believers in the Buddha (DN­a.i.160).

The upper reaches of the river were called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uddhagaṅgā</span> (Ja.ii.283, vi.427) or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uparigaṅgā</span> (Ja.iv.230), and the lower reaches <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Adhogaṅgā</span> (Ja.ii.283, 329, v.3).

See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosikī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhagīrathī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Gaṅgā</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāragaṅgā</span>.
