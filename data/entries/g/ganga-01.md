# Gaṅgā 01

## English source

Gaṅgā 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅga</span>,<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14"> Ganges</span>. One of the five great rivers (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Nadī</span>) that water <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>, the others being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamunā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aciravatī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhū</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahī</span> (e.g., Vin.ii.237; SN.ii.135; v.401; AN.iv.101; v.22; Mil.114 mentions ten).

The Commentaries give a long description of their origin (e.g., Snp­a.ii.438f; AN­a.ii.761ff; MN­a.ii.586; Ud­a.301). From the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anotatta</span> lake flow four rivers: that from the south circles the lake three times under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avaṭṭagaṅgā</span>, then as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇhagaṅgā</span> flows straight for sixty leagues along the surface of a rock, comes into violent contact with a vertical rock, and is thrown upwards as a column of water three <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gāvutas</span> in circumference; this column, known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Akāsagaṅgā</span>, flows through the air for sixty leagues, falls on to the rock <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tiyaggala</span>, excavating it to a depth of fifty leagues, thus forming a lake which is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tiyaggala</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pokkharaṇī</span>; then the river, under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bahalagaṅgā</span>, flows through a chasm in the rock for sixty leagues, then, under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ummaggagaṅgā</span>, through a tunnel for a further sixty leagues, and finally coming upon the oblique rock <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijjha</span>, divides into five streams, forming the five rivers above mentioned.

Among places mentioned as being on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgā</span> are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Campā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ayojjha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbhilā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkāvelā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Payāga</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgā</span> formed one of the most important means of communication and trade for the districts through which it flowed – e.g., from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>. The district to the north of the river and bordering on the kingdom of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span> was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttarāpa</span> (Snp­a.ii.439). The river was five hundred leagues in length (SN­a.ii.119).
