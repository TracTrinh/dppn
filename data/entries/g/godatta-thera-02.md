# Godatta Thera 02

## English source

Godatta Thera 02. His full name was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ābhidhammika Godatta</span>, showing that he was considered expert in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span>. He was evidently a well-known <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span> commentator and is quoted in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visuddhimagga</span> (p.138), but it is said that his explanation was rejected in the Commentaries because it was only the Elder’s personal view.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samantapāsādika</span> (ii.307, also 478; iii.588), however, relates a story showing that the Elder was recognised as an authority on the Vinaya. A monk of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Antarasamudda</span> made a drinking bowl out of a coconut shell and, leaving it in the monastery, went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyagiri</span>. Another monk, fancying the bowl, stole it and also went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyagiri</span>, where he met the owner and was charged with theft. Unable to settle the dispute where they were, they went to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>. There, by beating of drums, the monks were assembled near the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Cetiya</span>, and convicted the accused of theft, holding him guilty of a *pārājikā* offence.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godatta</span>, being interviewed, pointed out that the value of the bowl was only a penny or two and that the Buddha had nowhere laid down that the theft of such an insignificant object could be held a *pārājikā* offence. His decision was greeted with applause, and when the report thereof reached the reigning king, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhātiya</span>, he decreed that, henceforth, in all their disputes, his subjects should abide by the decision of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godatta</span>.
