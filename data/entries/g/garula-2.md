# Garuḷā

## English source

Garuḷā, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Garuḷas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Garuḍā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Garuḍas</span>. A class of mythical birds generally mentioned in company with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> (e.g., Ja.iv.181, 202).

They live in *Simbali*-groves (e.g., Ja.i.202) and are usually huge in size, sometimes one hundred and fifty leagues from wing to wing (Ja.iii.397). The flapping of their wings can raise a storm, known as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Garuḍa</span>-wind (Ja.v.77). This wind can plunge a whole city in darkness and cause houses to fall through its violence (Ja.iii.188).

A *Garuḷa* has strength great enough to carry off a whole banyan tree, tearing it up from its roots (Ja.vi.177). The *Garuḷas* are the eternal enemies of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> (Ja.ii.13; iii.103) and live in places, such as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Seruma</span> Island (Ja.iii.187), where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> are to be found. The greatest happiness of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> is to be free from the attacks of the *Garuḷas* (Ja.iv.463). A *Garuḷa*’s plumage is so thick that a man – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Naṭakuvera</span> (Ja.iii.91) – could hide in it, unnoticed by the bird. Sometimes *Garuḷas* assume human form; two *Garuḷa* kings are said to have played dice with kings of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> and to have fallen in love with their queens, whom they took to the *Garuḷa* city – one of the queens being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sussondī</span> (Ja 360, Ja.iii.187) and the other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kākātī</span> (Ja 327, Ja.iii.91). In each case the queen, being found unfaithful to her *Garuḷa* lover, was returned to her husband. The *Garuḷas* know the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ālambāyana</span> spell, which no <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> can resist (Ja.vi.178, 184). It is said that in olden days the *Garuḷas* did not know how to seize <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> effectively; they caught them by the head, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> who had swallowed big stones were too heavy to be lifted from the ground; consequently the *Garuḷas* died of exhaustion in trying to carry them. Later the *Garuḷas* learnt this secret through the treachery of the ascetic <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Karambiya</span>, as related in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍarajātaka</span> (Ja 518, Ja.vi.175f).

*Garuḷas* are mentioned as sometimes leading virtuous lives, keeping the fast and observing the precepts. One such was the *Garuḷa* king mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍarajātaka</span>, and another, the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vināta</span>, who visited the park of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya Koravya</span> and gave a golden garland as present after hearing <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vidhurapaṇḍita</span> preach (Ja.vi.261f).

The *Garuḷa’s* body was evidently considered to be specially formed for quick flight, for the ancient prototype of the aeroplane was based on the *Garuḷa* (Dhp­a.iii.135). One of the five guards appointed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> to protect <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span> was formed of *Garuḷas* (Ja.i.204).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bodhisatta</span> (Ja.iii.187) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> (Ja.iii.400) were both, on different occasions, born as *Garuḷa* kings. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Simbalī</span> is the special tree of the *Garuḷa*-world (Vsm.i.206). The *Garuḷa* is often represented in art as a winged man (see Fergusson: *Tree and Serpent Worship*, pl.xxvi.1; xxviii.1. etc.; also Gründwedel: *Buddhistische Kunst*, pp.47-50).

The *Garuḷas* are sometimes called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Supaṇṇas</span> (*Suvaṇṇas*). Vv-a.9.
