# Garuḷā

## English source

Garuḷā, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Garuḷas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Garuḍā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Garuḍas</span>. A class of mythical birds generally mentioned in company with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> (e.g., Ja.iv.181, 202).

They live in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Simbali</span>-groves (e.g., Ja.i.202) and are usually huge in size, sometimes one hundred and fifty leagues from wing to wing (Ja.iii.397). The flapping of their wings can raise a storm, known as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Garuḍa</span>-wind (Ja.v.77). This wind can plunge a whole city in darkness and cause houses to fall through its violence (Ja.iii.188).

A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷa</span> has strength great enough to carry off a whole banyan tree, tearing it up from its roots (Ja.vi.177). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷas</span> are the eternal enemies of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> (Ja.ii.13; iii.103) and live in places, such as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Seruma</span> Island (Ja.iii.187), where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> are to be found. The greatest happiness of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> is to be free from the attacks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷas</span> (Ja.iv.463). A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷa</span>’s plumage is so thick that a man – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Naṭakuvera</span> (Ja.iii.91) – could hide in it, unnoticed by the bird. Sometimes <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷas</span> assume human form; two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷa</span> kings are said to have played dice with kings of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> and to have fallen in love with their queens, whom they took to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷa</span> city – one of the queens being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sussondī</span> (Ja 360, Ja.iii.187) and the other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kākātī</span> (Ja 327, Ja.iii.91). In each case the queen, being found unfaithful to her <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷa</span> lover, was returned to her husband. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷas</span> know the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ālambāyana</span> spell, which no <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> can resist (Ja.vi.178, 184). It is said that in olden days the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷas</span> did not know how to seize <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> effectively; they caught them by the head, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> who had swallowed big stones were too heavy to be lifted from the ground; consequently the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷas</span> died of exhaustion in trying to carry them. Later the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷas</span> learnt this secret through the treachery of the ascetic <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Karambiya</span>, as related in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍarajātaka</span> (Ja 518, Ja.vi.175f).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷas</span> are mentioned as sometimes leading virtuous lives, keeping the fast and observing the precepts. One such was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷa</span> king mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍarajātaka</span>, and another, the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vināta</span>, who visited the park of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya Koravya</span> and gave a golden garland as present after hearing <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vidhurapaṇḍita</span> preach (Ja.vi.261f).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷa’s</span> body was evidently considered to be specially formed for quick flight, for the ancient prototype of the aeroplane was based on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷa</span> (Dhp­a.iii.135). One of the five guards appointed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> to protect <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span> was formed of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷas</span> (Ja.i.204).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bodhisatta</span> (Ja.iii.187) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> (Ja.iii.400) were both, on different occasions, born as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷa</span> kings. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Simbalī</span> is the special tree of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷa</span>-world (Vsm.i.206). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷa</span> is often represented in art as a winged man (see Fergusson: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Tree and Serpent Worship</span>, pl.xxvi.1; xxviii.1. etc.; also Gründwedel: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Buddhistische Kunst</span>, pp.47-50).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Garuḷas</span> are sometimes called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Supaṇṇas</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suvaṇṇas</span>). Vv-a.9.
