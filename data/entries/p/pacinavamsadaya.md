# Pācīnavaṁsadāya

## English source

Pācīnavaṁsadāya, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pācīnavaṁsamigadāya</span>. A park in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiya</span> kingdom (AN.iv.228f). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> visited it during his tours, and once, while staying there with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meghīya</span>, the latter stopped in a mango grove in the village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jantugāma</span>, refusing to go any farther (AN.iv.163).

The park lay between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bālakaloṇakāragāma</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārileyyakavana</span> (Dhp­a.i.47).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandiya</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbila</span> were living there during the time of the quarrel of the monks at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>, and the Buddha visited them on his way to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārileyyaka</span> (Vin.i.350f.; cp. MN.i.205ff). It was there that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> (AN.iv.228f.; AN­a.i.108).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> explains that the park was so-called because it was to the east of where the Buddha lived and it was covered with green bamboos (AN­a.iv.117).
