# Piṇḍolyasutta

## English source

Piṇḍolyasutta. The Buddha retires for his siesta to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vana</span>, near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, and the thought occurs to him that he should admonish the monks and look after them tenderly as some of them had only lately joined the Saṅgha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> appears before him and confirms his intention. The Buddha thereupon goes to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span>, makes the monks come to him in ones and twos, and talks to them. The life of a recluse is the meanest of callings to be called a “scrap-gatherer.” It is entered on by householders solely as a means of escaping from woe. The man who leaves the world and who yet does not fulfil the life of a recluse, is like a faggot from a funeral pyre, burnt at both ends and smeared with filth. Therefore should the monks shun thoughts of lust, ill-will and hurt, and practise the four *satipaṭṭhāna*s. Thus will they obtain release. SN.iii.91ff.
