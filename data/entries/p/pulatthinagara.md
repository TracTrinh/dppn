# Pulatthinagara

## English source

Pulatthinagara (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulatthipura</span>). A capital of the Sinhalese kings. It is first heard of in the reign of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggabodhi III</span>., who built in the town the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Pānadīpa</span> *vihāra* (Cv.xliv.122). But it was probably an important centre even earlier, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijitapura</span>, wrested from the Tamils by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span>, was probably near by (See Codr.20). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sena I</span>. first made Pulatthipura the capital (Cv.l.9, 46, 85), though even before his time it seems to have been used as a royal residence – e.g., by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggabodhi IV</span>. (Cv.xlvi.34), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggabodhi VII</span>. (Cv.xlviii.74), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udaya I</span>. (Cv.xlix.9, 18), who built a hospital there. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa IV</span>. is also mentioned as building a hospital against an epidemic (Cv.lii.25).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda II</span>. built in the city the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dāmavihāra</span> *pariveṇa* and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sannīratittha</span> *vihāra* (Cv.xlviii.134). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sena I</span>. reigned in Pulatthipura for twenty years and erected there several buildings, including the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Senaggabodhi</span> shrine near the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thusavāpī</span> (Cv.l.73). The successors of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sena I</span>. found in Pulatthipura a certain amount of protection from the inroads of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Coḷas</span> and the Pāṇḍiyans; but in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sena V</span>. the town fell into the hands of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Damiḷas</span>, through the treachery of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sena</span>’s mother and his commander-in-chief, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sena</span>. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sena V</span>. recovered the city by making a treaty with his commander-in-chief (Cv.liv.64, 68). About 1017 CE. the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Coḷas</span> overran the country, captured Pulatthipura, and made the reigning king, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda V</span>., their prisoner. He died, after twelve years, as a prisoner in India (Cv.lv.22ff). During this period many of the Hindu shrines in the city were erected.

For many years the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Coḷas</span> held the sovereignty of the city, though the Sinhalese made several vain attempts to drive them out. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Coḷas</span> named the city <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jananāthapura</span> and put down all rebellion with a strong hand. Finally, a young prince named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kitti</span>, born about 1039 CE., assumed the title of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijayabāhu</span> and determined to rescue Pulatthipura. His first attempts failed, partly owing to rebellion among his own people; but finally, civil war broke out in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Coḷa</span> country itself, and thus, about 1070, he captured Pulatthipura after a great deal of fierce fighting both on sea and land. But, owing to dissensions among his subjects, it was only several years later that he was able to hold his coronation (Cv.lvii.66; lviii.22 ff.; lix.6 ff). He renamed the city <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijayarājapura</span>, and erected there many religious buildings, chief among which was the Temple of the Tooth Relic (Cv.x.2ff). It was not, however, till the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu I</span>. that Pulatthipura reached the pinnacle of its greatness. He enlarged it to the size of four *gāvutas* in length and seven in width and called it <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamapura</span>. The city had three suburbs <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāvjavesībhujaṅga</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājakulantaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijita</span> – and fourteen gates. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkama</span> adorned it with various parks, chief of which were the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandanavana</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīpuyyāna</span>, and with ponds and numerous buildings, both secular and religious (for details see chiefly Cv.lxxiii.1ff.; lxxviii.44 ff). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kittinissaṅka</span> added a stone temple for the Tooth Relic (Cv.lxxx.19). In the reign of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Līlavatī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lokissara</span> captured the city and ruled there for nine months. He was ousted by the general <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkama</span>, and later <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamapaṇḍu</span> ruled as king, till he was deposed about 1215 CE. by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgha</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaliṅga</span> race, who, coming with a large host of Keralas and Malabars, captured the city and mercilessly plundered its possessions (Cv.lxxxiii.15 ff). From this spoliation the city never completely recovered, and it gradually lost its importance, though <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu II</span>., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijayabāhu IV</span>. and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu III</span>. made attempts to restore it to its original splendour. Cv.lxxxvii.67; Cv.lxxxviii.28, 35, 89, 92, 120f.; Cv.lxxxix.1.
