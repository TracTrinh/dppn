# Paramaṭṭhakasutta

## English source

Paramaṭṭhakasutta. The fifth *Sutta* of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṭṭhakavagga</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span>. It was preached in reference to the divergence of views held by various teachers in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>. The king, on hearing of their constant disputes, gave orders that a company of blind men be collected and an elephant placed before them. They were then asked to touch the elephant, and each one described what it appeared like to him. Each described that part which he had touched. The king told the heretics that their divergent views were as unreliable as the blind men’s descriptions of the elephant. The Buddha, hearing of this, preached the *Sutta* in order to confirm the king’s judgment.

One should not give oneself up to philosophical speculations which lead nowhere and promote wrangles (Snp.vs.796-803; Snp­a.ii.1529ff). The *Sutta* is commented upon in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Niddesa</span> (MNid.102ff).
