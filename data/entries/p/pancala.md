# Pañcāla

## English source

Pañcāla, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcālajanapada</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcālaraṭṭha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcālā</span>. One of the sixteen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janapadas</span> (AN.i.213; iv.252, etc.). It consisted of two divisions: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara Pañcāla</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇa Pañcāla</span>. The river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhagīrathi</span> formed the boundary between the divisions. According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumbhakārajātaka</span> (Ja 408), the capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara Pañcāla</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kampillanagara</span>, where a king named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dummukha</span> once reigned. Ja.iii.379; also Mvu.iii.26; but the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Divyāvadāna</span> (435) calls the capital <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hastināpura</span>. According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Bhārata</span> (i.138, 73-4), the capital was Ahicchatra or Chatravatī, while the capital of Dakṣina Pañcāla was Kāmpilya.

Pañcāla was to the east of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuru</span> country, and, in ancient times, there seems to have been a constant struggle between the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurus</span> and the Pañcālas for the possession of Uttara Pañcāla. Thus, sometimes, Uttara Pañcāla was included in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuru</span> kingdom (e.g., Ja.v.444; also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Bhārata</span> i.138), but at other times it formed a part of Kampillaraṭṭha (e.g., Ja.iii.79; v. 21, 289) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kampilla</span> probably being the capital of Dakkhiṇa Pañcāla. So it happened that sometimes the kings of Kampillaraṭṭha had their capital in Uttara Pañcālanagara, while at others the kings of Uttara Pañcālanagara had their capital in Kampillanagara. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷanī Brahmadatta</span> is described in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Ummaggajātaka</span> (Ja 546) as king of Pañcāla, with his capital in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kampilla</span>. Ja.vi.329, 396, etc.; also Pv-a. 161; see also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Uttarādhiyayana Sūtra</span> (SBE. xlv. 57-61) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Rāmāyaṇa</span> (i.32).

Similarly <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambhūta</span> was king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara Pañcāla</span> (Ja.iv.392ff.). Sometimes the king of Pañcāla is merely spoken of as Pañcāla e.g., Ja.iv.430, v. 98. See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayaddisa</span>.

There seems to have been a chieftain (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">rājā</span>) of Pañcāla even in the Buddha’s day, for we are told (Thag­a.i.331) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pañcāliputta</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) was the son of the daughter of the “Pañcālarājā.” Pañcāla is generally identified with the country to the north and west of Deḷhi, from the foot of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span> to the river Chambal. Law: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Geography of Early Buddhism</span>, p.19.
