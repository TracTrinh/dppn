# Pañcavudhajātaka

## English source

Pañcavudhajātaka (Ja 55). The Bodhisatta born as king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. He was the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span>, and was named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcāvudhakumāra</span> because eight hundred Brahmins prophesied that he would win glory through prowess in arms. He studied in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span>, and, at the end of his course, his teacher gave him a set of five weapons. On the way home he had a conflict with an ogre named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Silesaloma</span>, against whom his weapons were of no avail, as they could not penetrate the ogre’s hair. But he fought on, and the ogre, marvelling at his courage and his fearlessness, set him free. He thereupon preached to the ogre and converted him. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcāvudha</span> later became king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. Ja.i.272ff.

The ogre is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgulimāla</span>. The story was related in reference to a monk who had renounced all effort. Ja.i.272ff.
