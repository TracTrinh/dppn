# Puṇṇā 03

## English source

Puṇṇā 03. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇā Therī</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">v.l.</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇikā Therī</span>). An <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. She was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>’s household, as the daughter of a domestic slave. She was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇā</span> because, with her birth, the number of children in the household reached one hundred.

On the day, on which she heard the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīhanādasutta</span> she became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>. She converted the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sotthiya</span>, who believed in purification through water (the conversation is recorded in Thīg. vs. 236-51), and thereby won the esteem of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>, so that he freed her. Thereupon she entered the Saṅgha and in due course became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span> she was born in a clansman’s family and entered the Saṅgha. She learned the three <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Piṭakas</span> and became a distinguished preacher. She did the same under five other Buddhas – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sikhī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessabhū</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakusandha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇāgamana</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> but, owing to her tendency to pride, she was unable to root out the defilements. Thīg­a. 199 ff.; Ap.ii.611.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>, however, says of this Therī that she was a slave girl of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span> (MN­a.i.347f.; the story, with very different details, is given in AN­a.ii.716f). On one occasion, when the Buddha was about to set out on a tour, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span> and the other chief patrons of the Buddha, loth to lose him for several months, begged him to remain with them. But the Buddha declined this request, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇā</span>, seeing <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span> very dejected and learning the reason, offered to persuade the Buddha to stay. So she approached him and said that she would take the Three Refuges with the Five Precepts if he would postpone his tour. The Buddha at once agreed, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇā</span> was freed and adopted as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>’s daughter. She later joined the Saṅgha, and became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> after listening to an admonition of the Buddha, who appeared before her in a ray of glory (Thīg, vs.3, about <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇā</span> 2). Here we undoubtedly have a confusion of legends. See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇā</span> (2).

It may be this same <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇā</span> who is mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span> (p.115) as one of the seven people whose acts of devotion brought them recompense in this very life.
