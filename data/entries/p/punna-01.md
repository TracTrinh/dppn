# Puṇṇa 01

## English source

Puṇṇa 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa Thera</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇaka Thera</span>. He was born in the family of a householder of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppāraka</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunāparanta</span> country. When he was grown up, he went with a great caravan of merchandise to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> where, having heard the Buddha preach, he left the world and joined the Saṅgha. He won favour by attention to his duties. One day he asked the Buddha for a short lesson so that, having learnt it, he might go back to dwell in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunāparanta</span>. The Buddha preached to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇovādasutta</span> (*q.v.*). So <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span> departed, and, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunāparanta</span>, he became an *Arahant*. There he won over many disciples, both male and female, and having built for the Buddha a cell out of red sandalwood (*Candanāsālā*), he sent him a flower by way of invitation. The Buddha came with five hundred *Arahants*, spent a night in the cell, and went away before dawn.

Ninety-one *kappas* ago, when there was no Buddha alive, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span> was a learned Brahmin, and later became a hermit in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span>. Near his abode a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> died, and at the moment of his death there appeared a great radiance. The ascetic cremated the body and sprinkled scented water on the pyre to extinguish the flames. A *Deva*, witnessing the event, prophesied his future greatness. His name throughout his many lives was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇaka</span>. Thag. vs. 70; Thag­a.i.156 ff.; Ap.ii.341.

In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunāparanta</span> he first lived at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambahatthapabbata</span>, but, on being recognised by his brother, he went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samuddagiri</span> *vihāra*, where was a magnetised walk which none could use. The waves of the sea breaking made great noise, and, in order to help him to concentration, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span> caused the sea to be quiet. From there he went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātulagiri</span>, where the incessant cries of birds disturbed him; he finally went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makulakagāma</span>. While he was there, his brother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Puṇṇa</span>, with five hundred others, sailed in a trading ship, and, before embarking, he visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span>, took the precepts from him, and asked for his protection during the voyage. The ship reached an island where red sandalwood grew; with this the merchants filled the ship, and the spirits of the island, angered by this, raised a great storm and appeared before the sailors in fearful forms. Each merchant thought of his guardian deity and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Puṇṇa</span> of his brother. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span>, sensing his brother’s need, travelled through the air to the ship, and, at sight of him, the spirits disappeared. In gratitude for their deliverance, the merchants gave to the Elder a share of their sandalwood. It was with this material that the *Candanasālā*, above referred to, was built.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇḍadhāna</span> was the first among the *Arahants* to be chosen to accompany the Buddha to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunāparanta</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> provided five hundred palanquins for the journey, one of which was empty. This was subsequently taken by the ascetic <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saccabandha</span>, whom the Buddha converted and ordained on the way. On his return journey, the Buddha stopped at the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nammadā</span>, and was entertained there by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> king. MN­a.ii.1014 ff.; SN­a.iii.14ff.; Khp­a.149.
