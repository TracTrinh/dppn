# Pañcasikha

## English source

Pañcasikha. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhabba</span>. His favourite instrument was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Beluvapaṇḍuvīnā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). He was considered a favourite of the Buddha (DN­a.iii.699), and when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> visited the Buddha at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indasālaguhā</span> in order to ask him certain questions, he sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span> in advance, that he might obtain permission for the interview. The episode in given in full in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakkapañhasutta</span> (DN.ii.263ff.).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span> approached the Buddha and playing on his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vinā</span>, sang of the beauties of the Buddha, the Dhamma, Arahants and Love. The verses really formed a love poem addressed to his beloved, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā Suriyavaccasā</span>, daughter of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhabba Timbarū</span>. The Buddha praised his music and song and questioned him about the poem. He confessed that when the Buddha was staying under the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajapālanirgodha</span>, before the Awakening, he (Pañcasikha) had met <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suriyavaccasā</span> going with her father to dance before <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>. Pañcasikha thereupon fell in love with her; but she favoured the suit of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sikhaṇḍi</span>, son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātali</span>. Pañcasikha thereupon composed a song, which he sang to her. She was greatly pleased with the references in the song to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> sage of whom she had heard when she went to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhammāsabhā</span> (on this occasion <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, pronounced his 8-fold eulogy of the Buddha, contained in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Govindasutta</span>, says <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>, DN­a.ii.704), and she consented to marry <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span>. It is said that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> blessed the marriage in return for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span>’s intercession with the Buddha on his behalf.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janavasabhasutta</span> (DN.ii.211; also in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Govindasutta</span>, DN.ii.230) it is stated that when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sanaṅkumāra</span> appeared before the assembly of the gods of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> and materialized himself he assumed the form of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (DN­a.ii.640), by way of explanation, that all the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> loved <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span> and wished to resemble him. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Govindasutta</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span> is represented as conveying to the Buddha a full report of the happenings in the assembly of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> spoke the Buddha’s praises (DN.i.220; cp. Mvu.iii.197ff).

No really satisfactory explanation is found in the Commentaries of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span>’s name. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (DN­a.ii.647) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pañcasikho ti pañcacūḷo, pañcakuṇḍaliko</span>, and goes on to say that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span> was born once as a human being, and, while yet a boy wearing his hair in five knots (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pañcacūḷakadārakakāle</span>), he became chief of those who tended the calves.

This is done even now in Ceylon, where young boys’ hair is tied round their heads in several knots. But in one place <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says that one way of insulting a man was to shave his head, leaving him five locks of hair (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t18">garahāyā ti pañcasikhā muṇḍakaraṇaṁ</span>) (DN­a.i.296). And, again, he mentions that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sanaṅkumāra</span> retained his eternal youth because in a previous life he had developed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jhāna</span> while yet a lad (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pañcasikhakumārakāle</span>) (SN­a.i.171). See also Ja.vi.496, where a traitor had his hair tied in five knots as a sign of disgrace.

Together with other lads he engaged in works of public utility, such as repairing roads, digging wells, building rest houses, etc., and he died while young. He was reborn in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cātummahārājika</span> world, destined to live for ninety thousand years, his body three <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gāvutas</span> in height. He wore on his person one hundred cartloads of ornaments and rubbed nine pots of perfume on his body. He wore red robes, and on his head a chaplet of red gold, round which his hair was arranged in five locks (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kuntalikehi</span>), which fell back as in the case of a young boy (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">pañcacūḷikadārakaparihāren’eva</span>).

It was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span> who first received from the king of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cātummahārājika</span> worlds and their ministers reports of good deeds done by human beings. These he would pass on to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātali</span>, who, in his turn, presented them to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> (DN­a.ii.650). On the day of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devorohaṇa</span>, when the Buddha descended from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span> was present to render honour to the Teacher in song and music (Dhp­a.iii.225; AN­a.i.72; Vsm.392). According to the legends he was present with the Buddha on other occasions as well (e.g., Mhv.xxx.75; xxxi.82).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span> was evidently not only the name of a person, but also of an office (like <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>), for in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Biḷārikosiyajātaka</span> (Ja 450, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> is said to have been born as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span> and to have helped <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> and others to make of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bīlārakosiya</span> a generous man (Ja.iv.69). Similarly, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhābhojanajātaka</span> (Ja 535, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span>. Ja.v.412.
