# Pacceka Buddha

## English source

Pacceka Buddha, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>. The name given to one who is enlightened by and for himself – i.e., one who has attained to supreme and perfect insight, but who dies without proclaiming the truth to the world – hence the equivalent “Silent Buddha” sometimes found in translations. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> practise their <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pāramī</span> for at least two thousand <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">asaṅkheyya kappas</span>. They are born in any of the three <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kulas</span>: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brāhmaṇa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Khattiya</span>, or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gahapati</span> only in a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vivaṭṭamānakappa</span>, during which Buddhas are also born, but they never meet a Buddha face to face. They cannot instruct others; their realization of the Dhamma is “like a dream seen by a deaf mute.” They attain to all the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">samāpatti</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">paṭisambhidā</span> of the Buddhas, but are second to the Buddhas in their spiritual development. They do ordain others; their admonition is only in reference to good and proper conduct (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">abhisamācārikasikkhā</span>).

Sometimes it is stated that a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>’s knowledge and comprehension of ways and means is less than that of a Bodhisatta (e.g., at Ja.iv.341). They hold their <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">uposatha</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratanamālaka</span>, at the foot of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mañjūsarukkha</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhamādana</span>. It is possible to become a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> while yet a layman, but, in this case, the marks of a layman immediately disappear. Three caves in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandamūlapabbhāra</span> – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suvaṇṇaguhā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇiguhā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rajataguhā</span> – are the dwelling places of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>. Round the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratanamālaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabbaratanamālaka</span>), seats are always ready to receive the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>. When a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> appears in the world, he immediately seeks the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratanamālaka</span>, and there takes his appointed seat. Then all the other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> in the world assemble there to meet him, and, in reply to a question by the chief of them, he relates the circumstances which led to his enlightenment. Similarly, all the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> assemble at the same spot when one of them is about to die. The dying one takes leave of the others, and, after his death, they cremate his body and his relics disappear. These details are given in Snp­a.i.47, 51, 58, 63; Khp­a.178, 199; Ap­a.i.125; see also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhamādana</span>.

But, according to another account, they die on the mountain called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Papāta</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). There does not seem to be any limit to the number of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> who could appear simultaneously. In one instance, five hundred are mentioned as so doing, all sons of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumavatī</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), at the head of whom was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Paduma</span>. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isigilisutta</span> (MN.iii.68ff) appears a long list of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> who dwelt on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isigili</span> Mountain (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), and after whom the mountain was named.

According to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (MN­a.ii.889ff), the names in this list belonged to the five hundred sons of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumavatī</span>, but the number of the names is far less than five hundred. This discrepancy is explained by saying that as many as twelve bore the same name. Other names are found scattered over different texts, such as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka Commentary</span>, e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Darīmukha</span> (Ja.iii.240), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇaka</span> (v.249); see also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> Dhp-a.iv.120, etc.

The name occurring most frequently in the texts is that of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tagarasikhī</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). Mention is also made of the Pacceka Buddhas going among men for alms and spending the rainy season in dwellings provided by men, e.g., Dhp­a.ii.112f.; iii.91, 368; iv.200. Their <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t18">patthanā</span> (Snp­a.51). Their wisdom is less than that of a Bodhisatta (Ja.iv.341).

Among the teachings preserved of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>, the most important is the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khaggavisāṇasutta</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). For the definition of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puggalapaññatti</span> (p.14; cf. p.70). There he is described as one who understands the Truth by his own efforts, but does not obtain omniscience nor mastery over the fruits (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t18">phalesu vasībhāvaṁ</span>). See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span> (2).
