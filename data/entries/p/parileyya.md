# Pārileyya

## English source

Pārileyya, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārileyyaka</span>. A town (*nagara*) near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>. When the Buddha found that he could not persuade the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> monks to refrain from quarrelling, he left <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> alone and unattended, and passing through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bālakaloṇakāragāma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pācīnavaṁsadāya</span>, went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārileyyaka</span>, where he stayed at the foot of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddasāla</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rakkhitavanasaṇḍa</span>. There a certain elephant who, finding communal life distasteful, had left his herd, waited on the Buddha, ministering to all his needs. From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārileyyaka</span> the Buddha went on to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> (Vin.i.352f.; SN.iii.95; Ud.iv.5; Ja.iii.489; MN.i.320). This was in the tenth year after the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> (Bv­a., p.3).

The Commentaries say that the elephant’s name was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārileyya</span>, and describe in vivid detail the perfect manner in which he looked after the Buddha, omitting nothing, even to the extent of finding hot water for his bath (e.g., Dhp­a.i.48ff.; iv.26 ff. Ud­a.250f.; see Thomas: *Life and Legend of the Buddha*, 117n). There was also there a monkey who offered the Buddha a honeycomb. Soon after, the monkey fell on a tree stump and died and was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>.

Later, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> came with five hundred others to invite the Buddha to return to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārileyyaka</span> provided them all with food. He died of a broken heart when the Buddha left the forest, and was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> in a golden palace, thirty leagues high, where he came to be known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārileyyaka</span> *Devaputta*.

This elephant is identified with the elephant of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhisajātaka</span> (Ja 488). Ja.iv.314.
