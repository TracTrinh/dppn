# Paribbājakā

## English source

Paribbājakā, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paribbājaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paribbājakas</span>. The name given to the ascetics and recluses (not otherwise classified) of the Buddha’s time. They were not exclusively Brahmin. Their presence seems to have been recognized and respected from earlier times. Generally speaking, their creed is formulated as a belief in perfect bliss after death for the self purged from evil, and as a conviction that this bliss can be won by *brahmacariyā*, by freedom from all evil in acts, words, aims, and mode of livelihood (see, e.g., MN.ii.24).

All these four standards of conduct were bodily incorporated in the Buddha’s Noble Eightfold Path, and the last of the four gave to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ājīvakas</span> their specific name as a separate sect. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paribbājakas</span> claimed to be identical with the followers of the Buddha in their tenets and teaching, but the Buddha maintained that the two teachings were quite distinct (e.g., MN.i.64f, 84f). This is clearly indicated (e.g.,Vin.i.39.) in connection with the conversion of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>, who were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paribbājakas</span> under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sañjaya</span>. The goal of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paribbājakas</span> was deathlessness (*amata*) which, to them, probably meant birth in the world of *Brahma*. Their conversion to the Buddha’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span> followed the recognition that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> dealt, not with effects but with causes, and that he went to the root of the matter by teaching how casual states of consciousness arose and how they could be banished for ever (Chalmers: *Further Dialogues* i. Introd. xxi. For discussions on the views of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paribbājakas</span> as compared with those of the Buddha, see also AN.iv.35ff., 378; i.215).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paribbājakas</span> were not ascetics except in so far as they were celibates; some of them were women. They were teachers or sophists who spent eight or nine months of every year wandering from place to place for the purpose of engaging in friendly, conversational discussions on matters of ethics and philosophy, nature-lore and mysticism. They differed very much in intelligence, earnestness, and even in honesty. Some of the views discussed in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmajālasutta</span>, for instance, and described as those of “Eel-wrigglers” and “Hair-splitters”, were undoubtedly truly thus described. The books mention halls erected for the accommodation of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paribbājakas</span>, such as those in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span>’s park at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> (DN.i.178), and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kūṭāgārasālā</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>.

Sometimes special places were set apart for them in the groves near the settlements, as at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Campā</span> on the bank of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaggarā</span> lake (DN.i.111), at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moranivāpa</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> (AN.v.326), and on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sappinikā</span> (Ibid., i.185; ii.175). </p>It was in such places that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paribbājakas</span> met each other, and in the course of their journeys they would visit each other in order to exchange greetings of courtesy and to engage in profitable discussion. The utmost cordiality seems to have prevailed on these occasions, intercourse and discussions were free, there were no restrictions of creed, caste or pride. Thus

- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Dīghanakha</span> calls on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Gotama Buddha</span> (MN.i.497),
- the Buddha on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sakuladāyī</span> (MN.ii.29; also AN.ii.175ff)
- and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sarabha</span> (AN.i.185).
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Vekhanassa</span> calls on the Buddha (MN.ii.40),
- as do <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Timbaruka</span> (SN.ii.22), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Vacchagotta</span> (SN.iii.257),
- and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sivaka Moliya</span> (SN.iv. 230).
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Poṭaliputta</span> calls on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Samiddhi</span> (MN.iii.207),
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Susīma</span> on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Ānanda</span> (SN.ii.119), and
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Jambukhādaka</span> on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sāriputta</span> (SN.iv. 251).

The inhabitants of the towns and villages, near which the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paribbājakas</span> stopped, visited them, both to show their respect and to benefit by their teachings. The names of a considerable number of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paribbājakas</span>, besides those already mentioned, who were well-known in the time of the Buddha, are given in the texts (e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Annabhāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Varadhara</span>, etc., AN.ii.175), also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmaṇḍaka</span> (SN.iv.26) and the *Paribbājikā* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sucimukhī</span> (SN.iii.238f). In most cases they are represented as having large followings, so that they were evidently regarded as distinguished teachers.
