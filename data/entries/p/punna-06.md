# Puṇṇa 06

## English source

Puṇṇa 06. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa Mantānīputta Thera</span>. He belonged to a Brahmin family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Doṇavatthu</span> near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>. His mother was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mantāṇī</span>, sister of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aññā Kondañña</span>. While <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> was at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, whither he had gone after preaching the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammacakkappavattanasutta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aññā Kondañña</span> went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> and ordained Puṇṇa. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇḍañña</span> then returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, whence, having taken leave of the Buddha, he retired to live on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chaddanta</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">daha</span>. But Puṇṇa remained in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, intent on his practices, and soon after became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. He gathered round him five hundred clansmen who all became monks, and he taught them the ten bases of discourse (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dasa kathāvatthūni</span>), which he himself had learnt, and they became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span>. When they wished to visit the Buddha, Puṇṇa sent them on in advance to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, asking them to pay homage to the Buddha in his name. Later, when the Buddha came from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, Puṇṇa visited him and was taught the Dhamma in the Buddha’s own <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhakuṭi</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, hearing of the fame of Puṇṇa, wished to meet him, and went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhavana</span>, where Puṇṇa was spending his siesta. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> questioned him on the seven acts of purity, and Puṇṇa answered him. The two monks found great joy in each other’s words. The interview with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> is given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rathavinītasutta</span> (MN.i.146 ff.). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>, says (MN­a.i.362) that the two Elders had many things in common.

Later, the Buddha declared Puṇṇa to be pre-eminent among those who preached the Dhamma. (AN.i.23; SN.ii.156)

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>, Puṇṇa was born in a rich Brahmin family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsavatī</span>, before the birth of the Buddha. When grown up, he one day visited the Buddha, and as he sat on the edge of a large crowd, hearing him preach, the Buddha declared one of his monks pre-eminent among preachers, and Puṇṇa, wishing for a like honour under a future Buddha, paid great homage to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span>. (Thag­a.i.37 ff)

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttara Commentary</span>, however, we are told that in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>, Puṇṇa was named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> and was expert in the Vedas (AN­a.i.113 ff). But he found no solace in the teaching of the Vedas and became an ascetic with a following of eighteen thousand <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Jaṭilas</span>, all of whom, under his guidance, developed great <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span> powers. Puṇṇa was already old when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span> attained Awakening. One day the Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>’s hermitage, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> and his disciples entertained him to a meal. Afterwards the Buddha wished his chief disciple <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Deva</span> to come to the hermitage with one hundred thousand monks; this he did, and the ascetics provided flowers for their seats. For seven days the Buddha and his monks remained in jhāna on their seats, at the end of which period the Buddha asked the most pre-eminent preacher to render thanks. At the conclusion of the sermon, all except <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> wished to gain pre-eminence in preaching under a future Buddha, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span> proclaimed that his wish would find fulfilment. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> contains yet another version, according to which Puṇṇa’s name in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunanda</span> (Ap.i.38, quoted at Thag­a.i.362).

Besides the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rathavinītasutta</span> mentioned above, which bears testimony to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span>’s skill as a preacher, another <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span>, of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span>, represents <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> as saying to the assembled monks that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span> was of great help to himself and others when they were yet novices; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span> had preached to them on causation, and they were able to understand the Dhamma because of his skilful exposition (SN.iii.105f.; according to Thag­a.ii.124, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span> after hearing a sermon by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span>).

It is, perhaps, this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span> who is identified with the gate-keeper (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dovārika</span>) of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurudhammajātaka</span> (Ja 276, Ja.ii.381) and with one of the seven brothers of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhisajātaka</span> (Ja 488, Ja.iv.314).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Vastu</span> contains twenty verses attributed to Pūrṇa Maitrayānīputra (Mvu.iii.382).
