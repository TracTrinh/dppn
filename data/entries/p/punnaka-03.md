# Puṇṇaka 03

## English source

Puṇṇaka 03. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> chief, nephew of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> (Ja.vi.255). The story of how he won the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> maiden <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Irandatī</span> is related in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vidhurapaṇḍitajātaka</span> (Ja 545). In his previous birth he had been a young man named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāyana</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span> country. Ja.vi.273f.; he is also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kātiyāna</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāna</span>). He is also referred to as Puṇṇakarājā (Ja.iv.182). He is evidently identical with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> chieftain mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āṭānāṭiyasutta</span> (DN.iii.204) among those to be invoked by followers of the Buddha in times of tribulation. The gem used by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇaka</span> as a stake in his gambling with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koravya</span> was such that all things in the world could be seen in it (Mhv­ṭ. 552). The shout of victory uttered by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇaka</span> when he defeated <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koravya</span> was one of the four shouts heard throughout <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>. Snp­a.i.223.
