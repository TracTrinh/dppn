# Pāṭimokkha

## English source

Pāṭimokkha, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pātimokkha</span>. The name given to a set of two hundred and twenty-seven rules to be observed by members of the Buddhist Saṅgha. The rules are not simply ethical but mainly economic, regulating the behaviour of the members of the Saṅgha towards one another in respect of clothes, dwellings, furniture, etc., held in common. In four cases out of the two hundred and twenty-seven the punishment for infringement of a rule is exclusion from the Saṅgha; in thirteen of the remaining cases, it is merely suspension for a time (the rest are confessable offenses).

The rules are arranged in seven sections: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pārājikā, Saṅghādisesā, Aniyatā, Nissaggiyāpācittiyā, Pācittiyā, Pāṭidesanīyā, Sekkhiyā</span> corresponding very roughly to the degree of weight attached to their observance.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pātimokkha</span> is not included in the extant Buddhist Canon. The rules are included, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttavibhaṅga</span> (“<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span>” here meaning “rule”), which contains besides the rules themselves, an old Commentary explaining them and a new Commentary containing further supplementary information concerning them. The rules are divided into two parts: one for the monks (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Bhikkhu Pātimokkha</span>) and the other for the nuns (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Bhikkhunī Pātimokkha</span>). It is a moot point whether the rules originally appeared with the explanatory notes (as in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vibhaṅga</span>), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pātimokkha</span> being subsequently extracted, or whether the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pātimokkha</span> alone was the older portion, the additional matter of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vibhaṅga</span> being the work of a subsequent revision. For a discussion of this, see Vin.i. Introd.xvi; Law: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pāli Literature</span> 2ff.; Hastings: Encyclopaedia under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pātimokkha</span>.

It is sometimes suggested (Law: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pāli Literature</span> p.2) that the original number of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pātimokkha</span> rules numbered only about one hundred and fifty. A passage in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (i.231-232) is quoted in support of this suggestion (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sādhikaṁ diyaḍḍhasikkhāpadasataṁ</span>). According to this theory the twenty-five <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sekhiyā</span> rules were added later. See Law: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pāli Literature</span> 19f.; Law’s argument, however, that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pātimokkha</span> rules were among the texts not recited at the First Council, is due to a wrong understanding of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumaṅgalavilāsinī</span> passage (i.17).

The rules were recited at the gatherings of members of the Saṅgha (the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Uposathakkhandhaka</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vagga</span> (Vin.i.101-36) gives details of the procedure at these gatherings) in their respective districts on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">uposatha</span> days (the fourteenth or fifteenth days of the month). Each section of the rules is recited and, at the end of such recital, the reciter asks the members of the Saṅgha who are present if any one of them has infringed any of the rules. Silence implies absence of guilt. This practice of interrupting the recital seems to have been changed later (see Vin.ii.240 ff.) even though the old formula, asking the members to speak, continued as a part of the recital.

The word <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pātimokkha</span> is variously explained, the oldest explanation being that the observance of the rules is the face (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">mukhaṁ</span>), the chief (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pamukhaṁ</span>) of good qualities. The Sanskritised form of the word being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">prātimokṣa</span>, this led to a change in its significance, the completion of the recital being evidence that all those who have taken part are pure in respect of the specified offences – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pātimokkha</span> thus meaning acquittal, deliverance or discharge. But in most contexts the word simply means code – i.e., code of verses for the members of the Saṅgha.

For a detailed account of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pātimokkha</span> rules see Law: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pāli Literature</span>, 49 ff.
