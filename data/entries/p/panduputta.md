# Paṇḍuputta

## English source

Paṇḍuputta. An <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ājīvaka</span>. He came of a wagon building stock of bygone days. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anaṅgaṇasutta</span> (MN.i.31f), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> tells <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> that, one day, when begging alms in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, he came to the house of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samīti</span>, the wagon builder who was shaping a felloe. Standing by was Paṇḍuputta, watching him and wishing that he should shape it without blemish. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samīti</span> did so, Paṇḍuputta expressed his joy, saying that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samīti</span> had read his thoughts.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (MN­a.i.124) that Paṇḍuputta was so-called because he was the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍu</span>.
