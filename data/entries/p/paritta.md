# Paritta

## English source

Paritta, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parittā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parittas</span>. A collection of texts taken from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khuddakapātha</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhimanikāya</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span>, and recited on special occasions to ward off illness and danger. The word “*Paritta*” means protection. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span> (p.150f) gives a list of the chief <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parittas</span>:

- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Ratanasutta</span>,
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Khandhaparitta,</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Moraparitta,</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Dhajaggaparitta,</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Āṭānāṭiyaparitta</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Aṅgulimāla</span>

(for particulars of these see also Dial.iii.185).

To these are generally added, in the extant collection of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parittas</span>,

- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Maṅgalasutta</span> and
- the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mettasutta</span>.

The word *Paritta* first occurs in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Vagga</span> (Vin.ii.110) in connection with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khandhaparitta</span>, which was allowed by the Buddha as a watch, a guard, a protection for oneself, for the use of the Saṅgha. The occasion of the delivery of this general injunction was the death of a monk from snake bite. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span> states (see above) that the recital of the *Paritta* had the Buddha’s express sanction.

The collection of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parittas</span> is, to this day, more widely known by the laity of Burma and Ceylon than any other Pāli book, and is generally used in times of danger or of sickness, both individual and national. Thus, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sena II</span>., king of Ceylon, made the community of monks recite the *Paritta*, and by sprinkling the water charmed with *Paritta* he made the people free from illness, and so removed the danger of plague from the country.

He also decreed that this practice should continue every year (Cv.li.80).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa V</span>. is said to have had a *Paritta* ceremony performed by the three fraternities of monks to protect his people from danger and plague and bad harvest (Ibid., lii.80).

In the recent (1935) epidemic of malaria in Ceylon, monks were taken in carts through the badly affected areas reciting the *Paritta* and sprinkling water. The ceremony is held on most diverse occasions such as the inauguration of a new house, the starting of a journey, of a new business, etc. For a discussion on the *Paritta* see Dial.iii.180 ff.; also PLC.75f.

Bode says (Op. cit., 4) that in the days of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anorata</span> of Burma corrupt and cynical monks used the recital of the *Paritta* as an easy means of clearing man’s guilty conscience from all wrong-doing, even from matricide.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> is mentioned (Cv.xxxvii.226) as having once attempted to compile a *Parittaṭṭhakathā*. Geiger (Cv.Trs.i.24, 3) calls this a commentary on the *Paritta*, but it is more probable that *Paritta* is here used as an adjective, meaning short, concise, and that what is meant is a short or concise commentary on the *Piṭakas*.
