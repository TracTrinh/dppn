# Paṇḍita 02

## English source

Paṇḍita 02. One of the four novices invited by the Brahmin whose house came later to be known as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcachiddageha</span> (Dhp­a.iv.176ff). In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> he was known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Duggata</span>. In his last birth his mother was the daughter of a rich merchant of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>. During her pregnancy, she had a longing to give to five hundred monks, headed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, the choicest portions of red fish, to don yellow robes, to sit in the outer circle of the monks’ seats, and to partake of the food left over by the monks. This longing was satisfied, and seven times she held similar festivities. When the child was born he was called Paṇḍita because, from the day of his conception, various people of the household who had been stupid or deaf or dumb recovered their faculties. When seven years old, he was filled with the desire to become a monk, and was ordained by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, a constant visitor to the house. For seven days his parents held a festival in honour of his ordination. On the eighth day he went, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, into the village for alms; on the way, certain things which he saw – a ditch, arrow makers, carpenters – made him wish to strive for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship. Thereupon, with the leave of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, he returned to the monastery requesting the Elder to bring him some red fish on his return from the alms round. In the monastery, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> stilled all noises and held back the sun and the moon, lest Paṇḍita should be disturbed. The Buddha, seeing this, detained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> back on his way to the monastery, and engaged him in conversation until Paṇḍita should have succeeded in his effort. After a while, Paṇḍita became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> and the whole world rejoiced. Dhp­a.ii.139ff.
