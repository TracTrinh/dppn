# Pasenadi

## English source

Pasenadi. King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> and contemporary of the Buddha. He was the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kosala</span>, and was educated at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> where, among his companions, were the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī Mahāli</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malla</span> prince <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span>. On his return home his father was so pleased with his proficiency in the various arts that he forthwith made him king. (Dhp­a.i.338; for his genealogy see Beal-Xuan.ii.2, n.3).

As ruler, Pasenadi gave himself wholeheartedly to his administrative duties and valued the companionship of wise and good men (e.g., SN.i.74, 100); the Commentary (SN­a i.109f.) adds that the king tried to put down bribery and corruption in his court, but his attempt does not appear to have been very successful.

Thus he showed his favour to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pokkharasādi</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṅkī</span>, by giving them, respectively, the villages of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkaṭṭhā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Opasāda</span> free of all taxes. It is said that his alms halls were always open to everyone desiring food or drink (Ud.ii.6). Even after becoming the Buddha’s follower, he did not omit to salute holy men of other persuasions (Ud.vi.2). Quite early in the Buddha’s ministry Pasenadi became the Buddha’s follower and close friend, and his devotion to the Buddha lasted till his death (according to Tibetan sources, Pasenadi’s conversion was in the second year of the Buddha’s ministry (Rockhill, p.49)). We find the king referring to the Buddha, at their first meeting, as being young in years (SN.i.69). Their first meeting and conversation, which ended in Pasenadi’s declaring himself an adherent of the Buddha, are recorded in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Daharasutta</span> (*q.v.*).

But Pasenadi’s conversion did not prevent him from extending his favour, with true Indian toleration, to the members of other religious orders. Mention is even made of a great animal sacrifice which he once prepared, but which he abandoned on the advice of the Buddha, whom he sought at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallika</span>’s suggestion (SN.i.75; for details, see the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Supinajātaka</span> (Ja 77) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohakumbhījātaka</span> (Ja 314).

It is said (SN­a.i.111) that the king fell in love with a woman while riding round the city; on discovering that she was married, he ordered her husband to go, before sunset, and fetch clay and lilies from a pond one hundred leagues away. When the man had gone, the king ordered the gatekeepers to shut the gates early and not on any account to open them. The husband returned in the evening, and finding the gates shut, went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, to seek protection from the king’s wrath. The king spent a sleepless night owing to his passion and had bad dreams. When the Brahmins were consulted they advised a great animal sacrifice. The story is also found at Dhp­a.ii.1ff., with several variations in detail.

He frequently visited the Buddha and discussed various matters with him (it is said that he went three times a day to wait on the Buddha, sometimes with only a small bodyguard. Some robbers, knowing this, arranged an ambush in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhavana</span>. But the king discovered the plot, of which he made short work).

The whole of the Third Saṁyutta (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosalasaṁyutta</span>), consisting of twenty-five anecdotes, each with a moral bias, is devoted to him. The topics discussed are many and varied. The Buddha and Pasenadi were equals in age, and their talks were, therefore, intimate and frank. Pasenadi was extremely attached to the Buddha, and the books describe how, when he saw the Buddha, he bowed his head at the Buddha’s feet, covering them with kisses and stroking them (MN.ii.120).

The Chinese records say that when the Buddha went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, Pasenadi made an image of the Buddha in sandalwood, to which he paid honour (Beal-Xuan, xliv).

He was very jealous of the Buddha’s reputation, and put down with a firm hand any attempt on the part of heretics to bring discredit on him – e.g., in the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sundarīnandā</span> (*q.v.*).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggaññasutta</span> (DN.iii.83f.), the Buddha explains why Pasenadi honours him. For Pasenadi’s own explanation as to why people honoured the Buddha even more than the king, see MN.ii.123; see also AN.v.65 ff. Pasenadi was also jealous of the reputation of the Saṅgha, and if anything arose which seemed likely to bring discredit on it, he took prompt steps to have the matter remedied – e.g., in the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇḍadhāna</span> (*q.v.*) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumāra Kassapa</span>’s mother (*q.v.*).

Pasenadi’s palace overlooked the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aciravatī</span>, and when he once saw some monks sporting in the river in an unseemingly way, he made sure that the Buddha knew of it (Vin.iv.112). The story of the blind man and the elephant shows that he was anxious to justify the Buddha’s teaching as against that of other sects (Snp­a.ii.529).

On one occasion we find the Buddha telling him to eat less and teaching his nephew <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudassana</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span>) a verse on the advantages of moderation, to be repeated to the king whenever he sat down to a meal. This advice was followed and the king became slim. SN.i.81; Dhp­a.iii.264f.; iv.6f.; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta Commentary</span> (SN­a.i.136) states that the bowl out of which he ate (*paribhogapāti*) was the size of a cartwheel.

Pasenadi was always conscious of his own dignity – e.g., the incident with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chattapāṇī</span> (*q.v.*); but see Vin.iv.157f., which probably refers to the same story.

Pasenadi’s chief consort was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span>, daughter of a garland-maker (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span> for details of her marriage with the king). He loved her dearly and trusted her judgment in all things. When in difficulty he consulted her, realizing that her wisdom was greater than his own (e.g., in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asadisadāna</span>). There is an account given (SN.i.74) of Pasenadi seeking a confession from her that she loved him more than her own soul (*attā*) as a confirmation of their mutual trust. But the queen was pious and saw into the reality of things, and declared that nothing was dearer to her than her own soul. Piqued by this answer, Pasenadi sought the Buddha, who comforted him by explaining the true import of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span>’s words.

On another occasion, Pasenadi expressed to the Buddha his disappointment that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span> should have borne him a daughter instead of a son; but the Buddha pointed out to him that there was much, after all, to be said for daughters (SN.i.83).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span> predeceased Pasenadi (AN.iii.57); he had also other wives, one of them being the sister of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, and another <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ubbirī</span> (*q.v.*). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇṇakatthalasutta</span> (MN.ii.125) mentions two others who were sisters: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Somā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakulā</span>. Dhp­a.i.385.

Pasenadi’s relations with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> were very cordial. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> had five millionaires in his kingdom <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jaṭila</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meṇḍaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kākavaliya</span> while Pasenadi had none. Pasenadi therefore visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> and asked for one to be transferred to him. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> gave him <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meṇḍaka</span>’s son, and Pasenadi settled him in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span> (Dhp­a.i.385ff).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (v. 351), the king’s chamberlains, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Purāṇa</span>, speak of his harem. When he went riding in the park he took with him his favourite and lovely wives on elephants, one before and one behind. They were sweetly scented “like caskets of scent” and their hands were soft to the touch.

It is stated that Pasenadi wished to associate himself with the Buddha’s family so that their relationship might be even closer. For seven days he had given alms to the Buddha and one thousand monks, and on the seventh day he asked the Buddha to take his meals regularly at the palace with five hundred monks; but the Buddha refused the request and appointed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> to take his place. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> came daily with five hundred others, but the king was too busy to look after them, and the monks, feeling neglected, failed to come any more, only <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> keeping to his undertaking. When the king became aware of this he was greatly upset, and determined to win the confidence of the monks by marrying a kinswoman of the Buddha. He therefore sent messages to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> chiefs, who were his vassals, asking for the hand of one of their daughters. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> discussed the proposition in their Mote-Hall, and held it beneath the dignity of their clan to accede to it. But, unwilling to incur the wrath of their overlord, they sent him <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsabhakhattiyā</span>, daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> and of a slave woman, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgamuṇḍā</span>. By her, Pasenadi had a son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Viḍūḍabha</span>. When the latter visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, he heard by chance of the fraud that had been practised on his father and vowed vengeance. When he came to the throne, he invaded the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> territory and killed a large number of the clan without distinction of age or sex (Dhp­a.i.339ff.; Ja.i.133f.; iv.144ff). It is said that when Pasenadi heard of the antecedents of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsabhakhattiyā</span>, he withdrew the royal honours, which had been bestowed on her and her son and reduced them to the condition of slaves. But the Buddha, hearing of this, related to Pasenadi the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭṭhahārikajātaka</span> (Ja 7), and made him restore the royal honors to the mother and her son.

Mention is made of another son of Pasenadi, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span>, who entered the Saṅgha and became an *Arahant* (Thag­a.i.460; the *Dulva* says that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jeta</span>, owner of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, was also Pasenadi’s son (Rockhill, p.48)).

Pasenadi’s sister, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosaladevī</span>, was married to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kosala</span> gave her a village in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span> as part of her dowry, for her bath-money. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> killed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosaladevī</span> died of grief, and Pasenadi confiscated the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span> village, saying that no patricide should own a village which was his by right of inheritance. Angered at this, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> declared war upon his aged uncle. At first, victory lay with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, but Pasenadi had spies who reported to him a plan of attack suggested by the Thera <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanuggahatissa</span>, in the course of a conversation with his colleague <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mantidatta</span>, and in the fourth campaign Pasenadi took <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> prisoner, and refused to release him until he renounced his claim to the throne. Upon his renunciation, Pasenadi not only gave him his daughter <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajirā</span> in marriage, but conferred on her, as a wedding gift, the very village in dispute (Ja.ii.237, 403; iv.342f).

Three years later, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Viḍūḍabha</span> revolted against his father. In this he was helped by the commander-in-chief, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha Kārāyaṇa</span>, nephew of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span> (*q.v.*). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span>, chief of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span>, disgusted with the treachery of his own people, had sought refuge with his former classmate, Pasenadi, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span>’s wife, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span>, bore him thirty-two sons, brave and learned. Pasenadi, having listened to the tales of his corrupt ministers, contrived to have <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span> and all his sons killed while they were away quelling a frontier rebellion. BandhuIa’s wife was a devout follower of the Buddha’s faith, and showed no resentment against the king for this act of treachery. This moved the king’s heart, and he made all possible amends. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha Kārāyaṇa</span> never forgave him, and once when Pasenadi was on a visit to the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Medatalumpa</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uḷumpa</span>), leaving the royal insignia with his commander-in-chief, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha Kārāyaṇa</span> took advantage of this opportunity, withdrew the king’s bodyguard, leaving behind only one single horse and one woman servant, hurried back to the capital and crowned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Viḍūḍabha</span> king. When Pasenadi heard of this, he hurried on to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> to enlist <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>’s support; but as it was late, the city gates were closed. Exhausted by his journey, he lay down in a hall outside the city, where he died during the night.

When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> heard the news, he performed the funeral rites over the king’s body with great pomp. He wished to march at once against <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Viḍūḍabha</span>, but desisted on the advice of his ministers (MN.ii.118; MN­a.ii.753ff.; Dhp­a.i.353ff.; Ja.iv.150ff).

Pasenadi had a sister, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanā</span>, who was present at his first interview with the Buddha and decided to enter the Saṅgha, but she delayed doing so as she then had to nurse their aged grandmother. Pasenadi was very fond of his grandmother, and was filled with grief when she died in her one hundred and twentieth year. After her death, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanā</span> became a nun and attained *Arahant*-ship (Thīg­a.22; SN.i.97; AN.iii.32). The old lady’s possessions were given over to the monks, the Buddha giving special permission for them to be accepted (Vin.ii.169).

Among the king’s most valued possessions was the elephant <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Seta</span> (AN.iii.345); he had two other elephants, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhadderaka</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāveyyaka</span>) (Dhp­a.iv.25) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇḍarīka</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> Dhp-a.ii.1). Mention is also made (Ja.iii.134f) of a pet heron which lived in the palace and conveyed messages. Tradition says (SN­a.i.115; Ja.i.382ff) that Pasenadi had in his possession the octagonal gem which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> had given to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusa</span>. He valued it greatly, using it as his turban jewel, and was greatly upset when it was reported lost; it was, however, recovered with the help and advice of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka Commentary</span> records that Pasenadi built a monastery in front of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>. It was called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājakārāma</span>, and the Buddha sometimes stayed there (Ja.ii.15). According to Xuanzang, Pasenadi also built a monastery for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī Gotamī</span> (Beal-Xuan.ii.2).

Pasenadi’s chaplain, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggidatta</span> (*q.v.*) had originally been <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kosala</span>’s chaplain. Pasenadi therefore paid him great respect. This inconvenienced <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggidatta</span>, and he gave his wealth to the poor and renounced the world. Dhp­a.iii.241ff.

Snp­a. (580) says that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="119" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="120" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kosala</span>’s chaplain and Pasenadi studied under him. When Pasenadi came to the throne, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="121" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span> declared his wish to leave the world. The king tried to prevent him but failed; he did, however, persuade <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="122" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span> to live in the royal park. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="123" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span>, after staying there for some time, found life in a city uncongenial. The king thereupon detailed two of his ministers to establish a suitable hermitage for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="124" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span>.

Pasenadi’s minister, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="125" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Santati</span> (*q.v.*), who was once allowed to reign for a week in the king’s place as reward for having quelled a frontier dispute, gave his wealth to the poor and renounced the world like <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="126" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggidatta</span> (Dhp­a.iii.28ff). The king was always ready to pay honour to those who had won the praise of the Buddha, as in the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="127" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāṇā</span> (Dhp--a.ii.150ff), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="128" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Ekasātaka</span> (Dhp--a.iii.2ff) or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="129" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgulimāla</span> (MN.ii.100); on the other hand, he did not hesitate to show his disapproval of those who disregarded the Buddha’s teaching, e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="130" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upananda</span> (SN.i.153f).

Pasenadi liked to be the foremost in gifts to the Buddha and his Saṅgha. This was why he held the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="131" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asadisadāna</span> (*q.v.*) under the guidance and inspiration of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="132" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span>; but he was hurt when the Buddha’s sermon of thanksgiving did not seem to him commensurate with the vast amount (fourteen crores) which he had spent. The Buddha then explained to him that this lack of enthusiasm was out of consideration for the king’s minister <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="133" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷa</span>. When the king learned that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="134" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷa</span> disapproved of the lavish way in which money had been spent at the almsgiving, he banished him from the court, while he allowed the minister <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="135" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Juṇha</span>, who had furthered the almsgiving, to rule over the kingdom for seven days (Dhp­a.iii.188ff).

Pasenadi seems to have enjoyed discussions on topics connected with the Dhamma. Reference has already been made to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="136" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosalasaṁyutta</span>, which records several conversations which he held with the Buddha when visiting him in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="137" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>; even when Pasenadi was engaged in affairs of state in other parts of the kingdom, he would visit the Buddha and engage him in conversation if he was anywhere in the neighbourhood. Two such conversations are recorded in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="138" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammacetiyasutta</span> (*q.v.*) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="139" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇṇakatthalasutta</span> (*q.v.*). If the Buddha was not available, he would seek a disciple. Thus the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="140" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhitikasutta</span> (*q.v.*) records a discussion between Pasenadi and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="141" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="142" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aciravatī</span>. Once when Pasenadi was in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="143" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Toraṇavatthu</span>, midway between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="144" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="145" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, he heard that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="146" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemā Therī</span> was there, and went at once to visit and talk to her (SN.iv.374ff). Rhys Davids thinks (*Buddhist India*, p.10) that Pasenadi was evidently an official title and that the king’s personal name was Agnidatta. He bases this surmise on the fact that in the *Divyāvadāna* (p. 620) the king who gave <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="147" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkaṭṭhā</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="148" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pokkharasādi</span> is called Agnidatta, while in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="149" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghanikāya</span> (i.87) he is called Pasenadi, and that Pasenadi is used, as a designation for several kings. The evidence is, however, insufficient for any definite conclusion to be drawn (e.g., in Divy.369, for a king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="150" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> and again in the *Kathāsaritsāgara* i.268, 298). The Ud­a. (104) explains Pasenadi as “*paccantaṁ parasenaṁ jinātī ti Pasenadi*.” According to Tibetan sources he was so-called because the whole country was illuminated at the time of his birth (Rockhill, p. 16).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="151" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāgatavaṁsa</span> (JPTS 1886, p. 37), Pasenadi is a Bodhisatta. He will be the fourth future Buddha.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="152" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttavibhaṅga</span> (Vin.iv.298) mentions a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="153" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittāgāra</span> (? Art Gallery) which belonged to him.
