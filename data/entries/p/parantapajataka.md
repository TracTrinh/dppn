# Parantapajātaka

## English source

Parantapajātaka (Ja 416). The Bodhisatta was once born as the son of the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. He came to understand the speech of animals, and thus learned from a she-jackal whom he had offended that a hostile king with whom he would have to fight would march on to the city. His father bore him no love, and sent him to fight this king when he arrived. But all the citizens followed the prince, and his father, very alarmed, retired into the forest with his queen, his chaplain, and a servant named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parantapa</span>, and lived in a hut. When the king and the chaplain went for fruit, the queen, though with child by the king, sinned with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parantapa</span> and instigated him to kill the king while on his way to bathe in the river. The chaplain watched this deed but said nothing, and on his return feigned to have been suddenly blinded by a snake’s breath. The queen bore a son, and when he was sixteen, the chaplain told him of what had happened and taught him the use of a sword. Soon after, the boy killed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parantapa</span> and returned with his mother and the chaplain to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, where the Bodhisatta made him his viceroy.

The story was told in reference to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>’s attempts to kill the Buddha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> is identified with the old king. Ja.iii.415-21.
