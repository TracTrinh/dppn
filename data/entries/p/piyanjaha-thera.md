# Piyañjaha Thera

## English source

Piyañjaha Thera. He belonged to the family of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span> nobleman of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> and, when he grew up, his chief interest was war, hence his name (“ever destroying what is dear to his enemies”). When the Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, Piyañjaha found faith in him, joined the Saṅgha and became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>, dwelling in the forest.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span> he had been a tree sprite, and, standing on the edge of a concourse of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> because his influence was small, he had heard the Buddha preach. One day seeing a bank of pure sand by a beautiful river, he reflected that the Buddha’s virtues were more numerous than even the grains of sand. Seventy-three <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> ago he was a king named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulinapupphiya</span>. Thag.vs.76; Thag­a.i.168f.

He is probably identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ñāṇasaññaka</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>. Ap.i.161.
