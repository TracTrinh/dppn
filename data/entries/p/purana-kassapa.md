# Pūraṇa Kassapa

## English source

Pūraṇa Kassapa. *v.l.* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Purāṇa</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>. One of the six well-known teachers, contemporaneous with the Buddha. He is said to have taught the doctrine of non­action (*akiriya*), denying the result of good or bad actions (DN.i.52 f); probably the more correct description of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>’s teaching would be *niṣkriyavāda* – i.e., an affirmation that the soul is passive, unaffected by the good or the bad done by us, the ultimate reality lying beyond good or evil.

Elsewhere (SN.iii.69; v.126), however, he is mentioned as an *ahetuvādin*, denying *hetupaccaya* (condition and cause – i.e., the efficacy of *kamma*), which teaching, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmaññaphalasutta</span>, is attributed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makkhali Gosāla</span> (DN.i.53; see also AN.iii.383, where the teaching of *Chaḷabhijātiyo* is also attributed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūraṇa</span>).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūraṇa Kassapa</span> came by his name from the fact that as a result of his birth the number of slaves in a certain household reached one hundred (DN­a.i.142; he could not have been a slave. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> is a Brahmin *gotta*. The Snp­a (372) calls him an *ājīvaka*). Owing to this fact he was never found fault with, even when he failed to do his work satisfactorily. But, in spite of this, he was dissatisfied and fled from his masters. He then had his clothes stolen by thieves and went about naked. He had a following of five hundred, among whom was the *Devaputta* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asama</span> (SN.i.65, see also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>). He was consulted by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span> (SN.v.126) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāli</span> (SN.iii.68) and by the wanderer <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vacchagotta</span> (SN.iv.398). He claimed to be omniscient. (AN.iv.428; here we probably have a more correct explanation of his name, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūraṇa</span> i.e., in his claim to have attained perfect wisdom, *pūraṇañāṇa*).

A story in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> states that when the heretics were unable to prevent the Buddha from performing the Twin Miracle under the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṇḍamba</span>, they fled discomfited (Dhp­a.iii.208). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūraṇa Kassapa</span> was among them, and in the course of his flight, he came across one of his followers, a farmer, who was on his way to see him, carrying a vessel of broth and a rope. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūraṇa</span> took the vessel and the rope, and going to the banks of the river near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, tied the vessel round his neck and threw himself into the stream. There was a circle of bubbles on the water and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūraṇa</span> was reborn in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avīci</span>. For a different version see Rockhill, 80. According to this legend, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> must have died in the sixteenth year of the Buddha’s ministry. This is hardly reconcilable with the statement that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> consulted him. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span> (p. 4 f) also mentions a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūraṇa Kassapa</span>, contemporary with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milinda</span>. This perhaps refers to a teacher descended from the same school who is credited with the view that the earth rules or sustains the world.
