# Pāṭaligāma

## English source

Pāṭaligāma, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pupphapura</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusamapura</span>. The capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> and situated near the modern Patna. The Buddha visited it shortly before his death. It was then a mere village and was known as Pāṭaligāma. At that time <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>’s ministers, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunīdha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span>, were engaged in building fortifications there in order to repel the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjians</span>. The Buddha prophesied the future greatness of Pāṭaligāma, and also mentioned the danger of its destruction by fire, water, or internal discord. The gate by which the Buddha left the town was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotamadvāra</span>, and the ferry at which he crossed the river, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotamatittha</span> (Vin.i.226-30; DN.ii.86ff).

The date at which Pāṭaliputtta became the capital is uncertain. Xuanzang seems to record that it was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷāsoka</span> who moved the seat of government there (Beal-Xuan.ii.85, n.11). The Jains maintain that it was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span>, son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> (Vin. Texts ii.102, n.1). The latter tradition is probably correct as, according to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (iii.57) even <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Muṇḍa</span> is mentioned as residing at Pāṭaliputta. It was, however, in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> that the city enjoyed its greatest glory. In the ninth year of his reign <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s income from the four gates of the city is said to have been four hundred thousand <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kahāpaṇas</span> daily, with another one hundred thousand for his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sabhā</span> or Council (Vin-a.i.52).

The city was known to the Greeks as Pālibothra, and Megasthenes, who spent some time there, has left a vivid description of it (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Buddhist India</span> 262f). It continued to be the capital during the greater part of the Gupta dynasty, from the fourth to the sixth century CE. Near Pāṭaliputta was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkuṭārāma</span>, where monks (e.g. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhadda</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span>) stayed when they came to Pāṭaliputta (MN.i.349; AN.v.341; AN.iii.57; SN.v.15f., 171f). At the suggestion of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> Thera<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">,</span> the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghoṭamukha</span> built an assembly ball for the monks in the city (MN.ii.163).

Pāṭaligāma was so-called because on the day of its foundation several <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pāṭali</span>-shoots sprouted forth from the ground. The officers of Ajātasattu and of the Licchavī princes would come from time to time to Pāṭaligāma, drive the people from their houses, and occupy them themselves. A large hall was therefore built in the middle of the village, divided into various apartments for the housing of the officers and their retainers when necessary. The Buddha arrived in the village on the day of the completion of the building, and the villagers invited him to occupy it for a night, that it might be blessed by his presence. On the next day they entertained the Buddha and his monks to a meal (Ud.viii.6; Ud­a.407ff).

Pāṭaliputta was also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pupphapura</span> (Mhv.iv.31, etc.; Dpv.xi.28) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusamapura</span> (Mbv.p.153). The journey from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambukola</span>, in Ceylon, to Pāṭaliputta took fourteen days, seven of which were spent on the sea voyage to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāmalitti</span> (e.g., Mhv.xi.24). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asokārāma</span> built by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> was near Pāṭaliputta (Mhv.xxix.36). The Buddha’s water pot and belt were deposited in Pāṭaliputta after his death (Bv.xxviii.9). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Petavatthu</span> Commentary (p.271) mentions that trade was carried on between Pāṭaliputta and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suvaṇṇabhūmi</span>.
