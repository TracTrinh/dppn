# Pāṭala 01

## English source

Pāṭala 01. A dancer. He lived in a village near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. One day, having earned some money in a fete in the city, he sat down on the bank of the river, eating and drinking with his wife. He became drunk, and fastening his lute round his neck, he went with his wife down to the river. The water filled his lute and he began to sink. His wife thereupon let go of his hand and came out of the river. Seeing him about to drown, the wife begged of him one song wherewith to earn her living. He sang her a stanza to the effect that the water of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span>, which was the salvation of many, proved to be his bane.

This story was among those related by the Bodhisatta in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padakusalamāṇavajātaka</span> (Ja 432, Ja.iii.507f). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārupaṇas</span> made use of this story in poking fun at the *Ekaṁsikas*, because the texts chosen by the *Ekaṁsikas* to prove their case proved just the contrary (see Bode, 76, n.3).
