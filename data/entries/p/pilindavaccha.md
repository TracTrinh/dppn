# Pilindavaccha

## English source

Pilindavaccha, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pilindivaccha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pilindiyavaccha</span>. He was a Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, born before the Buddha’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>. Pilinda was his personal name, Vaccha being that of his family. He became a recluse and learnt the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Gandhāra</span> *vijjā*, but, when the Buddha appeared, the charm refused to work. Having heard that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Gandhāra</span> prevented the working of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Gandhāra</span> and having concluded that the Buddha knew the former, he entered the Saṅgha at the Buddha’s suggestion, in order to acquire it. The Buddha gave him exercises in meditation, and he became an *Arahant*.

Certain <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> who had been born in the *Deva* world as a result of Pilinda’s guidance in a former birth, out of gratitude, waited on him morning and evening. He thus became famous as being dear to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, and was declared by the Buddha to be chief among such monks (AN.i.24).

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>, he was a rich householder of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsavatī</span> and wished to become a monk beloved of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumedha Buddha</span> be was born in the world of men and paid great honour to the Buddha’s *Thūpa*. In a later existence he was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Varuṇa</span>, and established his subjects in righteousness, so that after death they were born in heaven (Thag­a.i.51f).

Pilinda had a habit of addressing everyone as *Vasala* (outcaste). When this was reported to the Buddha he explained that this was because Pilinda had, for one hundred lives, been born among *Vasalavādī* Brahmins (Ud.iii.6; Dhp­a.iv.181f). One day, on entering <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, Pilinda met a man carrying a bowl of *pipphalī* (long pepper). “What’s in your bowl, Vasala?” he asked, and the man, in anger, said, “The dung of mice.” “So be it,” said Pilinda, and the pepper turned into dung. The man was horrified, and, seeking Pilinda, persuaded him to right the matter (AN­a.i.154f).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vinayapiṭaka</span> mentions that on several different occasions Pilinda suffered from various ailments and the Buddha had to give permission for the provision of suitable remedies. Vin.i.204f.; some hold that the Thera of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, mentioned in the following stories, was distinct from the Thera of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> (e.g., *Brethren*, 14, n.4). See below.

Once <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> found Pilinda, clearing a cave in order to provide a cell for himself. The king promised to build a monastery for him if he could obtain the Buddha’s sanction. The permission was obtained and was reported to the king, but he forgot the matter until one hundred days later. On remembering, he made ample amends, gave Pilinda five hundred attendants to look after the monastery, and granted for their maintenance a village, which came to be called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Arāmikagāma</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pilindagāma</span>. One day, while in the village for alms, Pilinda went into a house where a girl was weeping because the day was a feast day and she had no ornament to wear, her parents being too poor to afford any. Pilinda gave her a roll of grass to put round her head and it turned instantly into solid gold. The king’s officers, hearing of this wreath, suspected the family of theft and cast them into prison. The next day Pilinda, discovering what had happened, visited the king and convinced him of his *iddhi* powers by turning the whole palace into gold. The family was released, and the king and his courtiers gave to Pilinda large quantities of the five medicaments, all of which Pilinda distributed among those who wished for them. Vin.i.206 ff.; iii.248ff. This was the occasion for the forming of the rule that all medicaments required by a monk should be used within seven days. It was in reference to this that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhārajātaka</span> (Ja.iii.363 ff.) was preached. The incident of the palace being turned into gold is referred to at Kv.608.

Another story is related of Pilinda’s *iddhi* powers (Vin.iii.67). Once a family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, which was wont to minister to Pilinda, was attacked by robbers and two girls were kidnapped. Pilinda, by his *iddhi* power caused them to be brought back, and the monks complained of this to the Buddha, but the Buddha held that no wrong had been done. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (i.59f.; 302-16) has two sets of verses ascribed to Pilinda, the second very much longer than the first, thus supporting the view mentioned earlier, that there were two Theras named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pilindavaccha</span>. In any event, there has evidently been a confusion of legends, and it is no longer possible to separate them. It is the first set of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> verses which is quoted in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> Commentary (loc.cit.). In the second set we are told that in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>, Pilinda was a very wealthy *dovārika*. He took many precious gifts to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span>’s father, and won from him a boon. He asked, as his boon, that he should be allowed to entertain the Buddha. The king refused to grant this, but the *dovārika* appealed to the judges and they gave the verdict in his favour. Thereupon he held a great almsgiving of unparalleled splendour for seven days and gave away all manner of gifts. As a result he was born one thousand times as king of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> and one thousand times also as king of men. In his last birth he suffered from neither heat nor cold, dust did not adhere to his body, and the rain did not wet him.
