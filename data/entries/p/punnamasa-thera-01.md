# Puṇṇamāsa Thera 01

## English source

Puṇṇamāsa Thera 01. He was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> as the son of a Brahmin, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samiddhi</span>; he was called Puṇṇamāsa because, on the day of his birth, all the empty vessels in the house were filled with golden pennies. He left the world after a son had been born to him, and, having entered the Saṅgha under the Buddha, took the formula of the five impurities (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">tacapañcaka</span>) as topic of meditation and became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. His former wife adorned herself and came with her child, seeking to seduce him, but without success.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span> he was a partridge (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cakkavāka</span>), and, pleased with the appearance of the Buddha, offered him a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sāla</span>-flower, holding it in his beak. Seventeen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> ago he became king eight times under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sucārudassana</span> (Thag. vs. 10; Thag­a.i.53f).

He is evidently identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paccāgamanīya</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>. Ap.i.113.
