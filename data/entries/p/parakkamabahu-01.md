# Parakkamabāhu 01

## English source

Parakkamabāhu 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu I</span>. King of Ceylon (1153-86). He was the son of the eldest of the three brothers, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māṇābharaṇa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kittisirimegha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirivallabha</span>, who ruled over <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇadesa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohaṇa</span>. He was born at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṅkhagāma</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇadesa</span>, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māṇābharaṇa</span> was ruler. His birth was accompanied by various miracles. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vikkamabāhu II</span>., the then reigning king at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulatthipura</span>, hearing of this, wished to bring the boy up at his own court and make him his heir in place of his own son. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māṇābharaṇa</span> refused to consent to this, and soon after died. Thereupon his brother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kittisirimegha</span> took over <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇadesa</span> and left <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohaṇa</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirivallabha</span>, who brought <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māṇābharaṇa</span>’s widow <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratnāvalī</span> with her two daughters <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mittā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pabhāvatī</span> and her son Parakkama, to his capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāgakula</span>. Meanwhile <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vikkamabāhu</span> dies and is succeeded by his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhu</span>, who maintains his position in spite of the attacks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kittisirimegha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirivallabha</span>. Parakkama, finding no scope for his talents in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohaṇa</span>, seeks his uncle <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kittisirimegha</span>, who receives him joyfully and takes him to live in his capital at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkhatthalī</span>. There Parakkama finishes his education, and his coming of age is celebrated under the direction of the *Senāpati* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkha</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Badalatthalī</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirivallabha</span> dies in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohaṇa</span> and is succeeded by his son, the younger <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māṇābharaṇa</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkama</span>’s ambitious spirit makes him restless, and he is discontented at the prospect of serving a petty principality. He wishes to seek royal dignity in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājaraṭṭha</span>, and refuses to listen to his uncle’s dissuasion, who assures him that he is only influenced by his love for him and fears for his safety. But Parakkama leaves <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkhatthalī</span> secretly and goes to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Badalatthalī</span>, where the general <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkha</span> is slain because he informed the king of Parakkama’s flight. Parakkama then goes to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhagāma</span> near the frontiers of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājaraṭṭha</span>. The inhabitants make repeated attempts to check his advance, but he repels these by his valour.

Meanwhile <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kittisirimegha</span>, after consultation with his ministers, sends a stronger force to try and overcome him, but the force is ambushed by the prince and completely routed after a night assault in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khīravāpi</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkama</span> then goes to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājaraṭṭha</span>, where messengers with gifts from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhu</span> meet him, the latter following to greet him in person and bring him to the capital. Parakkama lives at the court in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulatthipura</span>, but spends his time in spying out the country and intriguing with his host’s subjects. In order to lull the suspicions of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhu</span>, he gives him his sister <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddavatī</span> in marriage, keeping her dowry in his own hands. Later, feeling his position insecure, he returns to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇadesa</span>, meeting on the way with all kinds of adventures which put his courage to the test. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kittisirimegha</span>, delighted to hear of his return, sends messengers to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saraggāma</span> to meet him. But Parakkama hesitates to enter the capital until persuaded by his mother to do so. Shortly after, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kittisirimegha</span> dies and Parakkama succeeds to the throne. He consolidates his position by various captures, including that of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhu</span>, which follows on the storming of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulatthipura</span>. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māṇābharaṇa</span> comes to the rescue, defeats Parakkama’s army, and sets <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhu</span> free; but the latter, finding him unbearable, appeals to Parakkama for help. War ensues, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhu</span>, again at liberty, flees, while his officers fight with Parakkama. Utimately he abdicates in favour of the latter, and dies at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgātaṭa</span>. His ministers, however, send for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māṇābharaṇa</span>, while Parakkama hastens to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulatthipura</span>, where he is crowned. A campaign of varying fortunes ensues ending in the defeat of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māṇābharaṇa</span>, who flees to his own country, where he dies. Parakkama is then crowned a second time. Parakkama is now sole monarch, but his rule is not universally acceptable. In the fourth year of his reign, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sugalā</span>, mother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māṇābharaṇa</span>, raises the standard of revolt in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohaṇa</span>. The campaign against her is a protracted one and is conducted by the general <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rakkha</span>. In the early part of the campaign the Tooth and Bowl Relics are recovered and brought with great ceremony to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulatthipura</span>. The rebels are gradually cornered and defeated. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sugalā</span> is captured, and the revolt collapses. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohaṇa</span> is quiet for a time, but rises again after some years.

In the twelfth year of his reign, Parakkama goes to war with the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāmañña</span>, disputes having arisen about the elephant trade and the treatment of the Sinhalese ambassadors, the crowning offence being the seizing of a princess who was being sent from Ceylon to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kamboja</span>. A fleet is collected at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pallavavaṅka</span>, and the soldiers are landed at the port of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusumī</span>, with the *Nagaragiri* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kitti</span> at their head, a further attack being made by the *Damiḷādhikārī* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ādicca</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Papphālama</span>. After five months the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāmañña</span> king is slain and peace again restored.

Soon after, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍu</span> king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkama</span> being besieged by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Coḷa</span> king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kulasekhara</span>, appeals for help from Ceylon. Parakkamabāhu sends an army under his general <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Laṅkāpura</span>, but, in the meantime, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍu</span> king has been slain and his capital <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madhurā</span> taken. The Sinhalese army, however, landed on the opposite coast and carried on a war against the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Coḷas</span>, and built a fortress called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamapura</span>. As a result of this campaign, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kulasekhara</span> was defeated and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍu</span> king’s son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vīrapaṇḍu</span>, was crowned in the ancient capital. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Coḷa</span> prisoners were sent to Ceylon and employed in repairing the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratanavāluka</span> *cetiya*. The village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍuvijaya</span> was founded by Parakkama to commemorate the victory. The ultimate outcome of this expedition is not certain. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Coḷa</span> records claim that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Laṅkāpura</span> was defeated, and that his head was nailed to the gates of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madhurā</span> together with those of his generals. The war of the Paṇḍyan succession did not end there.

Parakkamabāhu now engaged in more peaceful pursuits and, after some trouble, he succeeded in reconciling the three sects of monks the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> and held a convocation under a Thera called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span>. The *Vaitulya* heresy now finally disappeared from Ceylon. The king built for the use of the monks the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavanārāma</span>, including a round Temple of the Tooth, in the vicinity of the royal palace, and, further to the north, he constructed the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ālāhaṇapariveṇa</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Laṅkātilaka</span> *vihāra* and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Baddhasīmāpāsāda</span>. He also built the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacchimārāma</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarārāma</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> (or *Damiḷa Thūpa*).

In the three suburbs he built the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārārāma</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span> *vihāras*, and, in addition, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapila</span> *vihāra*, while he restored the shrines at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>.

Parakkamabāhu also enlarged and fortified <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulatthipura</span> and adorned the city with numerous palaces and pleasure gardens. He paid great attention to irrigation, opening the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ākāsagaṅgā</span> and forming or improving a system of irrigation, its centre being in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamasamudda</span>, and building numerous tanks throughout the country.

The internal peace of the latter half of his reign was disturbed only by a rebellion near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Tittha</span>, this being easily quelled.

Parakkamabāhu I. was succeeded by his sister’s son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijayabahu II</span>. According to the *Nikāyasaṅgraha*, Parakkama was born after death as the god <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Naradeva</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span>.

Chaps. 62-79 of the Cv. are devoted to a description of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu</span> and his reign. The above is a very concise account of the contents of these chapters.
