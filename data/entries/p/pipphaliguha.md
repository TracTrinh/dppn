# Pipphaliguhā

## English source

Pipphaliguhā. A cave near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, evidently a favourite haunt of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span>. Once when he lay there grievously ill, the Buddha visited him and cheered him by talking to him of the seven <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">bojjhaṅgas</span> (SN.v.79). On another occasion, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> spent several days there in a jhāna, and when, at the end of that period, he entered <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> for alms, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujātā</span>, waited for him in the guise of a weaver, and his wife gave him a meal. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> discovered this, he asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> not to do such a thing again; but the Buddha heard <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s song of triumph when his meal was accepted and praised him for his gift (Ud.iii.7; Ud­a.195f.; Dhp­a.i.427f.; see also Ud.i.6).

On another occasion, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> was in the Pipphaliguhā, he had two novices waiting on him. One was lazy and a liar and took the credit for the work, all of which was done by the other. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> admonished him, and he then set fire to the Elder’s hut and ran away (Dhp­a.ii.19f). One day, as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> sat in the Pipphaliguhā, he tried to contemplate the rising and falling of living beings, but the Buddha discouraged him, saying that such a task was beyond his abilities (Dhp­a.i.258f). The goddess <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lājā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), who had been born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> as a reward for a meal which she once gave to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>, attempted to sweep his cell in the Pipphaliguhā and to look after it, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> rejected her services. Dhp­a.iii.6ff.
