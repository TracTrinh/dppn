# Pakudha Kaccāyana

## English source

Pakudha Kaccāyana (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pakudha Kātiyāna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakudha Kaccāyana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakuda Kātiyāna</span>). Head of one of the six heretical sects of the Buddha’s time. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmaññaphalasutta</span> (DN.i.56), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātassattu</span> is said to have visited him and obtained from him an exposition of his teaching, which was to the effect that the four elements – earth, fire, air, water; pleasure, pain, and the soul – these seven things were eternally existent and unchangeable in their very nature; that there is no volitional activity of consciousness in them. His doctrine is, therefore, one of non­action (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">akiriyavāda</span>). When one, with a sharp sword, cleaves a head in twain, no one is thereby deprived of life, a sword has merely penetrated into the interval between seven elementary substances (cf. the doctrine of the Cartesians, that there is no sin in taking the life of lower animals because they have no soul). In other words, there is no such act as killing, or hearing, or knowing, etc.; no conceptions of, or distinction between, good and bad, knowledge and ignorance, etc.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pakudha</span>’s teachings are also referred to in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sandakasutta</span> (MN.i.517), and there described at even greater length, but there his name is not mentioned.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> adds that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pakudha</span> avoided the use of cold water, using always hot; when this was not available, he did not wash (DN­a.i.144). If he crossed a stream he would consider this as a sin, and would make expiation by constructing a mound of earth. This is evidence of the ascetic tendency in his teaching on matters of external conduct. His teaching is, however, described as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nissirīkaladdhi</span>.

We are told (MN.i.250; ii.4) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pakudha</span>’s followers did not hold him in high esteem, in contrast to the devotion felt for the Buddha by his followers. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pakudha</span> did not welcome questions, and displayed annoyance and resentment when cross examined. Elsewhere however, he is spoken of as having been highly honoured by the people, a teacher of large and well reputed schools, with numerous followers (e.g., MN.i.198; SN.i.66; Snp.p.91). But he did not lay claim to perfect enlightenment (SN.i.68).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pakudha Kaccāyana</span>’s name is spelt in several ways. Some texts give his personal name as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakudha</span>, or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakuda</span>. In the Praśnopaniṣad (Barua.281; see also Divy.143; Mvu.i.253, 256, 259; iii.383) mention is made of a Kakuda Kātyāna, a younger contemporary of Pippalāda. There he is called Kabandhiṇ, which name, like Kakuda, means that he had a hump on his neck or shoulder.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pakudha</span> was his personal name and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāyana</span> that of his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gotta</span> (DN­a.i.144; SN­a.i.102). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāyana</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kātiyāna</span>, as it is sometimes called) was a Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gotta</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pakudha</span> is mentioned as having been, in a past life, one of the five <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">diṭṭhigatikas</span> mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Bodhijātaka</span> (Ja 528, Ja.v.246). He is also mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span> as one of the teachers visited by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milinda</span>. The whole account is either a plagiarism of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmaññaphalasutta</span> or else the teachers referred to only belonged to the same respective schools of thought.
