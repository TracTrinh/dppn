# Pūtimaṁsajātaka

## English source

Pūtimaṁsajātaka (Ja 437). Once, on the slopes of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span>, lived a jackal called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūtimaṁsa</span> with his mate <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veṇī</span>. Near by dwelt a flock of wild goats. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūtimaṁsa</span> formed a device for killing the goats one by one and eating their flesh, till only a she-goat, called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Melamātā</span>, was left. Wishing to devour her as well, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūtimaṁsa</span> suggested to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veṇī</span> that he should pretend to be dead and that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veṇī</span> should then entice <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Melamātā</span> into the cave by asking her to assist in the funeral rites. But the goat was wise and observant and discovered the ruse. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veṇī</span> went to her later and saying that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūtimaṁsa</span> had recovered consciousness at the very sight of her, invited her to join them in a feast to celebrate his recovery. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Melamātā</span>, agreed, saying that she would bring with her a large escort of her friends, fierce dogs, including <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maliya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṅgiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caturakkha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambuka</span>, in order that the celebration might be a great one. At this suggestion <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūtimaṁsa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veṇī</span> fled from their cave, taking refuge elsewhere.

The story was told to the monks in order to impress on them the necessity for keeping guard over their senses. Ja.iii.532ff.
