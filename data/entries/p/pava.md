# Pāvā

## English source

Pāvā. A city of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> visited during his last journey, going there from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhogagāma</span> and stopping at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span>’s mango grove. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span> lived in Pāvā and invited the Buddha to a meal, which proved to be his last. It was on this occasion that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cundasutta</span> (1) was preached (Snp­a.i. 159). From Pāvā the Buddha journeyed on to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>, crossing the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakkuṭṭhā</span> on the way. DN.ii.126 ff.; Ud.viii.5; the road from Pāvā to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusināra</span> is mentioned several times in the books – e.g., Vin.ii.284; DN.ii.162.

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅgītisutta</span>, at the time the Buddha was staying at Pāvā, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> had just completed their new Mote hall, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ubbhaṭaka</span>, and, at their invitation, the Buddha consecrated it by first occupying it and then preaching in it. After the Buddha had finished speaking, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> recited the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅgītisutta</span> to the assembled monks.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span> was also a centre of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span> and, at the time mentioned above, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭha Nāthaputta</span> had just died at Pāvā and his followers were divided by bitter wrangles (DN.iii.210). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Samaṇuddesa</span> was spending his rainy season at Pāvā, and he reported to the Buddha, who was at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmagāma</span>, news of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span>’ quarrels (DN.iii.117f.; MN.ii.243f).

The distance from Pāvā to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusināra</span> was three <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gāvutas</span>. It is said (Ud­a.403) that on the way between these two places, the Buddha had to stop at twenty-five resting places, so faint and weary was he.

Mention is made in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> (i.7) of the Buddha having stayed at the Ajakapālaka <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span> in Pāvā. This may have been during a previous visit.

After the Buddha’s death, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> of Pāvā claimed a share in his relics. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Doṇa</span> satisfied their claim, and a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thūpa</span> was erected in Pāvā over their share of the relics (DN.ii.167; Bv.xxviii.3).

The inhabitants of Pāvā are called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāveyyakā</span>. Pāvā was the birthplace of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khaṇḍasumana</span>.
