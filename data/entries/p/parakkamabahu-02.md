# Parakkamabāhu 02

## English source

Parakkamabāhu 02. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu II</span>. Son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijayabāhu III</span>. and brother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhuvanekabāhu</span>. He was born at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirivaḍḍhana</span>, and, in his youth, was entrusted to the care of the monks under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅgharakkhita</span>. On the death of his father he ascended the throne, and reigned for thirty-three years (1236-68), at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambuddoṇi</span>, as Parakkamabāhu <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">II</span>. On account of his profound erudition, he received the sobriquet of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kāḷikāla Sāhicca sabbaññupaṇḍita</span> (“the scholar entirely familiar with literature in the Dark Age”). The first act of his reign was the bringing of the Tooth Relic from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Billa</span> mountain to the capital, amidst the exhibition of various miracles. He then set about regaining <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulatthipura</span> from the Tamils, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māghinda</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayabāhu</span> at their head, and this was accomplished by 1244. In the eleventh year of his reign Ceylon was invaded by a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Jāvak</span>a (Javanese) named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candabhānu</span>, probably a sea-robber with a large force. The attack was repulsed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vīrabāhu</span>, the king’s nephew, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candabhānu</span> appeared again later. The rest of Parakkama’s life was devoted to pious works. He invited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Coḷa</span> monks over to Ceylon and held a convocation, with the object of reforming the priesthood, and showed special honour to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammakitti</span>, a monk of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tambaraṭṭha</span>. Among buildings erected by him were the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhuvenakabāhu</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pariveṇa</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Billasela</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Mahindabāhu</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pariveṇa</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthiselapura</span>. He also restored the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāras</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalyāṇi</span> and at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthavaṅgalla</span>. He added to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirivijayasundara</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> built by his father and inaugurated a yearly festival in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devanagara</span>. He made a pilgrimage to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samantakūṭa</span> and erected a bridge, so that pilgrims might reach it more easily. In all these works he was assisted by his minister <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devappatirāja</span>. Parakkama had five sons: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijayabāhu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhuvanekabāhu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tibhuvanamalla</span>, Parakkamabāhu and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayabāhu</span>. In his old age he abdicated in favour of his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijayabāhu</span>, who, because of his piety, was called Bodhisatta. Cv., chaps. 81-9.
