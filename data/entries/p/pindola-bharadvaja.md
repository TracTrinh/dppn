# Piṇḍola Bhāradvāja

## English source

Piṇḍola Bhāradvāja. The son of the chaplain of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>. He belonged to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhāradvāja</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gotta</span>. He learnt the Vedas and became a successful teacher, but, finding his work distasteful, he went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. There he saw the gifts and favours bestowed on the Buddha’s disciples and joined the Saṅgha. He was very greedy, and went about with a large bowl made of dried gourd, which he kept under his bed at night and which made a scraping sound when touched; but the Buddha refused to allow him a bag for it until it should be worn down by constant contact. Later he followed the Buddha’s advice, conquered his intemperance in diet, and became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. He then announced before the Buddha his readiness to answer the questions of any doubting monks, thus uttering his “lion’s roar.” The Buddha declared him chief of the “lion-roarers” (AN.i.23; AN­a.i.112f.; Thag­a.i.245f.; Ud­a. 252; SN­a.iii.26). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> (iv.6) contains the praise uttered by him of the Buddha, because of his perfected self-mastery.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍola</span> was in the habit of taking his siesta in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>’s park at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> (he had been king in a former birth and had spent many days in that park). One day <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>’s women, who had come to the park with him, left him asleep and crowded round <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍola</span> to hear him preach. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>, noticing their absence, went in search of them, and, in his anger, ordered a nest of red ants to be put on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍola</span>’s body. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍola</span> vanished and returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, where the Buddha related the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅgajātaka</span> (Ja 497, Ja.iv.375ff.) and also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Guhaṭṭhakasutta</span> (Snp­a.ii.514f) (SN­a.iii.26 says that when the king went to fetch the red ants from an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">asoka</span> tree, the ants fell on him and started to sting him. The women, under pretence of helping him, picked up the ants that fell from him and replaced them on his body, because they were angry at his rudeness to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍola</span>). Later, we find <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> consulting him at the same spot and following his advice regarding the control of the senses (SN.iv.110f.; SN­a.iii.26).

In the Vinaya we find the Buddha rebuking <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍola</span> for performing a cheap miracle (Vin.ii.110f.; the story is given in greater detail at Dhp­a.iii.201ff.; see also Ja.iv.263). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> had placed a sandalwood bowl on a high pole and challenged any holy person to bring it down. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍola</span> heard of this and, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>’s suggestion, rose in the air by magic power and brought it down. The Buddha blamed him for using his great gifts for an unworthy end. The bowl was given to the monks to be ground into sandal paste.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍola</span> had been a lion in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span>. The Buddha visited the lion in his cave, who waited on him for seven days, paying him great honour. Later, the lion died and was reborn in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsavatī</span>, where he heard the Buddha preach and declare one of his disciples chief of the “lion-roarers.” Eight <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> ago he was a king named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paduma</span>. AN­a.i.111f.; Ap.i.50f.; Thag­a.i.244f.; the last seems to identify him with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piyālaphaladāyaka</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>. (ii.444).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> contains two verses (vs.123-4) of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍola</span>, uttered by him to a former friend, to convince him that he was no longer greedy and self-seeking. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span> (pp. 398, 404) contains two other verses not traced elsewhere.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapāla</span> says that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍola</span> was so-called because he entered the Saṅgha from love of food (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">piṇḍaṁ ulamāno pariyesamāno pabbajito ti, Piṇḍolo</span>) (Ud­a.252; see also SN­a.iii.26).
