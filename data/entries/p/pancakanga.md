# Pañcakaṅga

## English source

Pañcakaṅga. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thapati</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>. He was a devoted follower of the Buddha and loved discussion.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bahuvedanīyasutta</span> (MN.i.396ff.; see also SN.iv.223f) is based on a discussion between him and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Udāyī</span>, which discussion <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> overheard and repeated to the Buddha. On another occasion, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcakaṅga</span> related to the Buddha the conversation he had had with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Paribbājaka</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uggāhamāna Samaṇa Maṇḍikāputta</span>, and the Buddha preached to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samaṇamaṇḍikāsutta</span> (MN.ii.23ff).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddhasutta</span> contains a discussion between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhiya Kaccāna</span>, which took place at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcakaṅga</span>’s house, whither he had invited them (MN.iii.144ff). The discussion was started by a question asked by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcakaṅga</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcakaṅga</span> was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thapati</span>’s personal name, and that he was so-called (“Five Tools”) because he carried the five tools of a carpenter: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vāsīpharasu</span> (adze), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nikhādana</span> (chisel), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">daṇḍa</span> (measuring stick), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">muggara</span> (gavel), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kāḷasutta</span> (blackened thread) (MN­a.ii.629; SN­a.iii.86). He explains <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thapati</span> by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vaḍḍhakī jeṭṭha</span>.
