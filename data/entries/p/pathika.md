# Pāṭhika

## English source

Pāṭhika (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭhiya</span>). An <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ājīvaka</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>. He was looked after by a woman of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> who, hearing one day her neighbours praise the Buddha, wished to invite him to her home. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭhika</span> dissuaded her from so doing; but one day, unbeknown to him, she sent her son to invite the Buddha. The boy called at Pāṭhika’s hermitage on the way, and the ascetic tried to dissuade him from going. Failing to do so, he told him not to tell the Buddha where to find the house, hoping that thus they would be able to eat all the food prepared for the Buddha themselves. The boy did as he was told, and the next day he and Pāṭhika hid in a room at the back of the house. The Buddha came, and, after the meal, thanked the woman. But when Pāṭhika heard her applaud the Buddha’s sermon, he could no longer forbear and rushed forth to abuse her. Dhp­a.i.376ff.
