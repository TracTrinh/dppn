# Pukkusāti

## English source

Pukkusāti. A young monk whom the Buddha met at the house of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaggava</span>, the potter, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. Pukkusāti was already occupying the guest room of the house, and the Buddha asked to be allowed to share it, to which Pukkusāti readily agreed. They sat together for sometime in silence, and then the Buddha preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhātuvibhaṅgasutta</span>. Pukkusāti recognised the Buddha at the end of the sermon and begged his forgiveness for not having paid him due honour; he then begged to have the *upasampadā* conferred on him. The Buddha consented and sent him to procure a begging bowl and a robe. On the way Pukkusāti was gored to death by a mad cow. When this was reported to the Buddha, he said that Pukkusāti was an *Anāgāmī* and had been born in the realms above, never more to return. MN.iii.237-47. In this context Pukkusāti is spoken of as a *kulaputta* (iii.238); see also Ja.iv.180 and Dhp­a.ii.35.

In his comments on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhātuvibhaṅgasutta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> gives a long account of Pukkusāti. MN­a.ii.979 ff. Cp. the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Roruva</span> (Thag­a.i.199f.)

He had been the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span>, contemporary of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> and of about the same age. A friendly alliance was established between the two kings through the medium of merchants who travelled between the two countries for purposes of trade. In the course of time, although the two kings had never seen each other, there grew up between them a deep bond of affection. Pukkusāti once sent to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, as a gift, eight priceless garments in lacquered caskets. This gift was accepted at a special meeting of the whole court, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> having nothing of a material nature, which he considered precious enough to send to Pukkusāti, conceived the idea of acquainting Pukkusāti with the appearance in the world of the Three Jewels (*ratanāni*) the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Saṅgha. He had inscribed on a golden plate, four cubits long and a span in breadth, descriptions of these Three Jewels and of various tenets of the Buddha’s teachings, such as the *satipaṭṭhānā*, the Noble Eightfold Path, and the Thirty-seven factors of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>. This plate was placed in the innermost of several caskets of various precious substances, and was taken in procession on the back of the state elephant up to the frontier of Bimbisira’s kingdom. Similar honours were paid to it by the chiefs of other territories, through which lay the route to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span>.

When Pukkusāti, in the solitude of his chamber, read the inscription on the plate, he was filled with boundless joy and decided to renounce the world. He cut off his hair, donned the yellow robes of a monk, and left the palace alone amid the lamentations of his subjects. He travelled the one hundred and ninety-two leagues to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, passing the gates of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>; but having understood from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>’s letter that the Buddha was at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, he omitted to enquire for him at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, and travelled on forty-five leagues more to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, only to find that the Buddha was all the time in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>. As it was then evening, he took lodging in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaggava</span>’s house. The Buddha, with his divine eye, saw what was in store for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span>, and travelling on foot from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, reached <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaggava</span>’s house at sundown, and, waiting his opportunity, engaged <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span> in talk and preached to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhātuvibhaṅgasutta</span>, as related above. After his untimely death <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span> was born in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avihā</span> world, where, together with six others, he became an *Arahant* at the moment of his birth (see SN.i.35, 60, for the names of the others.).

The cow that killed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span> is said to have been a *Yakkhinī* who was a cow in one hundred births. In her last birth as a cow, she killed, in addition to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhiya Dāruciriya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tambadāṭhika</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppabuddha</span> the leper (Dhp­a.ii.35).

Pukkhusāti was one of seven monks who, in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>, decided to abstain from eating until they should attain *Arahant*-ship. They lived on the top of a mountain. The senior monk attained *Arahant*-ship, the second became an *Anāgāmī*, but the remaining five died of starvation and were reborn in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span>. In this age they became, respectively, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumāra Kassapa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dārucīriya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba Mallaputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiya</span>. Ap.ii.473; Dhp­a.ii.212; Ud­a.81; but see MN­a.i.335, where only three are mentioned (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dārucīriya</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>).
