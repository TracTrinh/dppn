# Pañcavaggiyā

## English source

Pañcavaggiyā. The name given to the five monks:

- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Koṇḍañña</span>, (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Aññā Koṇḍañña</span>),
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Bhaddiya,</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Vappa,</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mahā Nāma</span>
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Assaji</span>

to whom the Buddha preached his first sermon at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>.

Of these, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇḍañña</span> was the youngest of the eight Brahmins who read the signs on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>’s body on the day of the name-giving festival. The four others were children of four of the other Brahmins. They had been advised by their fathers to watch <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>’s career and to join him should he renounce the world. This they did, and all five joined in the austerities of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span>. When he abandoned his austerities and started to follow the Middle Way, they left him in disappointment. But after the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>, the Buddha visited them and preached to them. At first they refused to pay heed to him, but gradually his powers of persuasion won their hearts and they became his first disciples.

It is noteworthy that, although warned by their fathers of the great destiny awaiting <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>, they were yet reluctant to accept the Buddha’s claim to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>. Ja.i.57, 67, 81, 82; Dhp­a.i.87, etc.

For details of the members of the group, see under their several names; see also article by Mrs. Rhys Davids in JRAS. for 1927 on them as the “Unknown co-founders of Buddhism,” where she suggests that the members of this group were responsible for certain tendencies in the Buddha’s teaching.
