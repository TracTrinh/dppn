# Pokkharasāti

## English source

Pokkharasāti, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pokkharasādi</span>. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Sāla</span> Brahmin of great wealth and learning who lived in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkaṭṭhā</span>, on a royal demesne given by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭha</span> was the pupil of Pokkharasāti, who sent him to the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Icchānaṅgala</span> to discover if the report of the Buddha’s greatness were true. When Pokkharasāti heard later that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭha</span> had been rude to the Buddha, he sought the Buddha by night and begged for his forgiveness. The next day he invited the Buddha to a meal, and having listened to his teaching, declared himself his follower and became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span> (DN.i.87f., 106ff). Owing to his eminence, he was present at the meetings of the Brahmins held in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manasākaṭa</span> (DN.i.235) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Icchānaṅgala</span> (Snp.p.115). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāseṭṭha</span>, of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāseṭṭhasutta</span>, was also his pupil (Snp.vs.594). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhasutta</span> (MN.ii.200ff), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subha Todeyyaputta</span>, another disciple, is reported to have said that Pokkharasāti – here described as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Opamañña</span> (of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upamañña</span> clan) and lord of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhagavana</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhagavanika</span>) treated as empty boasts the claims of Brahmins and recluses to transcend ordinary human bonds and rise to the height of Ariyan knowledge. This evidently refers to a time prior to his conversion. The same <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> mentions a slave girl of Pokkharasāti, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇikā</span> by name.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vimānavatthu</span> gives the name of another of his disciples, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chattamāṇava</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), who was killed while bringing presents to his teacher. (Vv.v.3; Vv-a.229ff.)

The Commentaries dwell at length on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pokkharasāti</span>’s attractive personality (DN­a.i.244f.; MN­a.ii.804; Snp­a.462). His body was of the color of the white lotus, like a silver pandal in heaven, his hair the color of sapphire, his eyes like blue lotus, etc. He evidently was of true regal appearance.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> he was a Brahmin versed in the three Vedas who, having heard the doctrine and given alms, was reborn in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span> world. Thereafter, scorning birth in the womb of a woman, he sprang to life in a lotus, which grew in a pond in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span>. An ascetic saw the lotus, adopted the boy, and taught him the Vedas. The king was pleased with his great learning, and gave him <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkaṭṭhā</span> as a mark of great favor. The name of Pokkharasāti was given to him owing to his birth in a lotus.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Divyāvadāna</span> calls him Puṣkarasārī, and tells a story of his daughter Prakṛti (p. 616 ff., 620).
