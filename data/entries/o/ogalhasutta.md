# Ogāḷhasutta

## English source

Ogāḷhasutta or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kulagharaṇīsutta</span>. A certain monk living in a forest tract in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> was held in very high esteem (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ajjhogāḷhappatto</span>) by a certain family. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span> of the forest, wishing to urge him to greater effort, went to the monk in the guise of the housewife (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kulagharaṇī</span>) of the family, and asked him why it was that people spent their time in endless talk. Was he not disturbed by it? The monk answered that a recluse should not be disturbed by other people’s talk (SN.i.201).

The Commentary (SN­a.i.227) explains that the monk was already an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>, but the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Devatā</span> did not know it and could not therefore understand why he spent his time in visiting householders, hence her question wishing to make him live in solitude.
