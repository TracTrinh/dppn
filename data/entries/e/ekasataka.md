# Ekasāṭaka

## English source

Ekasāṭaka. A very poor Brahmin who lived in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span>. He was so-called because he and his wife had, between them, only one upper garment, worn by whichever of them happened to be going out. The Buddha was in the habit of preaching every seven years. On one such occasion the Brahmin listened to the Buddha’s sermon, and being greatly pleased, wishing to make an offering, he gave the Buddha his upper garment after a hard mental struggle as to whether he could afford the gift. Having made the gift, he shouted with joy saying, “I have won.” The king of the city, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhumā</span>, having heard the shout and learnt the reason, gave to the Brahmin various rich gifts, including the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sabbaṭṭhaka</span>, all of which the latter presented to the Buddha, keeping, at the king’s special request, a single pair of garments for himself and his wife. The king later made <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekasāṭaka</span> his chaplain (AN­a.i.92ff; also Thag­a.ii.136).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sabbaṭṭhaka</span> seems to have been a gift of various things in groups of eight: eight elephants, eight horses, eight thousand pieces of money, etc. (Dhp­a.iii.3); but see Mil.Trs.ii.147, n.1, where it is spoken of as an office.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekasāṭaka</span> is given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttara Commentary</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">loc.cit.</span>) as a previous birth of the Elder <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp­a.iii.1ff) mentions another Brahmin, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Ekasāṭaka</span> by name. He is, however, stated as having lived in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>, although the story related is, in most respects, identical with that given above, except that the king in the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Ekasāṭaka</span> is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>. No mention is made of the Brahmin being created <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Purohita</span>. Moreover, this Brahmin received as gift only the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sabbacatukka</span> (groups of four) and not the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sabbaṭṭhaka</span>. More important still, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Ekasāṭaka</span> is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Ekasāṭaka</span>, the latter being mentioned as having lived in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span>. Here we evidently have a confusion of legends.

The story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekasāṭaka</span> is related in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span> (pp.115, 291), as one of seven cases in which an act of devotion received its reward in this very life.
