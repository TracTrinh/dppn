# Ekarāja 01

## English source

Ekarāja 01. King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. He was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bodhisatta</span>. A minister, whom he expelled on the ground of misconduct in the royal harem, took service under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabbasena</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>, and incited him to make war on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekarāja</span>. The latter was captured while sitting on the dais in the midst of his councillors and hanged head downwards by a cord from the lintel of a door. In this position <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekarāja</span> cultivated thoughts of loving-kindness towards his enemy and attained a stage of complete absorption in mystic meditation. His bonds burst and he sat cross-legged in mid-air. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabbasena</span> was, meanwhile, seized with a burning pain in his body and, on the advice of his courtiers, had <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekarāja</span> released, whereupon the pains disappeared. Realising <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekarāja</span>’s holiness, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabbasena</span> restored the kingdom to him and asked his forgiveness (Ja.iii.13-15).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekarājajātaka</span> (Ja 303), reference is made to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sīlavajātaka</span> (Ja 51) for details regarding the expulsion of the minister for misconduct and of the subsequent events. But there the king is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīlava</span> and not <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekarāja</span>. The two stories contain certain similarities but the details vary very much. See also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Seyyajātaka</span> (Ja 282), where the king is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṁsa</span>, and compare it with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghatajātaka</span> (Ja 355). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekarājajātaka</span> is given as an example of a birth in which the Bodhisatta practised *mettā* to perfection (e.g., Bv­a.51; Mbv.11). The story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekarāja</span> is the last in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cariyāpiṭaka</span> (No. xiv).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cariyāpiṭaka</span> Commentary (p.205), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekarāja</span> was a title given to the king on account of his great power, in which case his real name might have been <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīlava</span>, as mentioned above. The scholiast on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekarājajātaka</span> (Ja.iii.14), however, says that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekarāja</span> was the king’s personal name.
