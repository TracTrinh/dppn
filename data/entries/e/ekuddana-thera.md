# Ekuddāna Thera

## English source

Ekuddāna Thera (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekuddāniya Thera</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekudāna Thera</span>). An <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. He was the son of a wealthy Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, and being convinced of the Buddha’s majesty, as seen at the presentation of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, he entered the Saṅgha. He dwelt in the forest fulfilling his novitiate, and once came to the Buddha to learn of him. The Buddha, seeing <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> near him wrapt in contemplation, uttered a stanza, that to the monk of lofty thoughts and heedful, sorrow comes not (See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada</span> 259). The monk learnt this stanza, and returning to the forest, ever and anon reflected on it. He thus came to be called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekuddāniya</span>. One day he obtained insight and became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. Later, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> asked him to preach a sermon, it was this stanza that he took as his text.

We are told that in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atthadassī Buddha</span> he was a chief of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span>, and when the Buddha died he went about lamenting that he had not made use of his opportunities. A disciple of the Buddha, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgara</span>, meeting him, advised him to make offering to the Buddha’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thūpa</span>. In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>’s time he was a householder, and heard the Buddha utter the stanza mentioned above. He entered the Saṅgha, and for twenty thousand years practised meditation, repeating the stanza, but gained no attainment (Thag­a.i.153f; Thag.v.68).

It is said (Dhp­a.iii.384f) that on fast-days <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekuddāna</span>, alone in the forest, sounded the call for the deities of the forest to attend the preaching of the Dhamma, and uttered his stanza, whereupon the deities made loud applause. One fast-day two monks, versed in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Tipiṭaka</span>, visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekuddāna</span> with a retinue of five hundred each. Seeing them, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekuddāna</span>’s heart was glad and he said: “Today we will listen to the Dhamma.” On being asked for an explanation, he described how, when the Dhamma was expounded, the forest grove was filled with the applause of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>. Thereupon one of the Elders recited the Dhamma and the other expounded it, but there was no sound. In order to dispel their doubts, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekuddāna</span> took his seat and pronounced his one stanza. The sound of the plaudits of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> filled the forest. The Elders were greatly offended at the conduct of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> and complained to the Buddha. The Buddha explained to them that the important thing was not the amount of knowledge but the quality of the understanding.

It is noteworthy that the verse, attributed above to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekuddāniya</span>, occurs in the Vinaya (Vin.iv.54) as having been constantly used by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Panthaka</span>. Whenever it was his turn to preach to the nuns at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> they expected no effective lesson, since he always repeated the same stanza, namely, that which is above attributed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekuddāniya</span>. The Thera<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">,</span> bearing of their remarks, forthwith gives an exhibition of his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span>-power and of his knowledge of the Dhamma, thereby winning their tribute of admiration.
