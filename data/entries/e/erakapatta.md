# Erakapatta

## English source

Erakapatta. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> king. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> he was a monk. One day, while in a boat, he grasped an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">eraka</span>-leaf, and through his failing to let go, the leaf was broken off. Though he practised meditation for twenty thousand years in the forest, at the moment of his death he thought of the lapse with great remorse and was reborn in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> world as large as a dug-out canoe, much to his grief and despair. When a daughter was born to him he taught her a song and, once a fortnight, he appeared with her on the surface of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span>, where she danced on his hood and sang the song. She was offered in marriage to anyone who could sing a reply to her song. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Erakapatta</span> hoped thereby to become aware of it when a Buddha should appear in the world. Many suitors came, and an interval between two Buddhas passed and still no one was successful. At last a young Brahmin, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), well-schooled for the task by the Buddha, appeared before the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>-maiden and answered all her questions. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span> himself became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span> when he finished learning his lesson from the Buddha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Erakapatta</span> at once knew that a Buddha had come, and asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span> to take him to the Teacher. At the sight of the Buddha, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Erakapatta</span> was seized with great sorrow on account of his condition, but the Buddha preached to him and consoled him. It is said that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> king would have attained the Fruit of Conversion had it not been for his animal nature. Dhp­a.iii.230-6.
