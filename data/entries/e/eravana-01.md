# Erāvaṇa 01

## English source

Erāvaṇa 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s elephant. He was once the elephant of the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, who gave him to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgha</span> and his companions to help them in their good works on earth. As a result, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgha</span> and the others were reborn in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Erāvaṇa</span> was born there himself and became their companion. Ordinarily he was a *Deva* like the others, because there are no animals in the *Deva*-world, but when they went to the park to play, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Erāvaṇa</span> assumed the form of an elephant, one hundred and fifty leagues in size. For the thirty-three <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Erāvaṇa</span> erected thirty-three heads (*kumbha*), each two or three quarters of a league in girth. Each head had seven tusks, each fifty leagues long, each tusk bore seven lotus plants, each plant seven flowers, each flower seven leaves, and on each leaf danced seven nymphs (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumaccharā</span>). For <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> himself there was a special head, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudassana</span>, thirty leagues around, above it a canopy of twelve leagues all of precious stones. In the centre was a jewelled couch one league long, on which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> reclined in state. Dhp­a.i.273f; also Snp-a.i.368f. (where there are a few slight variations).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammikasutta</span> (Sn.vs.379) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Erāvaṇa</span> is mentioned among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> who visited the Buddha to pay him homage. He is also mentioned among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> present at the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Samayasutta</span> (DN.ii.258; perhaps here a king of snakes is meant, because he is mentioned with others who are avowedly snakes). It is emphasised in several places (e.g., MN­a.i.472; DN­a.ii.688; also Vv-a.15 and Kv.ii.599) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Erāvaṇa</span> is a *Devaputta* and a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> only by birth (*jātiyā*). The Jātakas (Ja.v.137) mention <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> as riding <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Erāvaṇa</span>, particularly when making comparisons between kings parading on the backs of elephants (e.g., Ja.iii.392). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Erāvaṇa</span> is one of the chief features of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> (V.vi.278).
