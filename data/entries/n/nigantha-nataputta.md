# Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta

## English source

Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭha Nāthaputta</span>). One of six eminent teachers, contemporary with the Buddha; he is described as a heretic (*aññatitthiya*, e.g., SN.i.66).

He was leader of a sect known as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhā</span>, and a summary of his teachings is found in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmaññaphalasutta</span> (DN.i.57; DN­a.i.166).

A *Nigaṇṭha* is restrained with a fourfold restraint (*cātuyāma saṁvara*)

- he is restrained as regards all water,
- restrained as regards all evil,
- all evil has he washed away, and
- he lives suffused with the sense of evil held at bay.

And, because of this fourfold restraint,

- he is called a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Nigaṇṭha</span> (free from bonds),
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t23">gatatta</span> (one whose heart has been in the attainment of his aim),
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t23">yattala</span> (one whose heart is under command) and
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t23">ṭhitatta</span> (one whose heart is fixed).

The meaning of this fourfold restraint is not clear; for a discussion of this *cātuyāma saṁvara*, see Barua: *Pre-Buddhistic Indian Philosophy*, pp. 378f. The first in evidently the well-known rule of the Jains against drinking cold water, as it contains “souls” (cp. Mil.259ff). The Buddha taught a corresponding fourfold restraint, which consisted of observing the four precepts against injury, stealing, unchastity and lying (DN.iii.48f.<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">)</span>

Nātaputta is also stated to have claimed omiscience – to be all-knowing, all-seeing, to have all-comprising (*aparisesa*) knowledge and vision. “Whether I walk or stand or sleep or wake,” he is mentioned as saying, “my knowledge and vision are always, and without a break, present before me.” (E.g., MN.ii.31; AN.i.220; MN.i.92f.; also MN.ii.214ff. It is curious, in view of this statement of Nātaputta’s doctrine of inaction, that the main ground on which he is stated to have objected to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīha</span>’s visit to the Buddha, was that the Buddha was an *akiriyavādī* (AN.iv.180)).

He taught that past deeds should be expirtated by severe austerities, fresh deeds should be avoided by inaction. By expelling through penance all past misdeeds and by not committing fresh misdeeds, the future becomes cleared. From the destruction of deeds results the destruction of *dukkha*; this leads to the destruction of *vedanā*. Thus all *dukkha* is exhausted and one passes beyond (the round of existence). It is said that Nātaputta did not employ the term *kamma* in his teaching; he used, instead, the word *daṇḍa*; and that, according to him, the *daṇḍa* of deed was far more criminal than the *daṇḍas* of word and mind (MN.i.371. *Daṇḍa* probably means sins or hurtful acts. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (MN­a.ii.595ff.) that the Jain idea was that *citta* (the *manodaṇḍa*) did not come into bodily acts or into words which were irresponsible and mechanical, like the stirring and sighing of boughs in the wind).

He is said to have shown no hesitation in declaring the destinies of his disciples after death (SN.iv.398); but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakuludāyi</span> says that when asked a question as to the past, he skipped from one matter to another and dismissed the question, evincing irritation, bad temper and resentment. (MN.ii.31; also ibid., i.93; and ii.214f.; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span> admit they did not know of the past).

Only one discussion is recorded between Nātaputta and a follower of the Buddha, and that was with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittagahapati</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Macchikāsaṇḍa</span> (SN.iv.298ff). He praises <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta</span> at the outset of the discussion, holding him up as an example to his own flock, and agreeing with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta</span> that knowledge is more excellent than faith. But later, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta</span> claims knowledge of the four *jhānas*, Nātaputta is represented as condemning him for a deceitful man. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta</span>, thereupon, asks him ten questions and, getting no answer, leaves him. The Commentary (SN­a.iii.99) explains that the questions <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta</span> asked were the same as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumārapañhā</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadahasutta</span> contains a detailed analysis and attributed to the Buddha, of the beliefs and teachings of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span> (MN.ii.214; cp. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Dukkhakkhandhasutta</span>; MN.i.91ff.; also AN.v.150; DN.iii.119). He there selects for his condemnation ten of their operative utterances, major and minor, and proves that the efforts and strivings of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span> are fruitless.

Nātaputta is said (Dhp­a.iii.201) to have claimed miraculous powers, but he did not, in fact, possess them. When, for instance, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagahaseṭṭhi</span> offered his bowl of red sandalwood to anybody who could remove it from its perch, Nātaputta tried to obtain it by a ruse, but was unable to deceive the *seṭṭhi*.

The books contain the names of several disciples of Nātaputta, among them a *Deva* called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Niṅka</span> (SN.i.66; the Buddha’s own paternal uncle, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vappa</span>, was a follower of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span>). Nātaputta is so convinced of the truth and the irrefutableness of his own doctrines, that he actually encourages his disciples to hold discussions with the Buddha. Some, like <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha Tapassī</span>, come away unscathed, without having carried the discussion to any conclusion; others are mentioned as being convinced by the Buddha in the end and as becoming his disciples. Such, for instance, are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asibandhakaputta</span> (SN.iv.317ff) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayarājakumāra</span> (MN.i.392ff). Nātaputta tries, without success, to dissuade <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīha</span>, general of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>, from visiting the Buddha (AN.iv.180ff). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīha</span> goes and is converted. The next day he holds an almsgiving, on a grand scale, to the Buddha and his monks, at which flesh is served. It is said that Nātaputta went about <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, sneering at the Buddha for encouraging slaughter. The Buddha, hearing of this, relates the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Telovādajātaka</span> (Ja 246, Ja.ii.262f.; Vin.i.233ff), to show that in the past, too, Nātaputta had sneered at him for a similar reason. Nātaputta is identified with the rich man of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span>.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāverujātaka</span> (Ja 339, Ja.iii.126f) he is identified with the crow who lost all his honour and glory when approached by the peacock, who was the Bodhisatta.

But the greatest blow to Nātaputta was when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli</span> *gahapati* joined the Buddha (MN.i.373ff). Nātaputta had allowed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli</span> to visit him in spite of the warning of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha Tapassī</span> as to the Buddha’s arresting personality. But Nātaputta thought <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli</span> would be proof against it, and, on hearing that he had renounced his allegiance to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span>, refused to believe it until he could verify the information himself. The discovery of the apostasy of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli</span> prostrated him with grief; he vomited hot blood and had to be carried away on a litter from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bālaka</span>, where he was then living, to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span>. There, soon after, he died, and immediately great dissensions arose among his followers. When the Buddha heard of the quarrels, he remarked that it was only to be expected (MN.ii.243f.; DN.iii.117, 210; it is stated that the quarrel was deliberately fostered by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nātaputta</span> before his death. See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhā</span>).

Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta is the name by which the Jaina teacher, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vīra</span>, was known to his contemporaries. He was also called Vardhamāna. Nāta (or Nāya) was the name of his clan (Snp­a. (ii.423) says Nāta was the name of his father), which belonged to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>. According to Jaina tradition, his father’s personal name was Siddhatha, and he was a *Kṣatriya*, his mother being *Triśālā* (for an account of Mahā Vīra’s life and philosophy, see Barua: *Pre-Buddhistic Indian Philosophy*, pp.372ff).
