# Nāgadīpa

## English source

Nāgadīpa. A province of Ceylon, identified with the modern Jaffna peninsula and the north west of Ceylon.

The Buddha’s second visit to Ceylon was to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadīpa</span>, to settle a dispute between two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahodara</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷodara</span> (Mhv.i.47).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambukola</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) was a harbour in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadīpa</span>, and there a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> was built by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span> (Mhv.xx.25) and later restored by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaniṭṭhatissa</span> (Mhv.xxxvi.9). This <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> was probably called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissavihāra</span> (see Mhv.36).

Another <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span>, called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sālipabbata</span>, was built by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahallaka Nāga</span> (Mhv.xxxv.124). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uṇṇalomaghara</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājāyatanadhātucetiya</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amalacetiya</span> were probably all places of worship in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadīpa</span> (Cv.xlii.62).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Valāhassajātaka</span> (Ja 196, Ja.ii.128) says that the coast of Ceylon, from the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalyāṇī</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadīpa</span>, was once infested by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yakkhinis</span>. Once (Ja.iii.187) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadīpa</span> was known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Serumadīpa</span>, and near by was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Karadīpa</span>, earlier known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ahidīpa</span> (Ja.iv.238).

An old story, given in the Commentaries (e.g., Vibh­a.444), speaks of a king called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīparāga</span>, who reigned over <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadīpa</span> in great splendour. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadīpa</span> was once an important centre of Buddhism in Ceylon (e.g., Vibh­a.446, Vibh­a.467; AN­a.i.422. MN­a.i.545; see also JRAS., vol. xxvi) and contained many places of pilgrimage. There is a legend (DN­a.iii.899; Vibh­a.433) which relates that, when the Buddha’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sāsana</span> comes to an end, all the Buddha’s relics in Ceylon will gather together at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Cetiya</span> and travel to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājāyatanacetiya</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadīpa</span>, and then from there to the Mahā Bodhi-tree at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā</span>.

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rasavāhinī</span> (ii.19) the place was so-called because it was given as gift to the woman named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgā</span>. See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgā</span> (7).
