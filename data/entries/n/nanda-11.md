# Nanda 11

## English source

Nanda 11. King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, a former birth of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span>. He belonged to a poor family, but, owing to his merit in having covered <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>’s *cetiya* with a golden coverlet, he came to be crowned king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. He had a *kapparukkha*, which provided him and his subjects with divine robes. With the help of his queen – who became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddakapilā</span> in this life – he held a great almsgiving to five hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>, led by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Paduma</span>, and entertained them up to the time of their death. Nanda was away, quelling a frontier rebellion, at the time of their death. On his return, he gave over his kingdom to his eldest son and became an ascetic. Ap.ii.582; Thag­a.ii.139ff.; SN­a.ii.140f.; the story is also found at Pv-a.73ff.; there it is said that Nanda was granted divine clothes because he had once given his shawl to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> for a robe; see also Thīg­a.72.

Nanda’s wealth was proverbial, e.g., Pv.ii.1 (vs. 16), iii.2 (vs.16).
