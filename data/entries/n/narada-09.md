# Nārada 09

## English source

Nārada 09. A Brahmin sage, called a *Devabrāhmaṇa*, and Nāradadeva. One day, having wandered about in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, he was returning to his dwelling in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kañcanaguhā</span> holding a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāricchattaka</span> flower over his head, when the four daughters of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> –<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āsā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saddhā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hirī</span> – asked him to give it to them. He agreed to give it to that one among them whom they should choose as their queen. They sought the advice of their father, who directed them to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Macchariya Kosiya</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosiya</span> decided in favour of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hirī</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span> is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>. For details see the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhābhojanajātaka</span> (Ja 535, Ja.v.392ff). It is probably this same <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span> who is mentioned as being present when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇāla</span> (*q.v.*) delivered his famous diatribe against women. He is described as possessing the *pañcābhiññā* and as being attended by ten thousand ascetics. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇāla</span> had finished his discourse, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span> supplemented it with all he knew of the vices of women (Ja 535, Ja.424, 450ff., 456). He is also mentioned as having admonished <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janaka</span> when the latter renounced the world. In that context, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span> is described as belonging to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapagotta</span>. Ja.vi.56, 58, 68. In Snp­a.i.359 he is called Nārada Devala. This may be a wrong reading for Nāradadeva.
