# Nigrodhamigajātaka

## English source

Nigrodhamigajātaka (Also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhajātaka</span>) (Ja 12). Once the Bodhisatta was born as king of the deer and was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodha</span>. With him was the leader of another herd, and he was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākha</span>. There was an agreement between these two leaders that, on alternate days, a deer from their herd should offer itself to be killed by the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. One day the turn fell on a pregnant doe of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākha</span>’s herd, and when she asked to be allowed to wait until she had brought forth her young she was refused by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākha</span>. She then appealed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodha</span>, who took her turn on himself. Immunity was then granted to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodha</span>, and when his act was reported to the king, he came in person to enquire into the matter. On hearing the story, he was greatly moved, and promised immunity both to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodha</span> and the doe. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodha</span> was not satisfied till the king promised immunity to all living beings. Later, on discovering that the deer, taking advantage of this, were destroying men’s crops, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodha</span>, gave orders to his herd to refrain from doing so.

The story was related in reference to the mother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumāra Kassapa</span> (*q.v.*). She had joined the Saṅgha under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> not knowing that she was pregnant. On discovering her condition, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> expelled her from the Saṅgha. She appealed to the Buddha, who caused an enquiry to be held, and, having been assured of her innocence, he restored her to all honour. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumāra Kassapa</span> was seven years old he, too, was admitted to the Saṅgha.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumāra Kassapa</span> with the doe’s young one, his mother with the doe, and the king with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>. Ja.i.145ff.; Dhp­a.iii.148f.

The story is figured in the *Bharhut Stūpa* (Cunningham: pl. xxv. (1) and xliii. (2)). It is given in *Mahā Vastu* (Mvu.i.359ff.) with several variations in detail.
