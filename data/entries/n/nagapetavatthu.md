# Nāgapetavatthu

## English source

Nāgapetavatthu. In a Brahmin family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, the two sons and the daughter heard the Dhamma from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkicca</span> Thera and became believers. The parents were anxious to give their daughter to their nephew, but he had joined the Saṅgha. Later, however, wishing to marry his cousin, he asked his teacher’s permission to become a layman. The latter withheld his consent for some time, and, while he hesitated, the house in which the family lived fell down and they were all killed.

The two sons and the daughter were born among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Bhumma</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> and the parents became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Petas</span>. The young monk’s teacher showed them to him one day as he passed behind the monastery at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>, and having heard their story from their own lips, the monk caused alms to be given in the name of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Petas</span>, and they were freed from their sufferings. Pv.i.11; Pv-a.53ff.
