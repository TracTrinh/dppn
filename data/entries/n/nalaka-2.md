# Nālaka

## English source

Nālaka. Nephew of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asita</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷadevala</span>). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asita</span> realized that he would not live to see the Buddha, he sought out <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālaka</span> and asked him to leave the world at once and become an ascetic and hold himself in readiness to profit by the Buddha’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>.

This <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālaka</span> did, though possessing eighty thousand crores of wealth, and he spent his time in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span>. When the time came, he visited the Buddha seven days after the Buddha’s first sermon and questioned him on the *Moneyyapaṭipadā* (also called the *Nālakapaṭipadā*, because it is included in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālakasutta</span>). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālaka</span> retired once more into <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span> and there attained *Arahant*-ship. There he spent seven months leaning against a golden rock, practising *paṭipadā* in its highest form. After his death the Buddha, with his monks, visited the scene of his death, cremated his remains, and had a *cetiya* built over them.

It is said that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālaka</span>’s aspiration to learn and practise the *Moneyyapaṭipadā* was made in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>. Ja.i.55; Snp­a.ii.483ff., 501. The story as drawn from Tibetan sources differs greatly from this story (see, e.g. Rockhill, p. 18, 45 f). In the *Mahā Vastu* (iii.380, 387) he is called Kātyāyana.
