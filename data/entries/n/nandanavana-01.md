# Nandanavana 01

## English source

Nandanavana 01. The chief of the parks in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, where the inhabitants of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, headed by Indra, go for their amusement. (mentioned e.g., Dhp­a.ii.266; AN.iii.40; Ja.vi.240; Vv-a.7, 34, 61, etc.; Pv-a.173, 176, 177, etc.; Mvu.i.32, etc.). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span> kings are born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> after death and spend their time in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandanavana</span> (SN.v.342). It is said (e.g., Ja.i.49) that there is a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandanavana</span> in each *Deva* world. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> go there just before their death and disappear in the midst of their revels. Thus, the Bodhisatta went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandanavana</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> world before his “descent” into <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Māyā</span>’s womb (Ja.i.50; see also Ja.vi.144). In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandanavana</span> is a lake called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandana</span> (Ja.ii.189) and evidently also a palace called *Ekapuṇḍarīkavimāna* (Mhv­ṭ.568). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandanavana</span> was so-called because it awoke delight in the hearts of all who visited it (Ja.v.158). Sometimes ascetics, like <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span> (ibid.392), possessed of great *iddhi*-power, would spend their siesta in the shadow of the grove.
