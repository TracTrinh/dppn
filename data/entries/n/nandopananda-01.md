# Nandopananda 01

## English source

Nandopananda 01. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> king, tamed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>. The Buddha and five hundred monks, on their way to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> one morning, travelled over the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> king’s abode as he was having a meal. In anger, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> coiled round <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span> and covered the road to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>.

Thereupon several members of the Buddha’s retinue, including <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Raṭṭhapāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span>, offered to quell the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>’s power, but the Buddha would not agree until <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> sought permission to do so. It is said that no other monk had the power to face all the dangers created by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> and remain unscathed. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandopananda</span> vied with one another in the exhibition of their <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span> power, and, in the end, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandopananda</span> had to acknowledge defeat. He was thereupon conducted to the Buddha, whose follower he became. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span> heard of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>’s victory, he celebrated it by holding a great alms festival, lasting for seven days, for the Buddha and his monks. Thag­a.ii.188f.; Ja.v.126.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Divyāvadāna</span> (p.395) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upananda</span> are spoken of as two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> kings.
