# Nandiya 01

## English source

Nandiya 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandiya Thera</span>. He belonged to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, and was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandiya</span> because his birth brought bliss. He left the world at the same time as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbila</span> and the others, and he soon attained *Arahant*-ship. Thereafter he dwelt with his companions in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pācīnavaṁsamigadāya</span> (Vin.i.350f). It was to them that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upakkilesasutta</span> was preached, MN.iii.155. Later, they seem to have lived in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gosiṅgasālavana</span>, MN.i.205. It is said that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> appeared before him in a terrible form, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandiya</span> drove him away.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandiya</span> built an altar of sandalwood at the Buddha’s *cetiya* and held great celebrations. Fifteen *kappas* ago <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandiya</span> was eight times born as king under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samatta</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samagga</span>) (Thag.25; Thag­a.82f.) He is probably identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saparivāriya</span> (*q.v.*) of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (i.172).

According to the *Mahā Vastu* (iii.177) Nandiya (Nandika) was the son of Sukrodana.

He was a special friend of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbila</span>. Thag­a.i.276.
