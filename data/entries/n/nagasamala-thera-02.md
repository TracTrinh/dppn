# Nāgasamāla Thera 02

## English source

Nāgasamāla Thera 02. An <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (Ap.i.119) distinguishes him from the above, whom it calls <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekachattiya</span>. Thirty-one <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> ago he placed a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pāṭalī</span>-flower on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thūpa</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sikhī Buddha</span>. Fifteen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> ago he was a king named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhūmiya</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna Commentary</span> says, however, that this Thera was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pacchāsamaṇa</span> (personal attendant) of the Buddha for some time and that he was called Nāgasamāla because his body was tender as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nāga</span>-buds.
