# Nārada 13

## English source

Nārada 13. A Thera<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">,</span> mentioned once as staying at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghositārāma</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mūsila</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saviṭṭha</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>. In the course of discussion he declares that, though aware of the nature of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nibbāna</span>, he is not an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> (SN.ii.115f). Elsewhere he is mentioned as staying in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkuṭārāma</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span> (AN.iii.57f). At that time King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Muṇḍa</span> was grieving over the death of his wife, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā</span>, to the neglect of everything else, and his treasurer, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piyaka</span>, suggested that he should visit <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span>. The king agreed, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span> preached to him on the inevitableness of old age, disease, death, etc. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Muṇḍa</span> was consoled, and buried the body of his wife, which he had till then preserved.

He may be identical with the Thera mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Petavatthu</span> Commentary (Pv-a.2, 10, 11, 14, 204, 208, 210, 211) as finding out from various <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Petas</span> the stories of their deeds, and in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vimānavatthu</span> Commentary (Vv-a.165, 169, 203) as visiting various <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vimānas</span> in the course of his wanderings among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span> worlds (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">devacārikā</span>). He is stated as having repeated the stories he learnt to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Dhammasaṅgāhakas</span> to be embodied in their rescensions.
