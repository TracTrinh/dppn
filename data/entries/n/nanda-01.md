# Nanda 01

## English source

Nanda 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda Thera</span>. Son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Pajāpatī</span>, and therefore half-brother of the Buddha. He was only a few days younger than the Buddha, and when the Buddha’s mother died, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span> gave her own child to nurses and suckled the Buddha herself (AN­a.i.186).

On the third day of the Buddha’s visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, after the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>, the Buddha went to Nanda’s house, where festivities were in progress in honour of Nanda’s coronation and marriage to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janapadakaḷyānī Nandā</span>. The Buddha wished Nanda good fortune and handed him his bowl to be taken to the *vihāra*. Nanda, thereupon, accompanied the Buddha out of the palace. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janapadakaḷyānī</span>, seeing him go, asked him to return quickly. Once inside the *vihāra*, however, the Buddha asked Nanda to become a monk, and he, unable to refuse the request, agreed with reluctance. But as the days passed he was tormented with thoughts of his beloved, and became very downcast and despondent, and his health suffered. The Buddha suggested that they should visit the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span>. On the way there, he showed Nanda the charred remains of a female monkey and asked him whether <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janapadakaḷyānī</span> were more beautiful than that. The answer was in the affirmative. The Buddha then took him to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, with his most beautiful nymphs, waited on them. In answer to a question by the Buddha, Nanda admitted that these nymphs were far more attractive than <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janapadakaḷyānī</span>, and the Buddha promised him one as wife if he would live the monastic life. Nanda was all eagerness and readily agreed. On their return to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> the Buddha related this story to the eighty chief disciples, and when they questioned Nanda, he felt greatly ashamed of his lustfulness. Summoning all his courage, he strove hard and, in no long time, attained *Arahant*-ship. He thereupon came to the Buddha and absolved him from his promise (Thag.157f.; Ja.i.91; ii.92ff.; Ud.iii.2; Dhp­a.i.96-105; Ud­a.168ff.; Snp­a.273f.)

When the Buddha was told of Nanda’s *Arahant*-ship by a *Devatā*, he related the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅgāmāvacarajataka</span> (Ja 182, *q.v.*) to show how, in the past, too, Nanda had been quick to follow advice. He also related the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kappaṭa</span> (*q.v.*) and his donkey to show that it was not the first time that Nanda had been won to obedience by the lure of the female sex. The male donkey in the story was Nanda and the female donkey <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janapadakaḷyānī</span>. (Dhp­a.i.103f.)

Nanda is identified with the sub-king (*uparājā*) in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurudhammajataka</span> (*q.v.*).

Later, on seeing how eminently Nanda was trained in self control, the Buddha declared him chief among his disciples in that respect (*indriyesu guttadvārānaṁ*). Nanda had aspired to this eminence in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atthadassi Buddha</span> he was a tortoise in the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vinatā</span>, and, seeing the Buddha on the bank waiting to cross, he took him over to the other side on his back (AN.i.25; AN­a.i.174f.; Thag­a.i.276ff.)

He is said to have been called Nanda because his birth brought joy to his kinsmen. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (i.57) says he was of golden hue, as reward for a gift of a costly robe given by him to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>. One hundred thousand *kappas* ago he became king four times under the name of Cela. Sixty thousand *kappas* ago he was again king in four births, under the name of Upacela. Later, five thousand *kappas* ago, he was four times <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>, and his name then, too, was Cela.

Nanda was very beautiful, and was only four inches shorter than the Buddha. He once wore a robe made according to the dimensions of the Buddha’s robe. Discovering this, the Buddha chided him for his presumption (Vin.iv.173). Perhaps this is another version of the story found at SN.ii.281. There, Nanda is said to have donned a robe which was pressed on both sides, painted his face, and gone to see the Buddha, carrying a bright bowl. The Buddha chided him, and Nanda thereupon became a forest dweller and a rag-robe man. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (SN­a.ii.174) says that Nanda dressed himself up in order to evoke some comment from the Buddha – either approval, so that he might dress thus for the remainder of his life, or censure, in which case he would put on rag robes and dwell in the forest.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (AN.iv.166f) contains a discourse in which the Buddha discusses Nanda’s claim to have achieved self control in all things.

He is probably to be identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Taraṇiya Thera</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>. (ii.428; cp. Thag­a.i.277.)
