# Nandivisālajātaka

## English source

Nandivisālajātaka (Ja 28). Once the Bodhisatta was born as a bull in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhāra</span> and was named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandivisāla</span>. When quite young, he was given to a Brahmin, who fed him on delicacies and looked after him. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandi</span> grew up, in order to show his gratitude to the Brahmin, he suggested that he should draw one hundred carts for a wager. The Brahmin boasted to his friends and had a wager with them. On the appointed day he loaded one hundred carts, lashed them together, and having tied <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandivisāla</span> to the first, took his seat on the pole and, flourishing his goad, shouted, “Now, you rascal, pull.” The bull, very offended, would not stir, and the Brahmin lost his money. As he lay groaning in bed, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandivisāla</span> went to him and said that he should not have abused him. He then asked him to wager two thousand, and said that this time he would win. This the Brahmin did, and the next day, having tied one hundred carts together, he yoked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandivisāla</span> to the first and stroked his back saying, “Now then, my fine fellow, pull.” With one heave, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandivisāla</span> pulled the carts, and the last stood where the first had been. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandivisāla</span>’s master received many presents in addition to the wager.

The story was related in reference to the taunts uttered by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chabbaggiyas</span> against the virtuous monks.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> was the Brahmin of the story (Ja.i.191ff.; the story is also given at Vin.iv.5). It was also related in connection with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamakapāṭihāriya</span>. Dhp­a.iii.213.
