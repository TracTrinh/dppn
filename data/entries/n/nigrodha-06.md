# Nigrodha 06

## English source

Nigrodha 06. Commonly known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodha</span> *Sāmaṇera*. He was the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span>, the eldest of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>’s children, and his mother was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanā</span>. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> slew <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span>, his wife, who was with child, fled to a *Caṇḍāla* village, where the guardian deity of a *nigrodha* tree built her a hut. Here she gave birth to her son, whom she named after her benefactor. The chief *Caṇḍāla* looked after them. When Nigrodha was seven years old, the Thera <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Varuṇa</span> ordained him, and he became an *Arahant* in the tonsure hall. One day, while walking near the palace, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> saw him and, because of their connection in a past life, was attracted by him. Nigrodha had been one of the three brothers who gave honey to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> in a past life (for the story see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> and Mhv.v.49ff). Nigrodha had called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> a *Caṇḍāla*, hence he was born in a *Caṇḍāla* village. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> invited Nigrodha to the palace and entertained him, and Nigrodha preached to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Appamādavagga</span> of the Dhammapada. The king was greatly pleased, and offered to give food daily at the palace to thirty-two monks in Nigrodha’s name. It was this visit of Nigrodha to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> which ultimately resulted in the conversion of the latter to the faith of the Buddha (for details see ibid., 37-72; Dpv.vi.34ff.; vii.12, 31; Vin-a.i.45ff). It is said that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> paid great honour to Nigrodha throughout his life (MN­a.ii.931). Three times a day he sent to Nigrodha gifts of robes carried on the backs of elephants, with five hundred measures of perfume and five hundred caskets of garlands. All these Nigrodha would distribute among his colleagues, and most of the monks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> at that time wore robes, which were the gift of Nigrodha.
