# Ñātika

## English source

Ñātika (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">v.l.</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nādikā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nātika</span>). A locality in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjī</span> country on the highway between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṭigāma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>. The Buddha ﬁrst went there in the course of one of his tours, and the inhabitants, being greatly attracted by him, built for him a residence in brick, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Giñjakāvasatha</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), which, in course of time, became a great <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span>. MN-a.i.424. The Buddha, subsequently, seems to have stayed several times in Ñātika.

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Gosiṅgasutta</span> he stayed there soon after the schism of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> monks and sought the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gosiṅgasālavana</span>, evidently in the neighbourhood where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandiya</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbila</span> were in residence (MN.i.205. but according to Vin.i.350f, which relates the incident, they were in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pācīnavaṁsadāya</span> and the Buddha went there from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bālakaloṇakāragāma</span>).

The Buddha also visited Ñātika on his last journey, while on his way to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>, and was staying there on the day that he accepted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambapālī</span>’s hospitality and her gift of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambapālivana</span> (Vin.i.232f.).

It was evidently during this stay that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> questioned the Buddha as to the lot of various pious inhabitants of Ñātika who had been zealous followers of the Buddha’s teaching. Among them several are mentioned by name – the monk <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāḷha</span>, the nun <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudatta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakudha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāliṅga</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nikaṭa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭissabha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tuṭṭha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Santuṭṭha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhadda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhadda</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t18">upāsikā</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sugatā</span>. The Buddha tells <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> of their destiny, and informs him that more than ninety people of Ñātika had become <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sakadagāmīs</span> and more than ﬁve hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpannas</span>. He then proceeds to proclaim the discourse which has become famous as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Dhammadāsa</span>. D.ii.91ff.; SN.v.356ff., also records what is evidently the same incident. Two additional names, Asoka and Asokī, occur in the Saṁyutta passage.

The Janavasabhasutta, which was also preached at Ñātika, is evidently based on this incident and is probably an elaboration of the same (DN.ii.200ff.). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> record other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span> on different topics preached at Ñātika. See s.v. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Giñjakāvasathasutta</span> and also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ñātikasutta</span>; also SN.iv.90; AN.iii.303ff., AN.iii.306f., AN.iv.316f. and AN.iv.320f.

Mention is also made of discussions between the Buddha and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāyana</span> (SN.ii.153) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sandha</span> (AN.v.322f.), and also of a discussion between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiya Kaccāyana</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vacchagotta</span> (SN.iv.401f.).

The books spells the name of the village in two ways: Ñātika and Nādika. This doubt as to the spelling seems to have existed from quite early times, as the apparent confusion of the etymology leads us to believe. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta Commentary</span> (SN-a.ii.56), Buddhaghosa says “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ñātiketi dvinnaṁ ñātakānaṁ gāme</span>.” In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha Commentary</span> (DN-a.ii.543), however, he says “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ñādikā ti etaṁ taḷākaṁ nissāya dviṇṇaṁ cūlapitumahāpituputtānaṁ dve gāmā. Nādike ti ekasmiṁ ñātigāme</span>.” These explanations seem to support both spellings – Ñātika and Nādika— Ñātika because it was a “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ñātigāma</span> ”and Nādika because it was near the pond Nādikā. (MN-a.ii.424 definitely states that the name of the pond was also Nādikā (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nādike viharati ti Nādiki nāma eka</span>, etc.). Rhys Davids thinks that Nādikā (pl.) was a clan-name and Nādika the name of the clan’s village (Dial.ii.97.n.1). Woodward also supports the reading Nādika, and suggests that the name is connected with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nadī</span>, and refers to Walters’ Chwang (ii. 86), where reference is made to Nātaka on the Ganges between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> and Patna. (GS.iii.217.n.4).
