# Nāgā 08

## English source

Nāgā 08. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> are a class of beings classed with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Garuḷas</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Supaṇṇas</span> and playing a prominent part in Buddhist folk lore. They are gifted with miraculous powers and great strength. Generally speaking, they are confused with snakes, chiefly the hooded Cobra, and their bodies are described as being those of snakes, though they can assume human form at will. They are broadly divided into two classes: those that live on land (*thalaja*) and those that live on water (*jalaja*). The *Jalajanāgā* live in rivers as well as in the sea, while the *Thalajanāgā* are regarded as living beneath the surface of the earth. Several <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> dwellings are mentioned in the books: e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mañjerikabhavana</span> under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Daddarabhavana</span> at the foot of Mount <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Daddara</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span>, the *Dhataraṭṭhanāgā* under the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamunā</span>, the *Nābhasānāgā* in Lake <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nābhasa</span>, and also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tacchaka</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Payāga</span> (DN.ii.258). The Vinaya (ii.109) contains a list of four royal families of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> (*Ahirājakulāni*): *Virūpakkhā*, *Erāpathā*, *Chabyāputtā* and *Kaṇhagotamakā*. Two other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> tribes are generally mentioned together: the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kambalas</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assataras</span>. It is said (SN­a.iii.120) that all <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> have their young in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span>.

Stories are given – e.g., in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhūridattajātaka</span> – of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span>, both male and female, mating with humans; but the offspring of such unions are watery and delicate (Ja.vi.160). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> are easily angered and passionate, their breath is poisonous, and their glance can be deadly (Ja.vi.160, 164). They are carnivorous (Ja.iii.361), their diet consisting chiefly of frogs (Ja.vi.169), and they sleep, when in the world of men, on ant hills (ibid., 170). The enmity between the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Garuḷas</span> is proverbial (DN.ii.258). At first the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Garuḷas</span> did not know how to seize the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span>, because the latter swallowed large stones so as to be of great weight, but they learnt how in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍarajātaka</span> (Ja 518, *q.v.*). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> dance when music is played, but it is said (Ja.vi.191) that they never dance if any <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Garuḷa</span> is near (through fear) or in the presence of human dancers (through shame).

The best known of all <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kāḷa</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mañjerikabhavana</span> (*q.v.*). He lives for a whole *kappa*, and is a very pious follower of the Buddha. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> of his world had the custodianship of a part of the Buddha’s relics till they were needed for the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māha Thūpa</span> (Mhv.xxxi.27f.), and when the Bodhi-tree was being brought to Ceylon they did it great honour during the voyage (Mbv.p.163f.). Other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> kings are also mentioned as ruling with great power and majesty and being converted to the Buddha’s faith – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āravāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apalāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Erapatta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandopananda</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇṇaka</span> (see also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ahicchatta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ahināga</span>.) In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āṭānāṭiyasutta</span> (DN.iii.198f.), speaking of dwellers of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cātummahārājika</span> world, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> are mentioned as occupying the Western Quarter, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Virūpakkha</span> as their king.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> had two chief settlements in Ceylon, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadīpa</span> (*q.v.*) and at the mouth of the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalyāṇī</span>. It was to settle a dispute between two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> chiefs of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadīpa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahodara</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷodara</span>, that the Buddha paid his second visit to Ceylon. During that visit he made a promise to another <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>-king, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇi­akkhika</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalyāṇī</span>, to pay him a visit, and the Buddha’s third visit was in fulfilment of that undertaking (Mhv.i.48f.).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> form one of the guards set up by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span> against the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span> (Ja.i.204). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> were sometimes worshipped by human beings and were offered sacrifices of milk, rice, fish, meat and strong drink (Ja.i.497f.). The jewel of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> is famous for its beauty and its power of conferring wishes to its possessor (Ja.vi.179, 180).

The word <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> is often used as an epithet of the Buddha and the Arahants, and in this connection the etymology given is *āguṁ na karotī ti Nāgo* (e.g., MNid.201). The Bodhisatta was born several times as king of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span>: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atula</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Campeyya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhūridatta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Daddara</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkhapāla</span>.

In the accounts given of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span>, there is undoubtedly great confusion between the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> as supernatural beings, as snakes, and as the name of certain non-Aryan tribes, but the confusion is too difficult to unravel.
