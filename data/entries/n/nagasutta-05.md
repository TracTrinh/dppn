# Nāgasutta 05

## English source

Nāgasutta 05. The Buddha goes to the bathing place near the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāramātupāsāda</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, bathes there, and, while drying his limbs, sees <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>’s elephant, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Seta</span>, coming out of the bathing place, attended by great ceremony. People, seeing him, express their wonder and admiration of the noble animal.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷudāyī</span>, says the Commentary, AN­a.ii.669), who is near by, asks the Buddha if it is only the elephant whom people praise for his bulk or do they praise other bulky things as well? They do, says the Buddha, praise all huge things – horses, bulls, snakes, trees, and big men, calling them <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span>, but really, the best <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> is he who commits no enormity in word or thought (*āguṁ na karoti, taṁ nāgo*). Thereupon <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span> breaks forth into song, praising the Buddha’s teaching, comparing the Buddha to an elephant, each limb representing a different virtue (AN.iii.345). This *Sutta* is also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgopamasutta</span>. Thag­a.ii.7.
