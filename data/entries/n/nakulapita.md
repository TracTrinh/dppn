# Nakulapitā

## English source

Nakulapitā and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nakulamātā</span>. A man and his wife, householders of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suṁsumāragiri</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhagga</span>-country. When the Buddha visited the village and stayed at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhesakalāvana</span>, they went to see him. They immediately fell at his feet, calling him “son” and asking why he had been so long away. It is said that they had been the Bodhisatta’s parents for five hundred births and his near relations for many more. The Buddha preached to them and they became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpannas</span>. The Buddha visited their village once more when they were old. They entertained him, telling of their devotion to each other in this life and asking for a teaching which should keep them likewise together in the after-life. The Buddha referred to this in the assembly of the Saṅgha, declaring them to be the most intimate companions (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vissāsikā</span>) among his disciples. (AN.I.26, AN.II.61f, AN­a.i.216f, 246; ii.514; SN­a.ii.182)

Once, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nakulapitā</span> lay grievously ill, his wife noticed that he was fretful with anxiety. She assured him there was no need for anxiety on his part, either on behalf of her or his children. She spoke with such conviction that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nakulapitā</span> regained his composure of mind and grew well. Later he visited the Buddha and told him of this, and was congratulated by the Buddha on having such an excellent wife (AN.III.295ff)

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> contains records of conversations between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nakulapitā</span> and the Buddha (SN.3.1, SN.4.116; AN.IV.268). Both husband and wife are mentioned in lists of eminent disciples (AN.iii.465; AN.iv.348).

It is said that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nakulapitā</span>’s desire for eminence was first conceived in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padamuttara Buddha</span>. He was then a householder of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsavatī</span>, and was present at an assembly where the Buddha declared someone to be chief of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vissāsikas</span>. AN.I.216.
