# Nandā 06

## English source

Nandā 06. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandā Therī</span>. Declared by the Buddha (AN.i.25) to be foremost among nuns in meditative power (*jhāyīnaṁ*). She was the daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Pajāpatī</span> and was therefore Sister of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span> Thera.

She is evidently to be identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sundarīnandā</span>.

There were three *Therīs* (Snp­a.i.241) of the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandā</span> who were ordained with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span>:

- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Nandā</span>, sister of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Nanda</span> Thera (also evidently called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sundarīnandā</span> and sometimes <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Rūpanandā</span> and even <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Janapadakaḷyāni Nandā</span>) (e.g. AN­a.i.198; and Thīg­a.80),
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Abhirūpānandā</span> (daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Khema</span> the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sākyan</span>) and
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Janapadakaḷyāni Nandā</span> (evidently sometimes also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Rūpānandā</span>, e.g.,Dhp­a.iii.113f.).

The legends about them seem to have been confused from very early times.
