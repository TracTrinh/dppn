# Nigaṇṭhā

## English source

Nigaṇṭhā, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭḥas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jainas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jains</span>. The name given to the Jains, the followers of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta</span>. Unlike the *Acelakas*, they wore one garment, a covering in front. But when praised for their modesty, they answered that their reason for wearing a garment was to prevent dust and dirt from falling into their alms dishes. For even dust and dirt are actual individuals and endowed with the principle of life (Dhp­a.iii.489).

The chief precepts of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhā</span> are included in the *cātuyāma saṁvara* – the fourfold restraint (for their beliefs and practices see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta</span>). The chief centres of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span>, in the time of the Buddha, seem to have been <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> (e.g., Ja.iii.1; MN.i.228) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālandā</span> (MN.i.371), though they had settlements in other important towns, such as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> (e.g., at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷasilā</span>, on the slopes of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isigili</span>, MN.i.92).

The chief patrons of the Buddha’s time were:

- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sīha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t23">senāpati</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Vesālī</span>,
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Upāli</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t23">gahapati</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Nālandā</span> and
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Vappa</span> the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sākyan</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kapilavatthu</span> (AN­a.ii.751).

The books contain several names besides that of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nātaputta</span> of distinguished members of the *Nigaṇṭha Saṅgha* – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha Tapassī</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saccaka</span>, and also of several women, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saccā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lolā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avavādakā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭācārā</span> (Ja.iii.1).

The lay followers of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span> wore white garments (MN.ii.244).

In the *Chaḷabhijāti* classification of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūraṇa Kassapa</span>, the *Ekasāṭaka* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span> occupied the third rank, the red (AN.iii.384). The Buddha condemned the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span> as unworthy in ten respects:

- (1) They were without faith,
- (2) Unrighteous,
- (3) Without fear and shame,
- (4) They chose wicked men as friends,
- (5) Extolled themselves and disparaged others,
- (6) Were greedy of present gain,
- (7) Obstinate,
- (8) Untrustworthy,
- (9) Sinful in their thoughts, and
- (10) Held wrong views (AN.v.150).

Their fast resembled a herdsman looking after the kine by day, which were restored to their owners at eventide (AN.i.205f). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span> were so-called because they claimed to be free from all bonds (*amhākaṁ ganthanakileso palibujjhanakileso natthi, kilesaganthirahitā mayan-ti evaṁ vāditāya laddhanāmavasena Nigaṇṭho*) (e.g., MN­a.i.423).

The Buddhist books record that there was great dissension among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span> after the death of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nātaputta</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span> (MN.ii.243f.; DN.iii.117, 210). The Commentaries state that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nātaputta</span>, realizing on his death bed the folly and futility of his teaching, wished his followers to accept the Buddha’s teaching (DN­a.iii.906; MN­a.ii.831). In order to bring this about, he taught his doctrine in two different ways to two different pupils, just before his death. To the one he said that his teaching was Nihilism (*uccheda*), and to the other that it was Eternalism (*sassata*). As a result, they quarrelled violently among themselves, and the Saṅgha broke up.

That the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span> lasted till, at least, the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgasena</span>, is admitted by the fact that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milinda</span>, was asked to consult a teacher called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta</span>, who, if at all historical, was probably the direct successor to the teacher of the same name, contemporary with the Buddha (Mil.p.4).

There is evidence in the Jātakas to show that the *Nigaṇṭha Saṅgha* was in existence prior to the life of the Buddha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saccatapāvī</span>, mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇālajātaka</span> (Ja 536, Ja.v.427), is described as a *setasamaṇī*, and may well have belonged to the Saṅgha of the Svetambaras, while in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Bodhijātaka</span> (Ja.v.246) mention is made of a teacher who is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta</span> himself.

There seems to have been a settlement of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span> in Ceylon from very early times. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍukābhaya</span> laid out the city of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>, he built also hermitages for several <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span> – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Giri</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumbhaṇḍa</span> (Mhv.x.97f). These continued to be inhabited even after the establishment of Buddhism in the Island, for we hear of them in the reign of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṭṭagāmaṇī</span> (circa 44 CE.). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṭṭagāmaṇī</span> pulled down the residence of the *Nigaṇṭha* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Giri</span>, because of his disloyalty to the king, he built on its site the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagirivihāra</span> (Mhv.xxxiii.42f.)
