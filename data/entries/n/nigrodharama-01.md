# Nigrodhārāma 01

## English source

Nigrodhārāma 01. A grove near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, where a residence was provided for the Buddha when he visited the city in the first year after his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> (MN­a.i.289). It belonged to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodha</span>, who gave it to the Saṅgha. In order to convince his proud kinsmen of his attainments, the Buddha performed there the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamakapāṭihāriya</span>, and when, at the conclusion of the miracle a shower of rain fell, wetting only those who wished to be wetted, he related to them the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantarajātaka</span> (Vin.i.82; Ja.i.88f.;vi.479; Bv­a.22; Dhp­a.iii.163; also Mvu.iii.101, 107, 114,138,141, 179). It was during this visit that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Pajāpatī Gotamī</span> first asked permission for women to enter the Saṅgha. This was refused, and from there the Buddha went on to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> (Vin.ii.253; AN.iv.274). The Buddha stayed at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span> on several other occasions, and several Vinaya rules are mentioned as being first promulgated there (e.g., Vin.iii.235, 244; iv.55, 101, 167, 181, 262, 314). Various <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> came to see the Buddha at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span>, among them, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarakāni</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandiya</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vappa</span> (SN.v.369-78; 395-7, 403-4, 408; AN.ii.196; iii.284; iv.220; v. 83, 328, 332, 334). The Buddha himself visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷigodhā</span> during his residence there. It was during a discussion with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Dukkhakkhandhasutta</span> (*q.v.*) was preached. During one of the Buddha’s residences in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> invited him to consecrate their new Mote Hall, which he did by preaching there far into the night and then asking <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> to continue his discourse (SN.iv.182ff.; also MN.i.353, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sekhasutta</span>). On another occasion the Buddha is mentioned as having spent a period of convalescence at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span> (AN.i.219f); he was there also when the quarrel broke out between the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyans</span> regarding the water of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohiṇī</span> (Snp­a.i.357; but see Ja.v.413, where he is said to have been in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>). It seems to have been the Buddha’s custom, when staying at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span>, sometimes to spend the noonday siesta in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vana</span> near by (e.g., SN.iii.91f).

Among others mentioned as having stayed at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span> are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> (Dhp­a.iii.295) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lomasakaṅgiya</span>. MN.iii.200; a *Deva* called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candana</span> there taught him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddekarattasutta</span>. Is this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lomasakaṅgīya</span> the same as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lomavaṅgīsa</span>, who is also mentioned as having lived in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span> (SN.v.327)?

Near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span> was once the site of the dwelling of a hermit (*Isi*) called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇha</span>. The Buddha, remembering this, once smiled, and, when asked the reason for his smile, related the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇhajātaka</span> (Ja 440, Ja.iv.6).

There is a tradition (Cp-a.1,7; Bv­a.3) that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cariyāpiṭaka</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhavaṁsa</span> were preached by the Buddha to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> during his first stay in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span>. It was probably there that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span>’s sister built, at his request, an assembly hall of two storeys for the Saṅgha (Dhp­a.iii.295f). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷa Khemaka</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span>, built a special *vihāra* near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span>, on one side of the grounds (MN­a.ii.906; MN.iii.109f).
