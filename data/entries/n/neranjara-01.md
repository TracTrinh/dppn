# Nerañjarā 01

## English source

Nerañjarā 01. A river. After the Awakening, the Buddha lived under the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajapāla Nigrodha</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span>, on the banks of this river. There <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> tempted him, and, later, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> persuaded him to preach the Dhamma. Vin.i.1ff.; Snp.vs.425ff.; cp. Mvu.ii.238; Lal.327 (261); SN.i.103f; 122, 136ff.; v.167, 185, 232; Ud.i.1-4; ii.1; iii.10; AN.ii.20f; DN.ii.267.

The Commentaries say that when the Buddha, having realized the futility of austerities, left the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcavaggiyas</span>, he retired to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span>, on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nerañjarā</span>, and there, just before the Awakening, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujātā</span> gave him a meal of milk-rice, taking him to be a god (e.g., Ja.i.68ff.; Dhp­a.i.71; Bv­a.238). Before eating the food, he bathed in the ford called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppatiṭṭha</span>. Under the bed of the river lay the abode of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> king, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷa</span>. There was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sāla</span> grove on the banks, where the Buddha spent the afternoon previous to the night of the Awakening.

Three explanations are given of the name: (1) Its waters are pleasant (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nelaṁ jalaṁ assā ti nelañjalā</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">r</span> being substituted for the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">l</span>); (2) it has blue water (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nīla jalāyā ti vattabbe Nerañjarāyā ti vuttaṁ</span>); (3) it is just simply the name of the river. Ud­a.26f.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nadī Kassapa</span>’s hermitage was on the bank of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nerañjarā</span> (Thag­a.i.45).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nerañjarā</span> is identified with the modern Nīlājanā, with its source in Hazaribagh, which, together with the Mohanā, unites to form the river Phalgu. CAGL 524.
