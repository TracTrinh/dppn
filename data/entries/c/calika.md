# Cālikā

## English source

Cālikā <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">v.l.</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jālikā</span>. A village near the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cālikapabbata</span>, where the Buddha spent the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vassas</span> of the thirteenth, eighteenth and nineteenth years after the Awakening (Bv­a.3).

His attendant on one of these visits was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meghiya</span>. Close to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cālikā</span> was the village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jantu</span> where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meghiya</span> went for alms. In the neighbourhood was the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimikālā</span>, on whose banks was a mango grove (AN.iv.354; Ud.iv.1; Dhp­a.i.287f).

Outside the city gate and all around the city was a bog (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">calapaṅka</span>), owing to which the city gave the impression of moving, hence the name (Ud­a.217; AN­a.ii.793).
