# Cakkavattisīhanādasutta

## English source

Cakkavattisīhanādasutta. Preached to the monks at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātulā</span>. It is a sermon on the necessity of living in accordance with the Dhamma, with the Dhamma as one’s refuge.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> contains the story of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Daḷhanemi</span> and his eldest son, and the manner in which a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span> administers the law, ruling by righteousness, over a people made virtuous by his instruction. But, later, there is a gradual corruption of morals, followed by the decay and destruction of human life with all its attendant comforts. This is followed by a gradual restoration of virtuousness, accompanied by the return of prosperity and longevity.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> also records the prophecy of the coming of the Buddha <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Metteyya</span> (DN.iii.58ff). It is said (DN­a.iii.858) that at the end of this discourse twenty thousand monks became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span> and eighty-four thousand others realised the Truth.
