# Caṇḍappajjota

## English source

Caṇḍappajjota. King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>. His name was Pajjota, the sobriquet being added on account of his violent temper.

Once, when ill with jaundice, he asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> to lend him the services of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span>, as no other doctor could cure him. The cure for the malady was ghee, for which Pajjota had a strong aversion. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span>, therefore, decided to administer it disguised in an astringent decoction, and obtained the king’s permission to use any of the royal animals or to leave the city at any time he wished, on the plea that he must go in search of various medicines. When all preparations were complete, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span> gave the king the medicine and escaped on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddavatikā</span>, the king’s she-elephant, before the truth was discovered (the elephant could travel fifty *yojanas* in one day, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāka</span>, sixty). The king sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāka</span> in pursuit, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span> gave <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāka</span> a purgative and so delayed his return until the medicine had taken effect on the king. Later, when Pajjota was cured, he sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span> many costly presents, including a garment of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siveyyaka</span> cloth (Vin.i.276ff; AN­a.i.216).

King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> was Pajjota’s rival in splendour, and Pajjota decided to take him captive by taking advantage of his fondness for elephants. The plan succeeded and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> was taken prisoner, but in the end <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> eloped with Pajjota’s daughter, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsuladattā</span>, and made her his queen consort. Besides the she-elephant and the slave <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāka</span>, already mentioned, Pajjota had three other fleet-footed conveyances: two mares, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Celakaṇṭhī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Muñjakesī</span>, both capable of travelling one hundred leagues a day, and an elephant, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālāgiri</span>, able to go one hundred and twenty leagues a day. In a past birth Pajjota had been the servitor of a certain chief. One day, when the chief was returning from the bath, he saw a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> leaving the city, where he had begged for alms without receiving anything. The chief hurried home and, finding that his meal was ready, sent it to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> by the hand of his fleet-footed servant. The servant travelled with all possible haste and, having given the meal to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>, expressed certain wishes, as the result of which in this birth he gained possession of the five conveyances. He had authority equal to the power of the sun’s rays (this may be another explanation of the nickname <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍa</span>). His last wish was that he should partake of the Truth realised by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> (Dhp­a.i.196ff).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span> was the son of Pajjota’s chaplain and later succeeded to his father’s post. When the king heard of the Buddha’s appearance in the world, he sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāna</span> with seven others to the Buddha, to bring him to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>. But the Buddha sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāna</span> and his companions, now become *Arahants*, to preach to the king and establish the *Sāsana* in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>. The mission was successful. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> contains stanzas uttered by the Thera in admonition to the king. It is said that the king had faith in the Brahmins and held sacrifices involving the slaughter of animals; he was wicked in his deeds. One night he had a dream which frightened him and went to the Thera to have it explained. The Thera told him of the necessity for leading a virtuous life. We are told that from that day the king abandoned his evil ways and lived righteously (Thag.vs.496-501; Thag­a.i.483ff; AN­a.i.116f).

According to the *Dulva* (Rockhill, op.cit., 17), Pajjota was the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anantanemi</span> and was born on the same day as the Buddha. He was called Pajjota (Pradyota), because at the time of his birth the world was illumined as if by a lamp. He became king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span> at the time of the Buddha’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> (Rockhill, op.cit., 32, n.1). He had a minister called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bharata</span>, a clever mechanic (Rockhill, op.cit., 70, n.1).

It would appear from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samantapāsādikā</span> (Vin-a.i.214) that Pajjota was born as the result of an ascetic, or some other holy person, having touched the navel of his mother.

Pajjota was the friend of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, and when the latter was put to death by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, Pajjota seems to have made preparations to wage war on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>. The defences of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> were strengthened to meet the threatened attack, but nothing further happened (MN.iii.7).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhaṅgajātaka</span> (Ja 522, Ja.v.133) mentions a king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍapajjota</span>, in whose dominion was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lambacūḷaka</span>, where lived the ascetic <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sālissara</span>. This either refers to another king of the same name or, more probably, it is an attempt to identify <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lambacūḷaka</span> with some place in the country over which Pajjota ruled in the time of the Buddha.
