# Campā 01

## English source

Campā 01. A city in India on the river of the same name; it was the capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span> and was celebrated for its beautiful lake, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaggarā</span> *pokkharaṇī*, which was excavated by Queen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaggarā</span>. On its banks was a grove of *campaka*-trees, well-known for the fragrance of their marvellous white flowers, and there, in the Buddha’s time, wandering teachers were wont to lodge. The Buddha himself stayed thereon several occasions (Vin.i.312; SN.i.195; AN.iv.59, 168; v.151, 189). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> (AN.iv.59) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅgīsa</span> (SN.i.195) are also said to have stayed there. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Parinibbānasutta</span> (DN.ii.147) mentions <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Campā</span> as one of the six important cities of India, its foundation being ascribed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Govinda</span> (DN.ii.235). It lay at a distance of sixty *yojanas* from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span> (Ja.iv.32). In the Buddha’s time the people of Campā owed allegiance to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, as king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> had given a royal fief in Campā to the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇadaṇḍa</span> (DN.i.111). Campā was evidently an important centre of trade, and we are told that merchants travelled from there to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suvaṇṇabhūmi</span> for purposes of trade (e.g., Ja.vi.539). Most probably it was the Indian colonists from Campā who named one of their most important settlements in Indo-China after this famous old town. The ancient name of Campā was probably *Mālini* or *Mālina* (*Campasya tu puri Campā, yā Mālinyabhavat purā*. *Mahā Bhārata*.xii.5, 6, 7; *Matsyapurāṇa* 48, 97, etc.; Law, *Ancient Geography of India* p.6, n.2).

The ninth chapter of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vagga</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vinayapiṭaka</span> (Vin.i.312ff; see also Vin.iī.307) contains several important regulations laid down by the Buddha at Campā regarding the validity and otherwise of formal acts of the Saṅgha.

Campā is mentioned as the birthplace of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa Koḷivisa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambugāmika</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bharata</span>, and among those who resided there were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhuna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjiyamāhita</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thullanandā</span> and her companions.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇadaṇḍasutta</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dasuttarasutta</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kandarakasutta</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāraṇḍavasutta</span> were preached there.

According to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (MN­a.ii.565), Campā was so-called because the whole place abounded in large *Campaka*-trees.

Campā is generally identified with a site about twenty-four miles to the east of the modern Bhagalpur, near the villages of Campānagara and Campāpura (C.A.G.I.5). It was visited by Xuanzang (Beal-Xuan.ii.187f), and Faxian calls it a great kingdom with many places of worship (p.65).

The Buddha’s bathing-robe was enshrined in Campā (Bv.xxviii.9). See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷa Campā</span>, probably another name for Campā.
