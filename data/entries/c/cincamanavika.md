# Ciñcāmāṇavikā

## English source

Ciñcāmāṇavikā (*v.l.* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ciñcīmāṇavikā</span>). A *paribbājikā* of some ascetic Saṅgha. When the heretics of this Saṅgha found that their gains were grown less owing to the popularity of the Buddha, they enlisted the support of Ciñcā in their attempts to discredit him. She was very beautiful and full of cunning, and they persuaded her to pretend to pay visits to the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>. She let herself be seen going towards the *vihāra* in the evening, spent the night in the heretics’ quarters near by, and in the morning men saw her returning from the direction of the *vihāra*. When questioned, she said that she had passed the night with the Buddha. After some months she simulated pregnancy by tying a disc of wood round her body and appearing thus before the Buddha, as he preached to a vast congregation, she charged him with irresponsibility and callousness in that he made no provision for her confinement. The Buddha remained silent, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s throne was heated and he caused a mouse to sever the cords of the wooden disc, which fell to the ground, cutting Ciñcā’s toes. She was chased out of the *vihāra* by those present, and as she stepped outside the gate the fires of the lowest hell swallowed her up (Dhp­a.iii.178f; Ja.iv.187f; Iti-a.69).

In a previous birth, too, she had helped in various ways to harm the Bodhisatta. For details see:

- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Cūḷa Padumajātaka</span> (Ja 193)
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mahā Padumajātaka</span> (Ja 472)
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Bandhanamokkhajātaka</span> (Ja 120)
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Vānarindajātaka</span> (Ja 57)
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Vessantarajātaka</span> (Ja 547)
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sumsumārajātaka</span> (Ja 208)
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Suvaṇṇakakkaṭajātaka</span> (Ja 389)

It is stated (Ap.i.299; Ud­a.263f) that the Buddha was subjected to the ignominy of being charged by Ciñcā with not being celibate, because in a previous birth he had reviled a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>; cp. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sundarī</span>.
