# Ceti

## English source

Ceti, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetis</span>. One of the sixteen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janapadas</span> (AN.i.213, etc.), probably identical with Cedi of the older documents (e.g., Rv.viii.5, 37-9). The people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ceti</span> seem to have had two distinct settlements: one, perhaps the older, was in the mountains, probably the present Nepal (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Buddhist India</span>, p.26). It is evidently this older settlement which is mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantarajātaka</span> (Ja 547); it was passed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span> on his way into exile in the Himalayas, and was thirty <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojanas</span> distant from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetuttara</span> (Ja.vi.514, 518). The other, probably a later colony, lay near the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamunā</span>, to the east, in the neighbourhood of and contiguous to the settlement of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurus</span>; for we are told (Vin.iv.108f; Ja.i.360f) that the Buddha, having dwelt in the Ceti country, went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddavatikā</span>, where, at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambatittha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgata</span> tamed a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>, and from there he went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>. This part of the country corresponds roughly to the modern Bundelkhand and the adjoining region Law: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Geography of Early Buddhism</span>, p.16).

It was probably of the older Ceti that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sotthivatī</span> was the capital, where once reigned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apacara</span>, who uttered the first lie in the world (Ja.iii.454ff <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sotthivatī</span> is probably identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suktimatī</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suktisāhvaya</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Bhārata</span> (iii.20, 50; xiv.83, 2); see also PHAI.81).

The journey from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> to Ceti lay through a forest which was infested by robbers (Ja.i.253, 256). The settlement of Ceti was an important centre of Buddhism, even in the time of the Buddha. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (AN.iii.355f; v.41f; 157ff) mentions several discourses preached to the Cetis, while the Buddha dwelt in their town of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahajāti</span>. While dwelling in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pācīnavaṁsadāya</span> in the Ceti country, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> after a visit which the Buddha paid to him (AN.iv.228; see also Vin.i.300f). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janavasabhasutta</span> (DN.ii.200 and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t18">passim</span>) leads us to infer that the Buddha visited the Ceti country several times. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (SN.v.436f) records a discussion on the four Ariyan Truths among a number of monks, including <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gavampati</span>, dwelling at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahajāti</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t18">v.l.</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahañcanika</span>).

It is said (e.g., AN­a.ii.765) that the country was called Ceti because it was ruled by kings bearing the name of Ceti or Cetiya (Snp­a.i.135).
