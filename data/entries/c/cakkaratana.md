# Cakkaratana

## English source

Cakkaratana. One of the seven treasures of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>. When a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span> is born into the world, the Cakkaratana appears before him from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkadaha</span>, travelling through the air (Ja.iv.232, but see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepulla</span>).

The Cakkaratana is the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>’s chief symbol of office; on its appearance before him, he sprinkles it with water and asks it to travel to the various quarters of the world, winning them for him. This the Cakkaratana does, carrying with it through the air the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span> with his fourfold army. Wherever the Cakkaratana halts, all the chiefs of that quarter acclaim the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span> as their overlord and declare their allegiance to him. Having thus traversed the four quarters of the earth, it returns to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>’s capital, and remains fixed as an ornament on the open terrace in front of his inner apartments (DN.ii.173f; MN.iii.173ff).

The Commentaries (e.g., DN­a.ii.617ff; MN­a.ii.942ff) contain lengthy descriptions of the Cakkaratana: it is shaped like a wheel, its nave is of sapphire, the centre of which shines like the orb of the moon, and round it is a band of silver. It has one thousand spokes, each ornamented with various decorations; its tyre is of bright coral; within every tenth spoke is a coral staff, hollow inside, which produces the sounds of the fivefold musical instruments when blown upon by the wind. On the staff is a white parasol, on either side of which are festoons of flowers. When the wheel moves, it appears like three wheels moving one within the other.

When a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span> dies or leaves the world, the Cakkaratana disappears from the sight of men for seven days; it gives warning of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>’s impending death by slipping from its place some time before the event (DN.iii.59f.; MN­a.ii.885). When his successor has lived righteously for seven days, it reappears (DN.iii.64).

It is the most precious and the most honoured thing in the world. Ud­a.356.
