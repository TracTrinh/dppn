# Candanamālā

## English source

Candanamālā <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">v.l.</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candanasālā</span>. One of the residences occupied by the Buddha (Snp­a.ii.403). It was in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunāparanta</span> and was built of red sandalwood at the instigation of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunāparanta</span>. When the work was completed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span> sent a flower as message to the Buddha, who came with five hundred monks and performed the ceremony of dedication before dawn (Thag­a.i.158). During the festival, the Buddha showed the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamakapāṭihāriya</span> (Thag­a.i.312).
