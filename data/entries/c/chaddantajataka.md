# Chaddantajātaka

## English source

Chaddantajātaka (Ja 514). The story of the Bodhisatta, born as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chaddanta</span>, king of elephants. It was related in reference to a nun of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> who, while listening one day to a sermon by the Buddha, admired his extreme beauty of form and wondered if she had ever been his wife. Immediately the memory of her life as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Subhaddā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chaddanta</span>’s consort, came to her mind and she laughed for joy; but on further recollecting that she had been the instrument of his death, she wept aloud. The Buddha related this story in explanation of her conduct. Ja.v.36; Speyer (ZDMG.lxxv.2, 305ff) suggests an allegorical explanation of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chaddantajātaka</span>. Feer (JA.1895 v.) gives a careful study of the story based on a comparison of five different versions – two Pāli, two Chinese and one Sanskrit. This <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> forms the theme of many illustrations – e.g., in Barhut (Cunningham, pl.xxxvi.6), also Ajanta Caves x. and xvii.
