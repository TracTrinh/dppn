# Cūḷa Dukkhakkhandhasutta

## English source

Cūḷa Dukkhakkhandhasutta, Cūla Dukkhakkhandhasutta. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span>, visits the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span> and asks him why it is that evil states of mind arise in one. The Buddha replies that it is because of certain undiscarded states of consciousness, which can be got rid of through renunciation. He proceeds to describe how he, too, failed to find satisfaction before his Awakening. He then tells of a conversation he once had on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span> with some <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span>, who believed that suffering could be got rid of through austerities and how he told them of his happiness which was far greater even than that of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>. MN.i.91ff.
