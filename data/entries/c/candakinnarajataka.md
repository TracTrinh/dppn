# Candakinnarajātaka

## English source

Candakinnarajātaka (Ja 485). Once the Bodhisatta, born as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kinnara</span> named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Canda</span>, lived with his mate <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candā</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Canda</span> mountain in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span>. One day, while they were disporting themselves near a little stream, singing and dancing, the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, who had gone hunting, saw <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candā</span> and fell in love with her. So he shot <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Canda</span> with an arrow, and when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candā</span> lamented aloud at the sight of her dead husband the king revealed himself and offered her his love and his kingdom. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candā</span> scorned the offer and protested to the gods that they should have allowed harm to befall her husband. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s throne was heated by her such great loyalty and, coming in the guise of a Brahmin, he restored to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Canda</span> his life.

The king was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candā</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulamātā</span>. The story was related by the Buddha when he visited his father’s palace at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> and heard from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> how devotedly <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulamātā</span> had continued to love the Buddha. He said it was not the first time that she had shown her undying affection. Ja.iv.282ff; Dhp­a.i.97.
