# Cariyāpiṭaka

## English source

Cariyāpiṭaka. One of the fifteen books of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khuddakanikāya</span>, generally placed last in the list. It contains tales in metrical verse of the Buddha’s previous births, chiefly setting forth the ten <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pāramī</span>, by which he attained Awakening. Each story is called a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Cariyā</span>. The stories told here in verse are parallel to the corresponding <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> stories in prose, and presuppose a familiar acquaintance with all the incidents of the prose tales. The first two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pāramī</span> are illustrated by ten stories each, while the remaining <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pāramī</span> have only fifteen stories between them.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghabhāṇakas</span> refused to include the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cariyāpiṭaka</span> in their canonical books, but it was accepted by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhimabhāṇakas</span> (DN­a.i.15, 23).

There exists a Commentary on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cariyāpiṭaka</span> which is ascribed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapāla</span> and which forms a part of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paramathadīpanī</span> (published in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Hewavitarane Bequest Series</span>, vol.xxvi.; 1929).

According to the Commentary (Cp-a.1, 2), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cariyāpiṭaka</span> was preached by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span>, after the conclusion of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhavaṁsa</span> and at the request of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>.

It was preached by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandanavana</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>, soon after his arrival in Ceylon (Dpv.xiv.45; but see Mhv.xv.179).
