# Candagutta 01

## English source

Candagutta 01. King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>. He belonged to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moriya</span> dynasty and gained the throne through the scheming of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cāṇakka</span>, who slew the rightful king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanananda</span> and his heir <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pabbata</span> (the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsaṭīkā</span> gives details of how <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cāṇakka</span> contrived to make <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candagutta</span> king (pp.181ff)). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candagutta</span> reigned twenty-four years and was succeeded by his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Biṇḍusāra</span>. His grandson was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> (Mhv.v.16ff). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candagutta</span>’s senior contemporary in Ceylon was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍukābhaya</span>, who died in the fourteenth year of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candagutta</span>’s reign (Dpv.vi.15; Vin-a.i.72; see also Dpv.v.69, 73, 81; both the Dpv. and the Vin-a. talk of *Pakuṇḍakābhaya* (*sic*). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milinda</span> mentions a soldier <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddasāla</span>, in the service of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span> royal family, who waged war against <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candagutta</span> (p.292; see also *Kathāsaritsāgara* i.30). In this war there were eighty “Corpse Dances” in which dead bodies danced. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> Commentary (i., p.440) states that the father of the Thera <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tekicchakāri</span> incurred the displeasure of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candagutta</span>, who, at the instigation of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cāṇakka</span>, cast him into prison.
