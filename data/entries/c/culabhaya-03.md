# Cūḷābhaya 03

## English source

Cūḷābhaya 03. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūlābhaya</span>. A Thera of Ceylon and a well-known commentator; he was known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Tipiṭaka</span> Cūḷābhaya. He is several times quoted in the Commentaries (e.g., Vibh­a.11, 16; Vsm.69, 394, 397). He had a prodigious memory. Once he shut three of the gates of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>, allowing only one door for the use of those leaving the city. As each person went out he asked his name, and each of these names he was able to repeat when the owner returned in the evening to the city (DN­a.ii.530). He was a contemporary of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Nāga</span> (Dhs­a.230; SN­a.iii.206), with whom he held discussions. His description (given at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span>) (DN­a.ii.442) of the details connected with the first words uttered by the Buddha after his birth, and the manner in which he walked, is accepted as authentic. He once went with a company of monks on a pilgrimage to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadīpa</span>. On the way he stayed in a monastery. One of his followers, who lacked self-control, made friends with another of like temperament in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span>, and Cūḷābhaya, seeing them laugh aloud, pointed out how truly the Buddha had said in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhātusaṁyutta</span> that like joins with like. Vibh­a.457f.
