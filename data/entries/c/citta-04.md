# Citta 04

## English source

Citta 04. called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthirohaputta</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthisāriputta</span>). A Thera<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">,</span> son of an elephant trainer, who, having entered the Saṅgha, studied under the Buddha and gained special proficiency in distinguishing subtle differences in the meanings of words. Six times he left the Saṅgha and six times returned. His last quarrel was with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Koṭṭhita</span>, who objected to his constant interruptions of the Elder’s discussions regarding the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span> (this incident is recorded at AN.iii.392ff).

It is said that in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>, Citta and a friend entered the Saṅgha (DN­a.ii.378f.; AN­a.ii.688). When the friend expressed a desire to return to household life, Citta encouraged him to do so, coveting his belongings. This was the reason for Citta’s inability to remain in the Saṅgha. He was a friend of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Poṭṭhapāda</span>, and when he had returned for the sixth time to a householder’s life, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Poṭṭhapāda</span> brought him to the Buddha. Citta listened to their conversation and asked questions regarding personality. At the end of the discourse (recounted in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Poṭṭhapādasutta</span>) Citta once more joined the Saṅgha, never again to leave it, for he soon after became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> (DN.i.199ff).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuddālajātaka</span> (Ja 70, Ja.i.311f) gives the circumstances in which he first joined the Saṅgha. He was a youth of good family at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>. One day, while on his way home from ploughing, he received from the bowl of a certain Elder some rich and dainty food. In order to gain similar food for himself, he became a monk, but soon after, lust overcame him and he left the homeless life. Even after he became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> his colleagues are said to have taunted him, asking when he would be leaving them, and it was only when the Buddha told them that such a time would never be were they satisfied.

The same story, except for certain details, is also found in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp­a.i.305ff), but there Citta is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittahattha</span>, and a different explanation is given of his name: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">esa cittavasiko hutvā vicarati ti Cittahatthan-ti nāmaṁ kariṁsu</span>. It is further stated that on the last occasion of his leaving home he saw his pregnant wife lying asleep and was so filled with revolt that he returned to the Saṅgha. He started forth at once, a yellow robe tied round his waist, and as he walked to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span>, he became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>. The monks were at first reluctant to re-ordain him, but his importunity was so great that they relented, and in a few days he became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>.
