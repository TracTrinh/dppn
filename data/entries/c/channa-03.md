# Channa 03

## English source

Channa 03. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>’s charioteer and companion, born on the same day as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> (Ja.i.54; Mvu.ii.156, 164, 189, 233; iii.91, 262; Bv­a.233; SN­a.ii.231; Dhs­a.34. Thag­a. (i.155) says he was the son of a servant woman of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span>). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> left household life, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> rode with him on the horse <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kanthaka</span> as far as the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anomā</span>. There <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> gave him his ornaments and bade him take <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kanthaka</span> back to his father’s palace (a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thūpa</span> was later erected on the spot where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> turned back; Divy.391). When, however, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kanthaka</span> died of a broken heart, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span>’s grief was great, for he had suffered a double loss. It is said that he begged for leave to join <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> as a recluse, but this leave was refused (Ja.i.64f). He therefore returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, but when the Buddha visited his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> kinsfolk, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> joined the Saṅgha. Because of his great affection for the Buddha, however, egotistical pride in “our Buddha, our Dhamma” arose in him and he could not conquer this fondness nor fulfil his duties as a bhikkhu (Thag­a.i.155; his verse (No.69) quoted in Thag. does not, however, refer to any such remissness on his part).

Once, when in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghositārāma</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> committed a fault but was not willing to acknowledge it. When the matter was reported to the Buddha, he decreed that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ukkhepaniyakamma</span> be carried out against him, forbidding him to eat or dwell with the Saṅgha. He therefore changed his residence, but was everywhere “boycotted,” and returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> subdued and asking for reprieve, which was granted to him. Vin.ii.23ff. His obstinacy and perverseness are again mentioned elsewhere – e.g., Vin.iv.35, 113, 141. A patron of his once erected a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> for him, but he so thatched and decked it that it fell down. In trying to repair it he damaged a Brahmin’s barley field (Vin.iii.47. See also Vin.iii.155f., 177).

Later, in a dispute between the monks and the nuns, he deliberately sided with the latter; this was considered so perverse and so lacking in proper <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">esprit de corps</span>, that the Buddha decreed on him the carrying out of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahmadaṇḍa</span> whereby all monks were forbidden to have anything whatsoever to do with him. This was the last disciplinary act of the Buddha, and the carrying out thereof was entrusted to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>. DN.ii.154. It would, however, appear from Dhp­a.ii.110 that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahmadaṇḍa</span> was inflicted on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> for his having repeatedly reviled <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> in spite of the Buddha’s warning. In this version other details also vary.

When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghositārāma</span> and pronounced on him the penalty, even his proud and independent spirit was tamed; he became humble, his eyes were opened, and dwelling apart, earnest and zealous, he became one of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span>, upon which the penalty automatically lapsed (Vin.ii.292). In the past, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> met <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siddhattha Buddha</span> going towards a tree, and being pleased with him, spread for him a soft carpet of leaves round which he spread flowers. Five <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> ago he became king seven times, under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tinasanthāraka</span> (Thag­a.i.155).

He is probably identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Senāsanadāyaka</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (i.137).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> is identified with the hunter in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suvaṇṇamigajātaka</span> (Ja 359) (III.187), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhajātaka</span> (Ja 399) (III.332), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohantamigajātaka</span> (Ja 501) (IV.423), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Haṁsajātaka</span> (Ja 533) (V.354), and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Haṁsajātaka</span> (Ja 534) (V.382), with the wrestler in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sālikedārajātaka</span> (Ja 484) (IV.282) and with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetaputta</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantarajātaka</span> (Ja 547) (VI.593). See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channasutta</span> (1) below.
