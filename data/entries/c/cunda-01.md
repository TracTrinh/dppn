# Cunda 01

## English source

Cunda 01. A worker in metals (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kammāraputta</span>) living in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span>. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> reached <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span> on his way to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>, he stayed in Cunda’s Mango grove. There Cunda visited him and invited him and the monks to a meal the next day. The meal consisted of sweet rice and cakes and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sūkaramaddava</span>. At the meal the Buddha ordered that he alone should be served with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sūkaramaddava</span>, and that what was left over should be buried in a hole. This was the Buddha’s last meal, as very soon after it he developed dysentery (DN.ii.126; Ud.viii.5). The Buddha, a little while before his death, gave special instructions to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> that he should visit Cunda and reassure him by telling him that no blame at all attached to him and that he should feel no remorse, but should, on the contrary, rejoice, in that he had been able to give to the Buddha a meal which, in merit, far exceeded any other (DN.ii.135f).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span> Commentary (Snp­a.i.159) mentions that, at this meal, Cunda provided golden vessels for the monks’ use; some made use of them, others did not. One monk stole a vessel and put it in his bag. Cunda noticed this but said nothing. Later, in the afternoon, he visited the Buddha and questioned him as to the different kinds of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">samaṇas</span> there were in the world. The Buddha preached to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cundasutta</span>.

The Commentary adds (p.166; also Ud­a.399) that Cunda reached no attainment, but merely had his doubts dispelled. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha Commentary</span>, however, says (DN­a.ii.568) that he became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span> at the first sight of the Buddha and built for him a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambavana</span>. This latter incident, probably, took place at an earlier visit of the Buddha, for we are told (DN.iii.207) that while the Buddha was staying in Cunda’s Mango grove, he was invited by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> to consecrate their new Mote-hall, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ubbhaṭaka</span>. He accepted the invitation, preached in the hall till late at night, and then requested <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> to continue, which he did by preaching the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅgītisutta</span>. This was soon after the death of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta</span> (DN.iii.210).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (v.263ff) mentions another conversation between the Buddha and Cunda. Cunda tells the Buddha that he approves of the methods of purification (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">soceyyāni</span>) laid down by the Brahmins of the west (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pacchābhūmakā</span>). The Buddha tells him of the teaching of the Ariyans regarding the threefold defilement and purification of the body, the fourfold defilement and purification of the speech, and the threefold defilement and purification of the mind. Cunda accepts the Buddha’s explanations and declares himself his follower.
