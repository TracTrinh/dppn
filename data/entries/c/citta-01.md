# Citta 01

## English source

Citta 01. (called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittagahapati</span>). A householder of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Macchikāsaṇḍa</span>, where he was Treasurer. He was later declared by the Buddha to be pre-eminent among laymen who preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span> (AN.i.26). On the day of his birth the whole city was covered knee-deep with flowers of various hues, hence his name.

When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Macchikāsaṇḍa</span>, Citta, pleased with his demeanour, invited him to his park, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambāṭakārāma</span>, and built for him a monastery there. And there the Elder preached to Citta the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saḷāyatanavibhatti</span> and Citta became an *Anāgāmī*. Thereafter many monks visited the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambāṭakārāma</span> and accepted Citta’s hospitality. Among them was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span> (*q.v.*), a former acquaintance of Citta, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span> left when he found that his identity had been discovered. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahaka</span> did likewise, after having performed miracles at the request of Citta.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittasaṁyutta</span> (SN.iv.282ff) contains a record of conversations between Citta and members of the Saṅgha, among whom, besides those already mentioned, were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāmabhū</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godatta</span>. Citta is also said to have had discussions with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Acela Kassapa</span> and to have refuted their views.

A Thera named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhamma</span> was a permanent resident in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambāṭakārāma</span> and was looked after by Citta. Once, when the two Chief Disciples and several other eminent Elders came to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambāṭakārāma</span>, Citta invited first these and then <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhamma</span>; the latter, feeling slighted, blamed Citta beyond measure, but the Buddha, hearing of this, sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhamma</span> to ask for Citta’s pardon (Vin.ii.15ff; Dhp­a.ii.74f; for details see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhamma</span>).

Some time later, Citta visited the Buddha. He was accompanied by two thousand others and took with him five hundred cartloads of offerings to the Buddha and the Saṅgha. As he fell at the feet of the Buddha, flowers of five hues showered from the sky and the Buddha preached to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saḷāyatanavibhatti</span>. For a fortnight he continued distributing his gifts to the Saṅgha and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> filled his carts with all kinds of valuables (AN­a.i.210).

When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta</span> lay ill just before his death, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> visited him and advised him to wish for kingship among them, but he refused to aspire to anything so impermanent, and instructed the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> and his kinsfolk gathered round him, telling them of the Buddha and his teachings (SN.iv.302f). He is regarded as the ideal layman (e.g., at AN.i.88; ii.164; iii.451).

He owned a tributary village called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migapattaka</span> (SN­a.iii.93).

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>, Citta conceived his desire to be placed first among laymen in the teaching of the Dhamma. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> he was a huntsman. One day, seeing a monk in a glen, and being pleased thereat, he hurried home, prepared a meal and brought it to the monk, together with flowers he had gathered on the way. After the offering, he made a wish that he should never lack for tribute and that showers of flowers should fall on him. In the *Deva*-world he surpassed all others in his great beauty (AN­a.i.209). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhisajātaka</span> (Ja.iii.314), he is identified with the slave.

Though <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta</span> was not an *Arahant*, he possessed the *paṭisambhidā* of a probationer (*sekha*). Vsm.442.
