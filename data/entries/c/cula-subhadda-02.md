# Cūḷa Subhaddā 02

## English source

Cūḷa Subhaddā 02. Cūla Subhaddā. Daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>. At the insistent request of his friend Uggaseṭṭhi, and after consultation with the Buddha, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span> agreed to give her in marriage to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span>’s son (according to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttara Commentary</span> (ii.482ff), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span>’s name was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷaka</span> and his city was not <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span>, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span>). But he was an unbeliever, and when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span> came to his house on invitation, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhaddā</span> refused to do obeisance to them. For this she was ordered out of the house by her father-in-law; but she convinced her mother-in-law that the reasons for her behaviour were sound, and at the suggestion of the latter she prepared a meal and invited to it the Buddha and the congregation of monks, by throwing into the air from the top storey of the house eight handfuls of jasmine. The Buddha divined her thoughts and arrived with five hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span>. After the meal the Buddha preached the Dhamma and Ugga and his family were converted. As a mark of favour towards <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhaddā</span> the Buddha requested <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> to stay behind at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugganagara</span> (Dhp­a.iii.465ff; the story is also given in AN­a.ii.482ff, but with several variations in detail. There seems to be a comparison between the stories of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Subhaddā</span> and Cūḷa Subhaddā. See also AN­a.i.146 and Vsm.390).

Cūḷa Subhaddā, while still in her father’s house, had become a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>, and with her sisters, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Subhaddā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanā</span>, she had been entrusted with the distribution of food to the monks. Dhp­a.i.128; Ja.i.93; Ap­a.i.81; see also Mil.383, 387.
