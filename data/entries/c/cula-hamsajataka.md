# Cūḷa Haṁsajātaka

## English source

Cūḷa Haṁsajātaka, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t15" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t15">Culla Haṁsajātaka</span> (Ja 533). The Bodhisatta was once born as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhataraṭṭha</span>, king of ninety thousand golden geese living in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittakūṭa</span>. One day some of his flock came upon Lake Mānusiya, near the haunts of men, and finding it a rich feeding-ground, persuaded him much against his will to go there with them. But immediately he alighted he was caught in a fowler’s noose and found escape impossible. He waited till the flock had fed, then gave the cry of alarm at which all the geese flew away except his commander-in-chief, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumukha</span>. When the fowler came, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumukha</span> offered to give his life for his king, and thereby softened the fowler’s heart. The latter set <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhataraṭṭha</span> free and tended his wounds, and because of the man’s great charity the king of the geese became whole again. When the fowler suggested that they should fly home, the two geese insisted that they should be taken to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakula</span>, the king of the land, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahiṁsaka</span> country, that they might obtain for the fowler a suitable reward. When the king heard the story he gave to the fowler a village yielding one hundred thousand annually, a chariot and a large store of gold. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhataraṭṭha</span> preached to the king the moral law and, after being paid great honour, returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittakūṭa</span>.

The story was related in reference to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>’s attempt to offer his life in order to save the Buddha from being killed by the elephant <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālāgiri</span> (*q.v.*). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> is identified with the fowler, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> with the king, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumukha</span>. Ja.v.333-.54; Dhp­a.i.119; cf. the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Haṁsajātaka</span> (Ja 534) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsajātaka</span> (Ja 502).
