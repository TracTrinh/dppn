# Cunda

## English source

Cunda 02. The books appear to refer to two Theras by the name of Cunda, the better known being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Cunda</span> and the other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Cunda</span>. But the legends connected with them are so confused that it is not possible to differentiate clearly one from the other.

Mention is also made of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Samaṇuddesa</span> whom, however, the Commentaries identify with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Cunda</span> (e.g. DN­a.iii.907). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Cunda</span> is, for instance, described in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> Commentary as the younger brother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, under whom he joined the Saṅgha, winning <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship after arduous and strenuous effort (Thag­a.i.261; see also Dhp­a.ii.188 and AN­a.ii.674).

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span> he had been a potter and had given to the Buddha a bowl made of clay. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> verses quoted in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> Commentary are, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> itself (Ap.ii.444), ascribed to a monk named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekapattadāyaka</span>. They make no mention whatever of his relationship to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>. On the other hand, there are to be found elsewhere in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (Ap.i.101f) certain verses ascribed to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span> Thera<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">,</span> which definitely state that he was the son of the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅganta</span>, and that his mother was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sārī</span>. But in these verses he is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Cunda</span>, and mention is made of his previous birth in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siddhattha Buddha</span>, to whom he gave a bouquet of jasmine flowers. As a result he became king of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> seventy-seven times and was once king of men, by name <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dujjaya</span>. It is further stated that he became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> while yet a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sāmaṇera</span> and that he waited upon the Buddha and his own brother and other virtuous monks. This account goes on to say that after his brother’s death, Cunda brought his relics in a bowl and presented them to the Buddha, who uttered praises of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>. This would identify <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Cunda</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Samaṇuddesa</span> who, according to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (SN.v.161f), attended <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> in his last illness and, after his death, brought to the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana Sāriputta</span>’s bowl and outer robe and his relics wrapt in his water-strainer. Therefore if <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> is correct in identifying <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Samaṇuddesa</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Cunda</span>, then all three are one and the same (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says that the monks called him <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Samaṇuddesa</span> in his youth before his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">upasampadā</span>, and he never lost the name, DN­a.iii.907).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Samaṇuddesa</span> was, for some time, the personal attendant of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> (Thag­a.ii.124; Ja.iv.95, etc.), and when the Buddha prepared to perform the Twin Miracle, offered to perform a miracle himself and so save the Buddha trouble and exertion (Dhp­a.iii.211). Cunda’s teacher was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, and it was to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> that he first brought the news of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>’s death (SN­a.iii.178; see also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāsādikasutta</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmagāmasutta</span>, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span> brings to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> and then to the Buddha the news of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta</span>’s death; see also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sallekhasutta</span>).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Cunda</span> was evidently a disciple of great eminence, and is mentioned by the Buddha (AN.iii.299; see also MN.iii.78; Ud.i.5) in company with the Two Chief Disciples, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Koṭṭhita</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span> and other very eminent Elders.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Piṭakas</span> contain several discourses (AN.iii.355; v.41, 157) given to the monks by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Cunda</span> while residing at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahajātī</span> among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetis</span>, probably after the Buddha’s death. Cunda (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cundaka</span> as he is called in this context) was with the Buddha in his last journey to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>, and spread a bed for him in the Mango grove by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakuṭṭhā</span> River (DN.ii.134f; Ud.viii.5).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span> is mentioned (SN.iv.50f.; MN.iii.263f) as having accompanied <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> when he went to see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalandakanivāpa</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, just before <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span>’s suicide. Once, when the Buddha lay ill in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalandakanivāpa</span>, Cunda visited him and they talked of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t18">bojjhaṅgas</span>. There and then the Buddha’s sickness vanished. SN.v.81.
