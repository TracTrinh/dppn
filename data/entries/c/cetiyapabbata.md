# Cetiyapabbata

## English source

Cetiyapabbata, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyagiri</span>. The later name of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Missaka</span> mountain given on account of its many shrines. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span> built a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> there – the second <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> in Ceylon – for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> and those ordained under him (Mhv.xvi.12-17). The relics, obtained by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sāmaṇera</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> and from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, were deposited there until they were needed. According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsa</span> (Mhv.xxii.23ff) this fact was the occasion for the name. One of the eight saplings of the Sacred Bodhi-tree at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> was planted in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ārāma</span> on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyagiri</span> (Mhv.xix.62). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> spent the last years of his life on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyagiri</span> and died there, and there his relics were enshrined (Mhv.xx.32, 45). Near the mountain was the village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dvāramaṇḍala</span> (Mhv.xxiii.23). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṭakaṇṇatissa</span> built an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">uposatha</span>-hall on the mountain and planted a Bodhi-tree, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Bhātikābhaya</span> supplied food daily to one thousand monks dwelling there (Mhv.xxxiv.30f, 64), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lañjakatissa</span> had the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> paved at a cost of one hundred thousand (Mhv.xxxiii.25). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Dāṭhika Mahā Nāga</span> made four gateways and a road round the mountain, and held the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Giribhaṇḍapūjā</span> with great pomp and ceremony; it is said that in order that the people might approach the mountain with clean feet he spread carpets right up to it from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kadamba</span> River (Mhv.xxxiv.75ff). Kanirajānutissa had sixty monks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyapabbata</span> put to death as traitors by flinging them into the cave called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kanira</span> (Mhv.xxxv.11). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vasabha</span> provided four thousand lamps to be lighted on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyagiri</span> (Mhv.xxxv.80), while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jeṭṭhatissa</span> gave to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> the income derived from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷamattika</span> Tank (Mhv.xxxvi.130; see also Dpv.xv.69; xvii.90; xix.13, and Vin-a.i.82ff).

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakusandha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyagiri</span> was known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devakūṭa</span>, that of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇāgamana</span> as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suvaṇṇakūṭa</span>, and in that of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhakūṭa</span> (Vin-a.i.86f). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammarucikas</span> once occupied the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambatthalavihāra</span> on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyapabbata</span>, it having been given to them by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhātusena</span> (Cv.xxxviii.75). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggabodhi</span> supplied a permanent supply of water for the bathing-tank called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgasoṇḍi</span>, on the top of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyagiri</span> (Cv.xlii.28; see Cv.Trs.i.68, n.8), while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggabodhi III</span>. gave to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> the village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambillapadara</span> (Cv.xliv.122). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggabodhi V</span>. restored the ruined buildings of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyapabbata</span> at a cost of one hundred and twenty thousand pieces (Cv.xlviii.7), while the queen of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udaya</span> I, built there the Kanthaka <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span>, and her husband decorated the mountain with brightly coloured flags and streamers (Cv.xlix.23, 27). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sena</span> I, gave to the monastery the income from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāṇavāpi</span> (Cv.l.72), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sena II</span>. Provided a hospital for the use of the monks there (Cv.li.73). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa VI</span>. built the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hadayuṇhapariveṇa</span> and gifted it to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammarucikas</span> (Cv.lii.18). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu I</span>. restored all the old buildings which had been destroyed and built sixty-four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thūpas</span> (Cv.lxxviii.108).

The Commentaries relate several anecdotes connected with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyapabbata</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maliyadeva</span> Thera recited there the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chachakkasutta</span>, and sixty listening bhikkhus became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span> (MN­a.ii.1064). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lomasanāga</span> Thera lived in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Padhānaghara</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piyaṅguguhā</span> there and overcame the cold he felt by meditating on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Lokantarikaniraya</span> (MN­a.i.65). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyapabbata</span> was the residence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷa Buddharakkhita</span>, and King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saddhātissa</span> spent some time there (MN­a.i.469f. See also Vsm.20, 64; Dhsa.194, 200; AN­a.i.44). At the time that Faxian came to Ceylon there were two thousand monks in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyagiri</span>, including a monk of great fame, called Dharmagupta (Giles: p.72).
