# Vacchagotta 01

## English source

Vacchagotta 01. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Paribbājaka</span>, who later became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> Thera. Several conversations he had with the Buddha are mentioned in the books. For details see the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tevijja Vacchagottasutta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggi Vacchagottasutta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vacchagottasutta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vacchasutta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vacchagottasutta</span>. Some of these <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span> are quoted in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kathāvatthu</span> (e.g., p. 267, 505). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> contains a whole section on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vacchagotta</span>; his discussions were chiefly concerned with such mythical questions as to whether the world is eternal, the nature of life, the existence or otherwise of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tathāgata</span> after death, etc. SN.iii.257ff.; see also SN.iv.391ff., for several discussions of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vacchagotta</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiya</span> Kaccāna on similar topics. The three <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vacchagottasutta</span>s of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhimanikāya</span> seem to contain the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vacchagotta</span>’s conversion, in due order: at the conclusion of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tevijja Vacchagotta</span> (No. 72) it is merely stated that “the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Paribbājaka</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vacchagotta</span> rejoiced in what the Blessed One has said.” At the end of the next, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggi Vacchagotta</span>, he is mentioned as having accepted the Buddha as his teacher. In the third, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vacchagotta</span>, he seeks ordination from the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, and receives it after the requisite probationary period of four months. He returns to the Buddha after two weeks and tells him that he has attained all that is to be attained by a non-<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>’s understanding and asks for a further exposition of the Dhamma. The Buddha tells him to proceed to the study of calm and insight, whereby sixfold <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">abhiññā</span> may be acquired. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vacchagotta</span> profits by the lesson and soon after becomes an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. He thereupon sends news of his attainment to the Buddha through some monks, and the Buddha says he has already heard the news from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> (MN.i.493-97).

This story definitely identifies the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Paribbājaka</span> with the Thera of the same name, whose verse of ecstasy is included in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> (vs. 112). According to the Commentary (Thag­a.i.221), he belonged to a rich Brahmin family of the Vaccha clan (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vacchagotta</span>). His personal name is not given. He became an expert in Brahmin learning, but failing to find therein what he sought, he became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Paribbājaka</span>, joining the Buddha’s Saṅgha later.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span> he was a householder of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhumatī</span>, and one day, when the Buddha and his monks were invited to the king’s palace, he swept the street along which the Buddha passed and set up a rag as decoration. As a result he was born, four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> ago, as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t18">rājā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhaja</span> by name. He is probably identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vīthisammajjaka</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>. Ap.i.177.
