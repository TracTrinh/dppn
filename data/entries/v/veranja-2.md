# Verañjā

## English source

Verañjā. A town in which the Buddha once spent the rainy season at the invitation of the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verañja</span> (in the twelfth year, according to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>, e.g., AN­a.ii.758; cf. Bv­a.3). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verañja</span> visits the Buddha at the foot of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Naḷerupucimanda</span>, where he is staying, and asks him a series of questions, the first of which is: whether it be true that the Buddha pays no respect to aged Brahmins. The Buddha replies that he has not seen a Brahmin in the whole world to whom such respect is due from him. If the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tathāgata</span> were so to honour anyone, that person’s head would split in pieces. Other questions follow on the Buddha’s doctrine and practices. The Buddha concludes by giving an account of his attainment of the threefold knowledge. The interview ends with the conversion of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verañja</span> and his invitation to the Buddha to spend his rainy season there. Here he spoke of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijjāttaya</span>, says Ud­a (p. 183), because all the monks with the Buddha were *chaḷabhiññā*, and therefore no special mention was needed of *abhiññā*.

At that time there was a famine, and five hundred householders of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarāpatha</span>, staying at Verañjā, supplied the monks with food. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> proposed to get food by the exercise of his magic power or by going with the monks to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span>, but he was dissuaded by the Buddha. During this stay <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> received from the Buddha an explanation as to why the religious systems of the three previous Buddhas lasted so long, while those of the three preceding them – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sikhī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessabhū</span>- did not.

At the conclusion of the *vassa*, the Buddha wished to take leave of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verañja</span> before setting out, as was the custom of Buddhas when they received hospitality. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verañja</span> admitted that, though he had invited the Buddha, he had not kept his promise, and this was due to his having too many duties in the house. The Commentators add that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verañja</span> forgot his invitation because <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>, being in a spiteful mood, had taken possession of him and of all the inhabitants of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verañja</span> (Vin-a.i.178f; Dhp­a.ii.153; cf. Ja.iii.494).

He invited the Buddha and the monks to a meal the next day, and, at the end of the meal, presented a set of three robes to the Buddha and a pair to each of the monks.

After leaving Verañjā the Buddha went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, passing through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soreyya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇṇakujja</span>, and crossing the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Payāgapatiṭṭhāna</span>. From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> he proceeded to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>. This account, of the Buddha’s visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verañja</span>, forms the introduction to the Vinaya and is found at Vin.iii.1-11. The interview with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verañja</span> is given at AN.iv.172ff. The road taken by the Buddha from Verañjā to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> was, according to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (Vin-a.i.201), the shortest, and the Buddha knew the monks were tired after their experiences in Verañjā. Soon after, he appears to have visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>. There he was visited by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span>, who asked permission to entertain him and the monks for four months that they might recover their strength. At the end of the four months he renewed his request, and thus looked after the monks for a whole year. It was this act that won for him the title of *aggo paṇītadāyakānaṁ* (AN.A.i.213).

It is said (Snp­a.i.154; Mil. 232) that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> put flavour (*ojā*) into every mouthful of food taken by the Buddha at Verañjā. According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>, the Bodhisatta was born of a noble house in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phussa Buddha</span>, and, once, seeing the monks eating good food, he had reviled them and asked them to eat oats (*yava*) (Ap.i.301; Ap­a.i.103f.; cf. Ud­a.265). It was for this reason the Buddha was condemned to eat *yava* during three months at Verañjā.

A road led from Verañjā to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madhurā</span>, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (AN.ii.57f) contains a sermon preached by the Buddha to a large number of people while he rested by the roadside. There was evidently frequent intercourse between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> and Verañjā, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verañjakasutta</span> (*q.v.*) was preached to some Brahmins who visited the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, whither they had gone on business. The books also record (AN.iv.198f) a visit paid by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asura</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pahārāda</span> to the Buddha at Verañjā. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vālodakajātaka</span> (Ja 183, *q.v.*) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Sukajātaka</span> (Ja 430, *q.v.*) were preached soon after the Buddha’s return from Verañjā.
