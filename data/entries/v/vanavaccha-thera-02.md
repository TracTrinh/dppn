# Vanavaccha Thera 02

## English source

Vanavaccha Thera 02. The son of a rich Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>; he joined the Saṅgha, impressed by the majesty of the Buddha’s visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>. Soon after, he attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship and, devoted to detachment, dwelt in the woods hence his name. When he went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> his kinsmen asked him to live near them, but he said he preferred the lonely life of the forest (Thag.113).

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span> he was a labourer, and, having committed a crime, while fleeing from justice he saw a Bodhi-tree. Pleased with the look of the tree, he gathered masses of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">asoka</span>-flowers and heaped them up round the tree. When his pursuers reached him, he remained as he was, looking at them, with no hatred in his heart. They hurled him into a precipice, and he died with the thought of the Bodhi-tree in his heart. Three <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> ago he was a king named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Santusita</span> (Thag­a.i.222). He is perhaps identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tambapupphiya</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>. Ap.i.176.
