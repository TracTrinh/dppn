# Vessabhū 01

## English source

Vessabhū 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessabhū Buddha</span>. The twenty-first of the twenty-four Buddhas. He was born in the pleasance of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anoma</span> (Commentary, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anūpama</span>), his father being the *Khattiya* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppatita</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Supatita</span>) and his mother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasavatī</span>.

On the day of his birth he roared “like a bull” a shout of triumph, hence his name (*vasabhanādahetuttā*). (But Mhv­ṭ. 63 gives another explanation: *hīnaṁ janānaṁ abhibhūto maggena abhibhavitakilesahīno ti vā*, one who has overcome inferior people through the Path, or one whose defilements have been overcome and are dimished. Divy.333 calls him *Viśvabhū*).

For six thousand years he lived in the household in three palaces: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ruci</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suruci</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaḍḍhana</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rativaḍḍhana</span>); his wife was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sucittā</span>, and their son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppabuddha</span>. He left home in a golden palanquin, practiced austerities for six months, was given milk-rice by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirivaḍḍhanā</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sucittanigama</span>, and grass for his seat by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Narinda</span>, and attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> under a *sāla* tree. He preached his first sermon at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurārāma</span> to his brothers, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span>, who became his chief disciples. Among women his chief disciples were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dāmā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samālā</span>, his constant attendant <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upasanta</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upasannaka</span>), his chief lay patrons <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sotthika</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāma</span> among men, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotamī</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷigotamī</span>) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span> among women. He was sixty cubits in height and lived for sixty thousand years. He died at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemārāma</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Usabhavatī</span> and his relics were scattered. The Bodhisatta was King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudassana</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhavatī</span> (Bv.xxii.1ff.; Bv­a.205ff.; DN.ii.5.; Ja.i.41). Vessabhū Buddha kept the *uposatha* once in every six years. Dhp­a.iii.236.
