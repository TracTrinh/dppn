# Vepacitti

## English source

Vepacitti. An <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asura</span> chieftain, who was present with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Namuci</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>) at the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Samayasutta</span> (DN.ii.259). It is said that among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhu</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pahārada</span> were the chiefs, e.g., AN­a.ii.758, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> being the highest (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sabbajeṭṭhaka</span>, SN­a.i.263).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> was the friend of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhu</span>, and when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhu</span> seized <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candimā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suriya</span> and these invoked the power of the Buddha, it was to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhu</span> fled for comfort (SN.i.50, 51). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span> being once defeated in a fight with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, the latter took <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> prisoner, and brought him, bound hand and foot, to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhammā</span> Hall. There <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> reviled and railed at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> with scurrilous words, both on entering and on leaving the hall, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> remained silent, and, when questioned by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātali</span>, said it was not proper for him to bandy words with a fool. SN.i.221f.; cf. SN.iv.201, according to which his bondage caused him no inconvenience so long as he remained with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, but the moment he experienced the wish to rejoin the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span>, he felt himself bound. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span>’s capture is referred to in Thag.vs.749.

On another occasion <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> suggested that victory should be given to him or to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, according to their excellence in speech. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> agreed to this, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span>, as the older god, was asked to speak a verse. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> spoke another, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> applauding. Several verses were spoken by each, and both <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span> decided in favour of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, because <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span>’s verses belonged, they said, to the sphere of violence, while those of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> belonged to one of concord and harmony (SN.i.222f). Once, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> was revolving in his mind the thought that he should not betray even his enemy, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> read his thoughts and came up to him. “Stop,” said <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, “you are my prisoner”; but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> reminded him of his thought, and was allowed to go free (SN.i.225).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (SN­a.i.266) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span>’s original name was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambara</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambara</span> refused to give to the seers who visited him a pledge that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span> would not harm them, the seers cursed him, and from that time onwards he slept badly and was plagued by nightmares. This so deranged his mind (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cittaṁ vepati</span>) that he came to be called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> (“Crazy nerve”). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> lay ill of this disease, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> visited him and offered to cure him if he would teach him <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambara</span>’s magic art. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> consulted the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span>, and, as they were unwilling, he refused <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s offer, warning him that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambara</span>, having practised magic, was suffering in purgatory and that he should avoid a similar fate (SN.i.238f).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> explains that, if <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> had taught him the art, it was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s intention to take <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> to the seers and persuade them to forgive him (SN­a.i.272). This episode seems to contradict <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>’s previous statement that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambara</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> were identical. Perhaps, as Mrs. Rhys Davids suggests (KS.i.305, n.4), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambara</span> was the name of an office rather than that of a person.

Mention is made (SN.i.226) of a visit once paid by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> to a company of seers dwelling in a forest hut. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span>, in his buskins, his sword hanging at his side and his state canopy borne over his head, entered by the main gate, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, in all humility, used the side gate. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> explains (SN­a.i.265) the strange relations of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> by saying that they were father- and son-in-law, and that they were sometimes at war with each other; sometimes, however, they lived in concord. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp­a.i.278; cf. Ja.i.205f) gives the story of the romantic marriage of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span>’s daughter, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kathāvatthu</span>, other members of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span>’s family appear to have intermarried with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kathāvatthu</span> Commentary says that a troop of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span>, belonging to the retinue of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span>, was once freed from the fourfold plane of misery and was taken up among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>. See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Points of Controversy</span>, p. 211.

The Sanskrit texts call him Vemacitra or Vemacitrī, e.g., Divy., pp. 126, 148; Mvu.iii.138,254.
