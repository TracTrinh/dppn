# Vasabha 02

## English source

Vasabha 02. King of Ceylon (127-171 CE.). He was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lambakaṇṇa</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarapassa</span> and served under his uncle, the general of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subha</span>. As it was declared by the soothsayers that one named Vasabha would be king, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subha</span> ordered the slaughter of all bearing that name and Vasabha’s uncle took him to the court to surrender him. But the general’s wife, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Potthā</span>, gave her husband betel without lime to take with him, and, on the way to the palace, Vasabha was sent back to fetch the lime. There <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Potthā</span> told him of the plot against his life, gave him one thousand pieces and helped him to escape. When his plans were ready, he fought against <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subha</span>, killed both him and his uncle in battle and became king. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Potthā</span> was made his queen. Soothsayers told him that he would live only twelve years, and, after consultation with the monks, Vasabha did many acts of merit in order to prolong his life; he reigned for forty-four years. Among the buildings erected by him were the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Valligotta</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurārāma</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mucela</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span>. He also built twelve tanks and raised the wall of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>. His son and successor was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅkanāsikatissa</span>. For details of Vasabha’s reign and works, see Dpv.xxii.1ff. and Mhv.xxxv.59ff.

It is said (DN­a.ii.635) that once Vasabha listened to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Dīghabhāṇaka</span> monks reciting the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sudassanasutta</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambalaṭṭhika</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pāsāda</span>, near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span>, and applauded the Buddha’s statement contained in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> that all things are transient. On another occasion he went to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Katthakasāla</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pariveṇa</span> to worship an Elder named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Saṭṭhivassa</span> (this may be only a descriptive title), but as he approached the door of his cell, he heard the Elder’s groans of pain, and, disappointed that he had not yet developed the power of suppressing pain even after sixty years of monastic life, the king turned away. The Elder was told of this by his attendant, and, putting forth great effort, attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship; he thereupon sent for the king. The king lay at full length on the ground and worshipped him, saying, “It is not your <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship I worship, but the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sīla</span> you observed as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">puthujjana</span>.” (DN­a.i.291)

Another story is related of Vasabha, of how once, in order to test a monk, he sat near him and began to crush a jujube fruit. The monk’s mouth watered, and Vasabha knew that he was not an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> (MN­a.ii.869).

Once when Vasabha’s queen was ill, she was cured by medicines suggested by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Paduma</span> Thera. Vin-a.ii.471.
