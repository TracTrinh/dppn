# Viḍūḍabha

## English source

Viḍūḍabha. Son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsabhakhattiyā</span>. On the birth of Viḍūḍabha, the king, glad at having a son, sent word to his own grandmother asking her to choose a name. The minister who delivered the message was deaf, and when the grandmother spoke of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsabhakhattiyā</span> as being dear to the king, mistook “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vallabha</span>” for “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">viḍūḍabha</span>,” and, thinking that this was an old family name, bestowed it on the prince. When the boy was quite young, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> conferred on him the rank of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">senāpati</span>, thinking that this would please the Buddha. It was for the same reason he married <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsabhakhattiyā</span>; both in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piyajātikasutta</span> (MN.ii.110) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇṇakatthalasuttas</span> (MN.ii.127) Viḍūḍabha is spoken of as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">senāpati</span>.

When Viḍūḍabha was seven years old, he wished to visit his maternal grandparents, hoping to be given presents, like his companions by theirs, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsabhakhattiyā</span> persuaded him against this, telling him that they lived too far away. But he continued to express this desire, and when he reached the age of sixteen she consented to his going. Thereupon, accompanied by a large retinue, he set out for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> sent all the younger princes away, there being thus none to pay obeisance to him in answer to his salute, the remaining ones being older than he. He was shown every hospitality and stayed for several days. On the day of his departure, one of his retinue overheard a contemptuous remark passed by a slave woman who was washing, with milk and water, the seat on which Viḍūḍabha had sat. This was reported to him, and, having discovered the deceit which had been practiced on his father, he vowed vengeance on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> cut off all honours from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsabhakhattiyā</span> and her son, but restored them later, at the Buddha’s suggestion.

After <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>’s death, which was brought about by the treachery of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha Kārāyaṇa</span> in making Viḍūḍabha king (for details see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>), Viḍūḍabha remembered his oath, and set out with a large army for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>. The Buddha, aware of this, stood under a tree, with scanty shade, just within the boundaries of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> kingdom. On the boundary was a banyan which gave deep shade. Viḍūḍabha, seeing the Buddha, asked him to sit under the banyan. “Be not worried,” said the Buddha, “the shade of my kinsmen keeps me cool.” Viḍūḍabha understood and returned home with his army. This exposure to the sun gave the Buddha a headache which lasted throughout his life (Ud­a.265; Ap.i.300).

Three times he marched against the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> and three times he saw the Buddha under the same tree and turned back. The fourth time the Buddha knew that the fate of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> could not be averted and remained away. In a previous existence they had conspired and thrown poison into a river.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> went armed into the battle, but not wishing to kill, they shot their arrows into Viḍūḍabha’s ranks without killing anyone. On this being brought to Viḍūḍabha’s notice, he gave orders that all the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span>, with the exception of the followers of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span>, should be slain. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> stood their ground, some with blades of grass and some with reeds. These were spared, and came to be known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tiṇasākiyā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Naḷasākiyā</span> respectively. According to Chinese records, Viḍūḍabha took five hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> maidens into his harem, but they refused to submit to him and abused him and his family. He ordered them to be killed, their hands and feet to be cut off, and their bodies thrown into a ditch. The Buddha sent a monk to preach to them, and they were reborn after death in heaven. Sakra collected their bones and burnt them (Beal, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Romantic Legend of the Buddha</span> ii.11f.). The eleventh <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pallava</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Avadānakalpalatā</span> has a similar story. Viḍūḍabha killed seventy-seven thousand <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> and stole eighty thousand boys and girls. The girls were rude to him, and he ordered their death.

The others were all killed, even down to the infants. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> was taken prisoner and went back with Viḍūḍabha, who wished him to share his meal. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> said he wished to bathe, and plunged into a lake with the idea of dying rather than eating with a slave woman’s child. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> of the lake, however, saved him and took him to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> world. That same night Viḍūḍabha pitched his camp on the dry bed of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aciravatī</span>. Some of his men lay on the banks, others on the river bed. Some of those who lay on the river bed were not guilty of sin in their past lives, while some who slept on the bank were. Ants appeared on the ground where the sinless ones lay, and they changed their sleeping places. During the night there was a sudden flood, and Viḍūḍabha and those of his retinue who slept in the river bed were washed into the sea. This account is taken from Dhp­a.i.346-9, 357-61; but see also Ja.i.133 and iv.146f., 151f.
