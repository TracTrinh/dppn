# Vaṅgīsa Thera

## English source

Vaṅgīsa Thera. He belonged to a Brahmin family and was proficient in the Vedas. He gained repute by tapping on skulls with his finger nail and telling thereby where the owners of the skull were reborn. During three years he thus gained much money. Then, in spite of the protests of his colleagues, he went to see the Buddha, who gave him the skull of an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> (according to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>, he saw <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> first and learnt from him about the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅgīsa</span> could make nothing of this and joined the Saṅgha to learn its secret. He was ordained by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhakappa</span>, and, meditating on the thirty-two constituents of the body, he won <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship. He then visited the Buddha again and praised him in various verses, full of similes and metaphors. This brought him reputation as a poet (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kāvyacitta</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kāveyyamatta</span>). Later the Buddha declared him foremost among those pre-eminent in ready expression (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">paṭibhāṇavantānaṁ</span>). His resolve to attain to this position was made in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>. AN.i.24; Dpv.iv.4; Thag­a.ii.192ff.; AN­a.i.149ff.; Dhp­a.iv.226f.; Snp­a.i.345f.; Ap.ii.495ff.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> contains numerous verses spoken by him on various occasions (Thag.1208-79; most of these are repeated at SN.i.183ff) some of them (1209-18) uttered about himself, his attempts to suppress desires excited by the sight of gaily-dressed women (Cf. SN.i.185; on one such occasion, he confessed his disaffection to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, who admonished him); others (1219-22) were self admonitions against conceit because of his facility of speech; some were spoken in praise of sermons preached by the Buddha – e.g., the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhāsitasutta</span> (1227-30), a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nibbāna</span> (1238-45), and a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> preached at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pavāraṇa</span> ceremony (1231-7). Several verses were in praise of his colleagues – e.g. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> (1231-3), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aññā Koṇḍañña</span> (1246-8), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> (1249-51). One of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅgīsa</span>’s long poems (vvs. 1263-74) is addressed to the Buddha, questioning him as to the destiny of his (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅgīsa</span>’s) teacher <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhakappa</span>. The Commentary (Thag­a.ii.211) explains that when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhakappa</span> died <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅgīsa</span> was absent and wished to be assured by the Buddha that his teacher had reached <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nibbāna</span>. But the poem is more than a question. It is really a eulogy of the Buddha. Another verse (1252) describes the Buddha as he sat surrounded by his monks on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaggarā</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Campā</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta</span> (SN.i.185ff.; SN­a.i.207ff) devotes one whole section to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅgīsa</span>, dealing with the incidents connected with his life and giving poems made by him on these occasions. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milinda</span> (p. 390) also contains a poem attributed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅgīsa</span> in praise of the Buddha. According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (Ap.ii.497, vs.27), he was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅgīsa</span>, both because he was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅga</span> and also because he was master of the spoken word (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vacana</span>). See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅgīsasutta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhāsitasutta</span>.
