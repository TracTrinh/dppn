# Vaṭṭagāmaṇī Abhaya

## English source

Vaṭṭagāmaṇī Abhaya. King of Ceylon (29-17 B.C.). He was the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saddhātissa</span>, and came to the throne by killing the usurper <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Rattaka</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">v.l.</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kammahārattaka</span>).

He married <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anulā</span>, wife of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khallāṭanāga</span>, and adopted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Cūḷika</span> as his own son; because of this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṭṭagāmaṇī</span> came to be known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pitirājā</span> (this name occurs several times in the Commentaries – e.g., Vibh­a. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">passim</span>, see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pitirājā</span>).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṭṭagāmaṇī</span> had a second wife, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Somadevī</span>, and also a son of his own, called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Coranāga</span>. In the fifth month of his reign a Brahmin, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span>, rose against him, but was defeated by seven <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Damiḷas</span> who landed at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Tittha</span>. After that, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Damiḷas</span> waged war against the king and defeated him at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kolambālaka</span>. It was a remark made by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nigaṇṭha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Giri</span> to Vaṭṭagāminī, as he fled from the battle, that led later to the establishment of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). The king hid in the forest in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessagiri</span> and was rescued by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kupikkala</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Tissa</span>, who gave him over to the care of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tanasīva</span>. In his flight he left <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Somadevī</span> behind, and she was captured by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Damiḷas</span>.

For fourteen years Vaṭṭagāmaṇī and his queen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anulā</span> lived under the protection of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tanasīva</span>, and, during this time, five <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Damiḷas</span> ruled in succession at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>; they were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulahattha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Panayamāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pilayamāra</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dāṭhika</span>.

After a time, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anulā</span> quarrelled with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tanasīva</span>’s wife, and the king, in his resentment, killed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tanasīva</span>. Later, when he also killed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapisīsa</span>, his ministers left him in disgust, but were persuaded by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Tissa</span> to return. When his preparations were complete, the king attacked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dāṭhika</span>, slew him, and took the throne. He then founded <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagirivihāra</span> and recovered <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Somadevī</span>. He also built the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Silāsobbhakaṇḍakacetiya</span>. He had seven ministers who themselves built several <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāras</span>; among them <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mūla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāliya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pabbata</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> are mentioned by name.

It was in the reign of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṭṭagāmaṇī</span> that the Buddhist Canon and its Commentaries were first reduced to writing in Ceylon, according to tradition, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ālokavihāra</span>. For details of Vaṭṭagāmaṇī’s reign see Dpv.xx.14ff.; Mhv.xxxiii.34ff. The foundation of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagirivihāra</span> formed the beginning of dissensions in the ranks of the monks (Cv.lxxiii.18). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṭṭagāmaṇī</span> was, however, regarded by later generations as a great protector of the faith (Cv.lxxxii.23). Various monasteries, chiefly rock temples, are traditionally ascribed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṭṭagāmaṇī</span>, and said to have been built by him during his exile; among these is the modern Dambulla <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Vaṁsa</span> calls him the founder of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhavelavihāra</span>. Cv.c.229.
