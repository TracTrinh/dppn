# Vajjī

## English source

Vajjī, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjīs</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjians</span>. The name of a country and of its people. It was one of the sixteen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janapadas</span>. The inhabitants appear to have consisted of several confederate clans of whom the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videhā</span> were the chief. A passage in the Commentaries (e.g., DN­a.ii.519) – which states that among those responsible for the administration of justice in the Vajjī country (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span>) were the *Aṭṭhakulakā* – has given rise to the conjecture that *Aṭṭhakulakā* meant heads of eight clans composing the Vajjī confederacy. There is no other evidence regarding the number of the clans. The *Aṭṭhakulakā* were probably a judicial committee.

As time went on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span> became the most powerful of these clans (*Licchavī Vajjiraṭṭhavāsīhi pasatthā*) (e.g., MN­a.i.394), and the names Vajjī and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span> were often synonymous. See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span>; in the *Trikandaseṣa*, quoted by Cunningham (*Ancient Geography of India*. 509), *Licchavī*, *Vaideha* and *Tirabhukti* were synonymous. In one passage (AN.iii.76) the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span>, seeing that a band of young <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> who had been out hunting were gathered round the Buddha, is represented as saying, “These <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> will yet become Vajjians” (*bhavissanti Vajjī*). This probably only means that there was great hope of these young men becoming true Vajjians, practising the seven conditions of welfare taught by the Buddha, conditions which ensured their prosperity. But see GS.iii.62, n.1 and 3.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> was the capital of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span>s. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>, both <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span> were republics, though <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span> had earlier been a kingdom under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janaka</span>.

In the time of the Buddha, and even up to his death, the Vajjians were a very prosperous and happy community. The Buddha attributed this to the fact that they practiced the seven conditions of welfare taught to them by himself in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sārandada</span> *cetiya*. The details of this teaching, and various other matters connected with the Vajjī, are given under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span>. But soon after the Buddha’s death (three years after the Buddha’s last visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, according to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>, DN­a.ii.522), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, with the help of his minister <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span>, sowed dissension among the Vajjians and conquered their territory.

The Buddha travelled several times through the Vajjī country, the usual route being through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malla</span>, Vajjī, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, and thus back (See, e.g., SN.v.348), and he preached to the people, mostly in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kūṭāgārasālā</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>. Among other places besides <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> visited by the Buddha, are mentioned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkācelā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṭigāma</span> (see, e.g., Ja.ii.232, where it is called a village of the Vajjians, on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span>), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nādikā</span> (in which were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Giñjakāvasatha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gosiṅgasālavana</span>), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Beluvagāma</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvagāma</span>), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaṇḍagāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhogagāma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthigāma</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pubbavijjhana</span>, the birthplace of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span>, is also mentioned as a village of the Vajjians (SN.iv.59). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaggumudā</span> river flowed through Vajjī territory (Ud.iii.3).

In one context (Ud­a., p. 382) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapāla</span> describes <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> as *Vajjirājā*. This is probably a mistake, for nowhere is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>, who was king of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vatsas</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṁsas</span>), called the king of the Vajjīs. The Vajjī are mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāradakassapajātaka</span> (Ja 544). It is significant that the first great schism in the Buddhist Saṅgha arose in Vajjī, when the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjiputtakā</span> brought forward their Ten Points. Even during the Buddha’s lifetime some monks of Vajjī joined <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> (Vin.ii.199f).

According to Xuanzang (Beal-Xuan: op.cit., 77), who visited it, the Vajjī (Vṛji) country was broad from east to west and narrow from north to south. The people of the neighbouring countries were called *Saṁvajji*, or United Vajjīs. For details see Cunningham, *Ancient Geography of India*. 512ff. The Commentaries contain a mythical account of the origin of the name Vajjī. See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span>.
