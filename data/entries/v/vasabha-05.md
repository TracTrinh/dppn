# Vasabha 05

## English source

Vasabha 05. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vasabha Thera</span>. He belonged to a family of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">rājā</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> and joined the Saṅgha when the Buddha visited that town, winning <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship in due course. According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> verses quoted, he was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> and was ordained under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> at the age of seven. Out of compassion for his patrons, he enjoyed what he received from them; the common minded thereupon deemed him self-indulgent. Near him lived a fraudulent monk who deceived the people by pretending to live the simple life and was greatly honoured by them. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, discerning this, visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vasabha</span> and questioned him concerning the ways of an impostor. The Elder replied in two verses (Thag.139-40), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> then warned the impostor and departed.

In the past, when the world was without a Buddha, Vasabha was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jaṭila</span> named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span> on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samagga</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pabbata</span>, with a retinue of fourteen thousand. Seeing no one deserving of his worship, he made a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span> of sand on the bank of the River <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadikā</span>, in the name of the Buddhas, gilded it and offered it his homage. Eighty times he was king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> and three hundred times king of men (Thag­a.i.257ff). He is evidently to be identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puliṇathūpiya</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>. Ap.ii.437f.
