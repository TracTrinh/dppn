# Vīrakajātaka

## English source

Vīrakajātaka (Ja 204). The Bodhisatta was once born as a marsh crow, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vīraka</span>, and lived near a pool. There was a drought in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span>, and a crow, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saviṭṭhaka</span>, finding no food, went with his wife to where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vīraka</span> lived, and, becoming his servant, ate of the fishes which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vīraka</span> caught in the pool. Later, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saviṭṭhaka</span>, in spite of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vīraka</span>’s warning, tried to catch fish himself and was drowned.

The story was told in reference to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>’s attempt to imitate the Buddha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> is identified with the crow <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saviṭṭhaka</span>. Ja.ii.148f.; quoted at Dhp­a.i.122.
