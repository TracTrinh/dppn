# Vejayanta 01

## English source

Vejayanta 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vejayantapāsāda</span>. A *pāsāda* belonging to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> to discover if he had fully understood the Buddha’s teaching in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Taṇhāsaṅkhayasutta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> tried to evade his questions by showing him this palace. It has one hundred towers, each seven stories high, with seven nymphs in each storey, waited on by seven attendants. The palace appeared in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> on the day of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s decisive victory over the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> allowed himself to be shown round, and then, with his big toe, he made the palace quake and rock. MN.i.252f.; cf. Thag. 1196f.; Thag­a.ii.184. The palace was also made to rock by the novice <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅgharakkhita</span> (*q.v.*) on the day he joined the Saṅgha (DN­a.ii.558).

The palace is one thousand leagues high, and is so-called because it arose in the hour of victory (Ja.i.203). It is decked with banners, each three hundred leagues long, banners of gold on jewelled staffs and *vice versa*; and the whole palace is built of the seven precious substances. It arose as the result of the rest house built by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, in his birth as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magha</span>, for the use of the multitude (Dhp­a.i.273; cf. DN­a.iii.698). When the Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> was in the palace with his crimson footed (*kakuṭapādiniyo*) nymphs and came forward with them to greet him. The nymphs had given oil for the massaging of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>’s feet, hence the colour of their own feet. Snp­a.i.274.

When King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sādhīna</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span> went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, he lived, according to human computation, seven hundred years in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vejayanta</span> (Ja.iv.357).

The Vejayantapāsāda is illustrated on the Bharhut Tope. Cunningham, Bharhut Tope, p. 137.
