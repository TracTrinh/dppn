# Vesālī

## English source

Vesālī. A city, capital of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>. The Buddha visited it in the fifth year after the Awakening, and spent the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vassa</span> there (Bv­a., p. 3). The Commentaries give detailed descriptions of the circumstances of this visit. Khp-a.160ff. = Snp­a.i.278; Dhp­a.iii.436ff.; cp. Mvu.i.253ff.

Vesālī was inhabited by seven thousand and seven <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">rājās</span>, each of whom had large retinues, many palaces and pleasure parks. There came a shortage in the food supply owing to drought, and people died in large numbers. The smell of decaying bodies attracted evil spirits, and many inhabitants were attacked by intestinal disease. The people complained to the ruling prince, and he convoked a general assembly, where it was decided, after much discussion, to invite the Buddha to their city. As the Buddha was then at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī Mahāli</span>, friend of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> and son of the chaplain of Vesālī, was sent to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> with a request that he should persuade the Buddha to go to Vesālī. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> referred him to the Buddha himself, who, after listening to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāli</span>’s story, agreed to go. The Buddha started on the journey with five hundred monks. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> decorated the route from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span>, a distance of five leagues, and provided all comforts on the way. He accompanied the Buddha, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span> was reached in five days. Boats, decked with great splendour, were ready for the Buddha and his monks, and we are told that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> followed the Buddha into the water up to his neck. The Buddha was received on the opposite bank by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>, with even greater honour than <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> had shown him. As soon as the Buddha set foot in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjī</span> territory, there was a thunderstorm and rain fell in torrents. The distance from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span> to Vesālī was three leagues; as the Buddha approached Vesālī, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> came to greet him, and, at the sight of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, all the evil spirits fled in fear. In the evening the Buddha taught <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratanasutta</span>, and ordered that it should be recited within the three walls of the city, the round of the city being made with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span> princes. This <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> did during the three watches of the night, and all the pestilences of the citizens disappeared. The Buddha himself recited the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratanasutta</span> to the assembled people, and eighty-four thousand beings were converted. After repeating this for seven consecutive days, the Buddha left Vesālī (according to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> account the Buddha stayed only seven days in Vesālī; Khp­a. says two weeks). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> accompanied him to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span> with redoubled honours, and, in the river itself, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> vied with each other in paying him honour. On the farther bank, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> awaited his arrival and conducted him back to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. On his return there, the Buddha recited the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkhajātaka</span> (Ja 442).

It was probably during this visit of the Buddha to Vesālī that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> died (see Thīg­a., p. 141; AN­a.i.186). It was during this visit of the Buddha to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t18">tadā</span>) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Pajāpatī Gotamī</span> first asked his permission to join the Saṅgha, but her request was refused (AN­a.i.186). According to one account, the Buddha went through the air to visit his dying father and to preach to him, thereby enabling him to attain <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship before his death.

It is not possible to know how many visits were paid by the Buddha to Vesālī, but the books would lead us to infer that they were several. Various Vinaya rules are mentioned as having been laid down at Vesālī, see, e.g., Vin.i.238, 287f; ii.118, 119-27. The visit mentioned in the last context seems to have been a long one; it was on this occasion that the Buddha ordered the monks to turn their bowls down upon the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī Vaḍḍha</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). For other Vinaya rules laid down at Vesālī, see also Vin.ii.159f.; iii. and iv. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t18">passim</span>.

It was during a stay in Vesālī, whither he had gone from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Pajāpatī Gotamī</span> followed the Buddha with five hundred other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> women, and, with the help of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>’s intervention, obtained permission for women to enter the Saṅgha under certain conditions. Vin.ii.253ff.; see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Pajāpatī Gotamī</span>.

The books describe (e.g., DN.ii.95ff) at some length the Buddha’s last visit to Vesālī on his way to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>. On the last day of this visit, after his meal, he went with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cāpāla</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span> for his siesta, and, in the course of their conversation, he spoke to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> of the beauties of Vesālī: of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotamaka</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sattambaka</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bahuputta</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span>, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sārandada</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span>. Cf. Mvu.i.300, where a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kapinayhacetiya</span> is also mentioned. All these were once shrines dedicated to various local deities, but after the Buddha’s visit to Vesālī, they were converted into places of Buddhist worship. Other monasteries are also mentioned, in or near Vesālī e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭikārāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vālikārāma</span>.

The Buddha generally stayed at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kūṭāgārasālā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) during his visits to Vesālī, but it appears that he sometimes lived at these different shrines (See DN.ii.118). During his last visit to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cāpāla</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span> he decided to die within three months, and informed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> and, later, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, of his decision. The next day he left Vesālī for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaṇḍagāma</span>, after taking one last look at the city, “turning his whole body round, like an elephant” (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nāgāpalokitaṁ apaloketvā</span>) (DN.ii.122). The rainy season which preceded this, the Buddha spent at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Beluvagāma</span>, a village near Vesālī, while the monks stayed in and around Vesālī. On the day before he entered into the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vassa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambapālī</span> invited the Buddha and the monks to a meal, at the conclusion of which she gave her <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambavana</span> for the use of the Saṅgha (DN.ii.98; but see Dial.ii.102, n.1).

Vesālī was a stronghold of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span>, and it is said that of the forty-two rainy seasons of the latter part of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vīra</span>’s ascetic life, he passed twelve at Vesālī. Jacobi: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Jaina Sūtras</span> (SBE.) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kalpasūtra</span>, sect. 122; Vesālī was also the residence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kandaramasuka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭikaputta</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). Among eminent followers of the Buddha who lived in Vesālī, special mention is made of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span> (chief of those who gave pleasant gifts), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṅgiyāni</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāraṇapāli</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāseṭṭha</span> (AN.iv.258), and the various <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>.

The Buddha’s presence in Vesālī was a source of discomfort to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span>, and we find mention of various devices resorted to by them to prevent their followers from coming under the influence of the Buddha (see, e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīha</span>).

At the time of the Buddha, Vesālī was a very large city, rich and prosperous, crowded with people and with abundant food. There were seven thousand seven hundred and seven pleasure grounds and an equal number of lotus ponds. Its courtesan, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambapālī</span>, was famous for her beauty, and helped in large measure in making the city prosperous (Vin.i.268). The city had three walls, each one <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gāvuta</span> away from the other, and at three places in the walls were gates with watch towers. Ja.i.604; cf.i.389. Perhaps these three walls separated the three districts of Vaiśālī mentioned in the Tibetan <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Dulva</span> (Rockhill, p.62); Hoernle (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Uvāsagadasāo</span> Translation ii., p.4, n.8) identifies these three districts with the city proper, Kuṇḍapura and Vāṇiyagāma, respectively mentioned in the Jaina books.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (e.g., Vin-a.ii.393) that Vesālī was so-called because it was extensive (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t18">visālībhūtatā Vesālī ti uccati</span>); cf. Ud­a.184 (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t18">tikkhattuṁ visālabhūtattā</span>); and MN­a.i.259.

Outside the town, leading uninterruptedly up to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span>, was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vana</span> (DN­a.i.309) (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), a large, natural forest. Near by were other forests, such as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gosiṅgasāla</span>. (AN.v.134)

Among important <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span> preached at Vesālī are the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāli</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sīhanāda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Saccaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Saccaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tevijja</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vacchagotta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunakkhatta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratana</span>. See also AN.i.220, 276; ii.190, 200; iii.38, 49ff., 75, 142, 167, 236, 239; iv. 16, 79, 100, 179, 208, 274ff., 279ff., 308ff.; v. 86, 133, 342; SN.i.29, 112, 230; ii.267, 280; iii.68, 116; iv. 109, 210ff., 380; v. 141f, 152f, 258, 301, 320, 389, 453; DN.ii.94ff.; the subjects of these discourses are mentioned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t18">passim</span>, in their proper places; see also Dhp­a.i.263; iii.267, 279, 460, 480.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekapaṇṇajātaka</span> (Ja 149) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Telovādajātaka</span> (Ja 246) were preached at Vesālī. After the Buddha’s death a portion of his relics was enshrined in the city (DN.ii.167; Bv.xxviii.2) One hundred years later Vesālī was again the scene of interest for Buddhists, on account of the “Ten Points” raised by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjiputtakā</span>, (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), and the second Council held in connection with this dispute at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vālikārāma</span>. The city was also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="119" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Visālā</span>. (e.g., AN­a.i.47; Cv.xcix.98). There were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="120" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> living in Vesālī; these were called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="121" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Vesālā</span> (DN.ii.258).

Vesālī is identified with the present village of Basrah in the Muzafferpur district in Tirhut. See Vincent Smith, JRAS. 1907, p. 267f., and Marshall, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="122" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arch. Survey of India</span>, 1903-4, p. 74.
