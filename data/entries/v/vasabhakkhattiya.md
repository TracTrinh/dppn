# Vāsabhakkhattiyā

## English source

Vāsabhakkhattiyā. Daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> by a slave-woman named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgamuṇḍā</span> (Ja.i.133). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> asked for a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> girl in marriage, she was given to him. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> went through the pretence of eating with her in order to allay <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>’s suspicions. It is said (Ja.iv.145) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> sat down to eat with her, but that as he was about to take the first mouthful, a messenger arrived, as prearranged, and brought him an urgent letter. He, thereupon, left the food uneaten in order to read the letter, and asked Vāsabhakkhattiyā to finish her meal.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Viḍūḍabha</span> was the son of Vāsabhakkhattiyā. It was this deceit practised on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> which made <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Viḍūḍabha</span> take his revenge on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> (Dhp­a.i.345f; Ja.iv.145f).

It is said that when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> discovered Vāsabhakkhattiyā’s servile origin, he degraded both her and her son from their rank, and that they never went outside the palace (Ja.i.133f.; iv.148; see also MN.ii.110, where she is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsabhā</span>). When the Buddha heard of this, he visited the king, preached to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭṭhahārijātaka</span>, and had the queen restored to honour.
