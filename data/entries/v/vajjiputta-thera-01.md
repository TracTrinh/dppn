# Vajjiputta Thera 01

## English source

Vajjiputta Thera 01. He belonged to the family of a minister of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, and, seeing the majesty of the Buddha who visited the city, he joined the Saṅgha and lived in a wood near by. A festival took place in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, with much singing and dancing and gaiety. This distracted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjiputta</span>, and he expressed his disgust in a verse spoken in scorn of the forest life. A woodland sprite heard him and upbraided him, saying, “Though you spurn life in the forest, the wise, desiring solitude, think much of it,” and she then uttered a verse praising it. This verse, which the monk afterwards repeated, is included in Thag.vs.62. Urged on by the sprite’s words, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjiputta</span> developed insight and became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>.

Ninety-one <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> ago he had been a householder and had paid homage to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span>, with pollen from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nāga</span>-flowers. Forty-five <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> ago he was a king, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Reṇu</span>. Thag­a.i.142f. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjiputta</span>’s story is given very briefly in SN.i.201f. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjiputta</span>’s story is also given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> There he is called a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">rājā</span>, and is said to have renounced his kingdom when his turn came to rule. On the day of the festival, on the full moon day of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kattika</span>, he was filled with discontent. After his conversation with the woodland sprite, he sought the Buddha, who preached to him. He attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship at the end of the Buddha’s sermon. Dhp­a.iii.460f.; see also SN­a.i.228, where also he is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">rājā</span>. There may be some confusion between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjiputta</span> (1) and (2). He is evidently to be identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Reṇupūjaka</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>. Ap.i.146.
