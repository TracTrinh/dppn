# Vessavaṇa

## English source

Vessavaṇa. One of the names of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuvera</span>, given to him because his kingdom is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visāṇā</span> (DN.iii.201; Snp­a.i.369, etc.). He is one of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cātummahārājikā</span> and rules over the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span>, his kingdom being in the north (e.g., DN.ii.207). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āṭānāṭiyasutta</span> he is the spokesman, and he recited the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āṭānāṭiya</span>-rune for the protection of the Buddha and his followers from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> who had no faith in the Buddha. DN.iii.194; he was spokesman because “he was intimate with the Buddha, expert in conversation, well-trained” (DN­a.iii.962).

He rides in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārīvāhana</span>, which is twelve <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojanas</span> long, its seat being of coral. His retinue is composed of ten thousand crores of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span>. (Snp­a.i.379; the preacher’s seat in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> was made in the design of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārīvāhana</span>, Mhv.xxvii.29). He is a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span> and his life-span is ninety thousand years (AN­a.ii.718). The books record a conversation between him and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷukaṇṭakī Nandamātā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), when he heard her recite the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārāyaṇavagga</span> and stayed to listen. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Subhaddā</span> wished to invite the Buddha and his monks to her house in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span>, and felt doubtful about it, Vessavaṇa appeared before her and said that the Buddha would come at her invitation (AN­a.ii.483).

On another occasion (AN.iv.162; on his way to see the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>) he heard <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span> Thera preaching to the monks in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhavajālikā</span> on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkheyya</span> Mountain, near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahisavatthu</span>, and went and told <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, who visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span> and had a discussion with him.

Once when Vessavaṇa was travelling through the air, he saw <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambhūta Thera</span> wrapt in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">samādhi</span>. Vessavaṇa descended from his chariot, worshipped the Thera<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">,</span> and left behind two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> with orders to wait until the Elder should emerge from his jhāna. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> then greeted the Thera in the name of Vessavaṇa and told him they had been left to protect him. The Elder sent thanks to Vessavaṇa, but informed him, through the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span>, that the Buddha had taught his disciples to protect themselves through mindfulness, and so further protection was not needed. Vessavaṇa visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambhūta</span> on his return, and finding that the Elder had become an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>, went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> and carried the news to the Buddha. Thag­a.i.46f. Just as he encouraged the good, so he showed his resentment against the wicked; see, e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revatī</span>.

Mention is made of Vessavaṇa’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gadāvudha</span> and his mango tree, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atulamba</span>. Snp­a.i.225; the books (e,g., SN­a.i.249; Vin-a.ii.440) are careful to mention that he used his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gadāvudha</span> only while he was yet a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">puthujjana</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atulamba</span> was also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abbhantaramba</span> (see the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abbhantarajātaka</span>, Ja 281, Ja.iv.324). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span>’s abode was near that of Vessavaṇa (Snp­a.i.240).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, after death, was born seven times as one of the ministers (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t18">paricaraka</span>) of Vessavaṇa, and, while on his way with a message from Vessavaṇa to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Virūḷhaka</span>, visited the Buddha and gave him an account of a meeting of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> which Vessavaṇa had attended and during which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sanaṅkumāra</span> had spoken in praise of the Buddha and his teachings (DN.ii.206f). Vessavaṇa seems to have been worshipped by those desiring children, see, e.g., the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājadatta</span> (Thag­a.i.403). There was in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> a banyan tree dedicated as a shrine to Vessavaṇa in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍukābhaya</span> (Mhv.x.89). Vessavaṇa is mentioned as having been alive in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span>. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī</span> died, there was a great earthquake which terrified the people, but Vessavaṇa appeared and quieted their fears (Thag­a.i.149). Vessavaṇa accompanied <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> when he showed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> round <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vejayantapāsāda</span>. MN.i.253; because he was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s very intimate friend (MN­a.i.476).

As lord of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span>, it was in the power of Vessavaṇa to grant to any of them special privileges, such as the right of devouring anyone entering a particular pond, etc. See, e.g., Dhp­a.iii.74; Ja.i.128; iii.325 (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makhādevajātaka</span>, Ja 9). Sometimes, e.g., in the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avaruddhaka</span> (Dhp­a.ii.237), a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> had to serve Vessavaṇa for twelve years in order to obtain a particular boon (cf. Ja.ii.16,17, it says three years at Ja.iii.502.) Vessavaṇa sometimes employs the services of uncivilized human beings (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t18">paccantamilakkhavāsika</span>) DN­a.iii.865f. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> fear him greatly. If he is angry and looks but once, one thousand <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> are broken up and scattered “like parched peas hopping about on a hot plate” (Ja.ii.399). This was probably before he became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>.

Vessavaṇa, like <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, was not the name of a particular being, but of the holder of an office. When one Vessavaṇa died, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> chose another as his successor. The new king, on his accession, sent word to all the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span>, asking them to choose their special abodes (Ja.i.328). It was the duty of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Yakkhinīs</span> to fetch water from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anotatta</span> for Vessavaṇa’s use. Each <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Yakkhinī</span> served her turn, sometimes for four, sometimes for five months. But sometimes they died from exhaustion before the end of their term (Dhp­a.i.40; also Ja.iv.492; v.21).

Vessavaṇa’s wife was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhuñjatī</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), who, like himself, was a devoted follower of the Buddha (DN.ii.270). They had five daughters: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Latā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sajjā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pavarā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Acchimatī</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sutā</span>. For a story about them, see Vv-a.131f.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇaka</span> was Vessavaṇa’s nephew. Ja.vi.265, 326. The pleasures and luxuries enjoyed by Vessavaṇa have become proverbial. See, e.g., Vv.iv. 3, 46 (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t18">bhuñjāmi kāmakāmī rājā Vessavaṇo yathā</span>, I enjoy like the pleasure-seeking King Vessavaṇa); Mhv­ṭ. 676 (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Vessavaṇassa rājaparihārasadisaṁ</span>); cf. Ja.vi.313.

An ascetic named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kañcanapatti</span> (Ja.ii.399) is mentioned as having been the favourite of Vessavaṇa. See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>.
