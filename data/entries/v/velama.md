# Velāma

## English source

Velāma. The Bodhisatta born as the chaplain of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. He was son of the preceding chaplain, and went with the crown prince to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> to study. There, in due course, he became a famous teacher, with eighty-four thousand princes among his pupils. Later, he became chaplain to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> king. Every year the eighty-four thousand princes came to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> to pay their respects to the king, causing great suffering to the people. These complained to the king, and he asked Velāma to find a way out of the difficulty. Velāma marked out eighty-four thousand provinces for the princes, and, thereafter, they obtained their supplies from their respective dominions.

Velāma was exceedingly wealthy and wished to give alms. Therefore, turning his water jar upside down, he wished that if there were holy men in the world, the water should flow downwards. The water, however, remained in the jar. He then discovered by the same means that his gifts would be free from blame. He thereupon held great almsgivings, distributing during seven years the seven precious things and gifts of great value, pouring forth his riches as though “making into one stream the five great rivers.” A list of his gifts is found at AN.iv.393f. Velāma’s story is given in AN­a.ii.802ff.; it is referred to in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Velāmasutta</span> and in the introductory story to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khadiraṅgārajātaka</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). Velāma’s almsgiving became famous in literature as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Velāmamahāyañña</span>, e.g., MN­a.ii.616.
