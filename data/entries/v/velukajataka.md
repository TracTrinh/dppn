# Veḷukajātaka

## English source

Veḷukajātaka (Ja 43). The Bodhisatta was once at the head of five hundred hermits, one of whom had a pet viper which was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuka</span>, because it was kept in a bamboo. The Bodhisatta warned the ascetic against the snake, but his warning was unheeded. The hermit thus came to be called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷukapitā</span>. One day the hermits went into the forest and were away for a few days, and when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷukapitā</span> touched the viper on his return, the animal, hungry and angry, bit him, and he fell down dead.

The story was told in reference to a headstrong monk who is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷukapitā</span>. Ja.i.245f.
