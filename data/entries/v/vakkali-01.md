# Vakkali 01

## English source

Vakkali 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vakkali Thera</span>. He belonged to a Brahmin family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> and became proficient in the three Vedas. After he once saw the Buddha he could never tire of looking at him, and followed him about. In order to be closer to him he became a monk, and spent all his time, apart from meals and bathing, in contemplating the Buddha’s person. One day the Buddha said to him, “The sight of my foul body is useless; he who sees the Dhamma, he it is that sees me” (*yo kho dhammaṁ passati so maṁ passati; yo maṁ passati so dhammaṁ passati*) (Cp. Iti.92). But even then <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vakkali</span> would not leave the Buddha till, on the last day of the rains, the Buddha commanded him to depart. Greatly grieved, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vakkali</span> sought the precipices of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span>. The Buddha, aware of this, appeared before him and uttered a stanza; then stretching out his hand, he said: “Come, monk.” Filled with joy, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vakkali</span> rose in the air pondering on the Buddha’s words and realized *Arahant*-ship. AN­a.i.140f.; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> account (Ap.ii.465f.) is similar. It says that the Buddha spoke to him from the foot of the rock. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vakkali</span> jumped down to meet the Buddha, a depth of many cubits, but he alighted unhurt. It was on this occasion that the Buddha declared his eminence among those of implicit faith; also Dhp­a.iv.118f. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> reports three verses uttered by the Buddha in which he assures <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vakkali</span> that he will help him and look after him.

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> Commentary (Thag­a.i.420), when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vakkali</span> was dismissed by the Buddha he lived on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span>, practising meditation, but could not attain insight because of his emotional nature (*saddhā*). The Buddha then gave him a special exercise, but neither could he achieve this, and, from lack of food, he suffered from cramp. The Buddha visited him and uttered a verse to encourage him. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vakkali</span> spoke four verses (Thag.350-4) in reply, and, conjuring up insight, won *Arahant*-ship. Later, in the assembly of the monks, the Buddha declared him foremost among those of implicit faith (*saddhādhimuttānaṁ*) (cp. AN.i.25; also Divy.49 and Vibh­a.276; Vsm.i.129). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārāyaṇavagga</span> (Snp. vs. 1146) the Buddha is represented as holding <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vakkali</span> up to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṅgiya</span> as an example of one who won emancipation through faith.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta</span> account (SN.iii.119ff.; SN­a.ii.229) gives more details and differs in some respects from the above. There, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vakkali</span> fell ill while on his way to visit the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, and was carried in a litter to a potter’s shed in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. There, at his request, the Buddha visited him and comforted him. He questioned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vakkali</span>, who assured him that he had no cause to reprove himself with regard to morals (*sīlato*); his only worry was that he had not been able to see the Buddha earlier. The Buddha told him that seeing the Dhamma was equivalent to seeing him, and because <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vakkali</span> had realized the Dhamma, there would be no hereafter for him. After the Buddha had left, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vakkali</span> asked his attendants to take him to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷasilā</span> on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isigili</span>. The Buddha was on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span> and was told by two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> that Vakkali was about to “obtain release.” The Buddha sent word to him: “Fear not, Vakkali, your dying will not be evil.” Vakkali rose from his bed to receive the Buddha’s message, and sending word to the Buddha that he had no desire or love for the body or the other *khandhas*, he drew a knife and killed himself. The Buddha went to see his body, and declared that he had obtained *Nibbāna* and that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>’s attempt to find the consciousness of Vakkali would prove useless.

The Commentary adds that Vakkali was conceited and blind to his remaining faults. He thought he was a *khīṇāsava*, and that he might rid himself of bodily pains by death. However, the stab with the knife caused him such pain that at the moment of dying he realized his *puthujjana* state, and, putting forth great effort, attained *Arahant*-ship.

His resolve to become chief among the *saddhādhimuttas* had been made in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>, when he saw a monk also named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vakkali</span> similarly honoured by the Buddha. Ap.ii.465f.; AN­a.i.140.
