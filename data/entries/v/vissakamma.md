# Vissakamma

## English source

Vissakamma, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissukamma</span>. A *Deva*, inhabitant of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>. He is the chief architect, designer and decorator among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> asks for his services whenever necessary. Thus he was ordered to build the palace called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhamma</span> for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sudassana</span> (DN.ii.180) and another for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Panāda</span> (Ja.iv.323; DN­a.iii.856).

He also built the hermitages for the Bodhisatta in various births – e.g., as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumedha</span> (Ja.i.7), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuddālapaṇḍita</span> (Ja.i.314), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthipāla</span> (Ja.iv.489), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ayoghara</span> (Ja.iv.499), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotipāla</span> (Ja.v.132), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sutasoma</span> (Ja.v.190), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Temiya</span> (Ja.vi.21, 29), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span> (Ja.vi.519f). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span> also built the hermitage for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dukūlaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārikā</span> (Ja.vi.72).

On the day that the Buddha renounced the world, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span> in the guise of a shampooer to bathe him and clothe him in his royal ornaments (Ja.i.60; Dhp­a.i.70; Bv­a.232; he also constructed ponds in which the prince might bathe, AN­a.i.379); he also sent him to adorn <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Temiya</span> on the day he left the kingdom (Ja.vi.12).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span> erected the jewelled pavilion, twelve leagues in compass, under the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṇḍamba</span>, where the Buddha performed the Twin Miracle and built the three stairways of jewels, silver and gold, used by the Buddha in his descent from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span> (Ja.iv.265f). He built the pavilions in which the Buddha and five hundred *Arahants* travelled to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uggapura</span>, at the invitation of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Subhaddā</span> (Dhp­a.iii.470; and again for the journey to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunāpuranta</span>, MN­a.ii.1017).

When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> deposited his share of the Buddha’s relics in a *Thūpa*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> ordered <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span> to construct around the *Thūpa* a *vālasaṅghātayanta* (fiery machine) to prevent anyone from approaching the relics. Later, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammāsoka</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piyadassī</span>) wished to obtain these relics for his *vihāra*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span> appeared before him in the guise of a village youth and, by shooting an arrow at the controlling screw of the machine, stopped its revolutions (DN­a.ii.613, 614).

He constructed the jewelled pavilion in which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇuttara</span> placed the relics he brought from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> world till the time came for them to be deposited in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> (Mhv.xxxi.76), and on the day of their enshrinement, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span>, acting on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s orders, decorated the whole of Ceylon (Mhv.xxxi.34). He also provided the bricks used in the construction of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> (Mhv.xxviii.8). Sometimes he would enter into a workman’s body and inspire him with ideas – e.g., in designing the form of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> (Mhv.xxx.11). He was also responsible for the construction of the golden vase in which the branch of the Bodhi-tree was conveyed to Ceylon (Mhv.xviii.24).

As in the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātali</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span> is evidently the name of an office and not a personal name. Thus, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Surucijātaka</span> (Ja 489, Ja.iv. 325), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span> is mentioned as a previous birth of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, while, according to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span>, the architect who helped <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magha</span> and his companions in their good works, was reborn as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span>. Dhp­a.i.272. The story given regarding <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span> in Snp­a.i.233, evidently refers to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaṇhajātaka</span>, Ja 469. The *Deva* who accompanied <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> in the guise of a dog in that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātali</span> and not <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span>. See Viśvakarma in Hopkins’ *Epic Mythology*.
