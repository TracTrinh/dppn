# Vassakāra

## English source

Vassakāra. A Brahmin, chief minister of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>. He and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunidha</span> were in charge of the fortifications of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaligāma</span>, built against the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjī</span>. Vin.i.228; Ud.viii.6; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha</span> account, DN.ii.72ff. omits <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunidha</span>. The Vinaya account omits <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span>’s questions to the Buddha; cf. AN­a.ii.705ff.

At <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>’s suggestion, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> visited the Buddha to discover, indirectly, whether, in the Buddha’s view, there were any chances of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> conquering the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjians</span> in battle. The Buddha said that as long as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjians</span> practised the seven conditions of prosperity which he had taught them at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sārandadacetiya</span>, they would prosper rather than decline, and this gave <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> the idea that the downfall of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjians</span> could be brought about by diplomacy (*upalāpana*) or disunion (*mithubheda*). He thereupon conspired with the king (DN.A.ii.522ff) and, by agreement, the latter expelled him on the charge of showing favour to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjians</span> during discussions in the assembly. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> then went to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjī</span> country, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>, all unsuspecting, welcomed him and appointed him as the teacher of their children. By means of cunning and questioning the children in secret, he made them quarrel with each other, and these quarrels soon spread to the elders. In three years the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> were completely disunited, and when the assembly drum was beaten, they failed to appear. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> then sent a message to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, who was able to capture <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> without meeting any resistance.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gopaka Moggallānasutta</span> (MN.iii.8ff), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> is represented as arriving in the middle of a conversation, which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gopaka Moggallāna</span> was holding with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, having been sent to inspect the works at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, which were in charge of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>. Having asked the subject of conversation, he inquired whether the Buddha himself or the Saṅgha had chosen a leader for the Saṅgha after the Buddha’s death. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> explains that the Buddha did not do so, that no special leader has been appointed, but that there were monks to whom they showed honour and reverence because of their virtue and insight. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> admits this as good, as does also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upananda</span>, the *Senāpati*, who is present. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> asks <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> where he lives, and is told, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> thinks this a good place for the practice of *jhāna*, and tells <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> of a conversation he once had with the Buddha regarding *jhāna*. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, remarks that all *jhānas* are not equally praiseworthy, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> takes his leave.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (MN­a.ii.854) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> knew well of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>’s residence at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span>, but that as the place was under his special protection, he wished to hear his work praised. Then follows a curious tale. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> once saw <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span> descending <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span> and remarked that he was just like a monkey. The Buddha, hearing of this, said that, unless <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> begged the Elder’s forgiveness, he would be born as a monkey in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span>, feeling sure that the Buddha’s prophecy would come true, had various fruit and other trees planted in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span>, to be of use to him as a monkey. After death he was actually reborn as a monkey and answered to the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span>.

Three conversations between the Buddha and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> are recorded in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span>, all three taking place at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span>. See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakārasuttas</span> (1) and (2), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sutasutta</span> ??. Another *Sutta*, also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> (3), repeats the conversation recorded in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha</span> regarding the possibility of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> defeating the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjians</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> had a daughter whom he wished to give in marriage to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span>, but the latter refused the proposal as he wished to join the Saṅgha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> was angry, and contrived to take his revenge even after <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span> had become a monk (Thag­a.i.240; see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span>).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (Vin-a.ii.295) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> was envious by nature, and, on discovering that a certain forest official had given tribute to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhaniya</span> (2) without the king’s special leave, he reported the man to the king and had him punished (the incident is repeated at Vin.iii.42ff). In this context we find that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> was *Mahā Matta* (? chief minister) to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> as well.
