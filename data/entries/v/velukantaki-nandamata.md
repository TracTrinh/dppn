# Veḷukaṇṭakī Nandamātā

## English source

Veḷukaṇṭakī Nandamātā, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷukaṇḍakī Nandamātā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷukaṇṭakiyā Nandamātā</span>. A lady of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷukaṇṭa</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷukaṇḍa</span>). She is mentioned as an exemplary lay woman (AN.i.88; ii.164). She founded, for the Saṅgha headed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>, an offering which the Buddha praised, because it was endowed with the six requisite qualities. See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dānasutta</span> (1).

Once she rose before dawn and sang the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārāyaṇa</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> happened to be passing over her house on his way from north to south (to see the Buddha, says Snp­a.i.369), and hearing the song, stopped at her window to praise it and to reveal his identity. She greeted him cordially, and in return for her greeting he announced to her that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> were on their way to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷukaṇṭa</span>. She, delighted with the news, made all preparations and sent word to the monastery, inviting the monks to the house. After the meal, she informed the Elders that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> had told her of their arrival. When they expressed their amazement, she told them of several other virtues possessed by her. Her only son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span> was seized by the king’s men and killed before her eyes, but she experienced no disquiet, nor did she when her husband, after his death, having been born as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Bhummadevatā</span> says the Commentary), revealed himself to her. She was guilty of no transgression of the precepts, could enter into the four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jhānas</span> at will, and had cast off the five lower fetters. The monks expressed their great admiration and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> preached to her (AN.iv.63ff).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (AN­a.ii.718; cf. Snp­a.i.370) that she was an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmī</span>, and that, when she promised to share with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> the merits she would gain by entertaining the monks, headed by the two Chief Disciples, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span>, to show his gratitude, filled her stores with rice, and these stores remained always full throughout her life. They thus became proverbial.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span> Commentary (Snp­a.i.370) states that she kept a daily fast and knew the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Piṭakas</span> by heart. It also says that, at the end of her recital of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārāyaṇa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> offered her a boon, and she asked that, as her servants were weary of carrying the harvest home from the fields, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> should allow his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> to do the work for them. To this he agreed, and his followers filled for her 1,250 store houses. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> then went to the Buddha and told him of what had happened.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp­a.i.340) mentions <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷukaṇṭakī Nandamātā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khujjattarā</span> as the chief lay women disciples of the Buddha. But in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttara</span> list of eminent lay women, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷukaṇṭakī Nandamātā</span>’s name does not occur, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khujjatarā</span> is mentioned. Mention is made of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandamātā</span>, eminent in meditation, but she is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span>. AN.i.26; cf. SN.ii.236, where the same two are mentioned; Mrs. Rhys Davids thinks that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷukaṇṭakī Nandamātā</span> is probably identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā Nandamātā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brethren</span> 4, n.1). This identification does not seem to be correct. See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā Nandamātā</span>; see also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda Kumāputta</span>.
