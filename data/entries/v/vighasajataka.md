# Vighāsajātaka

## English source

Vighāsajātaka (Ja 393). Once seven brothers of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span> village renounced the world and lived as ascetics in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mejjhārañña</span>, but they were given up to various amusements. The Bodhisatta, who was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, saw this, and, assuming the form of a parrot, visited them and sang the praises of the ascetic life. They expressed their joy at being thus praised, but the parrot went on to make them understand that their lives were useless; they were mere refuse eaters and not ascetics.

The story was related in reference to the monks mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāsādakampanasutta</span> (*q.v.*). The monks are identified with the seven ascetics. Ja.iii.310f.
