# Visākhā 05

## English source

Visākhā 05. The chief among the female lay disciples of the Buddha and declared by him to be foremost among those who ministered to the Saṅgha (*dāyikānaṁ aggā*) (AN.i.26; she is considered the ideal lay woman – e.g., AN.iv.348). Her father was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya</span>, son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meṇḍaka</span>, and her mother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanā</span>. She was born in the city of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span>. When she was seven years old, the Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span> with a large company of monks, out of compassion for the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sela</span> and others. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meṇḍaka</span> gave <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> five hundred companions, five hundred slaves, and five hundred chariots, that she might visit the Buddha. She stopped the chariots some distance away and approached the Buddha on foot. He preached to her and she became a *Sotāpanna*. For the next fortnight <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meṇḍaka</span> invited the Buddha and his monks daily to his house, where he fed them.

Later, when, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>’s request, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya</span> to live in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> accompanied her parents and lived in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span>. The messengers, sent by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> to find a suitable bride for his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇavaḍḍhana</span>, saw <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> on her way to the lake to bathe on a feast day. At that moment there was a great shower. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>’s companions ran for shelter, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> herself, walking at her usual pace, came to the place where the messengers, already greatly impressed, were awaiting her. When they asked her why she did not run to seek shelter and so preserve her clothes, she answered that she had plenty of clothes in the house, but that if she ran she might damage a limb which would be a great loss. “Unmarried girls,” she said, “are like goods awaiting sale, they must not be disfigured.” The messengers offered her a bouquet of flowers (*mālāgulaṁ*), which she accepted as a proposal of marriage, and then went on to her father’s house. The messengers followed and laid <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇavaddhana</span>’s suit before <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya</span>. The proposal was accepted and confirmed by an exchange of letters.

When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> heard of it, he offered to accompany <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇavaḍḍhana</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span>, as a mark of signal favour. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya</span> welcomed the king and his retinue, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇavaḍḍhana</span> and their followers, with all honour, attending personally to all the details of hospitality. He persuaded the king to stay with him during the rains, providing all that was necessary. According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> account (*loc.cit.*) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> superintended all the arrangements.

Five hundred goldsmiths were engaged to make the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Latāpasādhana</span> (ornament), *q.v.*, for the bride; three months passed, but it was still unfinished. The supply of firewood ran out, and orders were given that the wood of dilapidated houses should be used. This wood lasted for a fortnight, and then the storehouses containing cloths were opened, the cloths soaked in oil and used for cooking the food. The ornament was finished in four months. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> she gave bowls and robes to twenty thousand monks, also thread and needles and sewing materials; as a result of this, she received her parure in this life (Dhp­a.i.395).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya</span> gave his daughter, as dowry, five hundred carts full of money, five hundred with vessels of gold, five hundred each of silver, copper, various silks, ghee, rice husked and winnowed; also ploughs, ploughshares, and other farm implements, five hundred carts with three slave-women in each, everything being provided for them. The cattle given by him filled an enclosure three quarters of a league in length and eight rods across, standing shoulder to shoulder, and in addition to these, sixty thousand bulls and sixty thousand milk cows escaped from their stalls and joined the herd already gifted to her. In her birth as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghadāsī</span>, she gave the five products of the cow to twenty thousand monks, begging them to eat; hence the escaping of the cattle for her benefit (Dhp­a.i.397). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>’s relations continued to send her costly gifts even after her marriage. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> (ii.9) contains a story of a dispute she had with the customs officers regarding the duty they levied on one of her presents. She visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> several times, trying to get the matter settled; but he had no time to give to the matter, and, in the end, she sought consolation from the Buddha.

When the time came for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> to leave, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya</span> gave her ten admonitions, which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> overheard from the next room. These admonitions were:

- Not to give fire from the house outside;
- not to take into the house fire from without;
- to give only to those who give in return;
- not to give to those who do not give in return;
- to give to him that gives and to him that gives not;
- to sit, eat and sleep happily;
- to tend the fire and to honour household deities.

These riddles were later explained by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> to her father-in-law (Dhp­a.i.403f.). On the following day <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya</span> appointed eight householders to be sponsors to his daughter and to enquire into any charges which might be brought against her. When she left, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya</span> allowed any inhabitants of his fourteen tributary villages to accompany her if they so wished. As a result the villages were left empty; but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span>, fearing that he should have to feed them, drove most of them back. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> entered <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> standing in her chariot, so that all might see her glory. The citizens showered gifts on her, but these she distributed among the people.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> was a follower of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span>, and, soon after <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>’s arrival in his house, he sent for them and told her to minister to them. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>, repulsed by their nudity, refused to pay them homage. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span> urged that she should be sent away, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> bided his time.

One day, as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> was eating, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> stood fanning him, a monk was seen standing outside his house. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> stood aside, that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> might see him, but as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> continued to eat without noticing the monk, she said to the latter, “Pass on, Sir, my father-in-law eats stale fare.” <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> was angry and threatened to send her away, but, at her request, the matter was referred to her sponsors. They enquired into the several charges brought against her and adjudged her not guilty. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> then gave orders that preparations should be made for her return to her parents. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> begged her forgiveness which she granted, on condition that he would invite to the house the Buddha and his monks. This he did, but, owing to the influence of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span>, he left <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> to entertain them, and only consented to hear the Buddha’s sermon at the end of the meal from behind a curtain. At the conclusion of this sermon, however, he became a *Sotāpanna*. His gratitude towards <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> was boundless; henceforth she was to be considered as his mother and to receive all the honour due to a mother; from this time onwards she was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāramātā</span>. In Dhp­a.i.406 we are told that in order to confirm this declaration, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> sucked the breast of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>. This account adds that she had also a son named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span>; thus there was a double reason for the name. AN­a.i.313 says that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> was her eldest son.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> got made for her everyday use an ornament called *ghanamaṭṭhaka*, at a cost of one hundred thousand (some time after, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> sold the *Mahālatāpasādhana* and built the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāramātupāsada</span>). On the day of the presentation of this ornament, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> held for her a special festival in her honour, and she was made to bathe in sixteen pots of perfumed water. This account of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> is summarized from Dhp­a.i.384ff.; AN­a.i.219ff. contains a similar account but with far less detail. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> account contains numerous other particulars, some of which are given below.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> had ten sons and ten daughters, each of whom had a similar number of children, and so on down to the fourth generation. Before her death, at the age of one hundred and twenty, she had eighty-four thousand and twenty direct lineal descendants, all living (but see Ud.viii.8, which speaks of the death of a grand-daughter and of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>’s great grief; this evidently refers to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dattā</span>). She herself kept, all her life, the appearance of a girl of sixteen. She had the strength of five elephants, and it is said that once she took the trunk of an elephant, which was sent to test her, between her two fingers and forced him back on his haunches (Dhp­a.i.409). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> owned such a great reputation for bringing good fortune that the people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> always invited her to their houses on festivals and holidays (Dhp­a.i.409).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> fed five hundred monks daily at her house (thus, e.g., Ja.iv.144; two thousand, according to Dhp­a.i.128; later she appointed her grand-daughter, probably <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dattā</span>, to officiate for her.) In the afternoon she visited the Buddha, and, after listening to his sermon, would go round the monastery inquiring into the needs of the monks and nuns. Because she wished the Saṅgha well she was appointed on the committee set up to enquire into the charge of misbehaviour brought against the mother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumāra Kassapa</span> (*q.v.*); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>’s experience as the mother of several children stood her in good stead. In these rounds she was sometimes accompanied by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppiyā</span>. For an incident connected with one of these visits, see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppiyā</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp-a.i.100f.) says that once five hundred young men of good family entrusted the care of their wives to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>. On one occasion, when accompanying her to the monastery, they became drunk and committed improprieties in the presence of the Buddha. The Buddha frightened them by emitting a dark-blue ray of light, thus restoring them to their senses. This was the occasion of the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumbhajātaka</span> (Ja 512); see also Ja.v.11f. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> begged for, and was granted, eight boons by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>:

- that as long as she lived she be allowed to give robes to the members of the Saṅgha for the rainy season;
- food for monks coming into <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sāvatthī</span>;
- food for those going out;
- food for the sick;
- food for those who wait on the sick;
- medicine for the sick;
- a constant supply of rice gruel for any needing it;
- and bathing robes for the nuns.

Probably on account of this boon the monks who had been to see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khadiravaniya Revata</span> (*q.v.*) visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> immediately after their return to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>; but see the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pīṭhajātaka</span> (Ja 337). This list of boons and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>’s reasons for begging them are given at Vin.i.290ff. According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Surucijātaka</span> (Ja 489, *q.v.*), she obtained the boons owing to her virtue in the past as well, e.g., in her birth as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumedhā</span> (Ja.iv.315ff.); see also Vin.i.296, where the Buddha accepts a face towel as a special gift from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> but would not accept an earthenware foot scrubber (Vin.ii.129f.).

With the construction of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāramātupāsāda</span> (*q.v.*) in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pubbārāma Visākhā</span>’s ambitions were fulfilled, and it is said (Dhp­a.i.416f) that when the monastery was completed and the festival of opening in progress, as the evening drew on she walked round the monastery accompanied by her children, her grandchildren and her great-grandchildren, and in five stanzas sang her joy, saying, “Now is entirely fulfilled the prayer which I prayed in times of yore.” (The wishes mentioned in these stanzas as having been fulfilled differ from the eight boons mentioned above). The monks heard her sing and told the Buddha; he related to them how, in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> had been the friend of the principal women benefactors of that Buddha. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> she was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghadāsī</span>, youngest of the seven daughters of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kikī</span>, and for long after her marriage she gave alms and performed other good works with her sisters (AN­a.i.219).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vihāravimānavatthu</span> (Vv.iv.6; Vv-a.189,191), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> was born, after death, among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nimmānaratī</span> *Devā* as the consort of the *Deva* king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunimmita</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (DN­a.iii.740) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>, like <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>, will enjoy one hundred and thirty-one *kappas* of happiness in the *Brahma*-worlds before she finally passes away into *Nibbāna*.

Among <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>’s relations are also mentioned, in addition to her two sons <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migajāla</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="119" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span>, a sister <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="120" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujātā</span>, who became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="121" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>’s daughter-in-law (AN.iv.91; AN­a.ii.724; Ja.ii.347); a grandson, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="122" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saḷha</span> (*q.v.*); a grand-daughter, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="123" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dattā</span>, who died (Dhp­a.iii.278): and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="124" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uggaha</span> (*q.v.*), called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="125" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meṇḍakanattā</span>. Mention is also made of a grandson of hers on whose behalf she interceded with the Buddha when the monks refused to ordain him during the rainy season (Vin.i.153).

The books contain numerous *Suttas* preached by the Buddha to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="126" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> during her frequent visits to him, chief among such *Suttas* being the famous discourse on the keeping of the *uposatha* (AN.i.205ff.; cf.iv.255; Dhp­a.iii.58f), the discourse of the eight qualities which win for women power in this world and power and happiness in the next (AN.iv.269), and eight qualities which win for a woman birth among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="127" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manāpakāyika</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="128" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> (AN.iv.267).
