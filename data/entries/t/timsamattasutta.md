# Tiṁsamattasutta

## English source

Tiṁsamattasutta. Thirty monks from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span> (described as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pāveyyakā</span>), all forest-dwellers, visit the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. By means of various similes the Buddha tells them of the infiniteness of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Saṁsāra</span>, and at the end of the sermon they become <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span> (SN.ii.187f).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp­a.ii.32f) evidently refers to the same monks, but there it is stated that the Buddha first preached to them in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kappāsikavanasaṇḍa</span> while they were searching for a woman. We seem here to have a confusion of legends. cp. the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddavaggiyas</span>.
