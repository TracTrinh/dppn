# Tagarasikhī

## English source

Tagarasikhī. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> (MN.iii.69; Ap­a.i.106), third among the five hundred sons of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumavatī</span>, all of whom became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppabuddha</span>, a banker of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, having seen the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> on his way to a park, spoke insultingly to him, and, as a result, was born as a leper in this birth (Ud.v.3; Ud­a.291). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp-a.ii.36) says <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppabuddha</span> spat on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> contains the story of a man who often met <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tagarasikhī</span> begging for alms (i.92f; SN­a.i.126f; also Ja.iii.299f and Mhv­ṭ.597). One day, being attracted by him, he asked his wife to give him a meal and went on his way. His wife prepared excellent food and gave it to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tagarasikhī</span>. The husband met <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tagarasikhī</span> on his way back to the town, and seeing the excellence of the food, was displeased with his wife’s generosity, thinking to himself that it were better that slaves and workmen should have eaten the food. As a result, he was born as a very rich <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, but was never able to enjoy his wealth, and died intestate, all his possessions passing to the royal treasury.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp-a.iv.77f) calls him <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aputtaka</span>. It has been suggested (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Buddhist India</span>, p.31) that the “Tagara” in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tagarasikhī</span> was the name of a place, perhaps the modern Ter.
