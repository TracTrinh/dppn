# Telapattajātaka

## English source

Telapattajātaka (Ja 96).<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14"> Takkasilājātaka</span>. The Bodhisatta was once the youngest of one hundred sons of the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. He heard from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>, who took their meals in the palace, that he would become king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> if he could reach it without falling a prey to the ogresses who waylaid travellers in the forest. Thereupon, he set out with five of his brothers who wished to accompany him. On the way through the forest the five in succession succumbed to the charms of the ogresses, and were devoured. One ogress followed the Bodhisatta right up to the gates of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span>, where the king took her into the palace, paying no heed to the Bodhisatta’s warning. The king succumbed to her wiles, and, during the night, the king and all the inhabitants of the palace were eaten by the ogress and her companions. The people, realising the sagacity and strength of will of the Bodhisatta, made him their king.

The story was related in reference to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janapadakalyāṇisutta</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). The monks said it must be very hard not to look at a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">janapadakaḷyāni</span>, but the Buddha denied this and related the above story (Ja.i.393ff).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> seems also to have been called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilājātaka</span> (Ja.i.470).
