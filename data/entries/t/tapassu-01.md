# Tapassu 01

## English source

Tapassu 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tapussa</span>. A merchant of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkala</span>. He and his friend, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhalluka</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhalliya</span>), while on their way to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, saw the Buddha at the foot of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājāyatana</span> tree, in the eighth week after the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>. Urged by a deity, who had been their relation, they offered the Buddha rice-cakes and honey in a bowl provided by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Four Regent Gods</span>. They became the first lay disciples of the Buddha, and their formula of Refuge contained no reference to the Saṅgha (Vin.i.3f; AN.i.26; Ud­a.54; Ja.i.80).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> Commentary (i.48f), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tapassu</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhalluka</span> were brothers, sons of a caravan leader of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pokkharavatī</span>. Some time later they visited the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, where he preached to them; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tapassu</span>, thereupon, became a *Sotāpanna*, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhalluka</span> entered the Saṅgha and became an *Arahant*.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sikhī Buddha</span> they were Brahmins of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aruṇavatī</span>. Hearing that two caravan leaders, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujita</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ojita</span>, had given the first meal to the Buddha, they gave alms to the Buddha and his monks, and wished for a similar privilege for themselves under a future Buddha. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>, they were sons of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gopāla</span> *seṭṭhi*, and for many years provided the monks with milk-rice.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttara Commentary</span> (AN­a.i.207f) says that the deity, who caused <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tapassu</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhalluka</span> to give alms to the Buddha, was their mother in their previous birth. The Buddha gave them, for worship, eight handfuls of his hair, which he obtained by stroking his head. They took the hair with them to their city – which, according to this account, was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asitañjana</span> – and there built a *cetiya*, from which rays of blue light issued on fast-days. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tapassu</span> is called a *dvevācikaupāsaka* (AN­a.ii.696), and is included in a list of eminent *upāsakas*. AN.iii.450. The Sanskrit books call him Trapuṣa (Divy.393; Mvu.iii.303.)

See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tapassusutta</span> below.
