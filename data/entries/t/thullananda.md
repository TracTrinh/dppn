# Thullanandā

## English source

Thullanandā. A nun, one of four sisters who all joined the Saṅgha, the others being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandavatī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sundarinandā</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thullanandā</span> appears to have had charge of a large company of nuns, all of whom followed her in various malpractices (Vin.iv.211.239, 240, 280).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thullanandā</span> was well-versed in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span> and was a clever preacher. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>, is mentioned as having come on two occasions to hear her preach, and was so pleased with her eloquence that he allowed her to persuade him to give her the costly upper garments he was wearing (Vin.iv.254-256). She was greedy for possessions, and was later accused of misappropriating gifts intended for other nuns (Vin.iv.245-246, 258).

She was fond of the company of men, and frequented streets and cross-roads unattended that she might not be hindered in her intrigues with men (Vin.iv.270, 273).

She seems to have regarded with sympathy women who succumbed to temptation and to have tried to shield them from discovery (Vin.iv.216, 225, 230f).

She bribed dancers and singers to sing her praises. She could brook no rival, and especially disliked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā</span>, whom she deliberately annoyed on more than one occasion (Vin.iv.283, 285, 287, 290, 292).

She was fractious and would wish for something, but when that was procured for her, would say it was something else she really wanted (Vin.iv.248, 250).

She was evidently an admirer of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, and was greatly offended on hearing that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> had called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> “boy,” and gave vent to her displeasure at what she considered <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>’s presumption. But we are told that soon after that she left the Saṅgha (SN.ii.219ff).

She befriended <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariṭṭha</span> when he was cast out of the Saṅgha (Vin.iv.218). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suvaṇṇahaṁsajātaka</span> was related in reference to her, and she is identified with the Brahmin’s wife of the story (Ja.i.474f).
