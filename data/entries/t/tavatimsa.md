# Tāvatiṁsa

## English source

Tāvatiṁsa. The second of the six <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span>-worlds, the first being the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cātummahārājika</span> world. Tāvatiṁsa stands at the top of Mount <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudassana</span>). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> is king of both worlds, but lives in Tāvatiṁsa. Originally it was the abode of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span>; but when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgha</span> was born as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> and dwelt with his companions in Tāvatiṁsa he disliked the idea of sharing his realm with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span>, and, having made them intoxicated, he hurled them down to the foot of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span>, where the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asurabhavana</span> was later established.

The chief difference between these two worlds seems to have been that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāricchattaka</span> tree grew in Tāvatiṁsa, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittapāṭalī</span> tree in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asurabhavana</span>. In order that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span> should not enter Tāvatiṁsa, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> had five walls built around it, and these were guarded by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Supaṇṇas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumbhaṇḍas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cātummahārājika</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> (Ja.i.201ff; also Dhp­a.i.272f). The entrance to Tāvatiṁsa was by way of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittakūṭadvārakoṭṭhaka</span>, on either side of which statues of Indra (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Indapaṭimā</span>) kept guard (Ja.vi.97). The whole kingdom was ten thousand leagues in extent (Dhp­a.i.273), and contained more than one thousand <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pāsādas</span> (Ja.vi.279). The chief features of Tāvatiṁsa were its parks – the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phārusaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittalatā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Missaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandana</span> – the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vejayantapāsāda</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāricchatta</span> tree, the elephant-king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Erāvaṇa</span> and the Assembly-hall <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhammā</span> (Ja.vi.278; MN­a.i.183; cp. Mhv.i.32). Mention is also made of a park called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandā</span> (Ja.i.204). Besides the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāricchataka</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārijāta</span>) flower, which is described as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kovilāra</span> (AN.iv.117), the divine <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kakkāru</span> flower also grew in Tāvatiṁsa (Ja.iii.87). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittalatāvana</span> grows the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Āsāvatī</span> creeper, which blossoms once in a thousand years (Ja.iii.250f).

It is the custom of all Buddhas to spend the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vassa</span> following the performance of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamakapāṭihāriya</span>, in Tāvatiṁsa. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> went there to preach the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span> to his mother, born there as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Devaputta</span>. The distance of sixty-eight thousand leagues from the earth to Tāvatiṁsa he covered in three strides, placing his foot once on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yugandhara</span> and again on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span>.

The Buddha spent three months in Tāvatiṁsa, preaching all the time, seated on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s throne, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍukambalasilāsana</span>, at the foot of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāricchattaka</span> tree. Eighty crores of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> attained to a knowledge of the truth. This was in the seventh year after his Awakening (Ja.iv.265; Dhp­a.iii.216f; Bv­a. p.3). It seems to have been the frequent custom of ascetics, possessed of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span>-power, to spend the afternoon in Tāvatiṁsa (e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span>, Ja.vi.392; and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷadevala</span>, Ja.i.54).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> paid numerous visits to Tāvatiṁsa, where he learnt from those dwelling there stories of their past deeds, that he might repeat them to men on earth for their edification (Vv-a.p.4).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka Commentary</span> mentions several human beings who were invited by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, and who were conveyed to Tāvatiṁsa – e.g. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nimi</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Guttila</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mandhātā</span> and the queen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīlavatī</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mandhātā</span> reigned as co-ruler of Tāvatiṁsa during the life period of thirty-six <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>s, sixty thousand years (Ja.ii.312). The inhabitants of Tāvatiṁsa are thirty-three in number, and they regularly meet in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhammā</span> Hall (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhammā</span> for details). A description of such an assembly is found in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janavasabhasutta</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cātummahārājika</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) are present to act as guards. Inhabitants of other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span>- and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>-worlds seemed sometimes to have been present as guests – e.g. the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sanaṅkumāra</span>, who came in the guise of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span>. From the description given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span>, all the inhabitants of Tāvatiṁsa seem to have been followers of the Buddha, deeply devoted to his teachings (DN.ii.207ff). Their chief place of offering was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷāmaṇicetiya</span>, in which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> deposited the hair of Prince <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siddhattha</span>, cut off by him when he renounced the world and put on the garments of a recluse on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nerañjarā</span> (Ja.i.65). Later, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> deposited here also the eye-tooth of the Buddha, which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Doṇa</span> hid in his turban, hoping to keep it for himself (DN­a.ii.609; Bv.xxviii.6, 10).

The gods of Tāvatiṁsa sometimes come to earth to take part in human festivities (Ja.iii.87). Thus <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātali</span> are mentioned as having visited the earth on various occasions. Mention is also made of goddesses from Tāvatiṁsa coming to bathe in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anotatta</span> and then spending the rest of the day on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manosilātala</span> (Ja.v.392).

The capital city of Tāvatiṁsa was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Masakkasāra</span> (Ibid., p.400). The average age of an inhabitant of Tāvatiṁsa is thirty million years, reckoned by human computation. Each day in Tāvatiṁsa is equal in time to one hundred years on earth (Dhp­a.i.364). The gods of Tāvatiṁsa are most handsome; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>, among earth-dwellers, are compared to them (Dhp­a.iii.280). The stature of some of the Tāvatiṁsa dwellers is three-quarters of a league; their undergarment is a robe of twelve leagues and their upper garment also a robe of twelve leagues. They live in mansions of gold, thirty leagues in extent (Ibid., p.8). The Commentaries (e.g., SN­a.i.23; AN­a.i.377) say that Tāvatiṁsa was named after <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magha</span> and his thirty-two companions, who were born there as a result of their good deeds in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Macalagāma</span>. Whether the number of the chief inhabitants of this world always remained at thirty-three, it is impossible to say, though some passages, e.g. in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janavasabhasutta</span>, lead us to suppose so.

Sometimes, as in the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandiya</span>, who built the great monastery at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>, a mansion would appear in Tāvatiṁsa, when an earth-dweller did a good deed capable of obtaining for him birth in this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span>-world; but this mansion would remain unoccupied till his human life came to an end (Dhp­a.iii.291). There were evidently no female <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> among the Thirty-three. Both <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māyā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gopikā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Devaputtas</span> when born in Tāvatiṁsa. The women there were probably the attendants of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> (but see, e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jālini</span> and the various stories of Vv-a).

There were many others besides the Thirty-three who had their abode in Tāvatiṁsa. Each <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span> had numerous retinues of attendants, and the dove-footed (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kakuṭapādiniyo</span>) nymphs (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t18">accharā</span>) of Tāvatiṁsa are famous in literature for their delicate beauty. The sight of these made <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span>, when escorted by the Buddha to Tāvatiṁsa, renounce his love for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janapadakaḷyānī Nandā</span> (Ja.ii.92; Ud.iii.2).

The people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span> excelled the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> of Tāvatiṁsa in courage, mindfulness and piety (AN.iv.396). Among the great achievements of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asadisakumāra</span> was the shooting of an arrow as far as Tāvatiṁsa (Ja.ii.89). Tāvatiṁsa was also known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tidasa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tidiva</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>).
