# Takkasilā

## English source

Takkasilā. The capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhāra</span>. It is frequently mentioned as a centre of education, especially in the Jātakas. It is significant that it is never mentioned in the *Suttas*, though, according to numerous <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> stories, it was a great centre of learning from pre-Buddhistic times. The Commentaries mention that in the Buddha’s day, also, princes and other eminent men received their training at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāli</span>, chief of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span>, prince of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span>, were classmates in the university of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> (Dhp­a.i.337). Among others described as being students of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgulimāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapāla</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇhadinna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhāradvāja</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasadatta</span> (*q.v.*).

From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> to Takkasilā was a distance of two thousand *yojanas* (Ja.i.395), though we are told that sometimes the journey was accomplished in one day (Ja.ii.47). The road passed through thick jungle infested by robbers (Dhp­a.iv.66). Takkasilā was, however, a great centre of trade; people flocked to it from various parts of the country (MNid.i.154), not only from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, but also from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, from which city the road lay through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soreyya</span> (Dhp­a.i.326). In ancient times students came to the university from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lāla</span> (Ja.i.447), from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuru</span> country (Dhp­a.iv.88), from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> (Ja.v.161), and from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sivi</span> country (Ja.v.210).

The students in the university studied the three Vedas and the eighteen sciences (*vijjā*, Ja.i.159), which evidently included the science of archery (Ja.i.356; Dhp­a.iv.66; also medicine and surgery, Vin.i.269f), the art of swordmanship (Ja.v.128), and elephant-craft (*hatthisutta*, Ja.ii.47). Mention is also made of the study of magic, such as the *Ālambanamanta*, for charming snakes (Ja.iv.457), and the *Nidhi-uddharaṇamanta*, for recovering buried treasure (Ja.iii.116). The students were also taught the science of ritual (*manta*, Ja.ii.200); but in this branch of learning <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> seems to have had a greater reputation, for we find students being sent there from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> in order to learn the *mantas* (Dhp­a.iii.445).

The students generally paid a fee to the teacher on admission, the usual amount being one thousand gold pieces. They waited on the teacher by day and were taught by him at night. The paying students were entitled to various privileges, and lived with the teacher as members of his family, enjoying his constant company. The students seem mostly to have done their own domestic work, leading a cooperative life, gathering their own firewood and cooking their meals, though mention is made of servants, both male and female, helping in the various tasks (Ja.i.319).

Only *Brāhmaṇas* and *Khattiyas* appear to have been eligible for admission to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> (Ja.iv.391).

Discipline was evidently very rigorous, a breach of the rules being severely punished, irrespective of the status of the pupil, who was sometimes flogged on the back with a bamboo stick (Ja.ii.277f). Often the most promising students were given the daughters of the teachers in marriage as a mark of very special favour. (e.g., Dhp­a.iv.66. Elsewhere (Ja.vi.347) it is stated that the teacher’s daughter was given to the eldest pupil).

Sometimes the teacher and his pupils were invited to a meal at the house of a chief man of the city (Ja.iv.391). The principal teacher was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Disāpāmokkhācariya</span>; under him were assistants, usually chosen from among his students, who were called *piṭṭhi-ācariyā* (e.g., Ja.ii.100).

Takkasilā, being the capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhāra</span>, was probably also the seat of government. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>’s contemporary in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhāra</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span> (Ja.i.399; ii.218). Mention is made in the Jātakas of a Takkasilā-*rājā* (AN­a.i.153; MN­a.i.335; ii.979, 987f). According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumbhakārajātaka</span> (*q.v.*), Takkasilā was the capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Naggaji</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīpavaṁsa</span> (iii.31) records that twelve kings, descendants of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīpaṅkara</span>, ruled in succession at Takkasilā.

It is said in the *Divyāvadāna* (p.371) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Biṇḍusāra</span>’s empire included Takkasilā. There was once a rebellion there and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> was sent to quell it. From the minor Rock Edict <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">II</span>. of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> it would appear that Takkasilā was the headquarters of a provincial government at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhāra</span>, placed under a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumāra</span> or Viceroy. A rebellion broke out there again in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>, who sent his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇāla</span> to settle it.

Takkasilā is identified with the Greek Taxila, in Rawalpindi in the Punjab.
