# Tindukajātaka

## English source

Tindukajātaka. *v.l.* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tiṇḍukajātaka</span> (Ja 177). The Bodhisatta was once the leader of eighty thousand monkeys. Near their dwelling place was a village where grew a *tinduka* tree, whose sweet fruits were eaten by the monkeys. But the people came and built a village near the tree and the monkeys could no longer take the fruit. One night, when the villagers were asleep, they crept up to the tree and began eating the fruit. A villager gave the alarm and the monkeys were in great danger of being slain when dawn came. But the Bodhisatta comforted them and kept them in good humour until they were rescued by his nephew, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Senaka</span>, who set fire to the village, distracting the attention of the people, thus allowing the monkeys to escape.

The story was related in illustration of the Bodhisatta’s sagacity (Ja.ii.76f).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Senaka</span> is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span>.
