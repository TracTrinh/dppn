# Tusita 02

## English source

Tusita 02. The fourth of the six <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span> worlds (AN.i.210, etc.). Four hundred years of human life are equal to one day of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> world and four thousand years, so reckoned, is the term of life of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span> born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> (AN.i.214; iv.261, etc.).

Sometimes <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sakadāgāmīs</span> (e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Purāṇa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span>) are born there (AN.iii.348; v.138; also Dhp­a.i.129; Ud­a.149, 277).

It is the rule for all Bodhisattas to be born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> in their last life but one; then, when the time comes for the appearance of a Buddha in the world, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> of the ten thousand world systems assemble and request the Bodhisatta to be born among men. Great rejoicings attend the acceptance of this request (AN.ii.130; iv.312; Dhp­a.i.69f; Ja.i.47f).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>’s name, while in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span>, was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setaketu</span> (Vin-a.i.161), and the Bodhisatta <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Metteyya</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), the future Buddha, is now living in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāthadeva</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> world is considered the most beautiful of the celestial worlds, and the pious love to be born there because of the presence of the Bodhisatta (Mhv.xxxii.72f).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> is also the abode of each Bodhisatta’s parents (Dhp­a.i.110).

The king of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> world is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Santusita</span>; he excels his fellows in ten respects – beauty, span of life, etc. (AN.iv.243; but see Cv.lii.47, where the Bodhisatta <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Metteyya</span> is called the chief of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span>).

Among those reborn in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> are also mentioned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammika</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span>, the Thera <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> 10), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Dhana</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> are so-called because they are full of joy (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t18">tuṭṭhahaṭṭhāti</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusitā</span>) (Vibh­a.519; NidA.109).

The inhabitants of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> are called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusitā</span>. They were present at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Samaya</span> (DN.ii.161).
