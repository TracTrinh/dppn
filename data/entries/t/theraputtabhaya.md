# Theraputtābhaya

## English source

Theraputtābhaya. One of the ten chief warriors of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span>. His personal name was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span>. His father was the headman of the village <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kitti</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohaṇa</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theraputtābhaya</span>, when sixteen, wielded a club thirty-eight inches round and sixteen cubits long. He was therefore sent to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kākavaṇṇatissa</span>’s court. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span>’s father was a supporter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Summa</span> and, having heard a discourse from him, became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>, entered the Saṅgha and soon afterwards became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. His son, thereupon, came to be called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theraputtābhaya</span> (Mhv.xxiii.2, 63ff). At the end of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span>’s campaigns, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span> took leave of the king and joined the Saṅgha, became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>, and lived with five hundred other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span> (Mhv.xxvi.2). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> lay on his deathbed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span> visited him and gladdened his heart by reminding him of the works of great merit he had done (Mhv.xxxii.48ff).

In a previous birth he had given milk-rice to monks, hence his great strength (Mhv­ṭ.453).
