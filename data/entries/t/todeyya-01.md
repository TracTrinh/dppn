# Todeyya 01

## English source

Todeyya 01. A *Mahā Sāla* Brahmin, mentioned in a list of eminent Brahmins gathered together at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Icchanaṅkala</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manasākaṭa</span> (DN.i.235; Sn., p.115). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (DN­a.ii.399; AN­a.ii.554) that his permanent residence was at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tudigāma</span>; hence his name. He was, very probably, the father of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subha</span>, who is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Todeyyaputta</span> (MN­a.ii.802). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subha</span>’s father was, we know, chaplain to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> and, though exceedingly rich, was a great miser; after death he was born in his own house as a dog of whom <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subha</span> was very fond. When the Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subha</span> the dog barked, and the Buddha chided it, addressing it by the name of Todeyya. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subha</span> was greatly offended but the Buddha proved the identity of the dog by getting him to show <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subha</span> some of his father’s buried treasure. The dog was later born in hell (MN­a.ii.962f).

There was in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍalakappa</span> a Mango-grove belonging to the Todeyya-Brahmins (MN.ii.210). A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Todeyya</span>-Brahmin was also the owner of the Mango-grove at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāmaṇḍā</span> (SN.iv.121). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (AN.ii.180) mentions the pupils of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Todeyya</span>-Brahmin speaking ill of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Eḷeyya</span> because the latter followed the teachings of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāmaputta</span>.
