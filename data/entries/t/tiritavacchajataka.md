# Tirīṭavacchajātaka

## English source

Tirīṭavacchajātaka (Ja 259). Once the Bodhisatta was a Brahmin in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span> named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tirīṭavaccha</span> and after the death of his parents he became an ascetic. The king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, fleeing from his enemies, arrived at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tirīṭavaccha</span>’s hermitage, riding on an elephant. Looking for water but finding none, he let himself down into the hermit’s well but was unable to get out again; the hermit rescued him and showed him every hospitality. Later the hermit visited the king, now restored to the throne, and was given a dwelling place in the royal park. The courtiers were inclined to be jealous of the attentions paid to the hermit, but the king told them of the incident in the forest and they acknowledged the hermit’s claim to honour.

The story was told in reference to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> having received five hundred robes from the women of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>’s palace. The king is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>. Ja.ii.314ff.
