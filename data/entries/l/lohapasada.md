# Lohapāsāda

## English source

Lohapāsāda. A building at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>, forming the *uposatha* hall of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>. It was originally built by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span> (see Mhv.xv.205), but it was then a small building erected only to round off the form of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> (*vihāraparipuṇṇamattasādhakaṁ*) (Mhv­ṭ. 364). Later, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> pulled it down and erected on its site a nine-storeyed building, one hundred cubits square and high, with one hundred rooms on each storey. The building was planned according to a sketch of the Ambalaṭṭhikapāsāda (the actual <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambalaṭṭhikā</span> (*q.v.*) of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span> was to the east of the building, DN­a.ii.635) in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bīraṇī</span>’s palace which eight *Arahants* obtained from the *Deva* world. The building was roofed with copper plates, hence its name. The nine storeys were occupied by monks, according to their various attainments, the last four storeys being reserved for *Arahants*. In the centre of the hall was a seat made in the shape of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span>’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārīvāhana</span> chariot (for details see Mhv.xxvii.1ff). The building was visible out at sea to a distance of one league (Mhv­ṭ. 505). Once <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> attempted to preach in the assembly hall of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span>, but he was too nervous to proceed. Realizing then how difficult was the task of preachers, he endowed largesse for them in every *vihāra* (Mhv.xxxii.42ff). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> had always a great fondness for the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span>, and as he lay dying he managed to have a last view of it (Mhv.xxxii.9). Thirty crores were spent on its construction; in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saddhātissa</span>’s day it caught fire from a lamp, and he rebuilt it in seven storeys at a cost of nine millions.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khallāṭanāga</span> built thirty-two other *pāsādas* round the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span> for its ornamentation (Mhv.xxxiii.6), while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhātikābhaya</span> carried out various repairs to the building (Mhv.xxxiv.39), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āmaṇḍagāmaṇī</span> added an inner courtyard and a verandah (*ājira*) (Mhv.xxxv.3). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirināga I</span>. rebuilt it in five storeys (Mhv.xxxvi.25,52), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayanāga</span> built a pavilion in the courtyard and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Goṭhābhaya</span> had the pillars renewed (Mhv.xxxvi.102).

He evidently started to rebuild the structure, because we are told (Mhv.xxxvi.124) that, after his death, his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jeṭṭhatissa</span> completed up to seven storeys the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span> which had been left unfinished (*vippakata*) by his father.

The building was worth one crore, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jeṭṭhatissa</span> offered to it a jewel worth sixty thousand, after which he renamed it <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇipāsāda</span>. Afterwards <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa</span>, a minister of his brother, the renegade king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span>, acting on the advice of heretical monks led by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghamitta</span>, destroyed the *pāsāda* and carried away its wealth to enrich <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagirivihāra</span> (Mhv.xxxvii.10f,59).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span>’s son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimeghavaṇṇa</span>, had the *pāsāda* restored to its original form (Mhv.xxxvii.62), and, later, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhātusena</span> renovated it (Mhv.xxxviii.54), as did <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggabodhi I</span>., who distributed the three garments to thirty-six thousand monks at the festival of dedication and assigned a village to provide for its protection (Mhv.xlii.20). His successor, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggabodhi II</span>., deposited in the *pāsāda* the Buddha’s right collar-bone, which relic was later transferred to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thūpārāma</span> (Mhv.xlii.53,59). In the reign of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggabodhi IV</span>., the ruler of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malaya</span> repaired the central pinnacle (Mhv.xlvi.30), while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māṇavamma</span> provided a new roof (Mhv.xlvii.65). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sena II</span>. completely restored the *pāsāda* and placed in it an image of the Buddha in gold mosaic. The building was evidently not in use at the time, but he provided for its upkeep and assigned villages for its protection, and decreed that thirty-two monks should be in constant residence (Mhv.li.69f). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sena IV</span>. was in the habit of preaching in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span> periodical sermons to the monks (Mhv.liv.4) which were based on the *Suttas*, but, after his death, the place again fell into disrepair and was destroyed by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Coḷas</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu I</span>. restored it once again (Mhv.lxxviii.102), but it was soon after pillaged again and fell into ruin, in which state it remains to this day. There are now sixteen hundred monolithic stone columns (the same number as in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu I</span>.), which evidently formed the framework of the lowest storey.

Frequent mention is made of the books of sermons preached in the lowest storey of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span>, at which very large numbers were present. Once, when *Ambapāsāṇavāsī* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittagutta</span> preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rathavinītasutta</span>, there were twelve thousand monks and one thousand nuns (Mhv­ṭ.552f). On another occasion, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhātikābhaya</span> described the contents of the Relic chamber of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> to all the monks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> assembled in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span> (Mhv­ṭ.555).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (DN­a.ii.581) that, up to his day, it was customary for all the monks of Ceylon, who lived to the north of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vālukanadī</span>, to assemble in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span> twice a year, on the first and last days of the *vassa*, while those to the south of the river assembled at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa Mahā Vihāra</span>. When disputes arose as to the interpretation of various rules or teachings, the decision was often announced by a teacher of repute from the lowest storey of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span> (DN­a.ii.442, 514).

The hood of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mucalinda</span> was of the same size as the storehouse (*bhaṇḍāgāragabbha*) of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span> (Ud­a.101). A mass of rock, as big as the seventh storey of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span>, if dropped from the *Brahma*-world, would take four months to reach the earth. DN­a.ii.678.
