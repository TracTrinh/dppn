# Lambakaṇṇā

## English source

Lambakaṇṇā, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lambakaṇṇas</span>. A *gotta*, mentioned in the Chronicles as being among the inhabitants of Ceylon. The Lambakaṇṇas had, probably, certain duties to perform in connection with the consecration of a king. This was perhaps the reason why <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu I</span>. gave them a prominent place in the ceremonies held in honour of the Tooth Relic (Cv.lxxiv.213); see also below, in the text.

We find that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Iḷanāga</span>, when he went to take his ceremonial bath in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissavāpi</span>, was enraged on finding that the Lambakaṇṇā were not there. As a punishment, he ordered them to work at the remaking of a road along the bank of the tank, leading to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span>, and set *caṇḍālas* to supervise them. Full of anger, the Lambakaṇṇas rose in revolt and seized the throne. Three years later the king returned from exile, and, having defeated the Lambakaṇṇas, made them drag his chariot in triumphal procession. When he proposed to behead them his mother intervened, and he contented himself with having their noses and toes cut off (Mhv.xxxv.18ff).

The Lambakaṇṇas were evidently a powerful clan, and several members of the clan ruled as kings of Ceylon – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vasabha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghatissa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghabodhi</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Goṭhābhaya</span>, the last three of whom came from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahiyaṅgaṇa</span> and seized the throne from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijayakumāra</span> (Mhv.xxxvi.58ff). Between the reign of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kittisirimegha</span> and the Coḷiyan conquest in 1017 CE. i.e., between the third and the eleventh centuries out of thirty-six kings who occupied the throne of Ceylon the majority belonged to either the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moriyā</span> or the Lambakaṇṇā. A clan of Lambakaṇṇas lived also in South India in the twelfth century. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Laṅkāpura</span>, acting under the orders of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu I</span>., crowned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vīrapaṇḍu</span> as king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍu</span>, three Lambakaṇṇa chiefs were asked to carry out “the duties of the Lambakaṇṇas” (*Lambakaṇṇadhuraṁ*) (Cv.lxxvii.27f).

The name may have had a totemistic origin, but according to some Sinhalese Chronicles (e.g., the *Saddharmaratnākara* and the *Pārakumbāsirita*) the Lambakaṇṇas of Ceylon were a branch of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moriyas</span>. They claimed descent from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumitta</span> – a prince of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moriyan</span> clan, who formed one of the escort that brought the Bodhi-tree from India – and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanā</span>, a princess of the same race, who was at one time a nun, ordained under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghamittā</span>. According to these Chronicles most of the kings of Ceylon down to the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu VI</span>. were scions of this clan. In Ceylon, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lambakaṇṇas</span> had settlements in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohaṇa</span>. See, e.g., AN­a.i.262.
