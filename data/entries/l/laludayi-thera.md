# Lāḷudāyī Thera

## English source

Lāḷudāyī Thera. An Elder who possessed the knack of saying “the wrong thing.” He would go to a place where people were enjoying a holiday and recite stanzas suitable to a funeral and *vice versa* (Dhp­a.iii.123ff). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> heard of this he related the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Somadattajātaka</span> (Ja 211), showing that in past births, too, Lāludayī had possessed the same propensity. He is identified with the foolish father (*Agnidatta*) of the story (Ja.ii.167; Dhp­a.iii.125). We also read of his jealousy of the praises bestowed on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> for their knowledge and exposition of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span>, and he claimed that he possessed knowledge equal to theirs. But one day when asked to preach, he sat on a seat holding a painted fan, but found nothing to say. He thereupon agreed to preach in the evening; but the same thing happened, and he barely escaped with his life, so furious was his audience. The Buddha, on hearing of this, related the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sūkarajātaka</span> (Ja 153) (in which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lāḷudāyī</span> was the pig), showing that in the past, too, he had covered himself with disgrace because of his boastfulness. Ja.ii.344ff.; but according to the introductory story of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> itself (in Ja.ii.9ff.), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lāḷudāyī</span>’s name is not mentioned, and the incident described differs somewhat.

On another occasion, he had a dispute with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba Mallaputta</span> regarding the allotment of the rice tickets, and the monks, in order to teach him a lesson, handed him the tickets to distribute. But he created such confusion that there was a great uproar, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> was sent by the Buddha to find out what was happening. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> returned with this story, the Buddha related the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Taṇḍulanālijātaka</span> (Ja 5) to show that in the past <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lāḷudāyī</span> had been a foolish appraiser (Ja.i.123ff). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Naṅgalīsajātaka</span> (Ja 123) gives another example of his folly and ineptitude (Ja.i.446ff); so does also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padañjalijātaka</span> (Ja 247, Ja.ii.263f), where he is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padañjali</span>, an idle, lazy loafer. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Ummaggajātaka</span> (Ja 546, Ja.vi.478) he is identified with the somewhat foolish king, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vedeha</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lāḷudāyī</span> once had a discussion with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasūra</span>, who, at first, thought him to be clever and wise, and accepted him as teacher, being ordained by him. But, later, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāsura</span> easily defeated him in discussion (Snp­a.ii.540). Others, too, visiting <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, and seeing him in the Preacher’s seat, mistook him for an eminent Elder, but soon discovered their mistake (e.g., Dhp­a.ii.31).

On one occasion (AN.iii.192f.; AN­a.ii.628) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lāḷudāyī</span> even dared to contradict <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> regarding birth among the *manomayadevā*. Three times <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> repeated his statement and three times <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lāḷudāyī</span> contradicted him, and, there being no monk in the assembly who supported <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, he took <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lāḷudāyī</span> to the Buddha, where he three times repeated the same statement, being three times contradicted. Then the Buddha called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lāḷudāyī</span> “a witless fool” and silenced him. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> was witness to the dispute, but took no part in it, thereby winning the Buddha’s censure.

Elsewhere (AN.iv.414f.; AN­a.ii.810), however, we find <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lāḷudāyī</span> listening in all humility to a discourse by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> on *Nibbāna*, as the happiness which is not sensed (*avedayitasukha*). Though <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span>’s knowledge of the Dhamma was not profound, he did not hesitate to take part in a discussion, even with the Buddha himself, when occasion arose. We find him twice censured by the Buddha for this exhibition of his ignorance, once in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kammavibhaṅgasutta</span> (MN.iii.208) and once again in a discussion on *anussati* (AN.iii.322f). In both instances <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> is present, and, in the discussion on *anussati*, he earns the Buddha’s praise for his knowledge compared with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span>’s ignorance. This annoyed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span>, for we find him confronting <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> with the fact that though he had been in the constant society of the Buddha he had not profited by it, a remark which earned the censure of the Buddha and his assurance that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> would certainly reach perfection in that very life (AN.i.228; AN­a.i.441).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> calls Lāḷudāyī <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kovariyaputta</span> (Thag­a.ii.7; some of the MSS. read *nāṭakācariyaputta*). It is not clear whether this means that his father was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kovariya</span>.

The Vinaya (Vin.iii.110) mentions a monk called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span> who was a colleague of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Seyyasaka</span>. He persuaded <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Seyyasaka</span> to commit the first *Saṅghādisesa* offence, saying that he himself acted likewise. For this the *Mānatta* penalty was imposed on him.

According to the Commentaries (Vin-a.iii.517; Dhp­a.iii.5), this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span> is to be identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lāḷudāyī</span>, and if this be correct, it was perhaps the same monk who was guilty of several Vinaya offences attributed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span> – see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī (2)</span> – though the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vinaya Commentary</span> does not elsewhere definitely so identify him (e.g., Vin-a.iii.541, 549, 552, where he is simply called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span>), except once (Vin-a.iv.804), where he is mentioned as having made an embroidered robe for a nun, which he persuaded her to wear in the assembly of the nuns! Was this because the Commentator regarded the two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāyī</span>s as distinct persons (e.g., MN­a.i.348)?

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lāḷudāyī</span> is given as an example of a person who did no good either to himself or to others (*neva attahitāya paṭipanno no parahitāya*). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> elsewhere (Vin-a.iii.517) describes him as “*bhantamigasappaṭibhāgo niddārāmatādiṁ anuyuttānaṁ aññataro lolabhikkhu* (a greedy monk, comparable to a rutting deer, who was devoted to such things as delighting in sleep, etc.).”
