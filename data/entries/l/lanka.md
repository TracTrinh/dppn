# Laṅkā

## English source

Laṅkā, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Laṅkādīpa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Laṅkātala</span>. Pāli names for Ceylon, found in the Chronicles – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīpavaṁsa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsa</span> and the Commentaries. An ancient tradition recorded in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsa</span> (Mhv.xv. 57ff), and in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samantapāsādikā</span> (Vin-a.i.86ff), gives the names of the Island in the times of the three previous Buddhas, the names of the capital cities, the different names of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Meghavana</span>, and of the kings contemporary with these Buddhas.

- Thus, in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kakusandha</span>, the Island was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Ojadīpa</span>, the king was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Abhaya</span>, the capital <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Abhayapura</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mahā Meghavana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mahā Tittha</span>.
- In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Koṇāgamana</span>, the Island was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Varadīpa</span>, the capital <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Vaḍḍhamāna</span>, the king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Samiddha</span> and the park <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mahānoma</span>.
- In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kassapa</span>, the Island was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Maṇḍadīpa</span>, the king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Jayanta</span>, the capital <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Visālā</span> and the park Mahā Sāgara.

Besides <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Meghavana</span>, the other physical feature of Ceylon, mentioned in these accounts, is the mountain known in the present age as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanakūṭa</span>, whereon <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> placed his footprint. During the ages of the three previous Buddhas, it was known, respectively, as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devakūṭa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanakūṭaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhakūṭa</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> paid three visits to Ceylon, while the other three Buddhas came only once. During their visits they consecrated various spots by spending there a short time wrapt in meditation.

Laṅkā was once inhabited by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> obliged them to leave the Island and seek shelter in the neighboring <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Giridīpa</span>. Laṅkādīpa was later colonized by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijaya</span> and his three hundred companions. Two cities of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> are mentioned: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirīsavatthu</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Laṅkāpura</span>.
