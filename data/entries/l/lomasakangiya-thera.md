# Lomasakaṅgiya Thera

## English source

Lomasakaṅgiya Thera. An <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> he had been a monk. After <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> had preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddekarattasutta</span>, a certain monk had talked about it to Lomasakaṅgiya, who, unable to understand it, said, “May I, in the future, be able to teach thee this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span>!” And the other answered “May I ask thee!”

In the present age, Lomasakaṅgiya was born in a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, while the other monk became the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candana</span>. Lomasakaṅgiya (so-called because he was delicate and the soles of his feet were covered with hair; MN­a.ii.961 says he was so-called because he had only a little down on his body, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kāyassa īsakalomasākāratāya</span>) did not go with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> young men who joined the Saṅgha. Noticing this, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candana</span> appeared before him and questioned him on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddekarattasutta</span>. Lomasakaṅgiya did not know it, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candana</span> reminded him of his past wish. Lomasakaṅgiya, therefore, went to consult the Buddha, and, later, wished to join the Saṅgha. He was sent back to obtain his parents’ consent. His mother, fearing for his health, would not agree, but he uttered a verse which convinced her (Thag.27). After his ordination, he went into a forest, and, when his companions warned him against the cold, he repeated the verse, and, being devoted to meditation, soon won <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship. Thag­a.i.84; the story given in Ap.ii.504f., both of the past and the present, differs in several details.

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lomasakaṅgiyabhaddekarattasutta</span> (MN.iii.199f.; cf. Ap.ii.505, according to which, it was this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> which led to his becoming an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candana</span> visited Lomasakaṅgiya in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, where he lived after his ordination, and questioned him on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddekarattasutta</span>. When Lomasakaṅgiya again confessed his ignorance, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candana</span> taught him the verses, and then the former packed his bedding and went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, where the Buddha, at his request, taught him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span>.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span>, he offered <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> flowers to the Buddha. Thag­a.i.84; Ap.ii.504; cf. Ap.ii.450 (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgapupphiya</span>); it is these latter <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> verses which are quoted in Thag­a. See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lomasavaṅgīsa</span>.
