# Laṭṭhivana

## English source

Laṭṭhivana, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Laṭṭhivanuyyāna</span>. A grove to the south west of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. In it was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Supatiṭṭha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span>, where the Buddha stayed during his first visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayāsīsa</span>, after the Awakening. There <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> visited him with twelve <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nahutas</span> of followers, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā Kassapa</span> dispelled their doubts by declaring his acceptance of the Buddha as his teacher. It was during this visit that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> gifted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span> to the Buddha and his Saṅgha (Vin.i.35ff.; Dhp­a.i.88; AN­a.i.166; Bv­a.18, etc.). Eleven <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nahutas</span>, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> at their head, became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpannas</span> at the end of the Buddha’s sermon, which included the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāradakassapajātaka</span>. The remaining <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nahuta</span> was established in the Refuges (Ja.i.84; AN­a.i.57; also Ja.vi.219).

The grove evidently received its name from its green liquorice creepers, hence its description as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Laṭṭhimadhukavana</span> (e.g., Ja.i.68). Xuanzang calls it <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Yastivana</span> and describes it as a grove of bamboos, giving accounts of its origin and various stories connected with it. (Beal-Xuan, op.cit., 145f.; see VT.136).
