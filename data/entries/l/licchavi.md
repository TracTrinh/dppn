# Licchavī

## English source

Licchavī, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>. A powerful tribe of India in the time of the Buddha. They were certainly <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Khattiyas</span>, for on that ground they claimed a share of the Buddha’s relics. DN.ii.165; according to the Mhv.i.283, etc., they belonged to the Vāśiṣṭha <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gotta</span>; cp. the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), who are called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāseṭṭhas</span>.

Their capital was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, and they formed a part of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjī</span> confederacy, being often referred to as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjians</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). Their strength lay in their great unity; if one Licchavī fell ill, all the others would visit him. The whole tribe would join in any ceremony performed in the house of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span>, and they would all unite in honouring any distinguished visitors to their city (DN­a.ii.519). They were beautiful to look at and wore brilliantly coloured garments, riding in brightly painted carriages (DN.ii.96; AN.iii.219: cp. Mhv.i.259). The Buddha once compared them to the gods of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> (DN.ii.96; also Dhp­a.iii.280).

Though this would seem to indicate that they were very prosperous and rich, they do not appear to have lived in luxury and idleness. They are, on the contrary, spoken of (SN.ii.267f) as sleeping on straw couches, being strenuous and diligent and zealous in their service (as skilful hardy archers, says the Commentary). They also practised seven conditions of welfare (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">aparihānīyadhammā</span>), which the Buddha claimed to have taught them at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sārandadacetiya</span>:
