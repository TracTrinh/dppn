# Licchavī

## English source

Licchavī, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>. A powerful tribe of India in the time of the Buddha. They were certainly *Khattiyas*, for on that ground they claimed a share of the Buddha’s relics. DN.ii.165; according to the Mhv.i.283, etc., they belonged to the Vāśiṣṭha *gotta*; cp. the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> (*q.v.*), who are called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāseṭṭhas</span>.

Their capital was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, and they formed a part of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjī</span> confederacy, being often referred to as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjians</span> (*q.v.*). Their strength lay in their great unity; if one Licchavī fell ill, all the others would visit him. The whole tribe would join in any ceremony performed in the house of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span>, and they would all unite in honouring any distinguished visitors to their city (DN­a.ii.519). They were beautiful to look at and wore brilliantly coloured garments, riding in brightly painted carriages (DN.ii.96; AN.iii.219: cp. Mhv.i.259). The Buddha once compared them to the gods of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> (DN.ii.96; also Dhp­a.iii.280).

Though this would seem to indicate that they were very prosperous and rich, they do not appear to have lived in luxury and idleness. They are, on the contrary, spoken of (SN.ii.267f) as sleeping on straw couches, being strenuous and diligent and zealous in their service (as skilful hardy archers, says the Commentary). They also practised seven conditions of welfare (*aparihānīyadhammā*), which the Buddha claimed to have taught them at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sārandadacetiya</span>:

- (1) They held frequent public meetings of their tribe which they all attended;
- (2) they met together to make their decisions and carried out their undertakings in concord;
- (3) they upheld tradition and honoured their pledges;
- (4) they respected and supported their elders;
- (5) no women or girls were allowed to be taken by force or abduction;
- (6) they maintained and paid due respect to their places of worship;
- (7) they supported and fully protected the holy men (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Arahants</span>) among them (DN.ii.73f.; AN.iv.15f).

The young men among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> were evidently fond of archery, for mention is made (AN.iii.76) of large numbers of them roving about in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vana</span>, with bows and arrows, the strings set, and surrounded by hounds. They were a martial people and fond of “sport,” but we find one of their Elders, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span>, complaining of them to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>: “The Licchavī youths are quick tempered, rough and greedy fellows; such presents as are sent by the members of their tribe – sugar cane, jujubes, sweet cakes, sweetmeats, etc. – they loot and eat; they slap the women and girls of their tribe on the back.” (AN.iii.76, the *Lalitavistara* is even more condemnatory). Violation of chastity was considered a serious offence among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>, and the assembly would even give its consent to a husband’s request that his unfaithful wife should be murdered (Vin.iv.225).

According to the Buddhist books, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> were devout followers of the Buddha and held him in the highest esteem. Five hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> once gave a garment each to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṅgiyāni</span>, because he recited a verse in praise of the Buddha (AN.iii.239). Even careless boys, referred to above as wandering about with hounds and bows and arrows, would lay aside their arms when they saw the Buddha seated under a tree and would surround him with clasped hands, eager to hear him (AN.iii.76). There were numerous shrines in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> itself, several of which are mentioned by name: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cāpāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sattambaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bahuputta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sārandada</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says that these shrines were originally <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> *cetiyas*, where various <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> were worshipped, but that they were later converted into monasteries for the Buddha and his Saṅgha (e.g., Ud­a.322f). It is, however, apparent from the Buddhist books themselves (e.g., in the story of the general <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīha</span>), that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> was also a stronghold of the Jains. The Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> at least three times, and is frequently mentioned as staying in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kūṭāgārasālā</span> (*q.v.*) in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vana</span>. The first visit was in order to destroy the threefold panic of drought, sickness and non-human foes. It was probably this act which earned for the Buddha the gratitude of the Licchavīs. There the Licchavīs visited him in large numbers, sometimes disturbing the calm of the spot and obliging resident monks to seek peace in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gosiṅgasālavana</span> near by (e.g., AN.v.133f). Once, five hundred Licchavīs invited the Buddha to a discussion held by them at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sārandadacetiya</span> regarding the five kinds of treasures. The Buddha went and gave his opinion (AN.iii.167f).

But not all the Licchavīs were followers of the Buddha. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saccaka</span> the *Nigaṇṭha* visited the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vana</span>, he was accompanied by five hundred Licchavīs, who did not all salute the Buddha as their teacher, but showed him only such respect as was due to an honoured stranger (MN.i.229; MN­a.i.454 gives their reasons). Several eminent Licchavīs are specially mentioned by name as having visited and consulted the Buddha; among whom are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāḷha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍitakumāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāli</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ugga</span>. Several <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>, both men and women, joined the Saṅgha – e.g., the famous courtesan <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambapālī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jentī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīhā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsiṭṭhī</span>, and, among monks, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Añjanavaniya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjiputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambhūta</span>.

The Licchavīs were greatly admired for their system of government. It was a republic (*gaṇa*, *saṅgha*), all the leading members of which were called *rājā*. According to Mhv.i.271, there were 68,000 *rājās* in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>; the Jātakas (i.504; iii.1) speak of 7707; Dhp­a.iii.436.

They held full and frequent assemblies at which problems affecting either the whole republic or individual members were fully discussed. When the assembly drum was heard, all left other duties and assembled immediately in the *Santhāgārasālā* (DN­a.ii.517f). Sometimes, as appears from the story of the conversion of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīha</span>, religion was also discussed at these meetings. The rules of procedure adopted evidently resembled those followed in the *upasampadā* ordination of a monk. See DN.ii.76f., where the Buddha enjoins on the monks the observance of the same habits as practised by the Licchavīs. These are given at Vin.i.56 (VT.i.169f.).

Besides the *rājās* there were also numerous *uparājās*, *senāpatis*, and *bhaṇḍāgārikas* (Ja.iii.1). There was an elaborate judicial procedure by which any person charged with an offence was handed over, in turn, to the *Vinicchayamahāmattas* (inquirers), the *Vohārikas* (experts in law), *Suttadharas* (experts in tradition), the *Aṭṭhakulakas* (probably a judicial committee), the *Senāpati*, the *Uparājā*, and finally to the *Rājā*, who would inflict the proper sentence according to the *paveṇipotthaka* (DN­a.ii.519).

In their political relationships with their neighbours, the Licchavīs seem to have been on friendly terms with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> (*q.v.*), king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, and with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> (See, e.g., MN.ii.101, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> says this). Generally speaking, they were friendly also with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span>, though the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span> (*q.v.*) shows that a certain amount of rivalry existed between the two tribes.

After the death of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, in his desire for the expansion of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, resolved to destroy the Licchavīs. He was probably partly influenced by his fear of his foster brother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayarājakumāra</span> (*q.v.*), who had in him <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span> blood. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> gives another story. (DN­a.ii.516f.; AN­a.ii.703; was the port <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaligāma</span>? see Ud­a.408). There was a port on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span>, extending over one *yojana*, half of which territory belonged to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, and the other half to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>. Near by was a mountain, from which much fragrant material (? *gandhabhaṇḍa*) flowed into the river. While <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> was making preparations to claim his portion of this material, the Licchavīs would go before him and remove it all. This happened on several occasions, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> vowed vengeance. In order to discover what the Buddha thought of his chances of success, he sent to him his minister <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span>. The Buddha predicted (DN.ii.72ff) that as long as the Licchavīs remained united they were proof against any foe. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> then decided to bring about disunion among them. He was successful in this, with the aid of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> (for details see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span>). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> arrived at the gates of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, the Licchavīs, owing to their disunion, were unable to put up any opposition, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> captured the city without further trouble (DN­a.ii.524). The degeneration may have set in earlier among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>, for we find reference to their giving up their earlier austere habits and to their fondness for soft pillows, long sleep and other luxuries (SN.ii.268; see also Dhp­a.iii.280, where they quarrel over a woman; cp. Vin-a.i.284). Their power and prosperity were probably also weakened by the plague and drought which had ravaged <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>.

The Commentaries contain a mythical account of the origin of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>. (MN­a.i.258; Khp-a. etc.; for a very comprehensive account of the Licchavīs, see Law, *Kṣatriya Clans in Buddhist India*, pp.1ff). The queen of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> gave birth to a lump of flesh, and, wishing to avoid disgrace, her ladies in waiting put it in a sealed casket and threw it into the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span>. A Deva wrote the king’s name on the casket, which was picked up by an ascetic, who tended the embryo until two children, a boy and a girl, emerged from it. The ascetic fed them with milk. Whatever entered the stomachs of the children could be seen as though the stomach were transparent, so that they appeared skinless (*nicchavi*); some said the skin was so thin (*līnachavī*) that the stomach and whatever entered it appeared as though sewn together. From this the children came to be called Licchavī, and, as they grew, were brought up by the villagers living near the hermitage. The other children disliked them, saying they were to be avoided (*vajjitabbā*) because of their quarrelsome disposition. When they were sixteen years old the villagers obtained land for them from the king, founded a town, and married them together. Their country came to be called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjī</span>. They had sixteen pairs of twins, and their city had to be greatly enlarged – hence its name, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visālā</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>.
