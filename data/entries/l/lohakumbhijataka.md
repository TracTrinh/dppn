# Lohakumbhījātaka

## English source

Lohakumbhījātaka (Ja 314). The Bodhisatta was once an ascetic of great power. The king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, having heard at night four sounds – du, sa, na, so – uttered by four beings who dwelt in hell, was greatly frightened. When he consulted his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Purohita</span>, the latter ordained a fourfold sacrifice. The Bodhisatta, seeing the fate that lay in wait for numerous creatures, went to the king’s park and there, with the help of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Purohita</span>’s chief disciple – not himself in favour of the sacrifice – explained to the king the meaning of the sounds and had the sacrificial animals released. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Purohita</span>’s disciple is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>.

The story was told in reference to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, who one night heard four syllables uttered by four inhabitants of hell. These had once been nobles of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, guilty of adultery. After death they were born in four iron cauldrons. After sixty thousand years they had gradually come to the top of these cauldrons and had uttered these syllables in their attempt to proclaim their misery. The king, very frightened, consulted his priests, who ordered a sacrifice. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span> intervened and sent the king to see the Buddha, who explained the matter and allayed the king’s fears. Ja.iii.43-8; the stories, both of the past and present, appear also at Dhp­a.ii.5ff. and Pv-a.279ff. In both places the introductory story gives greater details.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> heard the sounds while lying sleepless, because he contemplated putting a helpless man to death in order to obtain his beautiful wife. There are also other differences in the two accounts. For details see Burlingame: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Buddhist Legends</span> ii.100, n.1.
