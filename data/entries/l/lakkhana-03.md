# Lakkhaṇa 03

## English source

Lakkhaṇa 03. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lakkhaṇa Thera</span>. Mentioned as having stayed with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Moggallāna</span> on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span>. Once, when they were going down to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> for alms, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lakkhaṇa</span> noticed that at a certain spot <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> smiled; on asking him why, he was told to wait till they saw the Buddha. When the question was repeated in the Buddha’s presence, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> said that he had smiled on seeing various *Petas* with gruesome forms flying through the air. From the text it would appear that these visions were seen again on several occasions. SN.ii.254; Vin.iii.104ff.; the stories of some of the *Petas* seen and of their past lives are given in detail in Dhp­a.ii.68ff.; iii.60ff.; 410ff., 479.

The Commentary (SN­a.ii.159) explains that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lakkhaṇa</span> was one of the thousand *Jaṭilas* ordained by the Buddha (when he converted the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tebhātikajaṭilas</span>). He attained *Arahant*-ship at the conclusion of the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ādittapariyāya</span> *desanā*. He was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lakkhaṇa</span> because of his marvellous personality, “like unto *Brahma*’s” (*Brahmasamena*). It adds further that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lakkhaṇa</span>’s failure to see the *Petas* was not because he lacked the divine eye but because he was not giving attention (*anāvajjento*), as a clairvoyant must. It is said (Vin.iii.105) that when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> related his vision, some of the monks blamed him for claiming superhuman powers (*uttarimanussadhamma*), but the Buddha declared him free from blame.
