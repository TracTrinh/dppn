# Lakuṇṭaka Bhaddiya Thera

## English source

Lakuṇṭaka Bhaddiya Thera. He was born in a wealthy family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> and was given the title of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Lakuṇṭaka</span> (Dwarf) owing to his very small stature. He was, nevertheless, beautiful in body, says the Ap­a.; but see below. Having heard the Buddha preach, he entered the Saṅgha and became learned and eloquent, teaching others in a sweet voice. Once, on a festival day, a woman of the town, driving with a Brahmin in a chariot, saw the Elder and laughed, showing her teeth. The Elder, taking the teeth as his object, developed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jhāna</span> and became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmī</span>. Later, after being admonished by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, he developed mindfulness regarding the body and became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> (vii.1, 2) makes reference to the admonitions of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and to the Buddha’s joy when these had the desired effect. The Commentary (Ud­a.360f.) gives details.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> he was a very rich householder of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsavatī</span>, and, having heard the Buddha describe one of his monks as the sweetest voiced among them all, he wished for a similar distinction for himself under a future Buddha. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phussa Buddha</span> he was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cittapattakokila</span>, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span> (the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">loc.infra</span> says he was the king’s general) who, seeing the Buddha in the royal park, placed in his bowl a ripe mango. In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> Buddha’s day he was the chief architect entrusted with the building of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thūpa</span> over the Buddha’s relics, and, when a dispute arose as to how big the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thūpa</span> should be, he decided in favour of a small one; hence his small stature in his last life. Thag­a.i.469ff.; Ap.ii.489f; the account in AN­a.i.110f. is slightly different; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Keḷisīlajātaka</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) gives a different reason for his shortness.

In the assembly of monks the Buddha ranked him as foremost among sweet voiced monks (AN.i.25) (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">mañjussarānaṁ</span>). Several stories connected with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span> are recorded in the books. Because of his shortness and his youthful appearance he was sometimes mistaken for a novice (Dhp­a.iii.387). Elsewhere it is said that, because he was ugly and hunch-backed, he was despised by his companions, and the Buddha had to proclaim to them his greatness and hold him up as an example of a man who, though small, was of great power (SN.ii.279; cp. Ud.vii.5). Another account relates how novices used to pull his hair and tweak his ears and nose saying, “Uncle, you tire not of religion? You take delight in it?” But he showed no resentment and took no offence. Dhp­a.ii.148; the introduction to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Keḷisīlajātaka</span> (Ja 202, Ja.ii.142), speaks of thirty monks from the country who, seeing <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, pulled him about until they were told by the Buddha who he was.

It was in reference to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span> that the Buddha preached two famous riddle stanzas in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada</span> (294, 295; for the explanation of the riddle see Dhp­a.iii.454), where he describes the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> as one who has killed father and mother and two kings and destroyed a kingdom, but who yet goes scatheless – the words having a metaphorical meaning. Several stanzas uttered by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambāṭakavana</span>, as he sat there enjoying the bliss of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship, are included in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> (Thag.vss.466-72). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Avadānaśataka</span> he is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Lakuñcika</span>. See Avś.ii.152-60.
