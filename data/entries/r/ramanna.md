# Rāmañña

## English source

Rāmañña. The Pāli name for Burma, referring particularly to the maritime provinces. After the conversion of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāmañña</span> to Buddhism, there was a constant intercourse between that country and Ceylon (so says also Cv.lxxvi.10f). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijayabāhu I</span>. sent an embassy to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāmañña</span>, and obtained from him learned and pious monks to re-establish the Saṅgha in Ceylon (Cv.lxviii.8; lx.5ff.; but see Cv.Trs.i.n.4).

The kings of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāmañña</span> seem to have been in the habit of giving a special maintenance to Sinhalese envoys sent to their country. The chief trade between the two countries was in elephants; the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāmañña</span> made a gift of an elephant to every vessel bringing gifts from foreign lands. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu I</span>., relations were strained between the two countries as a result of insults paid by the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāmañña</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu</span> sent a punitive expedition under the *Damiḷādhikārī*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ādicca</span>. This expedition started from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pallavaṅka</span>, and some of the forces landed at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusumī</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāmañña</span> and the others at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Papphālama</span>. It is said that in a battle fought at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkama</span>, the Sinhalese forces killed the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāmañña</span> king. Thereafter, through the intervention of the monks, peace was restored between the two countries, and the *Ramaṇas*, as the people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāmañña</span> were called, sent a yearly tribute to the king of Ceylon. For details of this expedition see Cv.lxxvi.10ff.; also Cv.Trs.ii.69, n.3.
