# Rohiṇī

## English source

Rohiṇī 03. A small river dividing the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyan</span> countries. A dam was constructed across the river, and the people on the two sides used the water to cultivate their fields. Once, in the month of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jeṭṭhamūla</span>, there was a drought, and a violent quarrel arose between the two peoples for the use of the water. A battle was imminent, when the Buddha, seeing what was about to happen, appeared in the air between the opposing forces in the middle of the river and convinced them of the folly of killing each other for the sake of a little water. It is said that he preached on this occasion the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Attadaṇḍasutta</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phandanajātaka</span> (Ja 475), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Laṭukikajātaka</span> (Ja 357) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṭṭakajātaka</span> (Ja 357).

To show their gratitude to the Buddha for his timely intervention, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyans</span> gave two hundred and fifty young men from each tribe to be ordained under him. Snp­a.i.358; cp. Ja.v.412; Dhp­a.iii.254ff. The accounts differ in details; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> account, which is the longest, mentions other Jātakas: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Daddabhajātaka</span> (Ja 322) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rukkhadhammajātaka</span> (Ja 74). DN­a.ii.672f. and SN­a.i.53ff substitute <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭhavudriyanajātaka</span> (??) for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Daddabhajātaka</span> (Ja 322). But see under these Jātakas.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohiṇī</span> is identified with a small stream which joins the Rapti at Goruckpore. It is now called the Rowai or Rohwaini. For details see Cunningham, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Archeological Survey of India</span> xii.190ff.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapāla</span> says (Thag­a.i.501) that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohiṇī</span> flows from north to south and that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> lies to the south-east of it.
