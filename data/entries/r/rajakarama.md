# Rājakārāma

## English source

Rājakārāma. A monastery in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, built by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> (Ja.ii.15). It was to the south-east of the city, corresponding to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thūpārāma</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> (MN­a.ii.1021). It was there that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandakovādasutta</span> was preached (MN.iii.271). This was probably the monastery built for nuns by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, at the Buddha’s suggestion, after the assault on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uppalavaṇṇā</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhavana</span>, referred to in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp­a.ii.52).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta</span> Commentary, however, gives a different account (SN­a.iii.218f.; the introductory story of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bharu Jātaka</span>; Ja.ii.170, gives the same account but omits the statement that the king built a *vihāra*). It states that the heretics, jealous of the Buddha and his popularity, desired to build a monastery for themselves in close proximity to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, and, in order that he might raise no objections, they presented <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> with one hundred thousand.

When the Buddha discovered their intentions, owing to the great uproar they made while preparing the preliminaries of the building, he sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> to the king, asking to have it stopped. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> refused to see him or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> (it was as a punishment for this discourtesy that he lost the throne before his death.) Thereupon the Buddha went himself. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> received him and entertained him to a meal, at the end of which the Buddha preached to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bharujātaka</span> (Ja 213, *q.v.*) on the evils of bribery and of creating an opportunity for virtuous people to quarrel among themselves. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> was filled with remorse; he had the heretics expelled, and, realizing that he had never built a monastery, proceeded to construct the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājakārāma</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (SN.v.360ff) contains several sermons preached by the Buddha at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājakārāma</span>.
