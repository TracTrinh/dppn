# Rāhula 01

## English source

Rāhula 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula Thera</span>. Only son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>. He was born on the day on which his father left the household life (Ja.i.60; AN­a.i.82, etc.; cf. Ja.i.62). When the Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> for the first time after his Awakening and accepted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span>’s invitation, Rāhula’s mother (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulamātā</span>) sent the boy to the Buddha to ask for his inheritance (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dāyajja</span>). The Buddha gave him no answer, and, at the conclusion of the meal, left the palace. Rāhula followed him, reiterating his request until at last the Buddha asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> to ordain him (according to Snp­a.i.340, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> taught him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kammavācā</span>; see also Ja.ii.393). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> heard of this he protested to the Buddha, and asked as a boon that, in future, no child should be ordained without the consent of his parents, and to this the Buddha agreed (Vin.i.82f.; the story of Rāhula’s conversion is also given at Dhp­a.i.98f).

It is said that immediately after Rāhula’s ordination the Buddha preached to him constantly (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">abhiṇhovādavasena</span>) many <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span> for his guidance (AN­a.i.145). Rāhula himself was eager to receive instruction from the Buddha and his teachers and would rise early in the morning and take a handful of sand, saying: “May I have today as many words of counsel from my teachers as there are here grains of sand!” The monks constantly spoke of Rāhula’s amenability, and one day the Buddha, aware of the subject of their talk, went amongst them and related the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tipallatthamigajātaka</span> (Ja 16, Ja.i.160ff) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tittirajātaka</span> (Ja 319, Ja.iii.64ff) to show them that in past births, too, Rāhula had been known for his obedience. When Rāhula was seven years old, the Buddha preached to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambalaṭṭhika Rāhulovādasutta</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) as a warning that he should never lie, even in fun. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span> used to accompany the Buddha on his begging rounds. Sometimes he would accompany <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> on his begging rounds. He was present when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> went to his (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>’s) mother’s house, where he was roundly abused by her for having left her. Dhp­a.iv.164f).

Rāhula noticed that he harboured carnal thoughts fascinated by his own physical beauty and that of his father, the Buddha preached to him, at the age of eighteen, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Rāhulovādasutta</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). Two other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span>, also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulovāda</span>, one included in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta</span> and the other in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttara</span> (see below), formed the topics for Rāhula’s meditation (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vipassanā</span>). To these <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (MN­a.i.635) adds the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sāmaṇerapañhā</span>, or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumārapañhā</span>, and proceeds to enumerate the different purposes which the Buddha had in view in preaching these <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span>; see also AN­a.ii.547. Snp­a.i.340 says, about the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulasutta</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), that the Buddha constantly preached it to Rāhula. See also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulasaṁyutta</span>.

Later, the Buddha, knowing that Rāhula’s mind was ripe for final attainment, went with him alone to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhavana</span>, and preached to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Rāhulovādasutta</span>. At the end of the discourse, Rāhula became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>, together with one hundred thousand crores of listening <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>. SN­a.iii.26 says these <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> were among those who, in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>, had heard Rāhula’s wish to be born as the son of a future Buddha. They were subsequently born in various <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span> worlds, but on this day they all assembled at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhavana</span> in order to be present at the fulfilment of Rāhula’s wish. This scene was one of the incidents sculptured in the Relic Chamber of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span>, as was also the ordination of Rāhula. Mhv.xxxi.81, 83.

Afterwards, in the assembly of monks, the Buddha declared Rāhula foremost among those of his disciples who were anxious for training (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sikkhākāmānaṁ</span>). AN.i.24; the Vinaya (iii.16) gives a story illustrating Rāhula’s extreme conscientiousness in the observance of rules. He arrived one evening at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>, when the Buddha was staying there in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Badarikārāma</span>. Rāhula was told there of a new rule which had been laid down to the effect that no novice should sleep under the same roof as a fully ordained monk. Unable to find any resting place which did not violate this rule, Rāhula spent the night in the Buddha’s jakes. When the Buddha discovered him there the next morning, he modified the rule. This incident and Rāhula’s keenness in observing rules are described again in greater detail at Ja.i.161f. There the Buddha is said to have found fault with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> for his neglect of Rāhula (see also Vin-a.iv.744). On another occasion, finding no place in which to sleep because monks who had arrived late had taken his sleeping place, Rāhula spent the night in the open, in front of the Buddha’s cell. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>, seeing him there, assumed the form of a huge elephant and trumpeted loudly, hoping to frighten him. But the plot failed. This was eight years after Rāhula had attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship (Dhp­a.iv.69f.).

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>, both Rāhula and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Raṭṭhapāla</span> were rich householders of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsavatī</span>, who, realizing the vanity of riches, gave all away to the poor. One day they entertained two ascetics of great power. The ascetic to whom Rāhula ministered was in the habit of visiting the abode of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> king, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭhavindhara</span>, and had been impressed by its magnificence. Therefore, in returning thanks to Rāhula for his hospitality, he wished that his host might resemble <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭhavindhara</span>. Rāhula remembered this, and after death he was born in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> world as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭhavindhara</span>, his friend being born as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>. He was, however, dissatisfied with his lot, and one day when, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Virūpakkha</span>, he was on a visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> recognized him, and finding out that he was dissatisfied, suggested to him a remedy. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭhavindhara</span> invited the Buddha to his abode. The Buddha, attended by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span> and one hundred thousand <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span>, came and was entertained by him. In the company of monks was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uparevata</span>, the Buddha’s son, seated next to him, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭhavindhara</span> was so fascinated by him that he could not take his eyes off him. Discovering who he was, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭhavindhara</span> expressed a wish that he, too, might be born as the son of a future Buddha. Later, in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>, Rāhula was born as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭhavindhara</span>, the eldest son of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kikī</span>, later becoming his viceroy. His seven sisters built seven residences for the Buddha, and, at their suggestion, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭhavindhara</span> built five hundred residences for the monks. The story of the past as given here is taken from AN­a.i.141ff.; part of it is given in MN­a.ii.722 under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Raṭṭhapāla</span>, but the account differs in details. There the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> world is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhumindhara</span>, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> king, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pālita</span>. Snp­a.i.341 differs again and calls the king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkha</span>. See also Thag­a.ii.30 on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Raṭṭhapāla</span>, where no mention is made of Rāhula. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (i. 60f.) gives a different version altogether. There Rāhula gave <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> a carpet (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t18">santhara</span>), as a result of which, twenty-one <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> ago, he was born as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Khattiya</span> named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vimala</span>, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Reṇuvatī</span>. There he lived in a palace, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudassana</span>, specially built for him by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span>.

Four verses uttered by Rāhula are included in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> (vs.295-98; Mil.413 contains several other stanzas attributed to Rāhula).

It is said that the news of Rāhula’s birth was brought to the Bodhisatta when he was enjoying himself in his pleasances on the banks of the royal pond after being decked by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span>. As soon as the news was announced, he made up his mind to renounce the world without delay, for he saw, in the birth of a son, a new bond attaching him to household life (“<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Rāhulajāto, bandhanaṁ jātaṁ</span>” the word <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t18">rāhula</span> meaning bond). Ja.i.60; Dhp­a.i.70. The Ap. Commentary, however, derives Rāhula from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhu</span>; just as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhu</span> obstructs the moon, so would the child be as obstruction to the Bodhisatta’s Renunciation.

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta</span> Commentaries (DN­a.ii.549; SN­a.iii.172), Rāhula predeceased the Buddha and even <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, and the place of his death is given as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>. For twelve years he never lay on a bed. (DN­a.iii.736).

In numerous Jātakas, Rāhula is mentioned as having been the Bodhisatta’s son – e.g., in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uraga</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapi</span> (Ja 250), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumbhakāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khaṇḍahāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Sutasoma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Daddara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhanāgāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makkaṭa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makhadeva</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sudassana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vidhurapaṇḍita</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīhakoṭṭhuka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇaka</span>. He was also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yaññadatta</span>, son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇḍavya</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>) and the young tortoise in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Ukkusa</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (ii.551) says that in many births <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="119" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uppalavaṇṇā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="120" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span> were born of the same parents (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="121" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ekasmiṁ sambhave</span>) and had similar tendencies (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="122" data-dppn-term-class="t18">samānacchandamānasā</span>).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="123" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span> was known to his friends as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="124" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulabhadda</span> (Rāhula, the Lucky). He himself says (Thag. vs. 295f) that he deserved the title because he was twice blest in being the son of the Buddha and an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="125" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> himself. Mention is often made in the books that, though Rāhula was his own son, the Buddha showed as much love for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="126" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="127" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgulimāla</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="128" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanapāla</span> as he did for Rāhula (Dhp­a.i.124; MN­a.i.537; Mil.410 attributes this statement to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="129" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>; Snp­a.i.202 expands it to include others).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="130" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> built a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="131" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thūpa</span> in honour of Rāhula, to be specially worshipped by novices. Beal-Xuan.i.180, 181.
