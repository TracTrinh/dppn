# Ratanasutta 01

## English source

Ratanasutta 01. One of the *Suttas* of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khuddakapāṭha</span>. It is also included in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span> (vss. 222-38); see also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgārohaṇasutta</span>. It was preached at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, on the occasion of the Buddha’s visit there at the invitation of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>, who begged him to rid the city of the various dangers which had fallen upon it. According to the Commentaries (Snp­a.i.278ff.; Dhp­a.iii.436ff.; Khp-a.164f), the Buddha first taught the *Sutta* to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> and asked him to go round the city, accompanied by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span> princes, reciting the *Sutta* and sprinkling water from the Buddha’s bowl. Immediately all the evil spirits fled from the city and the people recovered from their diseases. They then gathered at the Mote hall with various offerings and thither they conducted the Buddha. In the assembly were present not only all the inhabitants of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, but also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> of two *Deva* worlds, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> at their head. The Buddha preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratanasutta</span> to this great crowd. Another account, quoted by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (Dhp­a.iii.165), says that in the assembly the Buddha preached only the first five stanzas, the rest having been earlier recited by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>. Because this *Sutta* was first preached to ward off the evil from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, it became the most famous of Buddhist Ward runes (*Parittā*).

The *Sutta* consists of seventeen verses: the first two contain a request to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> to receive the homage and offerings of men and protect them in their danger; then follow twelve verses, descriptive of the virtues of the Buddha, the Dhamma and the Saṅgha. It ends with three verses purporting (Dhp­a.iii.195) to have been spoken by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> on behalf of all the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, expressing their adoration of the Buddha, his Dhamma and his Saṅgha. It is also said (Dhp­a.iii.196) that during this visit the Buddha stayed at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> for two weeks, preaching the *Sutta* on seven consecutive days; on each day eighty-four thousand beings realized the Truth. The *Sutta* seems also to have been known as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgārohaṇasutta</span> (Cv.xxxvii.191). When Ceylon was troubled by famine and plague in the reign of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upatissa II</span>., the king had the *Sutta* preached by monks while walking in the streets of the city. All troubles vanished, and he decreed that his successors should do likewise in times of need (Cv.xxxvii.195f). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sena II</span>. had the whole *Sutta* inscribed on a golden plate and held a great festival in its honour (Cv.li.79).

The *Sutta* is given in the *Mahā Vastu* (i.290ff), where it is described as *Svastyanagāthā*.
