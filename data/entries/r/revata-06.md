# Revata 06

## English source

Revata 06. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soreyya Revata</span>. He was one of the Elders who took a prominent part in the Second Council. He lived in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soreyya</span>, and, on discovering (by means of his divine ear) that the orthodox monks, led by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambhūta</span> Sānavāsī and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa Kākaṇḍakaputta</span>, were anxious to consult him, Revata left <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soreyya</span>, and, travelling through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇṇakujja</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udumbara</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggaḷapura</span>, reached <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahajāti</span>. There the monks met him and consulted him regarding the “Ten Points.”

He enquired into these, and, after condemning them as wrong, decided to end the dispute. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjiputtakas</span>, too, had tried to win Revata over to their side, but on failing to do so, persuaded Revata’s pupil, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span>, to accept robes, etc., from them, and speak to his teacher on their behalf. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span> did this, but was dismissed by Revata as an unworthy pupil.

Revata suggested that the dispute should be settled in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, and the monks having agreed, he visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabbakāmī</span> – who was the oldest monk then living and a pupil of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> (according to Mhv.iv.57, 60, Revata, himself was a pupil of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> and had seen the Buddha; cp. Dpv.iv.49) – during the night, and talked to him on matters of doctrine. During the conversation, Sānavāsī arrived and questioned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabbakāmī</span> regarding the Ten Points, but the latter refused to express an opinion in private. On Revata’s suggestion a jury of eight, four from either side, was appointed to go into the question. Revata himself was a member of this jury, and he it was who questioned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabbakāmī</span> during the meeting, held in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vālikārāma</span>, regarding the Ten Points. All the Ten Points were declared to be wrong, and, at the end of the questions, seven hundred monks – chosen from one hundred and twelve thousand, at the head of whom was Revata – held a recital of the Dhamma, which recital therefore came to be called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sattasatī</span> (“Seven Hundred”). This recital, according to the Mhv., lasted for eight months. Vin.ii.299ff.

The Mahāvastu iv.1ff gives an account of this Council, which account differs in numerous details. In both accounts it is Revata who takes the most prominent part in settling the dispute. The Mahāvastu introduces <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷāsoka</span> as the patron of the Second Council; cp. Dpv.iv.46ff.; v.15ff.; Vin-a.i.33f.; it would appear from the Dpv. account that the heretics refused to accept the decision of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span>’s Council and separated off, to the number of ten thousand, forming a new body called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Saṅghikas</span>.
