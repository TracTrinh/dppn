# Rāhulamātā

## English source

Rāhulamātā. The name, generally given in the texts, of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span>’s mother (e.g., Vin.i.82) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>’s wife. She is also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddakaccā</span> (e.g., Bv.xxvi.15) and, in later texts, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasodharā</span> (Bv­a., p.245; Divy.253), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbādevī</span> (Ja.ii.392f.; DN­a.ii.422) and, probably, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbāsundarī</span> (Ja.vi.478 12). Mhv.ii.24 calls her <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddakaccānā</span>; but see Thomas: *Life and Legend of the Buddha*, 49; she is also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhaddakā</span>, this being probably a variant of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddakaccānā</span>.

The Northern texts seem to favour the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasodharā</span>, but they call her the daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Daṇḍapānī</span> (see also Rockhill, op.cit., where various other names are given as well). It is probable that the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>’s wife was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbā</span>, and that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddakaccā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhaddakā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasodharā</span> and the others, were descriptive epithets applied to her, which later became regarded as additional names. It is also possible that in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>’s court there was also a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasodharā</span>, daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Daṇḍapānī</span>, and that there was a later confusion of names. The Commentarial explanation (e.g., AN­a.i.204), that she was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddakaccānā</span> because her body was the colour of burnished gold, is probably correct. To suggest (e.g., Thomas: *Life and Legend of the Buddha*, 49) that the name bears any reference to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccānagotta</span> seems to be wrong, because the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāna</span> was a Brahmin *gotta* and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> were not Brahmins.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulamātā</span> was born on the same day as the Bodhisatta (Ja.i.54; Bv­a. 106, 228). The following account is taken chiefly from Ja.i.58ff: She married him (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>) at the age of sixteen, and was placed at the head of forty thousand women, given to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span>, after he had proved his manly prowess to their satisfaction. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> left the household life on the day of the birth of his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span> (according to one account, referred to in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka Commentary</span>, i.62, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span> was seven days old). It is said that just before he left home he took a last look at his wife from the door of her room, not daring to go nearer, lest he should awake her. When the Buddha paid his first visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> after the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>, and on the second day of that visit, he begged in the street for alms. This news spread, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulamātā</span> looked out of her window to see if it were true. She saw the Buddha, and was so struck by the glory of his personality that she uttered eight verses in its praise. These verses have been handed down under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Narasīhagāthā</span>; on that day, after the Buddha had finished his meal in the palace, which he took at the invitation of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span>, all the ladies of the court, with the exception of Rāhulamātā, went to pay him obeisance. She refused to go, saying that if she had any virtue in her the Buddha would come to her. The Buddha went to her with his two Chief Disciples and gave orders that she should be allowed to greet him as she wished. She fell at his feet, and clasping them with her hands, put her head on them. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> related to the Buddha how, from the time he had left home, Rāhulamātā had herself abandoned all luxury and had lived in the same manner as she had heard that the Buddha lived – wearing yellow robes, eating only once a day, etc. And the Buddha then related the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candakinnarajātaka</span> (Ja 485), to show how, in the past, too, her loyalty had been supreme.

On the seventh day of the Buddha’s visit, when he left the palace at the end of his meal, Rāhulamātā sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span> to him saying, “That is your father, go and ask him for your inheritance.” <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span> followed the Buddha, and, at the Buddha’s request, was ordained by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>. The account of this event is given in Vin.i.82; this is probably the only passage in the *Piṭakas* where Rāhulamātā, is mentioned by name.

Later, when the Buddha allowed women to join the Saṅgha, Rāhulamātā became a nun under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Pajāpatī Gotamī</span> (AN­a.i.198).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> identifies (AN­a.i.204f) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulamātā</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddakaccānā</span> who, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (AN.i.25), is mentioned as chief among nuns in the possession of supernormal powers (*mahābhiññappattānaṁ*). She was one of the four disciples of the Buddha who possessed such attainment, the others being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bakkula</span>. She expressed her desire for this achievement in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>. In this account <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddakaccānā</span> is mentioned as the daughter of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan Suppabuddha</span> and his wife <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amitā</span>. Cf. Mhv.ii.21f. It is said that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppabuddha</span> did not forgive the Buddha for leaving his daughter (Dhp­a.iii.44f); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddakaccānā</span>’s sister, and it has been suggested that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>’s enmity against the Buddha was for reasons similar to her father’s. She joined the Saṅgha under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī Gotamī</span> in the company of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janapadakaḷyānī Nandā</span>, and in the Saṅgha she was known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddakaccānā</span> Therī. Later, she developed insight and became an *Arahant*. She could, with one effort, recall one *asaṅkheyya* and one hundred thousand *kappas* (AN­a.i.205).

In the Therī <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (Ap.ii.584ff) an account is found of a Therī, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasodharā</span> by name, who is evidently to be identified with Rāhulamātā, because she speaks of herself (vvs. 10, 11) as the Buddha’s *pajāpatī* before he left the household (*agāra*), and says that she was the chief (*pāmokkhā sabba-issarā*) of ninety thousand women.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīpaṅkara Buddha</span>, when the Bodhisatta was born as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumedha</span>, she was a Brahmin maiden, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumittā</span> by name, and gave eight handfuls of lotuses to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumedha</span>, which he, in turn, offered to the Buddha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīpaṅkara</span>, in declaring that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumedha</span> would ultimately become the Buddha, added that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumittā</span> would be his companion in several lives. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> account (vvs. 1ff) mentions how, just before her death, at the age of seventy-eight, she took leave of the Buddha and performed various miracles. It also states that eighteen thousand *Arahant* nuns, companions of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasodharā</span>, also died on the same day (Ap.ii.592f).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abbhantarajātaka</span> (Ja 281) mentions that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbādevī</span> (who is called the chief wife of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> and is therefore evidently identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulamātā</span>) was once, after becoming a nun, ill from flatulence. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span>, as was his custom, came to visit her, he was told that he could not see her, but that, when she had suffered from the same trouble at home, she had been cured by mango juice with sugar. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span> reported the matter to his preceptor, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, who obtained the mango juice from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> discovered why the mango juice had been needed, he arranged that from that day it should be regularly supplied. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> relates how, in a past birth too, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> had come to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulamātā</span>’s rescue. Ja.ii.392f.; cf. the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Supattajātaka</span> (Ja 292), where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span>’s request, obtained for her from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> rice with ghee, flavoured with red fish. This was for abdominal pain (Ja.ii.433).

Numerous stories are found in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka Commentary</span> in which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulamātā</span> is identified with one or other of the characters – e.g.,

- the queen consort in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Abbhantara</span> (Ja 281),
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sammillabhāsinī</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Ananusociya</span> (Ja 328),
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Samuddavijayā</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Āditta</span> (Ja 424),
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Udayabhaddā</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Udaya</span> (Ja 458),
- the potter’s wife (? <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Bhaggavī</span>) in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kumbhakāra</span> (Ja 408),
- the queen in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kummāsa</span> (Ja 416),
- the queen consort in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kurudhamma</span> (Ja 276),

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="136" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulasaṁyutta</span>. The eighteenth section of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="137" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span>. It consists of a series of lessons given by the Buddha to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="138" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span>, showing him the fleeting nature of all things (SN.ii.244-56). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="139" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (MN­a.ii.635f) that these *Suttas* were preached on various occasions, from the time <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="140" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span> entered the Saṅgha, to the time of his attainment of *Arahant*-ship. They contain mention of qualities which mature emancipation, *vimuttiparipācanīyadhammā* (SN­a.ii.159).
