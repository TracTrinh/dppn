# Rūpasārī

## English source

Rūpasārī, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Surūpasārī</span>. Mother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, who was called after her, his personal name being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upatissa</span> (Snp­a.i.326; Dhp­a.i.73, etc.; in Sanskrit texts (e.g., Divy.395) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sāradvatīputra</span>). Her husband was the brahmim <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅganta</span> (Dhp­a.ii.84), and she became the mother of seven children, all of whom became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span> – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upasena</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Cunda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata Khadiravaniya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cālā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upacālā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sisūpacālā</span> (Dhp­a.ii.188; SN­a.iii.172).

Both she and her husband were unbelievers, and she was very sad when, one after another, her children, giving up wealth worth eighty crores, joined the Saṅgha. She wished to keep at least the youngest of the boys, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span>, for herself, and had him married at the age of seven, but her plot miscarried (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span>). This embittered her against the monks, and, though she gave them alms when they came to the house, she blamed them for having enticed her children away.

Once when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> visited her with five hundred monks, among whom was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span>, she invited them in and gave them food, but did not fail to abuse her son, calling him “eater of leavings” (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ucchiṭṭhakhādaka</span>) (Dhp­a.iv.164f). She outlived <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, who visited her just before his death, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālakagāma</span>, in the house where he was born. There she provided lodging for him and his five hundred companions. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> fell ill of a violent attack of dysentery on the night of his arrival, and she saw various gods, including even <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span>, come to wait on him. Learning their identity from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Cunda</span>, she was amazed and went to see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> to have <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Cunda</span>’s words confirmed. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> told her how <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span> was a follower of the Buddha and talked to her about the marvellous virtues of his teacher. At the end of his talk, she became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> died the next day at dawn, and she made elaborate arrangements for his cremation (SN­a.iii.172ff.; for details see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>). She seems to have also been called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Surūpasārī</span>, e.g., Thīg­a.162.
