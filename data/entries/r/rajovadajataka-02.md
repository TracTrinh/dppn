# Rājovādajātaka 02

## English source

Rājovādajātaka 02. (Ja 334). Once the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, wishing to discover if he ruled justly, traveled about in disguise, and, in the course of his wanderings, came to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span>, where the Bodhisatta lived as an ascetic. The ascetic gave him ripe figs, and, when asked why they were so sweet, explained that the king of the country was evidently a just ruler. The king returned to his kingdom and ruled for a while unjustly; and returning again to the hermitage, he found that the figs had become bitter.

The story was related to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, in order to show the importance of a king ruling wisely and justly. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> is identified with the king of the story. Ja.iii.110-12; cp. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kapijātaka</span> (Ja 407).
