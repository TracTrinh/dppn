# Raṭṭhapāla 01

## English source

Raṭṭhapāla 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Raṭṭhapāla Thera</span>. Chief of those who had left the world through faith (*saddhāpabbajitānaṁ*) (AN.i.24). He was born at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thullakoṭṭhita</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuru</span> country as the son of a very wealthy councillor and was called by his family name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Raṭṭhapāla</span>. Given to the family because it retrieved the fortunes of a disrupted kingdom, says the Commentary. He lived in great luxury, and, in due course, married a suitable wife. When the Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thullakoṭṭhita</span>, Raṭṭhapāla went to hear him preach and decided to leave the world. His parents would not, however, give their consent till he threatened to starve himself to death. Realizing then that he was in earnest, they agreed to let him go on condition that he would visit them after his ordination. Raṭṭhapāla accompanied the Buddha to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, and there, dwelling alone, he attained *Arahant*-ship within a short time (but MN­a.ii.725 says he took twelve years, during which time he never slept on a bed, DN­a.iii.236). Then, with the Buddha’s permission, he returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thullakoṭṭhita</span> and dwelt in the deer park of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuru</span> king. The day after his arrival, while begging for alms, he came to his father’s house. His father was in the entrance hall having his hair combed, but, failing to recognize his son, he started to abuse him, taking him for an ordinary monk, one of those who had robbed him of his son. Just at that moment the slave girl of the house was about to throw away some stale rice, which Raṭṭhapāla begged of her. The girl recognized his voice, gave him the rice and told his parents who he was. When his father came to look for his son, he found him eating stale rice as though it were ambrosia (this eating of stale rice made of him an *agga-ariyavaṁsika*, Vin-a.i.208; MN­a.ii.726). Having already finished eating, when invited to enter the house, he would not do so, but on the next day he went again, and his father tried to tempt him by making a display of the immense wealth which would be his should he return to the lay life, while his former wives, beautifully clothed, asked him about the nymphs, for whose sake he led the homeless life. “For the sake of no nymphs, Sisters,” he said, and they fell fainting under the shock of being addressed as “Sisters.” Growing impatient at the conduct of his family, he asked for his meal, ate it, preached to them on the impermanence of all things, the futility of wealth, the snare of beauty, etc., and returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migācīra</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says that according to the Commentators of India, *parasamuddavāsītherānaṁ*, he preached standing; the stanzas so preached are given in MN.i.64f. and again in Thag.769-75). Through the air, says the Commentary (Thag­a.ii.34; MN­a.ii.730), because his father put bolts on the house and tried to keep him there. He also sent men to remove his yellow robes and clothe him in white.

There the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuru</span> king, who was feasting there, and had often heard of Raṭṭhapāla’s fame, visited him. Their conversation is recorded in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Raṭṭhapālasutta</span>. Raṭṭhapāla then returned to the Buddha. Raṭṭhapāla’s story is given in MN.ii.54ff.; MN­a.ii.722; Thag­a.ii.30ff.; AN­a.i.144ff., cp. Avś. ii.118ff.; Mhv.iii.41, n.1.

In a previous birth, before the appearance of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>, Raṭṭhapāla was one of two rich householders of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsavatī</span>, both of whom spent their wealth in good deeds. They once waited on two companies of ascetics from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span>; the ascetics left, but their leaders remained, and the two householders looked after them till they died. After death, one of them (Raṭṭhapāla) was reborn as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, while the other was born as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pālita</span> (*v.l.* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭhavindhara</span>), who, in this Buddha age, became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span>. At <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s request, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pālita</span> gave alms to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span> and wished to be like the Buddha’s son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uparevata</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> himself entertained the Buddha and his monks for seven days and wished to resemble the monk Raṭṭhapāla, whom <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> had declared to be foremost among those who had joined the Saṅgha through faith. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span> declared that the wish of both would be fulfilled in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>. MN­a.ii.722; Thag­a.ii.30 differs in many details; it makes no mention of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pālita</span>, and says that in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span>’s time, too, the householder’s name was Raṭṭhapāla. The name of the monk, disciple of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span>, whose example incited the householder to wish for similar honour, is not given. This account adds that in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phussa Buddha</span> (*q.v.*) he was one of those in charge of the almsgiving held in the Buddha’s honour by his three step-brothers (see also AN­a.i.143f.). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākha</span> were his colleagues (AN­a.i.165). The Ap.i.63f is again different. It says that in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span>’s time the householder gave the Buddha an elephant with all its trappings, and then, buying it back, built with the money a *saṅghārāma* containing fifty-four thousand rooms. As a result he was king of the gods fifty times and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span> fifty-eight times. AN­a.i.141 gives the story at greater length, some of the minor details varying.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Raṭṭhapāla</span> is mentioned with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa</span> *seṭṭhiputta* as one who enjoyed great luxury as a householder (e.g., Snp­a.i.232; at AN­a.ii.596 <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span>’s name is added). He is an example of one who attained to the higher knowledge through resolution (*chandaṁ dhuraṁ katvā*) (DN­a.ii.642; SN­a.iii.201; Vibh­a.306; Dhp­a.iv.195). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vinayapiṭaka</span> contains a stanza quoted by the Buddha, in which Raṭṭhapāla’s father enquires of his son why the latter never asked him for anything (Vin.iii.148; Raṭṭhapāla is here called a *kulaputta*. The incident probably refers to his lay life). “Because begging is a degrading thing,” says Raṭṭhapāla.
