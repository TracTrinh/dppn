# Rathavinītasutta

## English source

Rathavinītasutta. The twenty-fourth *Sutta* of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhimanikāya</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> visits <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa Mantāṇiputta</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhavana</span> and asks him why he lives the higher life. To attain absolute *Nibbāna*, answers <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span>, and, on being questioned further as to the nature of *Nibbāna*, he explains that *Nibbāna* is the goal and can only be attained by passing through various successive stages: purity of life, purity of heart, purity of view, purity through dispelling doubts, purity through full insight into paths, right and wrong, into the path to be followed, and the purity which arises from insight. It may be compared to a journey of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span>, by means of relays of seven carriages.

It is said in the introduction to the *Sutta* that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> had been awaiting the opportunity of a discussion with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span> ever since he heard the monks at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> speak of him to the Buddha in terms of the highest praise. But this opportunity did not arise until later, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span> visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span> was unaware of the identity of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> until the end of his discourse (MN.i.145-51).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsaṭīkā</span> mentions (Mhv­ṭ.552f) that once *Ambapāsāṇavāsī* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittagutta</span> preached this *Sutta* to a very large assembly of monks and nuns at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>, and in his exposition of the *Sutta* included a short account of the relics enshrined in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span>.

It has been suggested by Dr. Neumann that the *upatisapasina* mentioned in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s Bhabru Edict, refers to this *Sutta*. But see Rhys Davids, JRAS.1893, and Mukherji, *Asoka*, 118f., n.8.
