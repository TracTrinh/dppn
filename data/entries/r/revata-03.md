# Revata 03

## English source

Revata 03. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata Khadiravaniya</span>. An *Arahant* Thera. An eminent disciple of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>, declared by him foremost among forest dwellers (*araññakānaṁ*) (AN.i.24). He was the youngest brother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, and a marriage was arranged for him by his mother who was miserable at seeing her children desert her one after another to join the Saṅgha, and wished to keep the youngest at home. He was only seven years old, and, on the wedding day, the relations of both bride and bridegroom showered blessings on the couple and said to the bride: “May you live as long as your grandmother.” Revata asked to see the grandmother, and was shown a woman of one hundred and twenty, decrepit, and showing all the signs of advanced old age. Realizing that his wife would probably share the same fate, he left the bridal procession on some pretext on the way home, and ran away to a place where some monks lived. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, foreseeing this, had instructed the monks to ordain his brother without reference to his parents, and, when Revata revealed his identity, the monks at once admitted him into the Saṅgha.

When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> heard this, he wished to visit his brother, but was persuaded by the Buddha to wait. Revata, after waiting a long time for the visit from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, obtained from his teachers a formula of meditation and himself set out to see the Buddha. On the way he stopped at a *khadiravana* (acacia forest) during the rainy season and there won *Arahant*-ship.

At the end of the rains the Buddha, accompanied by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, with five hundred other monks, started out to visit Revata. There were two routes leading to the *khadiravana*, of which the shorter was thirty leagues long, straight, but infested with evil spirits. This the Buddha chose because <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalī</span> Thera was in the company of monks, and the Buddha knew that the deities of the forest would provide the monks with all they needed because of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalī</span>’s presence. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span> knew that the Buddha was approaching, he created, by his magic power, splendid dwellings for him and his monks. The Buddha spent two months in the forest and then returned to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pubbārāma</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>. There he found that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> had heard contradictory accounts of the dwelling erected by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span> for the monks who had accompanied the Buddha. He dispelled <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>’s doubts and spoke of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span>’s powers. Dhp­a.ii.188ff.; it was on this occasion that the Buddha related the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalī</span>’s past; see also Dhp­a.iv.186f.

One of the stanzas (No. 212), of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Munisutta</span> was also preached to the monks, according to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (Snp­a.i.261f.), in connection with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span>. This was immediately after the Buddha’s talk to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>, mentioned above. The story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span>’s ordination is also given at AN­a.i.126ff., with some variations in detail. The account given in Thag­a.i.108ff. is much shorter; no mention is made of the Buddha’s visit to the *khadiravana*. Here it is said that, after winning *Arahant*-ship, Revata went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> to greet the Buddha and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>.

Some time after, Revata returned to his native village and brought away with him his three nephews, sons of his three sisters, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cālā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upacālā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sisūpacālā</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> heard of this and went to see Revata. Revata knowing that he was coming, exhorted his nephews to be particularly heedful, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> expressed his pleasure at their behaviour. Thag­a.i.110; his admonitory verse is given at Thag.vs.43; two verses uttered by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> in praise of Revata are given at Thag.vss.991-2.

The Thag­a.i.551f mentions another incident which took place during Revata’s old age. He was in the habit of visiting the Buddha and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> from time to time after returning to his home in the *khadiravana*. Once, during a visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, he stayed in a forest near the city. The police, on the track of some thieves, came upon him, and, finding him near the booty which the thieves had dropped in their flight, arrested him and brought him before the king. When the king questioned him, the Elder spoke a series of verses (Thag.646-58; Mrs. Rhys Davids speaks of Revata as a teacher of the Jain doctrine of *ahiṁsā*, *Gotama the Man*, p.116), demonstrating the impossibility of his committing such an act, and also by way of teaching the king the Dhamma. It is said (Thag­a.l.555) that at the conclusion of the stanzas he sat cross-legged in the sky until his body burnt itself out.

Revata loved solitude, and, on one occasion (Dhp­a.iii.325f), a lay disciple named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atula</span>, hearing that he was in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, went with five hundred others to hear him preach. But Revata said that he delighted in solitude and refused to address them, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atula</span> went away complaining.

Revata’s delight in solitude was sometimes misunderstood. For instance, the Elder <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sammuñjani</span> went about continually sweeping, and, seeing Revata sitting cross-legged, thought him an idler. Revata read his thoughts and admonished him.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>, Revata was a boatman at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Payāga</span> on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span>, and once took the Buddha and his thousand followers across the river in a boat decked with canopies, flowers, etc. On that occasion he heard the Buddha declare one of the monks highest among forest dwellers, and wished for a similar honour for himself under a future Buddha (Thag­a.i.108; AN.A.i.126). Later, he was born in *Deva* worlds. Fifty-eight *kappas* ago he was a king named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāraṇa</span>, and a *kappa* later another king named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Campaka</span>. Ap.i.51f.
