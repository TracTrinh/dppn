# Roja 01

## English source

Roja 01. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malla</span>, inhabitant of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>. When the Buddha and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malla</span> chieftains decreed that whoever failed to pay homage to the Buddha would be fined five hundred coins. Roja was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>’s friend, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> was pleased when he arrived to pay homage to the Buddha, but when Roja said that he did so only out of regard for his kinsmen’s decree, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> was bitterly disappointed and asked the Buddha to discover some means by which Roja could be made to become his follower. The Buddha agreed to do this, and by means of the power of his compassion, Roja was induced to visit him again. The Buddha preached to Roja, who asked, as a boon, that the monks should accept hospitality only from him. This request was refused by the Buddha, who said that Roja must take his turn with others in showing hospitality to him and his monks. Finding that he had long to wait for his turn, Roja made enquiries, and, discovering that the monks had no supply of green vegetables (*dāka*) or pastry (*pittakhādaniya*), he consulted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, and, with the Buddha’s sanction, offered these things to the Buddha and his monks (Vin.i.247ff). It is said (Ja.ii.231f) that Roja once invited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> to his house, and, after entertaining him lavishly, tried to induce him to leave the Saṅgha by offering him half his wealth. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> refused this offer, explaining to him the miseries involved in household life. Later, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> repeated this conversation to the Buddha, who related the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vacchanakhajātaka</span> (Ja 235, *q.v.*) to show that Roja and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> had been friends in a past life too.

Once Roja forced on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> a linen cloth (*khomapilotikā*); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> had need of it, and accepted it with the Buddha’s permission (Vin.i.296).
