# Rājagaha

## English source

Rājagaha. A city, the capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>. There seem to have been two distinct towns; the older one, a hill fortress, more properly called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Giribbaja</span>, was very ancient and is said to have been laid out by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Govinda</span>, a skilled architect (Vv-a.p.82; but cp. DN.ii.235, where seven cities are attributed to his foundation). The later town, at the foot of the hills, was evidently built by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>.

Xuanzang says (Beal-Xuan, ii.145) that the old capital occupied by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> was called Kuśāgra. It was afflicted by frequent fires, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, on the advice of his ministers, abandoned it and built the new city on the site of the old cemetery. The building of this city was hastened on by a threatened invasion by the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>. The city was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> because <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> was the first person to occupy it. Both Xuanzang and Faxian (Giles: 49) record another tradition which ascribed the foundation of the new city to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>.

Pargiter suggests that the old city was called Kuśāgrapura, after Kuśāgra, an early king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> (*Ancient Indian Historical Tradition*, p.149). In the *Rāmāyaṇa* (i. 7, 32) the city is called Vasumatī. The *Mahā Bhārata* gives other names – Bārhadrathapura (ii.24, 44), Varāha, Vṛṣabha, Ṛṣigiri, Caityaka (see PHAI.,p.70). It was also called Bimbisārapurī and Magadhapura (Snp­a.ii.584).

But both names were used indiscriminately (e.g., SN.N. vs. 405), though <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Giribbaja</span> seems, as a name, to have been restricted to verse passages. The place was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Giribbaja</span> (mountain stronghold) because it was surrounded by five hills – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍava</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vebhāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isigili</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepulla</span> – and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, because it was the seat of many kings, such as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mandhātā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Govinda</span> (Snp­a.ii.413). At Snp­a.ii.382 it is said (MN.iii.68) that these hills, with the exception of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isigili</span>, were once known by other names e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅkaka</span> for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepulla</span> (SN.ii.191). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta</span> (i.206) mentions another peak near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indakūṭa</span>. See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷasilā</span>. It would appear, from the names given of the kings, that the city was a very ancient royal capital. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vidhurapaṇḍitajātaka</span> (Ja 545, Ja.vi.271), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> is called the capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span>. This evidently refers to a time when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span> had subjugated <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>.

The Commentaries (e.g., Snp­a. loc.cit.) explain that the city was inhabited only in the time of Buddhas and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span> kings; at other times it was the abode of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> who used it as a pleasure resort in spring. The country to the north of the hills was known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇāgiri</span> (SN­a.i.188).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> was closely associated with the Buddha’s work. He visited it soon after the Renunciation, journeying there on foot from the River <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anomā</span>, a distance of thirty leagues (Ja.i.66). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> saw him begging in the street, and, having discovered his identity and the purpose of his quest, obtained from him a promise of a visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> as soon as his aim should be achieved (see the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pabbajjāsutta</span> and its Commentary). During the first year after the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> therefore, the Buddha went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā</span>, after the conversion of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tebhātikajaṭilas</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> and his subjects gave the Buddha a great welcome, and the king entertained him and a large following of monks in the palace. It is said that on the day of the Buddha’s entry into the royal quarters, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> led the procession, in the guise of a young man, singing songs of praise of the Buddha. It was during this visit that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> gifted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span> to the Saṅgha and that the Buddha received <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> as his disciples. (Details of this visit are given in Vin.i.35ff). Large numbers of householders joined the Saṅgha, and people blamed the Buddha for breaking up their families. But their censure lasted for only seven days. Among those ordained were the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sattarasavaggiyā</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli</span> at their head.

The Buddha spent his first *vassa* in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> and remained there during the winter and the following summer. The people grew tired of seeing the monks everywhere, and, on coming to know of their displeasure, the Buddha went first to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇāgiri</span> and then to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> (Vin.i.77ff).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhavaṁsa Commentary</span> (p.13), the Buddha spent also in Rājagaha the third, fourth, seventeenth and twentieth *vassa*. After the twentieth year of his teaching, he made <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> his headquarters, though he seems frequently to have visited and stayed at Rājagaha. It thus became the scene of several important *Suttas* – e.g., the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āṭānāṭiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udumbarika</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapasīhanāda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sakuladāyī</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakkapañha</span>. For other incidents in the Buddha’s life connected with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>. The most notable of these was the taming of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālāgiri</span>.

Many of the Vinaya rules were enacted at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. Just before his death, the Buddha paid a last visit there. At that time, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> was contemplating an attack on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjians</span>, and sent his minister, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span>, to the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span>, to find out what his chances of success were (DN.ii.72). After the Buddha’s death, Rājagaha was chosen by the monks, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> at their head, as the meeting place of the First Convocation. This took place at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sattapaṇṇiguhā</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> extended to the undertaking his whole-hearted patronage (Vin.ii.285; Vin-a.i.7f.; DN­a.i.8f., etc.). The king also erected at Rājagaha a cairn over the relics of the Buddha, which he had obtained as his share (DN.ii.166). According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsa</span> (Mhv.xxxi.21; Mhv­ṭ. 564), some time later, acting on the suggestion of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span>, the king gathered at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> seven *doṇas* of the Buddha’s relics which had been deposited in various places – excepting those deposited at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāmagāma</span> – and built over them a large *Thūpa*. It was from there that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> obtained relics for his *vihāras*.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> was one of the six chief cities of the Buddha’s time, and as such, various important trade routes passed through it. The others cities were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Campā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bārāṇasī</span> (DN.ii.147).

The road from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> was one hundred and ninety-two leagues long and passed through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, which was forty-five leagues from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. This road passed by the gates of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> (MN­a.ii.987; SN­a.i.243). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārāyaṇavagga</span> (Snp. vss.1011-3) mentions a long and circuitous route, taken by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span>’s disciples in going from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Patiṭṭhāna</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, passing through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māhissati</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gonaddha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vedisā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vanasavhaya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setavyā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>, on to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, by way of the usual places (see below).

From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> was sixty leagues (AN­a.i.115; MN­a.i.360). From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> was a distance of twenty-five leagues (DN­a.ii.609), and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Parinibbānasutta</span> (DN.ii.72ff) gives a list of the places at which the Buddha stopped during his last journey along that road – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambalaṭṭhikā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālandā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaligāma</span> (where he crossed the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span>), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṭigāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nādikā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="119" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="120" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaṇḍagāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="121" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthigāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="122" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambagāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="123" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambugāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="124" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhoganagara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="125" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span>, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="126" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakuṭṭha</span> River, beyond which lay the Mango grove and the *Sāla* grove of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="127" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span>.

From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="128" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="129" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span> was a distance of five leagues, and when the Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="130" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> at the invitation of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="131" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>, the kings on either side of the river vied with each other to show him honour. Dhp­a.iii.439f.; also Mvu.i.253ff.; according to *Divyāvadāna* (p.55) the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="132" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span> had to be crossed between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="133" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="134" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, as well, by boat, some of the boats belonging to the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="135" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> and others to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="136" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="137" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>.

The distance between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="138" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="139" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālandā</span> is given as one league, and the Buddha often walked between the two (DN­a.i.35).

The books mention various places besides <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="140" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span>, with its <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="141" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalandakanivāpa</span> *vihāra* in and around <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="142" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="143" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sītavana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="144" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span>’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="145" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambavana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="146" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pipphaliguhā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="147" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udumbarikārāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="148" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moranivāpa</span> with its *Paribbājakārāma*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="149" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tapodārāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="150" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indasālaguhā</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="151" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vediyagiri</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="152" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sattapaṇṇiguhā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="153" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Laṭṭhivana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="154" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maddakucchi</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="155" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Supatiṭṭhacetiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="156" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāsāṇakacetiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="157" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sappasoṇḍikapabbhāra</span> and the pond <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="158" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumāgadhā</span>.

At the time of the Buddha’s death, there were eighteen large monasteries in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="159" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> (Vin-a.i.9). Close to the city flowed the rivers <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="160" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tapodā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="161" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sappinī</span>. In the city was a Potter’s Hall where travelers from far distances spent the night, e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="162" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span> (MN­a.ii.987); it had also a Town Hall (Ja.iv.72). The city gates were closed every evening, and after that it was impossible to enter the city. Vin.iv.116f.; the city had thirty-two main gates and sixty-four smaller entrances (DN­a.i.150; MN­a.ii.795). One of the gates of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="163" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="164" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Taṇḍulapāla</span> (MN.ii.185). Round <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="165" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> was a great *Peta* world (MN­a.ii.960; SN­a.i.31).

In the Buddha’s time there was constant fear of invasion by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="166" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="167" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span> (*q.v.*) is mentioned as having strengthened its fortifications. To the north-east of the city were the Brahmin villages of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="168" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambasaṇḍā</span> (DN.ii.263) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="169" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sālindiyā</span> (Ja.iii.293); other villages are mentioned in the neighborhood, such as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="170" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kīṭāgiri</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="171" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upatissagāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="172" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷitagāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="173" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhakavinda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="174" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakkhara</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="175" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Codanāvatthu</span> (*q.v.*). In the Buddha’s time, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="176" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> had a population of eighteen crores, nine in the city and nine outside, and the sanitary conditions were not of the best. SN­a.i.241; Dhp­a.ii.43; it was because of the city’s prosperity that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="177" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mettiyabhummajakas</span> made it their headquarters (Vin-a.iii.614). The city was not free from plague (Dhp­a.i.232).

The Treasurer of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="178" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="179" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span> had married each other’s sisters, and it was while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="180" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span> (*q.v.*) was on a visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="181" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> that he first met the Buddha.

The people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="182" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, like those of most ancient cities, held regular festivals; one of the best known of these was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="183" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Giraggasamajjā</span> (*q.v.*). Mention is also made of troupes of players visiting the city and giving their entertainments for a week on end (see, e.g., the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="184" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uggasena</span>).

Soon after the death of the Buddha, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="185" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> declined both in importance and prosperity. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="186" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Susunāga</span> transferred the capital to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="187" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="188" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷāsoka</span> removed it again to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="189" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span>, which, even in the Buddha’s time, was regarded as a place of strategic importance. When Xuanzang visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="190" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, he found it occupied by Brahmins and in a very dilapidated condition (Beal-Xuan, op.cit., ii.167). For a long time, however, it seems to have continued as a center of Buddhist activity, and among those mentioned as having been present at the foundation of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="191" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> were eighty thousand monks from Rājagaha led by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="192" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indagutta</span>. Mhv.xxix.30.
