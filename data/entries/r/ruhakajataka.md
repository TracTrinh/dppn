# Ruhakajātaka

## English source

Ruhakajātaka (Ja 191). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ruhaka</span> was the chaplain of the Bodhisatta, born as king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. The king gave him a horse with rich trappings, and, when he rode him, everybody was lost in admiration. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ruhaka</span>’s wife was a foolish old woman who, on hearing of the people’s praise, declared that their excitement was due, not to the qualities of the animal, but to its trappings, and that if <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ruhaka</span> would go out wearing the horse’s trappings, he would be similarly applauded. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ruhaka</span> agreed to the suggestion and suffered great humiliation; he went home in anger, determined to punish his wife, but she had escaped and had sought the king’s protection. The king persuaded <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ruhaka</span> to forgive her because “all womankind is full of faults.” (Ja.ii.113ff). The occasion for the story is given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indriyajātaka</span> (Ja 423) (*q.v.*).
