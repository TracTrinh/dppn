# Sineru

## English source

Sineru. A mountain, forming the center of the world. It is submerged in the sea to a depth of eighty-four thousand *yojanas* and rises above the surface to the same height. It is surrounded by seven mountain ranges – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yugandhara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Īsadhara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Karavīka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudassana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nemindhara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vinataka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assakaṇṇa</span> (Snp­a.ii.443; Vin-a.i.119; Vsm.206; cp. Mvu.ii.300; Divy.217; it is eighty thousand leagues broad, AN.iv.100).

On the top of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span> is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> (Snp­a.ii.485f), while at its foot is the *Asurabhavana* of ten thousand leagues; in the middle are the four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Dīpā</span> with their two thousand smaller *dīpā* (the *Asurabhavana* was not originally there, but sprang up by the power of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span> when they were thrown down from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, Dhp­a.i.272; see, e.g., Snp­a.i.201).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span> is often used in similes, its chief characteristic being its un-shakeability (*suṭṭhuṭhapita*) (e.g., Snp. vs.683). It is also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meru</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumeru</span> (e.g., Cv.xlii.2), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemameru</span> (e.g., Cv.xxxii.79) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Neru</span> (MN.i.338; also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Neru</span>, Ja.iii.247).

Each <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavāḷa</span> has its own <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span> (AN.i.227; v.59), and a time comes when even <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span> is destroyed (SN.iii.149).

When the Buddha went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, he covered the distance there from the earth in three strides he set his right foot down on the top of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yugandhara</span> and his left on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span>, the next step brought him to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, the whole distance so covered being sixty-eight hundred thousand leagues. Dhp­a.iii.216.
