# Sunāparanta

## English source

Sunāparanta. A country in which was the port of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppāraka</span>, birthplace of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa Thera</span>. From there he went with a caravan to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, and, after hearing the Buddha preach, entered the Saṅgha. Later, obtaining the Buddha’s permission, he returned to Sunāparanta (Thag­a.i.158). There he attained *Arahant*-ship, and five hundred men and five hundred women became lay followers of the religion. Under his direction they built a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhakuṭi</span>, called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candanasālā</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span>, wishing the Buddha to be present at the dedication festival, sent a flower through the air to the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> as invitation.

The Buddha accepted this invitation and went to Sunāparanta with four hundred and ninety-nine *Arahants*, including <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇḍadhāna</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, all in pinnacled palanquins, provided by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span>, acting under orders from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>. On the way the Buddha stopped at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saccabaddhapabbata</span>, where he converted the *tāpasa* of the mountain, who became an *Arahant* and travelled on with the party in the five hundredth palanquin. The Buddha spent the day in Sunāparanta, and, on his way back, stopped on the banks of the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nammadā</span>. There the *Nāgarājā* paid him homage, and the Buddha left his footprint in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>’s abode for him to worship. MN­a.ii.101f.; SN­a.iii.176; according to the latter account the Buddha spent seven days in Sunāparanta, at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṅkulārāma</span>.

The people of Sunāparanta were reported as being fierce and violent (MN.iii.268; SN.iv.61f). Sunāparanta was also the birthplace of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Puṇṇa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidinna</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span>) (*q.v.*). Sunāparanta is probably identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparanta</span>; the Burmese, however, identify it with the country on the right bank of the Irrawaddy River, near Pagan. Sās. Introd., p.ix.
