# Sahampati

## English source

Sahampati. A *Mahā Brahmā*. When the Buddha was at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajapālanigrodha</span>, hesitating as to whether or not he should preach the Dhamma, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> appeared before him and begged of him to open to the world the doors of Immortality. The Buddha agreed to this urgent request (Vin.i.5f.;SN.i.137f), and accepted from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> the assurance that all the Buddhas of the past had also had no other teacher than the Dhamma discovered by them. SN.i.139; see also SN.v.167f.,185, 232, where he gives the same assurance to the Buddha regarding the four *satipaṭṭhāna*s and the five *indriyas*; AN.ii.10f.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (e.g., SN­a.i.155) explains that the Buddha was reluctant to preach, not on account of indolence, but because he wished <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> to make him this request. For, thought the Buddha, the world honours *Brahma* greatly, and when people realized that *Brahma* himself had begged of the Buddha to spread his teaching, they would pay more attention to it. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> was, at this time, the most senior of the *Brahmas* (*jeṭṭha Mahā Brahmā*) (DN­a.ii.467).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> once saw that the *Brāhmiṇī*, mother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadeva Thera</span>*,* habitually made offerings to *Brahma*. Out of compassion for her, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> appeared before her and exhorted her to give her offerings to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadeva</span> instead (SN.i.140f). On another occasion, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokāliya</span> died and was born in Padumaniraya, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> appeared before the Buddha and announced the fact to him (Snp. p.125; cp. SN.i.151; AN.v.172).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta</span> (SN.i.154f) contains a series of verses spoken by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhakavinda</span>, when the Buddha sat out in the open during the night and rain fell drop by drop. The verses are in praise of the life and practices of the monks and of the results thereof. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> again visited the Buddha, simultaneously with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, and as they stood leaning against a doorpost of the Buddha’s cell, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> uttered a verse in praise of the Buddha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> then added another verse, exhorting the Buddha to preach the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span>, as there were those who would understand (SN.i.233). A verse spoken by him immediately after the Buddha’s death is included in the books (DN.ii.157; SN.i.158).

During the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> was a monk, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahaka</span>, who, having practised the five *indriyas* (*saddhā*, etc.), was reborn in the *Brahma* world. Thereafter he was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> (SN.v.233). The Commentaries say (Snp­a.ii.476; SN­a.i.155) that he was an *Anāgāmī Brahma* born in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhāvāsā</span>, there to pass a whole *kappa*, because he had developed the first *Jhāna* as a monk. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhavaṁsa Commentary</span> (Bv­a.p.11; see also p.29) says that, strictly speaking, his name should be “*Sahakapati*.”

When the Buddha attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> held over the Buddha’s head a white parasol three *yojanas* in diameter. Bv­a.239; this incident was sculptured in the Relic Chamber of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> (Mhv.xxx.74); cp. Ja.iv.266.

Once he offered to the Buddha a chain of jewels (*ratanadāma*) as large as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span> (Khp­a.171; Vin-a.i.115; Vsm.201). On the day that *Ālindakavāsi* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Phussadeva</span> attained *Arahant*-ship, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> came to wait upon him (Vibh­a.352).

It has been suggested (VT.i.86, n.1) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahma Sahampati</span> is very probably connected with Brahma Svayambhū of brahmanical literature.
