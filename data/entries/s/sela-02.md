# Sela 02

## English source

Sela 02. A Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttarāpa</span>. He was a great friend of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Keniya</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jaṭila</span>, and visited him when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Keniya</span> was making preparations to entertain the Buddha. Having heard the word “*Buddha*” from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Keniya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sela</span> was filled with joy and fortitude, and went with his two hundred and fifty pupils to visit the Buddha in the woodland near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āpana</span>. There he observed on the person of the Buddha the thirty-two marks of a Great Being all except two – viz., hidden privates and the long tongue. The Buddha read his thoughts and contrived, by his *iddhi* power, that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sela</span> should be satisfied on these two points as well (this is referred to at Mil.167; DN­a.i.276, etc.). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sela</span> then praised the Buddha in a series of verses and asked questions of him. At the end of his talk, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sela</span> entered the Saṅgha with his pupils, and, at the end of a week, he attained *Arahant*-ship (Snp. p.104 ff. = MN.ii.146f).

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> Sela had been the leader of the same guild of three hundred men, and, together with them, had built a *pariveṇa* for the Buddha and done many good acts. As a result of these they received the “*ehi Bhikkhu*” *pabbajjā* in this last life (Snp­a.ii.455; MN­a.ii.782; see also Ap.i.316; Thag.vs.818-41; Th4gA.ii.47f).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sela</span>, mentioned as the teacher of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sugandha Thera</span> (Thag­a.i.80f), is probably identical with this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sela</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sela</span> lived to the age of one hundred and twenty (DN­a.ii.413).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp­a.i.384; also AN­a.i.219), the Buddha first met <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sela</span> on his way to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span> to convert <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> and her kinswomen. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> was then seven years old. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> says (Ap.i.318) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sela</span>’s father was a wealthy Brahmin, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāseṭṭha</span>.
