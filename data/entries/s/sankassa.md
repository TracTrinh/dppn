# Saṅkassa

## English source

Saṅkassa. A city, thirty leagues from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> (Dhp­a.iii.224). It was there that the Buddha returned to earth, after preaching the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhammapiṭaka</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, following the performance of the Twin Miracle under the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṇḍamba</span> tree. As the time approached for the Buddha to leave <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span>, according to Snp­a.ii.570; cf. Vsm., p.391) announced his coming return to the multitude, who had been waiting at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, fed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Anāthapiṇḍika</span>, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> expounded the Dhamma. They then made their way to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span>. The descent of the Buddha took place on the day of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Pavāraṇa</span> festival. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> provides three ladders for the Buddha’s descent from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span> to the earth: on the right was a ladder of gold for the gods; on the left a silver ladder for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span> and his retinue; and in the middle a ladder of jewels for the Buddha. The assembled people covered the earth for thirty leagues round. There was a clear view of the nine <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> worlds above and of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avīci</span> below. The Buddha was accompanied by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātali</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suyāma</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> was the first to welcome him (followed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uppalavaṇṇā</span>, Snp­a.ii.570), and the Buddha preached the Dhamma, starting with what was within the comprehension even of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">puthujjana</span>, and ending with what only a Buddha could understand. On this occasion was preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parosahassajātaka</span> (Ja 99, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) to proclaim to the multitude the unparalleled wisdom of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> (Dhp­a.iii.224ff.; see also Snp­a.ii.570). It is said that the Buddha’s descent to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span> had provided opportunity for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> to show his eminence in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dibbacakkhu</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t18">puṇṇa</span> in skill in preaching, and the Buddha wished to give <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> a chance of shining in his wisdom (ibid., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t18">loc.cit.</span>; Ja.iv.266; see also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jhānasodhana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhamiga</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candābhajātaka</span>s). He therefore asked of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> questions which no one else could answer. The opening words of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputtasutta</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) are supposed to refer to this descent from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sic</span>). The site of the city gate of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span> is one of the “unchangeable” spots of the world (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t18">avijahitaṭṭhānaṁ</span>). All Buddhas descend at that spot to the world of men after preaching the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span> (Bv­a.106, 247; MN­a.i.371, etc.). From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span> the Buddha went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> (Ja.i.193). A shrine was erected on the spot where the Buddha’s right foot first touched the ground at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span> (Dhp­a.iii.227). When the Chinese pilgrims, Xuanzang and Faxian, visited the place, they found three ladders, which had been built of brick and stone by the ancients, to commemorate the Buddha’s descent, but the ladders were nearly sunk in the earth (Beal-Xuan, op.cit., i.203; Faxian, p.24).

There was, in the Buddha’s time, a deer park at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span> where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suhemanta Thera</span> heard the Buddha preach (Thag­a.i.212). During the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjiputta</span> controversy, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata Thera</span><span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t18">,</span> on his way from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soreyya</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahājāti</span>, went through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span>. The road he took passed through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇṇakujja</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udumbarā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggālapura</span> (Vin.ii.299f).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span> is now identified with Saṅkissa Basantapura on the north bank of the Ikkhumatī (Kālīnadī), between Atranji and Kanoj, twenty-three miles west of Fatehgarh and forty-five north of Kanoj.
