# Samantakūṭa

## English source

Samantakūṭa, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samantagiri</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanakūṭa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanagiri</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanācala</span>. A mountain peak in Ceylon. It was the residence of the *Deva* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sumana</span> (Mhv.i.33) and when the Buddha visited the Island for the third time, he left on the mountain the mark of his footprint (Mhv.i.77; cf. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nammadā</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saccabaddha</span>). Owing to this, the mountain became a sacred place of pilgrimage. In later times many kings of Ceylon paid the shrine great honour. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijayabāhu I</span>. gifted the village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gilīmalaya</span> for the feeding of pilgrims, and set up rest-houses for them on the different routes, for the maintenance of which he provided (Cv.ix.64f)

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kittinissaṅka</span> made a special pilgrimage to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanakūṭa</span> and mentioned it in his inscriptions (Cv.lxxx.24; Cv.Trs.ii.128, n.4). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu II</span>. did likewise, and also gave ten *gāvutas* of rich land for the shrine on the top of the peak (Cv.lxxxv.118). He further gave orders to his pious minister, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devappatirāja</span>, to make the roads leading to the mountain easy of access. The minister repaired the roads, and built bridges at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bodhitala</span> over the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khajjotanadī</span>, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ullapanaggāma</span>, and at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaggāma</span>. He constructed rest houses at suitable spots, and placed stepping stones on the way to the summit. Then the king himself visited the peak and held a great festival there lasting for three days (Cv.lxxxvi.9, 18 ff). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijayabāhu IV</span>., too, made a pilgrimage to the sacred mountain (Cv.lxxxviii.48).

King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vīravikkama</span> also went there and lit a lamp, fifteen cubits in girth and five cubits high (Cv.xcii.17). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājasīha I</span>., in his desire to take revenge on the Buddhist monks, handed the shrine over to Hindu priests (Cv.xciii.12), but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vimaladhammasūriya II</span>. restored to it all honours and held a great festival, lasting for seven days, at the peak (Cv.xcvii.16f). His son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Narindasīha</span>, made two pilgrimages there (Cv.xcvii.31), while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijayarājasīha</span> had a feast of lamps celebrated there (Cv.xcviii.84). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kittisirirājasīha</span> had a *maṇḍapa* built round the footprint surmounted by a parasol, and assigned the revenues from the village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṭṭāpiṭi</span> to the monks who looked after the shrine (Cv.c.221).

The districts round <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samantakūṭa</span> were, in early times, the habitation of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulindas</span>. It was believed (Mhv.vii.67) that, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijaya</span> forsook <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuveṇī</span>, her children fled thither and that their descendants were the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulindas</span>. In later times, too, mention is made (e.g., Cv.lxi.70) of the fact that the people dwelling in the neighbourhood of Samantakūṭa refused to pay taxes to the king. From very early times the mountain was the dwelling of numerous monks. Thus, in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span>, there were nine hundred monks there, under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malaya Mahā Deva</span> Thera (Mhv.xxxii.49). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Damiḷa Dīghajantu</span> offered a red robe to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ākāsacetiya</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samantagiri</span> *vihāra*, and, as a result, won heaven, because he remembered the gift at the moment of his death (AN­a.i.376; MN­a.ii.955). The rivers <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vāluka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalyāṇi</span> rise in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanakūṭa</span>.
