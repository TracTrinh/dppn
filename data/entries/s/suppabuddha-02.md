# Suppabuddha 02

## English source

Suppabuddha 02. A poor leper of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, who, one day seated in the outer circle of people, heard the Buddha preach and became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>. While waiting the departure of the crowd so that he could pay homage to the Buddha and express his gratitude, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, desiring to test him, approached him and offered him untold wealth if he would repudiate the Buddha, his teachings, and the Saṅgha. But although <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> revealed his identity, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppabuddha</span> rebuked him for a fool and said he had no need of more wealth, because he possessed already the seven stores of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ariyadhana</span> (Noble Wealth). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> reported this conversation to the Buddha, who said that no power in the world would change <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppabuddha</span>.

Soon after, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppabuddha</span> visited the Buddha, and, having worshipped him, was on his way to the city when he was gored to death by a cow, the cow which killed also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhiya Dāruciriya</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tambadāṭhika</span>. The cow was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Yakkhinī</span>, who had once been a courtesan. These four men had then been sons of wealthy merchants, who, having taken her one day to a pleasure garden, took their pleasure with her. In the evening they killed her and took the jewels and money which they themselves had given her. At the moment of her death she had vowed vengeance on them and had killed them in one hundred existences.

In a previous birth, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppabuddha</span> had insulted the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tagarasikhī</span> by calling him a “leper” (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kuṭṭhi</span>) – because he wore a patched robe – and by spitting on him Ud.v.3; Ud­a.279ff.; Dhp­a.ii.33f. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> account does not include the interlude of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>.
