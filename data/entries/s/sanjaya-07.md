# Sañjaya 07

## English source

Sañjaya 07. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sañjaya Belaṭṭhiputta</span>. One of the six famous heretical teachers of the Buddha’s day. He was a great skeptic, his teaching being the evasion of problems and the suspension of judgment. His doctrines seem to have been identical with those of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Amarāvikkhepikas</span> (eel-wrigglers) who, when asked a question, would equivocate and wriggle like an eel. Sañjaya’s teachings are given at DN.i.58; cf. the “eel-wrigglers” at DN.i.27.

It is probable that Sañjaya suspended his judgments only with regard to those questions the answers to which must always remain a matter of speculation. It may be that he wished to impress on his followers the fact that the final answer to these questions lay beyond the domain of speculation, and that he wished to divert their attention from fruitless enquiry and direct it towards the preservation of mental equanimity.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> gives us no particulars about Sañjaya, beyond the fact that he was the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Belaṭṭha</span> (DN­a.i.144). Sanskrit texts call him Sañjayī Vairatiputra (e.g., Mvu.iii.59f) and Sañjayi Vairattīputra (e.g., Divy.143,145).

He is evidently identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sañjaya</span> the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Paribbājaka</span> who was the original teacher of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> (Vin.i.39). It is said that when these two disciples left <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sañjaya</span> to become pupils of the Buddha, they were joined by two hundred and fifty others. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sañjaya</span> then fainted, and hot blood issued from his mouth. Vin.i.42; according to Dhp­a.i.78, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> tried to persuade <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sañjaya</span> to accept the Buddha’s doctrine, but they failed, and only one half of his disciples joined them. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Paribbājaka</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppiya</span> was also a follower of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sañjaya</span> (DN­a.i.35).

Barua thinks (Op. cit., 326) that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Aviruddhakas</span> mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttara</span> (AN.iii.276) were also followers of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sañjaya</span> – that they were called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Amarāvikkhepakā</span> for their philosophical doctrines, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Aviruddhakā</span> for their moral conduct.
