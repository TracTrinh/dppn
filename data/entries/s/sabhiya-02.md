# Sabhiya 02

## English source

Sabhiya 02. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiya Thera</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiya Kaccāna</span>. His mother was a nobleman’s daughter whose parents had committed her to the charge of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Paribbājaka</span>, that she might learn various doctrines and usages. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Paribbājaka</span> seduced her, and, when she was with child, the fraternity abandoned her. Her child was born in the open (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sabhāyaṁ</span>), while she was wandering about alone hence his name. When Sabhiya grew up he, in his turn, became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Paribbājaka</span> and was famous as a dialectician. He had a hermitage by the city gate, where he gave lessons to the sons of noblemen and others. He devised twenty questions, which he put before recluses and Brahmins, but none could answer them. These questions had been handed on to him by his mother who had developed insight and had been reborn in a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> world. (But see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiyasutta</span> 1). Then, as related in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiyasutta</span> 1, Sabhiya visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span> and, at the end of the discussion, entered the Saṅgha, where, developing insight, he won <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakusandha Buddha</span> he was a householder and gave the Buddha a pair of sandals. After <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>’s death he, with six others, joined the Saṅgha and lived in the forest. Failing to develop <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jhāna</span>, they went to the top of a mountain, determined to reach some attainment or to die of starvation. The eldest became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>, the next became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmī</span> and was reborn in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhāvāsa</span>. The remaining five died without achieving their aim. These five were, in this age, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span>, Sabhiya, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumāra Kassapa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba Mallaputta</span>. Thag­a.i.381f.; Snp­a.ii.419ff.; Ap.ii.473; Dhp­a.ii.212.

Sabhiya is mentioned as an example of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">paṇḍita Paribbājaka</span> (SN­a.ii.188). A series of verses spoken by him, in admonishing monks who sided with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>, are given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> (vs. 275-8; see also Mvu.iii.389ff.). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasadatta</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiya</span>’s companion.
