# Sunakkhatta

## English source

Sunakkhatta. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span> prince of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>. He was, at one time, a member of the Saṅgha and the personal attendant of the Buddha (*anibaddha-upaṭṭhāka*), but was later converted to the views of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Korakkhattiya</span> and went about defaming the Buddha, saying that he had nothing superhuman and was not distinguished from other men by preaching a saving faith: that the doctrine preached by him did not lead to the destruction of sorrow, etc. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, on his alms rounds in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, heard all this and reported it to the Buddha, who thereupon preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sīhanādasutta</span> (MN.i.68ff.; the Buddha was, at this time, eighty years old, MN.i.82) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lomahaṁsajātaka</span> (Ja 94, Ja.i.389f.; see also Ja.iv.95). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunakkhattasutta</span> (MN.ii.252ff) was evidently preached to Sunakkhatta before he joined the Saṅgha, while the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭikasutta</span> (DN.iii.1ff) gives an account of his dissatisfaction.

His grievance was that the Buddha showed no mystic superhuman wonders, that he had not shown him the beginning of things. The Buddha reminded him that he had not promised to do any of these things, and that, at one time, Sunakkhatta had been loud in his praise of the Buddha and the Dhamma. The Buddha warned him that people would say he had left the Saṅgha because its discipline had proved too hard for him. The Buddha had told him that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Korakkhattiya</span>, whom he so much admired, would be born after death among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷakañjaka</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span> within seven days. It happened as the Buddha prophesied, and the dead body of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kora</span> declared that he was right. But even so, Sunakkhatta was not convinced.

Later he transferred his allegiance to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kandaramasaka</span>, who died, as the Buddha had prophesied, fallen from grace and fame. The next teacher to win the admiration of Sunakkhatta was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭikaputta</span>, and Sunakkhatta wished the Buddha to pay honour to him. But the Buddha quoted to Sunakkhatta the words of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajita</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span> general who had been born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, to the effect that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭikaputta</span> was “a liar and a cheat,” and was later able to prove that these words were true. But Sunakkhatta did not return to the Saṅgha. He had probably remained in it for several years before actually leaving it. For we find in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahālisutta</span> (DN.i.152) the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Oṭṭhaddha</span> relating to the Buddha how Sunakkhatta had come to him three years after joining the Saṅgha, claiming that he could see divine forms but could not hear heavenly sounds. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> explains (DN­a.i.311) that he could not acquire the power of hearing divine sounds because in a previous birth he had ruptured the ear drum of a holy monk and made him deaf. The *Sutta* itself gives (DN.i.153) as the reason that he had only developed one sided concentration of mind.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunakkhatta</span> is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāṇāriṭṭha</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhūridattajātaka</span>. Ja.vi.219.
