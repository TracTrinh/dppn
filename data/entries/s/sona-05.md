# Soṇa 05

## English source

Soṇa 05. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa Koḷivisa Thera</span>. He was also called *Sukhumāla Soṇa* (AN­a.i.679). He was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Campā</span>, his father being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Usabha</span> *seṭṭhi*. From the time of his conception his father’s wealth continued to increase, and, on the day of his birth, the whole town kept festival. Because in a previous birth he had given a ring, worth one hundred thousand, to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>, his body was like burnished gold – hence his name (he was evidently called Koḷivisa because he was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyan</span>, Ap.i.95, 21). His hands and feet were soft like *bandhujīvaka*-flowers, and a fine down grew on them (four inches long on his feet, Ap.i.298) curved “like ear ornaments.” He lived in great luxury in three palaces, each having its own season. King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, hearing of him, sent for him and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa</span> went with eighty thousand fellow townsmen.

In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> he heard the Buddha preach, and, winning faith, entered the Saṅgha with his parents’ consent. The Buddha gave him a subject for meditation, and he went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sītavana</span>, but many people visited him and he was unable to concentrate. He strove hard, and, through pacing up and down in meditation, painful sores developed on his feet. But he won no attainment and was filled with despair. The Buddha saw this and visited him, and by preaching to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vīnūpamovādasutta</span> (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇasutta</span>), taught him how to temper energy with calm. Thus corrected, he put forth fresh effort and attained *Arahant*-ship (Thag.vss.632-644).

The Vinaya (i.179ff) gives details of Soṇa’s visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>. The king, being curious to see Soṇa’s feet, sent for him. He and his eighty thousand companions went to see the Buddha, and there they were greatly impressed by the *iddhi*-power of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgata</span>. Soṇa then sought the Buddha alone and joined the Saṅgha. After ordination he walked about meditating, his feet bled, and his *caṅkamana* was covered with blood “like a slaughter house for oxen.” After Soṇa attained *Arahant*-ship, the Buddha gave him permission to wear shoes with one lining. Soṇa said he had abandoned eighty cartloads of gold and a retinue of seven elephants. He did not wish, as a monk, to have any luxuries which his colleagues did not share, The Buddha then gave permission to all monks to wear shoes with one lining.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anomadassī Buddha</span> he was a very rich *seṭṭhi*, and, having gone with others to the *vihāra* and heard the Buddha preach, he decorated a *caṅkamana* for the Buddha and a long hall (*dīghasālā*) for the monks. On the *caṅkamana* he scattered various flowers, and, above it, he hung canopies. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> he was a *seṭṭhi* of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsavatī</span> named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirivaḍḍha</span>. It was then that he resolved to win eminence as foremost of those who strove energetically (*aggaṁ āraddhaviriyānaṁ*), and in this he was successful (AN.i.24). After the death of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>, Soṇa was a householder in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, and built a hut by the river for a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>, whom he looked after during the rainy season. He was king of the gods for twenty-five *kappas*, and seventy-seven times king among men under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasodhara</span>. Thag­a.i.544f.; cf. Ap.i.93f., where he is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyavessa</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna Commentary</span> confused his story with that of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa Kuṭikaṇṇa</span>; see also AN­a.i.130f., where the details are different, especially regarding the honour paid by Soṇa to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>. Once, on visiting the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>’s cell, he noticed that the ground outside it was muddy; so he spread on the ground a rug worth one hundred thousand, so that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>’s feet might not be soiled.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> mentions (Ap.i.298) a Thera*,* called *Soṇa Koṭivīsa*, evidently identical with the above, the reason given for the name being that he gave away wealth equal in value to twenty crores (*vīsa koṭi*). His eminence is ascribed to the fact that, in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span>, he made a *lena* (cave) for the Buddha and his monks and spread it with rugs. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (AN­a.i.130) gives a variant of his name, calling him *Koṭivessa*, and explains this by saying that he belonged to a *Vessa* (merchant) family worth a crore.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇasutta</span> (Cf. AN­a.ii.680, where he is described as *Gandhabbasippe cheko*, skilled in the Gandhabba art, mentions that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa</span> was a clever player of the *vīnā* before he joined the Saṅgha. It was the example of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa Koḷivisa</span> which urged <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandaka</span> and his brother, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bharata</span>, to leave the world. Thag­a.i.299.
