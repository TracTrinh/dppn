# Sabhiyasutta 01

## English source

Sabhiyasutta 01. The sixth <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vagga</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span> (Snp., pp. 91f). A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Devatā</span>, who in a previous life had been a relation of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiya</span> (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiya</span> 1), asks him a series of questions and exhorts him to join the Saṅgha of any recluse who can answer them satisfactorily. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiya</span> wanders about asking his questions of several well-known teachers; failing to find satisfaction, he visits the Buddha in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> and is given permission to ask anything he wishes, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> promising to solve his difficulties. Then follows a series of questions answered by the Buddha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiya</span>, in the end, asks permission to join the Buddha’s Saṅgha. This permission is given, and after the usual probationary period of four months, he becomes an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>.

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> Commentary (Thag­a.i.382), quoted also in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span> Commentary, the questions were formulated by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiya</span>’s mother, who, feeling revulsion for her womanhood, developed the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jhānas</span> and was reborn in a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> world. But the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span> Commentary itself (Snp­a.ii.421) says that they were taught to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiya</span> by an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmī Brahma</span>, who had been a fellow celibate of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiya</span> in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>’s dispensation. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> is also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sabhiyapucchā</span>, and is given (e.g., DN­a.i.155) as an example of the Buddha’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sabbaññupavāraṇa</span>, his willingness to answer any question whatever without restriction.

It is said (Thag­a.i.427) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasadatta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiya</span>’s companion, was present at the discussion with the Buddha and listened eagerly hoping for a chance of criticism. But the Buddha read his thoughts and admonished him at the end of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span>.

The questions contained in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> had been asked and answered in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>, too, but, while the questions remained, the answers disappeared. Vibh­a., p. 432.
