# Setaketujātaka

## English source

Setaketujātaka (Ja 377). The Bodhisatta was once a far-famed teacher, with five hundred pupils, chief among whom was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setaketu</span>, an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Udicca Brahmin</span>, who prided himself on his high caste. One day, meeting a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Caṇḍāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setaketu</span> told him to pass on his leeward side, but the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Caṇḍāla</span> refused, challenging <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setaketu</span> to answer a question. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setaketu</span> accepted the challenge, and he was asked what were the four “quarters.” <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setaketu</span> gave the usual answer, whereupon the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Caṇḍāla</span> forced him between his feet. When this was reported to the teacher, he told <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setaketu</span> that the four quarters were parents, teachers, generous householders, and deliverance from misery. Later, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setaketu</span> left for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span>, and, after finishing his studies there, went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> with five hundred ascetics. There they practised false penances and won the king’s approval; the king’s chaplain, however, warned him against them, saying that they possessed only false knowledge and had no morality. The chaplain persuaded <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setaketu</span> and his followers to become laymen and enter the king’s service.

The story was related in the same circumstances as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uddālajātaka</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setaketu</span> is identified with the false priest, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Caṇḍāla</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, and the king’s chaplain with the Bodhisatta. Ja.iii.232-7.
