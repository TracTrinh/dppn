# Suciloma

## English source

Suciloma, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sūciloma</span>. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>. Once, when the Buddha was at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ṭaṅkitamañca</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayā</span>, which was the abode of Suciloma, Suciloma and his friend, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khara</span>, happened to be passing by, and Suciloma, coming up to the Buddha, bent his body against the Buddha’s. The Buddha bent his body in the opposite direction, saying that contact with him was an evil thing. Then Suciloma asked him a question regarding the origin of various persuasions, and the Buddha answered him (Ap.ii.434; the same verses occur).

It is said that Suciloma was a lay follower of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> and used to visit the *vihāra* eight times a month to hear the Dhamma. (Snp­a.i.302, 305; in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta Commentary</span> (SN­a.i.233) he is said to have been a monk). One day, when he heard the gong announcing the preaching, he was working in a field near the *vihāra*, and thinking he would be late if he stayed to wash, he entered the *uposatha* hall, where he lay on a very costly rug. As a result of this action, the hairs of his body resembled needles hence his name. At the end of the Buddha’s sermon Suciloma became a *Sotāpanna*.

An expressive statue of Suciloma is to be found among the bas-reliefs of the Bhārhūt Stūpa. See Cunningham: Bhārhūt, p.136.
