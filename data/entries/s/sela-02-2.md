# Selā 02

## English source

Selā 02. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Selā Therī</span> She was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavi</span> as daughter of the king: therefore she was also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavikā</span>. When she was still unmarried the Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavi</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span>, whom he had converted, carrying his begging bowl and robe. On that occasion <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sela</span> went with her father to hear the Buddha preach. She became a lay disciple, but later, agitated in mind, she joined the Saṅgha and became an *Arahant*. After that she lived in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>. One day, as she was enjoying her siesta in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhavana</span> under a tree, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>, in the guise of a stranger, approached her and tried to tempt her. But she refuted his statements regarding the attractions of lay life, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> had to retire discomfited (SN.i.134; Thīg.vss.57-9).

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Selā</span> was born in the family of a clansman of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsavatī</span> and was given in marriage. After her husband’s death she devoted herself to the quest of good, and went from *ārāma* to *ārāma* and *vihāra* to *vihāra*, teaching the Dhamma to followers of the religion. One day she came to the Bodhi-tree of the Buddha and sat down there thinking, “If a Buddha be peerless among men, may this tree show the miracle of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>.” Immediately the tree blazed forth, the branches appeared golden, and the sky was all shining. Inspired by the sight, she fell down and worshipped the tree, and sat there for seven days. On the seventh day she performed a great feast of offering and worship to the Buddha (Thīg­a.61f). Her <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> verses, quoted in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Therīgāthā</span> Commentary, are, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> itself, attributed to a Therī called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcadīpikā</span>, and are twice repeated in these verses (Ap.ii.519, repeated at 527f), however, she is mentioned as having attained *Arahant*-ship at the age of seven, and there is no reference to her life as daughter of the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavi</span>. See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Selā</span> (3).
