# Subhūti Thera

## English source

Subhūti Thera. He was the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span> *seṭṭhī* and the younger brother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>. On the day of the dedication of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, he heard the Buddha preach and left the world. After ordination he mastered the two categories (of Vinaya rules), and, after obtaining a subject for meditation, lived in the forest. There he developed insight, and attained *Arahant*-ship on the basis of *mettā jhāna*. Teaching the Dhamma without distinction or limitation, he was declared chief of those who lived in peace (*araṇavihārinaṁ aggo*), and of those who were worthy of gifts (*dakkhiṇeyyānaṁ*) (AN.i.24; cf. Ud.vi.7, where the Buddha commends his proficiency in meditation). It is said that when he went begging for alms he would develop *mettā jhāna* at each door, hence every gift made to him was of the highest merit. In the course of his travels he came to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> promised to build him a dwelling-place. But the king forgot his promise, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhūti</span> meditated in the open air. There was no rain, and, discovering the cause, the king had a leaf hut built for him. As soon as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhūti</span> entered the hut and seated himself cross-legged on the bed of hay, rain began to fall.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> he belonged to a rich family and was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span>. Later he left the world and lived the hermit’s life, at the head of forty-four thousand others. The Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span>’s hermitage and accepted from him a gift of fruit and flowers. He asked one of his monks, proficient in *mettā* and eminent in receiving gifts, to preach the *anumodanā*. At the end of the sermon all the other hermits became *Arahants*, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span>’s attention was fixed on the majesty of the preaching monk, and he did not reach any attainment. Later, discovering the qualities in which the preacher had attained eminence, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span> resolved that he too would reach similar eminence. AN­a.i.124f.; Thag­a.i.17ff.; Ud­a.348f; see also Ap.i.67f., where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span> is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosiya</span>.

Verses attributed to him are included in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> (vs.1) and also in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span> (pp.356,391). See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhūtisutta</span>.
