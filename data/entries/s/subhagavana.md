# Subhagavana

## English source

Subhagavana. A wood in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ukkaṭṭhā</span>. Once, when the Buddha was there, he visited the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avihā</span> gods (DN.ii.50) and, again, from there he visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bakabrahma</span>, to whom he preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmanimantanikasutta</span> (MN.i.326; but see SN.i.142, where the Buddha is said to have been at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>). It was under a *sāla* tree in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhagavana</span> that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mūlapariyāyasutta</span> was preached (MN.i.1; also the Jātaka, of the same name, Ja 245, Ja.ii.259).

The wood was so-called because of its beauty. People often went there for pleasure, and, influenced by the romantic nature of the surroundings, would plan pleasant things, which would come to pass (MN­a.i.10).
