# Saṅgītisutta

## English source

Saṅgītisutta. The thirty-third <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghanikāya</span> (DN.iii.207ff), preached at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ubbhaṭaka</span>, the new Mote Hall of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span>. They had invited the Buddha to consecrate it by preaching there, and this he did until late into the night. Then, seeing that his audience wished for more, he asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> to continue the preaching while he himself rested. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> therefore preached the Saṅgītisutta, at the end of which the Buddha expressed his great appreciation of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>’s exposition.

This <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span>, like the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dasuttara</span>, is arranged in a new plan – which is regularly followed in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> of grouping the points or chief items brought forward, numerically, in arithmetical progression – in this case 1 to 10. This scheme is a kind of thematic index to the doctrines scattered through the Four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nikāyas</span>.

The Sarvāstivādins held this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> in high esteem, and included it (under the name of Saṅgītipariyāya) among the seven books constituting their <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhammapiṭaka</span>. The Tibetan recensions attribute the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Koṭṭhita</span>. See Takākusu’s article on the Sarvāstivādins (JPTS 1904-5).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> treats of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dasadhammā</span> (or ten conditions) in much the same way as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puggalapaññatti</span> deals with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dasapuggalā</span> (ten individuals).
