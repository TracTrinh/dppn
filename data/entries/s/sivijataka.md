# Sivijātaka

## English source

Sivijātaka (Ja 499). The Bodhisatta was once born as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sivi</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariṭṭhapura</span>, his father bearing the same name as himself. He ruled well, and daily gave alms to the amount of six hundred thousand. One day the desire came to him to give part of his body to any who might ask for it. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> read his thoughts, and, appearing before him as a blind Brahmin, asked for his eyes. The king agreed to give them, and sent for his surgeon <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sivaka</span>. Amid the protests and lamentations of his family and his subjects, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sivi</span> had his eyes removed and given to the Brahmin. It is said that the surgeon did his work in several stages, giving <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sivi</span> chances of withdrawing his offer. When the sockets healed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sivi</span> wished to become an ascetic, and went into the park with one attendant. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s throne grew hot, and appearing before <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sivi</span>, he offered him a boon. The king wished to die, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> insisted on his choosing something else. He then asked that his sight might be restored. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> suggested an Act of Truth (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">saccakiriyā</span>), as not even <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> could restore lost sight. The eyes reappeared, but they were neither natural eyes nor divine, but eyes called “Truth, Absolute and Perfect.” <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sivi</span> collected all his subjects, and, resting on a throne in a pavilion, taught them the value of gifts.

The story was related in reference to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asadisadāna</span>. On the seventh day of the almsgiving the king gave all kinds of requisites and asked the Buddha to preach a thanksgiving sermon, but the Buddha left without doing so. The next day, on being questioned by the king, he explained his reasons for this (for details see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asadisadāna</span>). The king, greatly pleased with the Buddha’s explanation, gave him an outer robe of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Siveyyaka</span>-cloth worth one thousand. When the monks started commenting on how tireless the king was in giving, the Buddha related to them the old story, in which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sivaka</span>, the physician, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> (Ja.iv.401-12; of. Cp-a.52f).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sivirājacariyā</span> is included in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cariyāpiṭaka</span> (Cp.i.8; the story is also given with variant details in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Avadānaśataka</span> i.183-6). It forms the topic of one of the dilemmas of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span>. Mil.p.119f.
