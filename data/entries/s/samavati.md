# Sāmāvatī

## English source

Sāmāvatī. One of the three chief consorts of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>. She was the daughter of the *seṭṭhi* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddavatiya</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddavatī</span>, who was a friend of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>. When plague broke out in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddavatī</span>, she and her parents fled to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>, and there obtained food from the alms hall provided by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span>. On the first day <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> asked for three portions, on the second for two, on the third for only one. For her father had died after the meal on the first day, her mother on the second. When, on the third day, she asked for only one portion, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mitta</span> who was distributing alms, teased her, saying: “Today you know the capacity of your belly.” She asked what he meant, and when he explained his words, she told him what had happened. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mitta</span> pitied her and adopted her as his daughter.

One day, when she arrived at the refectory, she found a great uproar going on, people rushing everywhere to get alms. She asked to be allowed to bring order into this chaos, and had a fence erected round the refectory with separate doors for entrance and exit. This put an end to the disturbances. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span>, hearing no noise in the refectory as before, inquired the reason, and, finding out what <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> had done, adopted her as his own child. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>’s original name was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmā</span>, but after building the fence (*vati*) round the refectory she was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>.

On a festival day <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> saw <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> going to the river to bathe, and, falling in love with her, asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span> to send her to the palace. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span> refused, and the king turned him and his wife out of doors and sealed up his house. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> discovered this, she made <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span> send her to the palace, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> made her his chief consort. Some time afterwards <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> took <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span> also as consort.

When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> at the request of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghosaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkuṭa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvāriya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khujjutarā</span>, the servant woman of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>, heard him preach and became a *Sotāpanna*. She had been on her way to the gardener, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span>, to buy flowers for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>, with the eight pieces of money given to her daily by the king for this purpose. On <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span>’s invitation, she had gone to hear the Buddha at his house. On other days she had spent only half the money on flowers, appropriating the rest for herself; but this day, having become a *Sotāpanna*, she bought flowers with the whole amount and took them to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>, to whom she confessed her story. At <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>’s request, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khujjuttarā</span> repeated to her and her companions the sermon she had heard from the Buddha. After this, she visited the Buddha daily, repeating his sermon to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> and her friends. Having learnt that the Buddha passed along the street in which the palace stood, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> had holes made in the walls so that she and her friends might see the Buddha and do obeisance to him. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span> heard of this during a visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>’s quarters, and, because of her hatred for the Buddha, she determined to have <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> punished. For details see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span>.

At first her plots miscarried, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>, convinced of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>’s goodness, gave her a boon, and she chose that the Buddha be invited to visit the palace daily and to preach to her and her friends. But the Buddha sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> instead, and they provided him with food every day and listened to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span>. One day they presented him with five hundred robes given to them by the king, who, at first, was very angry; but on hearing from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> that nothing given to the monks was lost, he gave another five hundred robes himself.

In the end, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span>’s plot succeeded, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> and her companions were burned to death in their own house. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> was in his park, and, on his arrival, he found them all dead. When the Buddha was asked, he said that some of the women had attained to the First Fruit of the Path, others to the second, yet others to the third.

It is said that in a previous birth <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> and her friends had belonged to the harem of the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. One day they went bathing with the king, and, feeling cold when they came out of the water, they set fire to a tangle of grass, near by. When the grass burned down, they found a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> seated in the tangle, and fearing that they had burnt him to death, they pulled more grass, which they placed round his body, and, after pouring oil on it, set fire to it so that all traces of their crime might be destroyed. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> was in *samādhi* and nothing could therefore harm him, but it was this act which brought retribution to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> and her companions.

The story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> is included in the story cycle of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>. For details see especially Dhp­a.i.187-91, 205-225; the story also appears, with certain variations in detail, in AN­a.i.232-4, 236ff., and is given very briefly in Ud­a.382f., omitting the account of the reason for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>’s death which is given at length in an explanation of an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> (Ud.vii.10) dealing with the incident. Cf. Divy.575f. According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visuddhimagga</span> (p.380f), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span>’s desire to kill <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> arose from her desire to be herself chief queen.

The two Therī’s named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmā</span> were friends of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>, and were so filled with grief over her death that they left home and joined the Saṅgha.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> is reckoned among the moist eminent of the lay women who were followers of the Buddha, and was declared by him foremost among those who lived in kindliness (*aggaṁ mettāvihārinaṁ*) (AN.i.26; cf. iv.348). Her *iddhi*, in warding off by loving-kindness the arrow shot at her by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>, is often referred to, e.g. Bv­a.24; Iti-a.23; Paṭis-a.498; AN­a.ii.791.
