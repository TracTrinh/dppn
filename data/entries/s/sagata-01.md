# Sāgata 01

## English source

Sāgata 01.<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14"> Sāgata Thera</span>. He was the personal attendant of the Buddha at the time when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa Koḷivisa</span> visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, with overseers of the eighty thousand townships of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>’s kingdom. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgata</span> was endowed with supernatural power, and the overseers, who went to visit the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span>, were very impressed by his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span>, so much so that even while the Buddha was preaching they could not take their eyes off him. The king thereupon asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgata</span> to show them a greater marvel, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgata</span>, having shown in the open sky wonders of various kinds, fell at the Buddha’s feet and declared the Buddha his teacher (Vin.i.179 f).

Later, when the Buddha went to stay in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddavatikā</span>, having heard men warn the Buddha of the proximity of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> of great power in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jaṭila</span> hermitage at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambatittha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgata</span> went there and lived in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>’s abode. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> showed great resentment, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgata</span> overpowered him with his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span> and then returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddavatikā</span>. From there he went with the Buddha to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>, where the lay disciples, hearing of his wondrous feat, paid him great honour. When they asked what they could do for his comfort, he remained silent, but the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chabbaggiyā</span> suggested that they should provide him with white spirits (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kāpotikā</span>).

The next day, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgata</span> went for alms, he was invited to various houses, where the inmates plied him with intoxicating drinks. So deep were his potations that on his way out of the town he fell prostrate at the gateway. The monks carried him, and at the monastery they laid him down with his head at the Buddha’s feet, but he turned round so that his feet lay towards the Buddha. The Buddha pointed out his condition to the monks, using it as an example of the evil effects of liquor; and he made this the occasion for the passing of a rule against the use of alcohol. Vin.iv.108f.; the story is also given as the introduction to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Surāpānajātaka</span> (Ja 81, Ja.i.360 ff.) which, too, was preached on this occasion; cf. AN­a.i.178f.

It is said (AN­a.i.179) that on the next day, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgata</span> came to himself and realized the enormity of his offence, he sought the Buddha and, having begged his forgiveness, developed insight, attaining <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship. The Buddha later declared him foremost among those skilled in the contemplation of the heat element (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">tejodhātukusalānaṁ</span>) (AN.i.25).

It is curious that no verses are ascribed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgata</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (Ap.i.83f) contains a set of verses said to have been spoken by him. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> he was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sobhita</span>, a Brahmin. The Buddha came to his hermitage with his disciples, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgata</span> spoke verses in praise of the Buddha, who declared his future destiny. The Commentary adds that he was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgata</span> because he was greatly welcome (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sāgata</span>) to his parents.
