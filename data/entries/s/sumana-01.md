# Sumana 01

## English source

Sumana 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana Buddha</span>. The fourth of the twenty-four Buddhas. He was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mekhala</span>, his father being the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Khattiya</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudatta</span> and his mother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span>. For nine thousand years he lived as a householder in three palaces – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Canda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sucanda</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vataṁsa</span> (Bv­a.125 calls them <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārivaḍḍhana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Somavaḍḍhana</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Iddhivaḍḍhana</span>) – his wife being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vataṁsikā</span> and his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anupama</span>. He left the world on an elephant, practised austerities for ten months, and attained enlightenment under a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> tree, being given a meal of milk-rice by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anupamā</span>, daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anupama</span>-<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anoma</span>, and grass for his seat by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ājīvaka</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anupama</span>. His first sermon was preached in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mekhala</span> Park, and among his first disciples were his step-brother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saraṇa</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Purohita</span>’s son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhāvitatta</span>. His Twin miracle was performed in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunandavatī</span>. The Bodhisatta was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atula</span>. One of the Buddha’s chief assemblies was on the occasion of his solving the questions of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Arindama</span> on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nirodha</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saraṇa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhāvitatta</span> were his chief monks and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upasenā</span> his chief nuns. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> was his personal attendant. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Varuṇa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saraṇa</span> were his chief lay supporters among men and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cālā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upacālā</span> among women. His body was ninety cubits in height, and he died at the age of ninety thousand in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgārāma</span>, where a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thūpa</span> of four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojanas</span> was erected over his ashes. Bv.v.1ff.; Bv­a.125f.; Ja.i.30,34,35, 40.
