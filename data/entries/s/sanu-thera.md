# Sānu Thera

## English source

Sānu Thera. He was born in a family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> after his father had left home for the ascetic life. The mother, naming him <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sānu</span>, took him at the age of seven to the monks for ordination, thinking thus to ensure for him supreme happiness. He was known as Sānu the Novice (*Sānu Sāmaṇera*), and became a very learned teacher of the doctrine, practising the meditation of love (*mettā*), and was popular among gods and men.

His mother in a previous birth was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>. Later, Sānu lost his intellectual discernment and grew distraught and longed to go roaming. His former <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> mother seeing this, warned his human mother as described in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sānusutta</span> (*q.v.*). The latter was overwhelmed with grief, and, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sānu</span> visited her, he found her weeping. She told him that he was as good as dead in that he had rejected the Buddha’s teaching and turned again to lower things, hence her sorrow. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sānu</span> was filled with anguish, and, strengthening his insight, he soon won *Arahant*-ship (Thag­a.i.113f).

He is evidently identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udakadāyaka</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (Ap.i.205). In the past, he saw <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siddhattha Buddha</span> having his meal and brought him water for his hands and feet and face and mouth. Sixty-one *kappas* ago he was a king, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vimala</span>.

The story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sānu</span> is given also in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta Commentary</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (SN­a.i.235ff.; Dhp­a.iv.18ff), but the details differ. There, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sānu</span>’s human mother is portrayed as encouraging him to return to the lay life. His <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>-mother went to his human mother’s home, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sānu</span> was waiting for a meal, took possession of his body, twisted his neck, and felled him to the ground, where he lay foaming at the mouth. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sānu</span>’s mother was filled with despair. The *Yakkhinī* then revealed herself and exhorted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sānu</span> not to behave foolishly by disregarding the Buddha’s teaching. When he regained his senses, his human mother, too, pointed out the disadvantages of household life. When he declared his intention of not returning to lay life, she fed him with choice food and gave him a set of three robes that he might receive the *upasampadā* ordination. He then sought the Buddha, who urged him to fresh and strenuous effort. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sānu</span> was famous as a mighty teacher throughout <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>. He lived to one hundred and twenty years.
