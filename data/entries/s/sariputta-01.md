# Sāriputta 01

## English source

Sāriputta 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta Thera</span>. The chief disciple (*Aggasāvaka*) of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>. He is also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upatissa</span>, which was evidently his personal name (MN.i.150). The commentators say that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upatissa</span> was the name of his village and that he was the eldest son of the chief family in the village, but other accounts give his village as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālaka</span>. His father was the Brahmin, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅganta</span> (Dhp­a.ii.84), and his mother, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rūpasārī</span>. It was because of his mother’s name that he came to be called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>. In Sanskrit texts his name occurs as Śāriputra, Śāliputra, Śārisuta, Śāradvatīputra. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (ii.480) he is also called Sārisambhava.

The name <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upatissa</span> is hardly ever mentioned in the books. He had three younger brothers – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upasena</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span> (afterwards called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khadiravaniya</span>) – and three sisters – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cālā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upacālā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sisūpacālā</span>; all of whom joined the Saṅgha. Dhp­a.ii.188; cf. Mvu.iii.50; for details of them, see *s.v.*; mention is also made of an uncle of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and of a nephew, both of whom he took to the Buddha, thereby rescuing them from false views (Dhp­a.ii.230-2); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uparevata</span> was his nephew (SN­a.iii.175).

The story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>’s conversion and the account of his past lives, which prepared him for his eminent position as the Buddha’s Chief Disciple, have been given under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Moggallāna</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> had a very quick intuition, and he became a *Sotāpanna* immediately after hearing the first two lines of the stanza spoken by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji</span>. After his attainment of *Sotāpatti*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷita</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>) wished to go with him to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span> to see the Buddha, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, always grateful to his teachers, suggested that they should first seek their teacher, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sañjaya</span>, to give him the good news and go with him to the Buddha. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sañjaya</span> refused to fall in with this plan. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> attained *Arahant*-ship on the seventh day after his ordination, but it was not till a week later that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> became an *Arahant*. He was staying, at the time, with the Buddha, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sūkarakhatalena</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, and he reached his goal as a result of hearing the Buddha preach the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vedānapariggahasutta</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghanakha</span>. This account is summarized from Dhp­a.i.73 ff.; AN­a.i.88 ff.; Thag­a.ii.93 ff. Ap.i.15ff.; the story of their conversion is given at Vin.i.38ff.

In the assembly of monks and nuns, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> was declared by the Buddha foremost among those who possessed wisdom (*etad-aggaṁ mahāpaññānaṁ*, AN.i.23). He was considered by the Buddha as inferior only to himself in wisdom. SN­a.ii.45; his greatest exhibition of wisdom followed the Buddha’s descent from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> to the gates of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span>, when the Buddha asked questions of the assembled multitude, which none but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> could answer. But some questions were outside the range of any but a Buddha (Dhp­a.iii.228 f.; cf. Snp­a.ii.570f.). Similarly knowledge of the thoughts and inclinations of people were beyond <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>; only a Buddha possesses such knowledge (Dhp­a.iii.426; Ja.i.182). Further, only a Buddha could find suitable subjects for meditation for everybody without error (Snp­a.i.18), and read their past births without limitation (Snp­a, ii.571).

The Buddha would frequently merely suggest a topic, and Sāriputta would preach a sermon on it in detail, and thereby win the Buddha’s approval (see, e.g., MN.i.13; iii.46, 55, 249). The Buddha is recorded as speaking high praise of him: “Wise are you, Sāriputta, comprehensive and manifold your wisdom, joyous and swift, sharp and fastidious. Even as the eldest son of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span> king turns the Wheel as his father has turned it, so do you rightly turn the Wheel Supreme of the Dhamma, even as I have turned it.” (SN.i.191; cf. Snp.vs.556 f., where the Buddha is asked by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sela</span>, who is his general, and the Buddha replies that it is Sāriputta who turns the Wheel of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span>; also MN.iii.29). He thus came to be called *Dhammasenāpati*, just as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> was called *Dhammabhaṇḍāgārika*. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anupadasutta</span> is one long eulogy of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> by the Buddha. He is there held up as the supreme example of the perfect disciple, risen to mastery and perfection in noble virtue, noble concentration, noble perception, noble deliverance. MN.iii.25ff. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Gosiṅgasutta Sāriputta</span> expresses his view that that monk is best who is master of his heart and is not mastered by it. The Buddha explains that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> was stating his own nature (MN.i.215 f.). The Buddha did not, however, hesitate to blame <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> when necessary e.g., the occasion when some novices, becoming noisy, were sent away by the Buddha, whose motive Sāriputta misunderstood (MN.i.459). And again, when Sāriputta did not look after <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span> properly, making it necessary for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span> to spend the whole night in the Buddha’s jakes (Ja.i.161f.).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saccavibhaṅgasutta</span> (MN.iii.248) he is compared to a mother teacher, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> is like a child’s wet nurse; Sāriputta trains in the fruits of conversion, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> trains in the highest good. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍapātapārisuddhisutta</span> (MN.iii.294f) the Buddha commends Sāriputta for the aloofness of his life and instructs him in the value of reflection. Other instances are given of the Buddha instructing and examining him on various topics e.g., on *bhūtaṁ* (“what has come to be”) (SN.ii.47f), on the five *indriyas* (SN.v.220f., 225f., 233f), and on *Sotāpatti*. SN.v.347; we find the Buddha also instructing him on the cultivation of tranquillity (AN.i.65); on the destruction of “I” and “mine” (AN.i.133); the reasons for failure and success in enterprises (AN.ii.81f.); the four ways of acquiring personality (*attabhāva*) (AN.ii.159); the methods of exhortation (AN.iii.198); the acquisition of joy that comes through seclusion (AN.iii.207); the noble training for the layman (211f.); six things that bring spiritual progress to a monk (424f.); seven similar things (AN.iv.30); the seven grounds for praising a monk (AN.iv.35); the things and persons a monk should revere (AN.iv.120f.); the eight attributes of a monk free from the cankers (AN.iv.223f.); the nine persons who, although they die with an attached remainder for rebirth, are yet free from birth in hell among animals and among *Petas* (379 f.); and the ten powers of a monk who has destroyed the cankers (AN.v.174 f.).

We also find instances of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> questioning his colleagues, or being questioned by them, on various topics. Thus he is questioned by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Koṭṭhita</span> on *kamma* (SN.ii.112 f.); and on *yoniso manasikāra* (progressive discipline, SN.iii.176 f.); on *avijjā* and *vijjā* (ibid., 172 f.); on the fetters of sense perception (SN.iv.162 f.); on certain questions pronounced by the Buddha as indeterminate (SN.iv.384 f.); on whether anything is left remaining after the passionless ending of the six spheres of contact (AN.ii.161); and on the purpose for which monks lead the *brahmacariya* under the Buddha (AN.iv.382). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vedallasutta</span> (MN.i.292 ff.) records a long discourse preached by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Koṭṭhita</span>. He is mentioned as questioning <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> on the terms *ātāpī* and *ottāpī* (SN.ii.195f.), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> on *sekha* (SN.v.174 f., 298f). On another occasion, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> tells <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> of his power of seeing the thousand-fold world system, his unshaken energy, and his untroubled mindfulness. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> tells him that his *Deva*-sight is mere conceit, his claims to energy conceit, and his mindfulness just worrying, and exhorts him to abandon thoughts of them all. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> follows his advice and becomes an *Arahant*. AN.i.281f.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> asks <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> regarding the “undefiled” (their conversation forms the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anaṅgaṇasutta</span>, MN.i.25 ff.), and, at the conclusion of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gulissānisutta</span>, inquires whether the states of consciousness mentioned in that *Sutta* were incumbent only on monks from the wilds or also on those from the villages (MN.i.472f.). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> questions <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upavāṇa</span> regarding the *bojjhaṅgā* (SN.v.76), and is questioned by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> regarding *Sotāpatti* (SN.v.346, 362) as regards the reason why some beings are set free in this very life while others are not (AN.ii.167), and on the winning of perfect concentration (AN.v.8, 320). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> also questions <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> (AN.iii.201f.) on the speedy knowledge of aptness in things (*kusaladhammesu khippanisanti*), and, again, on how a monk may learn new doctrines and retain old ones without confusion (AN.iii.361). In both these cases <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> asks <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> to answer the questions himself, and, at the end of his discourse, praises him. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rathavinītasutta</span> (MN.i.145 ff.) records a conversation between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa Mantānīputta</span>, for whom he had the greatest respect, after hearing the Buddha’s eulogy of him. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> had given instructions that he should be told as soon as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span> came to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> and took the first opportunity of seeing him. Among others who held discussions with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> are mentioned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samiddhi</span> (AN.iv.385), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamaka</span> (SN.iii.109f.), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candikāputta</span> (AN.iv.403), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Laḷudāyi</span> (AN.iv.414).

Among laymen who had discussions with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atula</span> (Dhp­a.iii.327), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nakulapitā</span> (SN.iii.2f.) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhānañjāni</span> (MN.ii.186); <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalī</span> (immediately after his birth; Ja.i.408), also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paribbājakas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambukhādaka</span> (SN.iv.251f.), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmaṇḍaka</span> (SN.iv.261 f.; AN.v.120), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasūra</span> (Snp­a.ii.538), and the female <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paribbājakas</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saccā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lolā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avavādakā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭācārā</span> (Ja.iii.1), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇḍalakesī</span> (Dhp­a.ii.223f.). He is also said to have visited the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paribbājakas</span> in order to hold discussion with them (AN.iv.378); see also SN.iii.238f., where a *Paribbājaka* consults him on modes of eating.

The care of the Saṅgha and the protection of its members’ integrity was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>’s especial concern by virtue of his position as the Buddha’s Chief Disciple. Thus we find him being sent with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> to bring back the monks who had seceded with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>. His admonitions to the monks sometimes made him unpopular e.g., in the case of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji-Punabbasukā</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chabbaggiyā</span> (who singled him out for special venom) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span> (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span>, who reviled both <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>, Dhp­a.ii.110 f.). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span> declared his intention of committing suicide, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> attempted to dissuade him, but without success (SN.iv.55ff.; see also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channovādasutta</span>). Monks sought his advice in their difficulties (see, e.g., SN.iv.103, where a monk reports to him that a colleague has returned to the household life, and asks what he is to do about it). He was greatly perturbed by the dissensions of the monks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>, and consulted the Buddha, at length, as to what he could do about it (Vin.i.354). He was meticulous about rules laid down by the Buddha. Thus a rule had been laid down that one monk could ordain only one *samaṇera*, and when a boy was sent to him for ordination from a family which had been of great service to him, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> refused the request of the parents till the Buddha had rescinded the rule (Vin.i.83). Another rule forbade monks to eat garlic (*lasuna*), and when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="119" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> lay ill and knew he could be cured by garlic, even then he refused to eat them till permission was given by the Buddha for him to do so (Vin.ii.140). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="120" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Vin.ii.140f) describes how, at the monastery in which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="121" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> lived, when the other monks had gone for alms, he made the round of the entire building, sweeping the un-swept places, filling empty vessels with water, arranging furniture, etc., lest heretics, coming to the monastery, should say: “Behold the residences of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="122" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>’s pupils.” But even then he did not escape censure from his critics.

A story is told (Dhp­a.iv.184f) of how he was once charged with greed, and the Buddha himself had to explain to the monks that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="123" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> was blameless. While <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="124" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> was severe in the case of those who failed to follow the Buddha’s discipline, he did not hesitate to rejoice with his fellow monks in their successes. Thus we find him congratulating <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="125" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> on the joy he obtained from his *iddhi* powers, and praising his great attainments (praise which evoked equally generous counter praise) (SN.ii.275f), and eulogising <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="126" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> on his perfected discipline won through the practice of the four *satipaṭṭhāna*s (SN.v.301f). It was the great encouragement given by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="127" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="128" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samitigutta</span> (*q.v.*), when the latter lay ill with leprosy in the infirmary, which helped him to become an *Arahant*. It was evidently the custom of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="129" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> to visit sick monks, as did the Buddha himself (Thag­a.i.176). So great was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="130" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>’s desire to encourage and recognize merit in his colleagues that he once went about praising <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="131" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>’s *iddhi* powers, which made it difficult for him when later he had to proclaim, at the bidding of the Saṅgha, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="132" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>’s evil nature (Vin.ii.189).

Several instances are given of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="133" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> instructing the monks and preaching to them of his own accord on various topics – apart from the preaching of the well-known *Suttas* assigned to him (e.g., SN.ii.274; v.70; AN.i.63; ii.160; iii.186, 190, 196, 200, 292, 340; iv.325, 328, 365; v.94, 102, 123, 315, 356f). Sometimes these *Suttas* were supplementary to the Buddha’s own discourses (e.g., MN.i.13, 24, 184, 469). Among the most famous of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="134" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>’s discourses are the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="135" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dasuttarasutta</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="136" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅgītisutta</span> (*q.v.*). Though <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="137" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> was friendly with all the eminent monks surrounding the Buddha, there was very special affection between him and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="138" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> and also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="139" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>. We are told that this was because <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="140" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> was the Buddha’s special attendant, a duty which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="141" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> would have been glad to undertake. For details of this see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="142" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Moggallāna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="143" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="144" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> himself had the highest regard and affection for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="145" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>. It is recorded in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="146" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (SN.i.63) that once, when the Buddha asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="147" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, “Do you also, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="148" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, approve of our <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="149" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>?” <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="150" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> replied, “Who, Sir, that is not childish or corrupt or stupid or of perverted mind, will not approve of him? Wise is he, his wisdom comprehensive and joyous and swift, sharp and fastidious. Small is he in his desires and contented; loving seclusion and detachment, of rampant energy. A preacher is he, accepting advice, a critic, a scourge of evil.”

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="151" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> was specially attached, also, to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="152" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span>, the Buddha’s son, who was entrusted to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="153" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> for ordination. Mention is made of a special *Sutta* in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="154" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhimanikāya</span> (the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="155" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Rāhulovādasutta</span>; MN.i.421f), in which he urges <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="156" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span> to practise the study of breathing. The special regard which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="157" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> had for the Buddha and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="158" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span> extended also to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="159" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulamātā</span>, for we find that when she was suffering from flatulence <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="160" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span> consulted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="161" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, who obtained for her some mango juice, a known remedy for the disease (Ja.ii.392f). On another occasion he obtained from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="162" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> rice mixed with ghee and with red fish for flavouring when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="163" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhulamātā</span> suffered from some stomach trouble (Ja.ii.433). Among laymen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="164" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> had special regard for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="165" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>; when the latter lay ill he sent for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="166" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, who visited him with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="167" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> and preached to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="168" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍikovādasutta</span>. At the end of the discourse <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="169" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span> said he had never before heard such a homily. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="170" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> said they were reserved for monks only, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="171" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span> asked that they could be given to the laity and to young men of undimmed vision. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="172" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span> died soon after and was reborn in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="173" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span>. MN.iii.258 ff.; cf. SN.v.380, which probably refers to an earlier illness of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="174" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>. He recovered immediately after the preaching of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="175" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>’s sermon, and served <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="176" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> with rice from his own cooking pot. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="177" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> also, evidently, had great esteem for the householder <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="178" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta</span>, for we are told (Dhp­a.ii.74) that he once paid a special visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="179" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Macchikāsaṇḍa</span> to see him.

Several incidents are related in the books showing the exemplary qualities possessed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="180" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> e.g., the stories of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="181" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tambadāṭhika</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="182" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span> and his wife, the poor woman in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="183" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇḍakakucchisindhavajātaka</span> (Ja 254) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="184" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Losakatissajātaka</span> (Ja 41) (*q.v.*). These show his great compassion for the poor and his eagerness to help them. Reference has already been made to his first teacher, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="185" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sañjaya</span>, whom he tried, but failed, to convert to the Buddha’s faith. His second teacher was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="186" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji</span>. It is said that every night on going to bed he would do obeisance to the quarter in which he knew <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="187" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji</span> to be and would sleep with his head in that direction. Dhp­a.iv.150 f.; cf. Snp­a.i.328. If <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="188" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji</span> were in the same *vihāra*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="189" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> would visit him immediately after visiting the Buddha. It was in connection with this that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="190" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammasutta</span> (*q.v.*) was preached.

The stories of the *Sāmaṇeras* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="191" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sukha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="192" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍita</span>, and of the monk <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="193" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rādha</span>, also show his gratitude towards any who had shown him favour (see also Vin.i.55 f). His extreme affection for and gratitude to the Buddha are shown in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="194" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sampasādanīyasutta</span> (*q.v.*). That <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="195" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> possessed great patience is shown by the story (Dhp­a.iv.146f) of the Brahmin who, to test his patience, struck him as he entered the city for alms. But when he was wrongly accused and found it necessary to vindicate his good name, he did not hesitate to proclaim his innocence at great length and to declare his pre-eminence in virtue (see, e.g., his “lion’s roar” at AN.iv.373ff). Another characteristic of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="196" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> was his readiness to take instruction from others, however modest. Thus one story relates how, in absent mindedness, he let the fold of his robe hang down. A novice said, “Sir, the robe should be draped around you,” and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="197" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> agreed, saying, “Good, you have done well to point it out to me,” and going a little way, he draped the robe round him (Thag­a.ii.116). A quaint story is told of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="198" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> who, going through the air at night, saw <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="199" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> wrapt in meditation, his head newly shaved (Ud.iv.4). The sight of the shining head was a great temptation to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="200" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>, and, in spite of his companion’s warning, he dealt a blow on the Thera*’*s head. The blow was said to have been hard enough to shatter a mountain, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="201" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> suffered only a slight headache afterwards.

Mention is made of two occasions on which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="202" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> fell ill. Once he had fever and was cured by lotus stalks which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="203" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> obtained for him from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="204" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mandākinī</span> Lake (Vin.i.214). On the other occasion he had stomach trouble, which was again cured by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="205" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> giving him garlic (*lasuna*), to eat which the rule regarding the use of garlic had to be rescinded by the Buddha (Vin.ii.140).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="206" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> was fond of meal-cakes (*piṭṭhakhajjaka*), but finding that they tended to make him greedy he made a vow never to eat them again (Ja.i.310).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="207" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> died some months before the Buddha. It is true that the account of the Buddha’s death in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="208" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Parinibbānasutta</span> ignores all reference to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="209" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, though it does introduce him (DN.ii.81 ff) shortly before as uttering his “lion’s roar” (*sīhanāda*), his great confession of faith in the Buddha, which, in the commentarial account, he made when he took leave of the Buddha to die. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="210" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (SN.v.161) records that he died at *Nālagāmaka* (the place of his birth), and gives an eulogy of him pronounced by the Buddha after his death (SN.v.163f).

There is no need to doubt the authenticity of this account. It merely states that when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="211" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> was at *Nālagāmaka* he was afflicted with a sore disease. His brother, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="212" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span> *Samaṇuddesa*, was attending on him when he died. His body was cremated, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="213" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span> took the relics to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="214" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="215" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>’s begging bowl and outer robe. The relics were wrapped in his water-strainer. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="216" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span> first broke the news to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="217" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, who confessed that when he heard it his mind was confused and his body felt as though drugged. Cf. Thag.vs.1034; see also the eulogy of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="218" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="219" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅgīsa</span> during his lifetime (Thag.1231-3). Together they sought the Buddha and told him of the event, and the Buddha pointed out to them the impermanence of all things.

Xuanzang saw the *stūpa* erected over the relics of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="220" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> in the town of Kāḷapināka (Beal-Xuan, op.cit., ii.177).

The Commentaries give more details. The Buddha returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="221" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> after his last *vassa* in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="222" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Beluvagāma</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="223" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> sought him there, and, realizing that his death would come in seven days, he decided to visit his mother, for she, though the mother of seven *Arahants*, had no faith in the Saṅgha. This was because all her children joined the Saṅgha and left her desolate in spite of the forty crores of wealth which lay in the house. It is said that when Sāriputta had gone home on a previous occasion, she abused both him and his companions roundly (Dhp­a.iv.164f.). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="224" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span> was also in the company. He therefore asked his brother, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="225" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span>, to prepare for the journey to *Nālagāmaka* with five hundred others, and then took leave of the Buddha after performing various miracles and declaring his faith in the Buddha and uttering his “lion’s roar.” A large concourse followed him to the gates of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="226" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, and there he addressed them and bade them stay behind. In seven days he reached <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="227" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālaka</span>, where he was met by his nephew, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="228" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uparevata</span>, outside the gates. He sent him on to warn his mother of his arrival with a large number of people. She, thinking that he had once more returned to the lay life, made all preparations to welcome him and his companions. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="229" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> took up his abode in the room in which he was born (*jātovaraka*). There he was afflicted with dysentery. His mother, unaware of this and sulking because she found he was still a monk, remained in her room. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="230" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Four Regent Gods</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="231" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="232" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span> waited upon him. She saw them, and having found out who they were, went to her son’s room. There she asked him if he were really greater than all these deities, and, when he replied that it was so, she reflected on the greatness of her son and her whole body was suffused with joy. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="233" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> then preached to her, and she became a *Sotāpanna*. Feeling that he had paid his debt to his mother, he sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="234" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span> to fetch the monks, and, on their arrival, he sat up with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="235" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span>’s help and asked if he had offended them in any way during the forty-four years of his life as a monk. On receiving their assurance that he had been entirely blameless, he wiped his lips with his robe and lay down, and, after passing through various *jhānas*, died at break of dawn.

His mother made all arrangements for the funeral, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="236" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span> assisted in the ceremony. When the cremation was over, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="237" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> extinguished the flames with perfumed water, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="238" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span> gathered together the relics. This account is summarized from SN­a.iii.172ff.; similar accounts are found at DN­a.ii.549f, etc. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="239" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>’s death is also referred to at Ja.i.391.

Among those who came to pay honour to the pyre was the goddess <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="240" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revatī</span> (*q.v.*). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="241" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> died on the full moon day of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="242" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kattika</span> (October to November) preceding the Buddha’s death, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="243" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> died a fortnight later. SN­a.iii.181; Ja.i.391; both <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="244" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="245" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> were older than the Buddha because they were born “*anuppanne yeva hi Buddhe*, before the arising of the Buddha” (Dhp­a.i.73).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="246" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> had many pupils, some of whom have already been mentioned. Among others were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="247" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="248" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇḍhadinna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="249" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Sārī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="250" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vanavāsikatissa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="251" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkicca</span> (*q.v.*), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="252" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhū</span>, who brought to Ceylon the Buddha’s collar-bone, which he deposited in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="253" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahiyaṅgaṇa</span> *cetiya* (Mhv.i.37f). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="254" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>’s brother, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="255" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upavāṇa</span>, predeceased him, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="256" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> was with him when he died of snake bite at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="257" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sappasoṇḍika</span> *pabbāra* (SN.iv.40f).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="258" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>’s special proficiency was in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="259" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span>. It is said that when preaching the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="260" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span>, to the gods of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="261" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, the Buddha would visit <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="262" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anotatta</span> every day, leaving a *nimitta*-Buddha, on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="263" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s throne to continue the preaching (Dhs-a.16f., DN­a.i.15, where it is said that at the end of the First Recital the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="264" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span> was given in charge of five hundred *Arahants*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="265" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> being already dead). After having bathed in the lake he would take his midday rest. During this time <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="266" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> would visit him and learn from the Buddha all that had been preached of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="267" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span> during the previous day. Having thus learnt the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="268" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="269" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> taught it to his five hundred pupils. Their acquirement of the seven books of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="270" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span> coincided with the conclusion of the Buddha’s sermon in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="271" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>. Thus the textual order of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="272" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span> originated with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="273" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, and the numerical series was determined by him.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="274" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> is identified with various characters in numerous Jātakas. Thus he was

- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="275" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Candakumāra</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="276" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Devadhamma</span>,
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="277" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Lakkhaṇa</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="278" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Lakkhaṇa</span>,
- the knight in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="279" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Bhojājānīya</span>,
- the monkey in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="280" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Tittira</span>,

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="399" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> 02. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="400" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta Thera</span>. A monk of Ceylon. He lived in the reign of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="401" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu I</span>., and was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="402" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgaramatī</span> (Sās-d.63) on account of his erudition. The king built for him a special residence attached to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="403" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavanavihāra</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="404" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulatthipura</span> (Cv.lxxviii.34).

Among his works are the *Vinayasaṅgaha* or the *Vinayavinicchaya*, a summary of the Vinaya, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="405" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāraṭṭhadīpanī</span> on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="406" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samantapāsādikā</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="407" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāraṭṭhamañjūsā</span> on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="408" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atthasālinī</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="409" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Līnatthappakāsinī</span> on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="410" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Papañcasūdani</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="411" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> had several well-known pupils, among whom were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="412" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅgharakkhita</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="413" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumaṅgala</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="414" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhanāga</span>, *Udumbaragiri* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="415" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Medhaṅkara</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="416" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vācissara</span> (Gv.67, 71; Svd.1203; SM. 69; PLC, 189ff).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="417" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> was also a Sanskrit scholar, and wrote the *Pañjikālaṅkāra* or *Ratnamatipañjikāṭīkā* to Ratnasrījñāṇa’s *Pañjikā* to the *Candragomivyākaraṇa*.
