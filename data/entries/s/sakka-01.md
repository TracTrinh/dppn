# Sakka 01

## English source

Sakka 01. Almost always spoken of as “*devānaṁ indo*,” chief (or king) of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (SN.i.229; Dhp­a.i.264) contains a list of his names:

he is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maghavā</span>, because as a human being, in a former birth, he was a Brahmin named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magha</span> (but see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magha</span>; cf. Sanskrit Maghavant as an epithet of Indra).

As such he bestowed gifts from time to time, hence his name <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Purindada</span> (generous giver in former births or giver in towns, cf. Indra’s epithet Purandara, destroyer of cities).

Because he gives generously and thoroughly (sakkaccaṁ) he is known as Sakka. Sakra occurs many times in the Vedas as an adjective, qualifying gods (chiefly Indra), and is explained as meaning “able, capable.” It is, however, not found as a name in pre-Buddhist times.

Because he gives away dwelling places (*āvasathaṁ*) he is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsava</span> (but see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsava</span>).

Because in one moment he can think of one thousand matters, he is called Sahassakkha (also Sahassanetta).

Because he married the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asura</span> maiden <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujā</span>, he is called Sujampati. For the romantic story of Sakka’s marriage, see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujā</span>. Thus <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujā</span>’s father, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span>, became Sakka’s father-in-law. Several quaint stories are related about father and son-in-law. The two sometimes quarrelled and at others lived together in peace (SN­a.i.265).

Because he governs the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> he is called Devānaṁ Indo (See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Inda</span>).

Elsewhere (e.g., DN.ii.270; MN.i.252) Sakka is addressed as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosiya</span>.

He is also spoken of as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>. MN.i.252; cf. SN.i.206 (*Sakkanāmako Yakkho*); at SN.i.47 <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgha</span> *Devaputta* (Sakka) is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vatrabhū</span>, slayer of Vṛtra (SN­a.i.83);

Sakka is also, in the Jātakas, called *Gandhabbarāja* (Ja.vi.260) and *Mahinda* (Ja.v.397, 411).

Sakka rules over <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> *devaloka*, the lowest heaven but one of the Deva plane. His palace is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vejayanta</span> and his chariot bears the same name. Though king of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, he is no absolute monarch. He is imagined rather in the likeness of a chieftain of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> clan. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> meet and deliberate in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhammā</span> *sabhā* and Sakka consults with them rather than issues them commands. On such occasions, the Four Regent Devas are present in the assembly with their followers of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cātummahārājika</span> world (see, e.g., DN.ii.207f., 220f). Among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, Sakka is more or less *primus inter pares*, yet he surpasses his companions in ten things: length of life, beauty, happiness, renown, power; and in the degree of his five sense experiences: sight, hearing, smelling, taste and touch. AN.iv.242; these are also attributed to the rulers of the other *Deva* worlds.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> the Buddha gives seven rules of conduct, which rules Sakka carried out as a human being, thus attaining to his celestial sovereignty (SN.i.228, 229, 231; cf. Mil. 90; for details of these see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magha</span>). When the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> fight the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span> they do so under the banner and orders of Sakka. For details of Sakka’s conquest of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span> see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asura</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span> called him *Jarasakka* (Ja.i.202). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Varuṇa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isāna</span> are also mentioned as having been associated with him in supreme command (SN.i.219).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> a whole <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta</span> – one of the shortest, consisting of twenty-five short *Suttas* – is devoted to Sakka.

- In the first and second <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Suttas</span> Sakka praises energy (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t23">viriya</span>);
- in the third he denounces timidity;
- in the fourth he shows forbearance to his enemy;
- in the fifth he advocates the conquest of anger by kindness;
- in the sixth kindness to animals;
- in the seventh he denounces trickery, even towards enemies (the enemy, in this case, is his father-in law, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Vepacitta</span>. Sakka had a reputation for great forbearance. In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Sutta</span> 22 a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Yakkha</span> is said to have come and to have sat on his throne, to anger him. But Sakka showed him great honour and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Yakkha</span> vanished. The Commentary adds (SN.A.i.272) that it was no <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Yakkha</span>, but a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Rūpāvacara Brahma</span>, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kodhabhakkha</span>, who had come to test Sakka’s patience);
- and in the ninth he preaches courtesy and honour towards the wise.
- In the eleventh are described the seven life-long habits which raised him to his present eminent position;
- twelve and thirteen repeat this and explain his titles.
- In the fourteenth Sakka explains how new gods, who outshine the old ones, do so because they have observed the Buddha’s teaching.
- In the fifteenth he describes as the most beautiful spot that where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Arahants</span> dwell;
- in the sixteenth he praises gifts to the Saṅgha (the story connected with this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Sutta</span> is that of Sakka, seeing the people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Aṅga</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Magadha</span> make preparations for a great sacrifice to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mahā Brahma</span>, feels pity for them and comes among them in the guise of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Brahma</span>, advising them to take their offerings to the Buddha and seek his counsel (SN­a.i.270;
- in the seventeenth he praises the Buddha, but is told by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sahampati</span> that he has selected the wrong attributes for praise.
- In eighteen to twenty he says that whereas Brahmins and nobles on earth and the gods of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Cātummahārājika</span> world and of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Tāvatiṁsa</span> worship him, he himself worships good men and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Arahants</span>.
- Numbers twenty-one, twenty-two, twenty-four and twenty-five are against anger, and twenty-three is against deceit.

These and other passages show that Sakka was considered by the early Buddhists as a god of high character, kindly and just, but not perfect, and not very intelligent. His imperfections are numerous: in spite of his very great age (at Ja.ii.312, Sakka’s life is given as lasting thirty million and sixty times one hundred thousand years), he is still subject to death and rebirth (AN.i.144); as an example of this, it is mentioned that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunetta</span> had thirty-five times been reborn as Sakka (AN.iv.105), a statement confirmed by the Buddha (AN.iv.89). Sakka is not free from the three deadly evils – lust, ill-will, stupidity (AN.i.144. The story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohiṇī</span> shows that Sakka was very susceptible to the charms of beauty. He evidently liked other people to enjoy life and sent a heavenly dancer to amuse <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Panāda</span> when nobody on earth could accomplish that feat (Snp­a.ii.400). On another occasion, as Sakka was rejoicing in his triumph over the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span>, he saw a crane on a hill top who wished to be able to eat fish without going down into the stream. Sakka immediately sent the stream in full flood, to the hill top (Ja.iii.252)); nor is he free from anxiety. He is timid, given to panic, to fright, to running away (he is mentioned in the Jātakas as frightened of ascetics who practised severe penances, lest they should unseat him from his throne, e.g., Ja.ii.394; also the stories of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visayha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lomasa Kassapa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Akitti</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kañcana</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isisiṅga</span>.)

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakkapañhasutta</span> (*q.v.*), Sakka is said to have visited the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vediyagiri</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambasaṇḍā</span> and to have asked him a series of questions. He sends <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span> with his *vinā* to play and sing to the Buddha and to obtain permission for him (Sakka) to visit him and question him. It was Sakka who had given the *Beluvapaṇḍuvīnā* to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span> (Snp­a.ii.394).

The Buddha says to himself that Sakka, for a long time past, has led a pure life, and gives him permission to question him on any subject. It is stated in the course of the *Sutta* (DN.ii.270) that it was not the first time that Sakka had approached the Buddha for the same purpose. He had gone to him at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saḷalaghara</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, but found him in meditation, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhuñjatī</span>, wife of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span>, waiting on him. He therefore left with a request to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhuñjatī</span> to greet the Buddha in his name. He also declares (DN.ii.286) that he has become a *Sotāpanna* and has earned for himself the right to be reborn eventually in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Akaniṭṭhā</span> world, whence he will pass entirely away.

The Commentary says that Sakka was constantly seeing the Buddha and was the most zealous of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> in the discharge of his duties to the *Sāsana*. DN­a.iii.697. In the *Sutta* Sakka admits (DN.ii.284) that he visited other Brahmins and recluses as well. They were pleased to see him, and boasted that they had nothing to teach him; but he had to teach them what he knew. But this visit to the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vediyagiri</span> had a special object. Sakka saw signs that his life was drawing to an end and was frightened by this knowledge. He therefore went to the Buddha to seek his help. It adds that, as Sakka sat listening to the Buddha, he died in his old life and was reborn a new and young Sakka (DN­a.iii.732; cp. Dhp­a.iii.270); only Sakka himself and the Buddha were aware of what had happened. The Commentary continues (DN­a.iii.740) that Sakka became an “*uddhaṁ sota*,” treading the path of *Anāgāmīs*. As such he will live in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avihā</span> for one thousand *kappas*, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atappa</span> for two thousand, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudassanā</span> for four thousand, and will end in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Akaniṭṭha</span> world, after having enjoyed life in the *Brahma*-worlds for thirty-one thousand *kappas*.

An account of another interview which Sakka had with the Buddha is given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Taṇhāsaṅkhayasutta</span> (*q.v.*). There the question arises regarding the extirpation of cravings. Sakka accepts the Buddha’s answer and leaves him. Anxious to discover whether Sakka has understood the Buddha’s teaching, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> visits Sakka and questions him. Sakka evades the questions and shows <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> the glories of his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vejayanta</span> palace. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> then frightens him by a display of *iddhi*-power, and Sakka repeats to him, word for word, the Buddha’s answer. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> departs satisfied, and Sakka tells his handmaidens that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> is a “fellow of his” in the higher life, meaning, probably, that he himself is a *Sotāpanna* and therefore a kinsman of the *Arahant*.

In a passage in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta</span> (SN.i.201) Sakka is represented as descending from heaven to make an enquiry about *Nibbāna*, and in another (SN.iv.269f.), as listening, in heaven, to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>’s exposition of the simplest duties of a good layman. On another occasion, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span>’s suggestion, Sakka visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span> Thera on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkheyyaka</span> Mountain and listened to a sermon by him (AN.iv.163f.). See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakkasutta</span> (2) and (3).

The later books contain a good deal of additional information regarding Sakka. His city extends for one thousand leagues, and its golden streets are sixty leagues long; his palace <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vejayanta</span> is one thousand leagues high; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhammā</span> hall covers five hundred leagues, his throne of yellow marble (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍukambalasilāsana</span>) is sixty leagues in extent, his white umbrella with its golden wreath is five leagues in circumference, and he himself is accompanied by a glorious array of twenty-five million nymphs (Ja.v.386). Other features of his heaven are the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāricchattaka</span> tree, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandā</span> *pokkharaṇī* and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittalatāvana</span> (DN­a.iii.716; see also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>). His body is three *gāvutas* in height (Dhp­a.iii.269); his chief conveyance is the marvellous elephant <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Erāvaṇa</span> (*q.v.*), but he goes to war in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vejayanta</span> *ratha* (*q.v.*). Reference is often made to his throne, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍukambalasilāsana</span> (*q.v.*), composed of yellow stone. It grows hot when Sakka’s life draws towards its end; or his merit is exhausted; or when some mighty being prays; or, again, through the efficacy of virtue in recluses or Brahmins or other beings, full of potency. Ja.iv.8; when the Buddha, however, sat on it, he was able to conceal it in his robe (Dhp­a.iii.218).

Sakka’s devotion to the Buddha and his religion is proverbial. When the Bodhisatta cut off his hair and threw it into the sky, Sakka took it and deposited it in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷāmaṇicetiya</span> (Ja.i.65). He was present near the Bodhi-tree, blowing his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijayuttara</span> *saṅkha* (*q.v.*), when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> arrived to prevent the Buddha from reaching <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> (Ja.i.72). When the Buddha accepted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>’s invitation to dine in his palace, Sakka, in the guise of a young man, preceded the Buddha and his monks along the street to the palace, singing the Buddha’s praises (Vin.i.38). When the Buddha performed his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamakapāṭihāriya</span> at the foot of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṇḍamba</span>, it was Sakka who built for him a pavilion, and gave orders to the gods of the Wind and the Sun to uproot the pavilions of the heretics and cause them great discomfort (Dhp­a.iii.206, 208). When the Buddha returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="119" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="120" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, whither he went after performing the Twin Miracle, Sakka created three ladders – of gold, of silver, and of jewels respectively – for the Buddha and his retinue (Dhp­a.iii.225).

Sakka was present at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="121" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> when the Buddha visited that city in order to rid it of its plagues. His presence drove away the evil spirits, and the Buddha’s task was thus made easier (Dhp­a.iii.441). When the Buddha and his monks wished to journey one hundred leagues, to visit <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="122" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Subhaddā</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="123" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uggapura</span>, Sakka, with the aid of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="124" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span>, provided them with pavilions (*kūṭāgāra*) in which they might travel by air (Dhp­a.iii.470). Once, when the ponds in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="125" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> were quite dry, the Buddha wished to bathe and Sakka immediately caused rain to fall and the ponds were filled (Ja.i.330). In Sakka’s aspect as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="126" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajirapāṇī</span> (*q.v.*) he protected the Buddha from the insults of those who came to question him. See also the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="127" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ciñcāmāṇavikā</span>, when Sakka protected the Buddha from her charges. Sakka also regarded it as his business to protect the Buddha’s followers, as is shown by the manner in which he came to the rescue of the four seven year old novices – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="128" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkicca</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="129" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍita</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="130" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sopāka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="131" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span> – when they were made to go hungry by a Brahmin and his wife (Dhp­a.iv.176f.).

During the Buddha’s last illness, Sakka ministered to him, performing the most menial tasks, such as carrying the vessel of excrement. Dhp­a.iv.269f. He did the same for other holy men – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="132" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>. Sakka also waited on the Buddha when he was in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="133" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayāsīsa</span> for the conversion of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="134" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tebhātikajaṭilas</span> (Vin.i.28f.); see also the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="135" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambuka</span> (Dhp­a.ii.59). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="136" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> (Ud.iii.7) contains a story of Sakka assuming the guise of a poor weaver and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="137" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujā</span> that of his wife, in order to give alms to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="138" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> who had just risen from a *jhāna*. They succeeded in their ruse, to the great joy of Sakka (cp. Dhp­a.i.424f). On other occasions – e.g., in the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="139" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Duggata</span> Sakka helped poor men to gain merit by providing them with the means for giving alms to the Buddha (Dhp­a.ii.135ff.).

He was present at the Buddha’s death, and uttered, in verse, a simple lament, very different from the studied verses ascribed to *Brahma*. (DN.ii.157; on the importance of this verse, however, see Dial.ii.176, n.1). At the distribution, by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="140" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Doṇa</span>, of the Buddha’s relics, Sakka saw <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="141" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Doṇa</span> hide the Buddha’s right tooth in his turban. Realizing that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="142" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Doṇa</span> was incapable of rendering adequate honour to the relic, Sakka took the relic and deposited it in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="143" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷāmaṇicetiya</span> (DN­a.ii.609). And when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="144" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> was making arrangements to deposit his share of the relics, Sakka gave orders to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="145" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span> to set up a *vālasaṅghātayanta* (fiery machine) for their protection (DN­a.ii.613).

Sakka did all in his power to help followers of the Buddha in their strivings for the attainment of the goal, as in the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="146" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍita</span> *Sāmaṇera*, when he sent the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="147" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Four Regent Gods</span> to drive away the birds, made the Moon deity shroud the moon, and himself stood guard at the door of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="148" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍita</span>’s cell, lest he should be disturbed (Dhp­a.ii.143; cf. the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="149" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sukha</span> Dhp­a.iii.96f.). Often, when a monk achieved his ambition, Sakka was there to express his joy and do him honour, see, e.g., the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="150" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Phussa</span> (Snp­a.i.55f.).

He was ready to help, not only monks and nuns, but also eminent laymen, such as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="151" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotika</span> for whom he built a palace of wondrous splendour, and provided it with every luxury (Dhp­a.iv. 207f). Sakka was always ready to come to the rescue of the good when in distress – e.g., in the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="152" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkhupāla</span> when he became blind; Sakka led him by the hand and took him to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="153" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>. Dhp­a.i.14f. Many instances are found in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="154" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> where Sakka rescued the good in distress – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="155" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammaddhaja</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="156" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Guttila</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="157" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāni</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="158" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candakinnara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="159" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambulā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="160" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="161" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janaka</span>’s mother, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="162" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candakumāra</span>’s mother, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="163" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candā</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="164" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span>.

He loved to test the goodness of men, as in the case of the leper <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="165" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppabuddha</span>, to see if their faith was genuine. Dhp­a.ii.34f.; see also the story of the courtesan in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="166" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurudhammajātaka</span> (Ja, 276, Ja.ii.380).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="167" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> contains several stories of his helping holy men by providing them with hermitages, etc. – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="168" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuddālapaṇḍita</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="169" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthipāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="170" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ayoghara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="171" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotipāla</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="172" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhaṅga</span>), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="173" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sutasoma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="174" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dukūlaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="175" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārikā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="176" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span>. Sometimes, when he found that ascetics were not diligently practising their duties, he would frighten them – e.g., in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="177" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vighāsajātaka</span> (Ja 393) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="178" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Somadattajātaka</span> (Ja 410). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="179" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (iii.370f) contains a story of Sakka punishing a *Deva* called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="180" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Supatiṭṭha</span>, who lived in a banyan tree, because he failed to keep the *rukkhadhamma*.

Sakka appears as the guardian of moral law in the world. When wickedness is rampant among men, or kings become unrighteous, he appears among them to frighten them so that they may do good instead evil. He is on the side of the good against the wicked, and often helps them to realize their goal. Instances of this are seen in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="181" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambacora</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="182" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ayakūṭa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="183" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udaya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="184" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāni</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="185" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="186" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāmanīta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="187" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumbha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="188" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Keḷisīla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="189" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kharaputta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="190" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Dhanuggaha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="191" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhajavihetha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="192" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Biḷārikosiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="193" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇīcora</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="194" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaṇha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="195" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="196" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhaṅga</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="197" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhamiga</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="198" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhābhojanajātaka</span>s. Sakka patronised good men; some of the more eminent he invited to his heaven, sending his charioteer <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="199" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātali</span> to fetch them, and he showed them all honour – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="200" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Guttila</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="201" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mandhātā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="202" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sādhīna</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="203" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nimi</span>; others he rewarded suitably – see, e.g., the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="204" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uragajātaka</span> (Ja 354).

The lesser gods consulted Sakka in their difficulties and problems e.g., in the case of the deity of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="205" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span>’s fourth gateway, who incurred the displeasure of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="206" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span> by advising him to refrain from too much generosity towards the Buddha and his monks (Ja.i.229). Sakka has also to deal with disputes arising among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="207" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> themselves (DN­a.iii.705). On several occasions Sakka helped the Bodhisatta in the practice of his Perfections e.g., as King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="208" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sivi</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="209" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Temiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="210" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nimi</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="211" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span>, also in his birth as a hare; in this last story, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="212" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sasajātaka</span> (Ja 316, *q.v.*), Sakka paints the picture of a hare in the moon to commemorate the Bodhisatta’s sacrifice.

Sakka sometimes answers the prayers of good and barren women and gives them sons – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="213" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumedhā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="214" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīlavatī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="215" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candādevī</span>. Mention is also made of other boons granted by Sakka to various persons. Thus in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="216" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sukajātaka</span> (Ja 429) he visited the parrot who clung to the dead stump of a tree through gratitude, and granted him the boon that the tree should once more become fruitful (Ja.iii.493). He granted four boons to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="217" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇha</span>, that he might be calm, bear no malice or hatred against his neighbour, feel no greed for others’ glory, and no lust towards his neighbour (Ja.iv.10). To <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="218" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Akitti</span> he granted several boons, the last of which was that he should have no more visits from Sakka (Ja.iv.240f)! When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="219" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sivi</span> became blind, Sakka gave him two eyes; these were not natural eyes, but the eyes of Truth, Absolute and Perfect (*saccapāramitā cakkhuṇī*). Sakka confesses that he has not the power of restoring sight; it was the virtue of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="220" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sivi</span> himself which had that power (Ja.iv.410f). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="221" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīlavatī</span> wished for a boon, Sakka, took her to heaven, where he kept her for seven days; then he granted that she should have two sons, one wise and ugly and the other a fool and handsome. He also presented her with a piece of *kusa* grass, a heavenly robe, a piece of sandalwood, the flower of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="222" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāricchattaka</span> tree and a *Kokanda*-lute. All this passed into the possession of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="223" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusa</span>, and, later, Sakka gave him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="224" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verocana</span> jewel (Ja.v.280f., 310). He gave <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="225" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phusatī</span>, mother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="226" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span>, ten boons (Ja.vi.481f) and to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="227" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span> himself he gave eight (Ja.vi.572).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="228" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhaṅgajātaka</span> (Ja 522, Ja.v.392) mention is made of four daughters of Sakka – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="229" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āsā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="230" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saddhā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="231" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hirī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="232" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirī</span>. His wife, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="233" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujā</span>, accompanied him everywhere on his travels (e.g., Ja.iii.491), even into the world of men, because that was the boon she had asked for on her marriage to him (Dhp­a.i.279). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="234" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> was Sakka’s special friend (MN­a.i.476f), and when one <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="235" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> died, it was Sakka’s duty to appoint a successor (Ja.i.328). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="236" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātali</span> (*q.v.*) is Sakka’s charioteer and constant companion. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="237" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span> (*q.v.*) is his “handy man.” Sakka has twenty-five million handmaids and five hundred dove-footed nymphs (*kakuṭapādiniyo*), famed for their beauty. It was the sight of these which tempted the Buddha’s step-brother, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="238" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span>, to give up thoughts of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="239" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janapadakaḷyānī Nandā</span> (Ja.ii.93). Sakka’s special weapon is the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="240" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajirāvudha</span> and his special drum the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="241" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷambara</span> (*q.v.*). His voice is sweet, like the tintinabulation of golden bells (SN­a.i.273).

It is Sakka’s special duty to protect the religion of the Buddha in Ceylon. As the Buddha lay dying, he enjoined on Sakka the task of looking after <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="242" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijaya</span> and his successors. This duty Sakka, in turn, entrusted to the god <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="243" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uppalavaṇṇa</span> (Mhv.vii.1ff). Sakka informed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="244" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> of the right moment for his visit to Ceylon (Mhv.xiii.15). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="245" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span> wished for relics to place in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="246" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thūpārāma</span> *Thūpa*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="247" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span> *Sāmaṇera* visited Sakka and obtained from him the right collar-bone of the Buddha, which Sakka had placed in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="248" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷāmaṇicetiya</span> (Mhv.xvii.9ff). Again, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="249" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> was in need of building materials for the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="250" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span>, it was Sakka who supplied them (Mhv.xxviii.6ff). On the occasion of the enshrining of the relics in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="251" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span>, Sakka gave orders to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="252" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span> to decorate the whole of Ceylon. He also provided the throne and casket of gold for the relics brought from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="253" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgā</span> world by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="254" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇuttara</span> and was himself present at the festival, blowing his conch shell. (Mhv.xxxi.34, 75, 78)

Other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="255" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavāḷas</span> have also their Sakka (*aññehi Cakkavālehi Sakkā āgacchanti*; Ja.i.203.), and in one place (Ja.i.204) mention is made of many thousands of Sakkas.

It is evident from the foregoing account that, as Rhys Davids suggests (Dial.ii.297f), Sakka and Indra are independent conceptions. None of the personal characteristics of Sakka resemble those of Indra. Some epithets are identical but are evidently borrowed, though they are differently explained. The conception of the popular god which appealed to a more barbarous age and to the clans fighting their way into a new country, seems to have been softened and refined in order to meet the ideals of a more cultured and peaceful civilization. The old name no longer fitted the new god, and, as time went on, Sakka came to be regarded as an entirely separate god.
