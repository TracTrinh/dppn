# Sakka 01

## English source

Sakka 01. Almost always spoken of as “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">devānaṁ indo</span>,” chief (or king) of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> (SN.i.229; Dhp­a.i.264) contains a list of his names:

he is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maghavā</span>, because as a human being, in a former birth, he was a Brahmin named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magha</span> (but see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magha</span>; cf. Sanskrit Maghavant as an epithet of Indra).

As such he bestowed gifts from time to time, hence his name <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Purindada</span> (generous giver in former births or giver in towns, cf. Indra’s epithet Purandara, destroyer of cities).

Because he gives generously and thoroughly (sakkaccaṁ) he is known as Sakka. Sakra occurs many times in the Vedas as an adjective, qualifying gods (chiefly Indra), and is explained as meaning “able, capable.” It is, however, not found as a name in pre-Buddhist times.

Because he gives away dwelling places (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">āvasathaṁ</span>) he is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsava</span> (but see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsava</span>).

Because in one moment he can think of one thousand matters, he is called Sahassakkha (also Sahassanetta).

Because he married the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asura</span> maiden <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujā</span>, he is called Sujampati. For the romantic story of Sakka’s marriage, see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujā</span>. Thus <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujā</span>’s father, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span>, became Sakka’s father-in-law. Several quaint stories are related about father and son-in-law. The two sometimes quarrelled and at others lived together in peace (SN­a.i.265).

Because he governs the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> he is called Devānaṁ Indo (See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Inda</span>).

Elsewhere (e.g., DN.ii.270; MN.i.252) Sakka is addressed as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosiya</span>.

He is also spoken of as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>. MN.i.252; cf. SN.i.206 (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sakkanāmako Yakkho</span>); at SN.i.47 <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Devaputta</span> (Sakka) is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vatrabhū</span>, slayer of Vṛtra (SN­a.i.83);

Sakka is also, in the Jātakas, called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Gandhabbarāja</span> (Ja.vi.260) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahinda</span> (Ja.v.397, 411).

Sakka rules over <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">devaloka</span>, the lowest heaven but one of the Deva plane. His palace is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vejayanta</span> and his chariot bears the same name. Though king of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, he is no absolute monarch. He is imagined rather in the likeness of a chieftain of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> clan. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> meet and deliberate in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhammā</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sabhā</span> and Sakka consults with them rather than issues them commands. On such occasions, the Four Regent Devas are present in the assembly with their followers of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cātummahārājika</span> world (see, e.g., DN.ii.207f., 220f). Among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, Sakka is more or less <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t18">primus inter pares</span>, yet he surpasses his companions in ten things: length of life, beauty, happiness, renown, power; and in the degree of his five sense experiences: sight, hearing, smelling, taste and touch. AN.iv.242; these are also attributed to the rulers of the other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span> worlds.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> the Buddha gives seven rules of conduct, which rules Sakka carried out as a human being, thus attaining to his celestial sovereignty (SN.i.228, 229, 231; cf. Mil. 90; for details of these see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magha</span>). When the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> fight the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span> they do so under the banner and orders of Sakka. For details of Sakka’s conquest of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span> see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asura</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span> called him <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Jarasakka</span> (Ja.i.202). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Varuṇa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isāna</span> are also mentioned as having been associated with him in supreme command (SN.i.219).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> a whole <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta</span> – one of the shortest, consisting of twenty-five short <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span> – is devoted to Sakka.
