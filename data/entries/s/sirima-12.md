# Sirimā 12

## English source

Sirimā 12. A courtesan of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> and younger sister of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span>. She was once employed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandamātā</span>) to take her place with her husband (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span>) while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span> herself went away in order to indulge in acts of piety. During this time <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span> tried to injure <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span>, on account of a misunderstanding, but on realizing her error, she begged forgiveness both of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span>, and, at the latter’s suggestion, of the Buddha (the details of this incident are given <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">s.v.</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā Nandamātā</span>). At the conclusion of a sermon preached by the Buddha in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span>’s house, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span> became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>. From that day onwards she gave alms daily to eight monks in her house.

A monk in a monastery, three leagues away, having heard of the excellence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span>’s alms and of her extraordinary beauty from a visiting monk, decided to go and see her. Having obtained a ticket for alms, he went to her house, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span> was ill, and her attendants looked after the monks. When the meal had been served she was brought into the dining hall to pay her respects to the monks. The lustful monk at once fell in love with her and was unable to eat. That same day <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span> died. The Buddha gave instructions that her body should not be burnt, but laid in the charnel ground, protected from birds and beasts. When putrefaction had set in, the king proclaimed that all citizens, on penalty of a fine, should gaze on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span>’s body. The Buddha, too, went with the monks, the lustful monk accompanying them. The Buddha made the king proclaim, with beating of the drum, that anyone who would pay a thousand could have <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span>’s body. There was no response. The price was gradually lowered to one eighth of a penny. Yet no one came forward, even when the body was offered for nothing. The Buddha addressed the monks, pointing out how even those who would have paid one thousand to spend a single night with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span> would not now take her as a gift. Such was the passing nature of beauty. The lustful monk became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span> (Dhp­a.iii.104f.; Vv-a.74ff).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (Snp­a.i.244f, 253f) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sālavatī</span>’s daughter, and succeeded to her mother’s position as courtezan. After death, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span> was born in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yāma</span> world as the wife of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suyāma</span>. When the Buddha was speaking to the monks at her cremation, she visited the spot with five hundred chariots. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janapadakaḷyānī Nandā</span>, who at that time was also a nun, was present, and when the Buddha preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāyavicchandanikasutta</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) she became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span> became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmī</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vimānavatthu</span> (pp.78f., 86) gives the same story, adding that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅgīsa</span> was also present at the preaching of the sermon, and, having obtained the Buddha’s permission, questioned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span> and made her reveal her identity. Here <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span> is said to have been born in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nimmānaratī</span>-world, and no mention is made of her becoming an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmī</span>; while the lustful monk is said to have become an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span> is mentioned in a list of eminent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t18">upāsikās</span> (AN.iv.347; AN­a.ii.791). Eighty-four thousand persons realized the truth after listening to the Buddha’s preaching at the cremation of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span>. Mil.350.
