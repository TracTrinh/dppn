# Sakyā

## English source

Sakyā, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyā</span>. A tribe in North India, to which the Buddha belonged. Their capital was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>. Mention is also made of other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> settlements e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cātumā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khomadussa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmagāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadaha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Silāvatī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nagaraka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Medatalumpa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakkhara</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uḷumpa</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). Within the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> tribe there were probably several clans, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gottā</span>. The Buddha himself belonged to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Gotamagotta</span>. It has been suggested (e.g., Thomas: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Life and Legend of the Buddha</span>, 22) that this was a Brahmin clan, claiming descent from the ancient <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Isi</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>. The evidence for this suggestion is, however, very meagre. Nowhere do we find the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> calling themselves Brahmins. On the other hand, we find various clans claiming a share of the Buddha’s relics on the ground that they, like the Buddha, were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Khattiyas</span> (DN.ii.165). It is stated that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> were a haughty people. Vin.ii.183; DN.i.90; Ja.i.88; Dhp­a.iii.163. Xuanzang, however, found them obliging and gentle in manners (Beal-Xuan, op.cit., ii.14).

When the Buddha first visited them, after his Awakening, they refused to honour him on account of his youth. The Buddha then performed a miracle and preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantarajātaka</span>, and their pride was subdued. They were evidently fond of sports and mention is made of a special school of archery conducted by a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> family, called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vedhaññā</span> (DN.iii.117; DN­a.iii.905). When the prince <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siddhattha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> (later the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>) wished to marry, no <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> would give him his daughter until he had showed his proficiency in sport (Ja.i.58).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> evidently had no king. Theirs was a republican form of government, probably with a leader, elected from time to time. The administration and judicial affairs of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gotta</span> were discussed in their <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Santhāgāra</span>, or Mote Hall, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>. See, e.g., DN.i.91; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> had a similar Mote Hall at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cātumā</span> (MN.i.457). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> also had a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Santhāgāra</span> (DN.ii.164); so did the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> (Vin.i.233; MN.i.228).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭha</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) once visited it on business; so did the envoys of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, when he wished to marry a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> maiden (see below). A new Mote Hall was built at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> while the Buddha was staying at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span>, and he was asked to inaugurate it. This he did by a series of ethical discourses lasting through the night, delivered by himself, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>. MN.i.353f.; SN.iv.182f; the hall is described at SN­a.iii.63; cf. Ud­a.409.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> were very jealous of the purity of their race; they belonged to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ādicca</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gotta</span>, (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ādiccā nāma gottena, Sākiyā nāma jātiyā</span>, Snp. vs.423) and claimed descent from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Okkāka</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). Their ancestors were the nine children of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Okkāka</span>, whom he banished in order to give the kingdom to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jantu</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kumāra</span>, his son by another queen. These nine children went towards <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span>, and, having founded <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span> for details), lived there. To the eldest sister they gave the rank of mother, and the others married among themselves. The eldest sister, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piyā</span>, later married <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāma</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, and their descendants became known as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyans</span> (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyā</span> for details). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Okkāka</span> heard of this, he praised their action, saying, “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sakyā vata bho kumārā, paramasakyā vata bho rājakumāra</span>; the young men are truly able, the young men are truly extremely able, hence their name came to be “Sakyā.” Snp­a.i.352f.; cf. DN­a.i.258. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Okkāka</span> had a slave girl, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Disā</span>, her offspring were the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇhāyanas</span>, to which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gotta</span> belonged <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭha</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). The Mhv.ii.12ff gives the history of the direct descent of the Buddha from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Okkāka</span>, and this contains a list of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> chiefs of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>:
