# Suddhāvāsā

## English source

Suddhāvāsā. The “Pure Abodes”; a name given to a group of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>-worlds – the five highest <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Rūpa</span> worlds – consisting of: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avihā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atappā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudassā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudassī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Akaniṭṭhā</span> (e.g., DN.iii.237). There <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmīs</span> are born, and there they attain <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship; such <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmīs</span> are divided into twenty-four classes (See, e.g., Khp­a.182f.; of. Paṭis-a.319; Vsm.710). Bodhisattas are never born there (Snp­a.i.50; Bv­a.224).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhāvāsā</span> are described as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Buddhānaṁ khandhāvāraṭṭhānasadisā</span>, like the Buddhas’ reliquary sites. Sometimes, for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">asaṅkheyya</span>s of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span>, when no Buddhas are born, these worlds remain empty (AN­a.ii.808; cf. MN­a.i.30).

The Buddha is mentioned as having visited the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhāvāsā</span> (e.g., DN.ii.50). When a Buddha is about to be born, the inhabitants of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhāvāsā</span> insert a knowledge of the signs of a Great Being in the Vedas and teach this among men in the guise of Brahmins, calling such knowledge <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">buddhamanta</span>. Men learn it and are thus able to recognize a Great Being (MN­a.ii.761; Snp­a.ii.448). The inhabitants of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhāvāsā</span> know how many Buddhas will be born in any particular <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappa</span> by observing the number of lotuses which spring up on the site of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Bodhipallaṅka</span> when the earth gradually emerges after the destruction of the world (DN­a.ii.411). It is the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhāvāsā</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahmas</span> who provide the four omens which lead to a Bodhisatta’s renunciation in his last lay life. See, e.g., DN­a.ii.455f.
