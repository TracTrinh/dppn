# Sānusutta

## English source

Sānusutta. Contains the conversation between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sānu</span>’s mother and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Yakkhinī</span>, who possessed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sānu</span> (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sānu</span>) in order to prevent him from “losing his soul.” <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sānu</span>’s mother says she cannot understand how <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> can possess holy men who keep the fasts and lead holy lives. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Yakkhinī</span> says she is right; but holiness consists in refraining from evil, both open and secret. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sānu</span>’s mother understands, and, when her son regains consciousness and asks her why she weeps as he is not dead, she replies that he is as good as dead in that he wishes to return to the household life, like goods, which, having been rescued from the fire, wish to be thrown into it once more. SN.i.208f.
