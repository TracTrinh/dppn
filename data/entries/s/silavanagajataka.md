# Sīlavanāgajātaka

## English source

Sīlavanāgajātaka (Ja 72). The Bodhisatta was once an elephant in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span>, head of a herd of eighty thousand. His name was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīlava</span>. One day he saw a forester of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> who had lost his way, and, feeling compassion for him, took him to his own dwelling, fed him with all kinds of fruit, and then, taking him to the edge of the forest, set him on his way to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. The wretched man noted all the landmarks, and, on reaching the city, entered into an agreement with ivory workers to supply them with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīlava</span>’s tusks. He then returned to the forest and begged <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīlava</span> for a part of his tusks, pleading poverty and lack of livelihood. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīlava</span> allowed the ends of his tusks to be sawn off. The man returned again and again, until, at last, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīlava</span> allowed him to dig out the stumps as well. As the man was on his way back to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, the earth opened and swallowed him up into the fires of hell. A tree sprite, who had witnessed all this, spoke a stanza illustrating the evils of ingratitude.

The story was related in reference to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>’s wickedness; he is identified with the forester and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> with the tree sprite (Ja.i.319-22; the story is referred to in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span>, p.202).

The birth as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīlava</span> is mentioned among those in which the Bodhisatta practised <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sīlapāramitā</span> to perfection, e.g., MN­a.ii.617.
