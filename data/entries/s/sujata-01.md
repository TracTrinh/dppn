# Sujāta 01

## English source

Sujāta 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujāta Buddha</span>. The twelfth of the twenty-four Buddhas. He was born in the city of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumaṅgala</span>, his father being the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Khattiya</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uggata</span> and his mother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pabhāvatī</span>. He was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujāta</span> because his birth brought happiness to all beings. He lived as a householder for nine thousand years in three palaces – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siri</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upasiri</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span> his wife being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirinandā</span> and his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upasena</span>. He left home on a horse, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsavaha</span>, practised austerities for nine months, and attained Awakening under a bamboo (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">mahā veḷu</span>) tree, after a meal of milk-rice given by the daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirinandana</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirinandana</span>; grass for his seat was given by an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ājīvaka</span> named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunanda</span>. His first sermon was to his younger brother, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudassana</span>, and the chaplain’s son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span>, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumaṅgala</span> Park. He performed the Twin Miracle at the gate of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudassana</span> Park. The Bodhisatta was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>, and entered the Saṅgha under the Buddha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujāta</span>’s chief disciples were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudassana</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudeva</span>) among monks and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgasamālā</span> among nuns. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span> was his attendant. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudatta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta</span> were his chief lay patrons among men and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhaddā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumā</span> among women. His body was fifty cubits high; he lived for ninety thousand years, and died at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Silārāma</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candavatī</span> city, where a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thūpa</span>, three <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gāvutas</span> in height, was erected in his honour. Bv.xiii.1ff.; Bv­a.168 ff.; Ja.i.38; Mhv.i.8, etc.
