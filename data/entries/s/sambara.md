# Sambara

## English source

Sambara. A chief of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span>. In the “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isayo Samuddakāsutta</span>” (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) we are told that, because <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambara</span> refused the request of the sages for a guarantee of safety, they cursed him, and his mind was deranged (SN.i.227). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> adds that, on account of this mental derangement, he came to be called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">s.v.</span>) (SN­a.i.266). Elsewhere (SN.i.239), however, it is said that once <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> to teach him <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambara</span>’s magic art (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sambarimāyā</span>). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vepacitti</span> consulted the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span> and then warned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> against learning it because, through his art, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambara</span> had fallen into purgatory, where he had been suffering for a century. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>, in this context (SN­a.i.272), calls <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambara</span> an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Asurinda</span>, a juggler (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">māyāvī</span>) who, having practised his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">māyā</span>, has roasted for the past century in purgatory.

Mrs. Rhys Davis (KS.i.306 n) thinks there was a rank of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambara</span> resembling that of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, and that each succeeding <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambara</span> learnt the magic art. See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁvara</span>.
