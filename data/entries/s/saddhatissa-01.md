# Saddhātissa 01

## English source

Saddhātissa 01. King of Ceylon (77-59 B.C.). He was the brother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> and was about a year younger. When he was ten, at the ceremony of initiation, he was forced to make a vow that he would never fight against his brother; but when his father, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kākavaṇṇatissa</span>, died, he seized the throne in the absence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span>. Up to that time he had been in charge of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghavāpi</span> district. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> now made war upon him, but was defeated at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷaṅganiyapiṭṭhi</span>. Later the tide turned, and Tissa had to flee to a monastery. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> surrounded the monastery, but some young monks carried Tissa out on a bed, covered up like a dead body. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gāmaṇī</span> discovered the ruse, but refrained from action. Through the intervention of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godhagattatissa Thera</span><span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">,</span> the brothers were reconciled, and, thereafter, seem to have been devoted to one another.

After <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gāmaṇī</span>’s conquest of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>, Tissa seems to have returned as governor of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghavāpi</span>. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gāmaṇī</span> lay dying, Tissa was sent for to complete the work of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span>, that the king might see it before his death. This he got done by means of temporary structures, cunningly devised. He was enjoined to retain unimpaired all the services on behalf of the religion inaugurated by his brother, and, when the latter died, he was succeeded by Tissa, who ruled for eighteen years. He rebuilt the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span> after it was burnt down, and erected many <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāras</span> – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇāgiri</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kallakālena</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalambaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pettaṅgavālika</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Velaṅgaviṭṭhika</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dubbalavāpitissaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dūratissaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātuvihāraka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghavāpi</span>. He built a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> to every <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojana</span> on the road from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghavāpi</span>. He had two sons, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lañjatissa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thūlathana</span>.

Tissa was reborn after death in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span>, and will be the second Chief Disciple of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Metteyya Buddha</span>. Mhv.xxii.73, 83; xxiv2ff.; xxxii.83; xxxiii.4-17; Dpv.xx 2, 4ff.

He was a very pious king, entirely devoted to the cause of religion. Various stories are mentioned about him in the Commentaries (see, e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷa Buddharakkhita</span>). He once walked five leagues to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṅgana</span> to pay his respects to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kujjatissa</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) (AN­a.i.384f). On another occasion, he gave snipe to a novice from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kanthakasāla</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pariveṇa</span> who would, however, accept only very little. Pleased with his moderation, the king paid him great honour (AN­a.i.264). He seems to have been specialty fond of the monks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyagiri</span> (see Vibh­a.473). He was, apparently, also known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammikatissa</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammikatissa</span> once distributed one hundred cartloads of sugar (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t18">guḷa</span>) among twelve thousand monks. A seven year old novice was sent by a monk who had just come to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyagiri</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> to fetch for him some sugar, about the size of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kapiṭṭha</span> fruit. The attendant offered to give him a plateful, but the novice refused to take so much. The king heard the conversation, and, pleased with the novice, sent a further four hundred cartloads of sugar to be given to the Saṅgha (e.g., SN­a.iii.48). See also the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkuṭagiri</span>.
