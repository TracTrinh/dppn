# Suddhodana

## English source

Suddhodana. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Rājā</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> and father of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>. He was the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīhahanu</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccānā</span>. His brothers were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhotodana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakkodana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sukkodana</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amitodana</span>, and his sisters were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amitā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pamitā</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māyā</span> was his chief consort, and, after her death, her sister <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span> was raised to her position (Mhv.ii.15f.; Dpv.iii.45; Ja.i.15, etc.).

When soothsayers predicted that his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> had two destinies awaiting him, either that of universal sovereignty or of Buddhahood, he exerted his utmost power to provide the prince with all kinds of luxuries in order to hold him fast to household life. It is said (e.g., Ja.i.54) that when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asita</span>, who was his father’s chaplain and his own teacher, visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> to see the newly-born prince, and paid homage to the infant by allowing his feet to rest on his head, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> was filled with wonder and himself worshipped the child. And when, at the ploughing ceremony, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> saw how the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jambu</span>-tree under which the child had been placed kept its shadow immoveable in order to protect him, and that the child was seated cross-legged in the air, he again worshipped him (Ja.i.57f).

Later, when, in spite of all his father’s efforts, the prince had left household life and was practising austerities, news was brought to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> that his son had died owing to the severity of his penances. But he refused to believe it, saying that his son would never die without achieving his goal (Ja.i.67). When this was afterwards related to the Buddha, he preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Dhammapālajātaka</span> (Ja 447) and showed that in the past, too, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> had refused to believe that his son could have died even when he was shown the heap of his bones.

When news reached <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> that his son had reached Awakening, he sent a messenger to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> with ten thousand others to invite the Buddha to visit <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>. But the messenger and his companions heard the Buddha preach, entered the Saṅgha, and forgot their mission. Nine times this happened. On the tenth occasion, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷudāyī</span> with permission for him to enter the Saṅgha on the express condition that he gave the king’s invitation to the Buddha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷudāyī</span> kept his promise and the Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, staying in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span>. There, in reference to a shower of rain that fell, he preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantarajātaka</span> (Ja 547). The next day, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> remonstrated with the Buddha because he was seen begging in the streets of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, the Buddha told him that begging was the custom of all Buddhas, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> hearing this became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>. He invited the Buddha to his palace, where he entertained him, and at the end of the meal the Buddha preached to the king, who became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sakadāgāmī</span> (Ja.i.90; cf. Dhp­a.iii.164f). He became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmī</span> after hearing the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Dhammapālajātaka</span> (Ja 447) (Dhp­a.i.99; Ja.iv.55), and when he was about to die, the Buddha came from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> to see him and preach to him, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> and died as a lay <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> (Thīg­a.141).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span>’s son by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Pajāpatī</span>, and he had also a daughter called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sundarīnandā</span>. When the Buddha ordained both <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> was greatly distressed lest other parents should be similarly afflicted, and persuaded the Buddha to establish a rule that none should be ordained without the permission of his parents (Vin.i.82f).
