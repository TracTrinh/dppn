# Sujātā 06

## English source

Sujātā 06. Daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Senānī</span>, a landowner of the village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Senānī</span> near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span>. She made a promise to the god of the banyan tree near by that she would offer a meal of milk-rice to the god if she gave birth to a son. Her wish was fulfilled, the son was born, and every year she would make her offerings. One year she sent her maid, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇā</span>, to prepare the place for the offering. This was on the very day of the Buddha’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇā</span>, finding <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> sitting under the banyan, thought that he was the tree god present in person to receive the offering. She brought the news to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujātā</span>, who, in great joy, brought the food in a golden bowl and offered it to him.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span> took the bowl to the river bank, bathed at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppatiṭṭhita</span> ford and ate the food. This was his only meal for forty-nine days. Ja.i.68f.; Dhp­a.i.71, etc. In Lalitavistara 334-7 (267f.) nine girls are mentioned as giving food to the Buddha during his austerities. Cf. Divy.392, where two are given, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandabalā</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujātā</span>’s meal was considered one of the most important of those offered to the Buddha, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, therefore, added to it divine flavours.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span> (*q.v.*) was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujātā</span>’s son, and when he attained *Arahant*-ship his father, who had come in search of him, became the Buddha’s follower and invited him to a meal. The Buddha accepted the invitation and went with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span> to the house. The Buddha preached at the end of the meal, and both <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujātā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa</span>’s wife became *Sotāpannas*. On that day <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujātā</span> took the threefold formula of Refuge. She thus became foremost among lay women who had taken the threefold formula (*aggaṁ upāsikānaṁ paṭhamaṁ saraṇaṁ gacchantīnaṁ*) (Snp­a.i.154; DN.ii.135). She had made an earnest resolve to attain this eminence in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>. AN.i.26; AN­a.i.217f.
