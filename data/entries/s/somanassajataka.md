# Somanassajātaka

## English source

Somanassajātaka (Ja 505). Once, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Reṇu</span> was king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarapañcāla</span>, an ascetic, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Rakkhita</span>, visited him with five hundred others from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span>. The king entertained them and told them of his worry because he had no sons. Some time later, when the ascetics were returning, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Rakkhita</span> saw that the king would have a son and told his companions so. One of the ascetics, a cheat, hoping to get gain thereby, feigned illness, and, returning to the palace, told the king that a son would be born to his queen, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhammā</span>. The king showed him great honour, and he came to be called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dibbacakkhuka</span>. In due course, the Bodhisatta was born as the king’s son, and was named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Somanassa</span>. When the boy was seven years old the king had to leave home to quell a border rising, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Somanassa</span> was left in the charge of the cheating ascetic. The boy soon discovered his real nature and paid him no honour. As soon as the king returned, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dibbacakkhu</span> complained to him that the prince had ill-treated him. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Somanassa</span> was ordered to be executed, but he exposed the cheat’s knavery, and men, sent to search his hut, found bundles of money in it. Disgusted with life at court, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Somanassa</span> obtained the king’s leave and became an ascetic in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span>, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span>, commanded by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, built a hermitage for him. The cheat was stoned to death by the people.

The story was related in reference to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>’s attempt to kill the Buddha. He is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dibbacakkhu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Rakkhita</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Somanassa</span>’s mother with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Māyā</span>. Ja.iv.445ff.
