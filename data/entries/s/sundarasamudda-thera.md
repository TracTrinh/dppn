# Sundarasamudda Thera

## English source

Sundarasamudda Thera. He was the son of a wealthy <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> and was called Sundara on account of his beauty. While yet young, he realized the majesty of the Buddha, when he visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, and Samudda entered the Saṅgha. He once went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, where he stayed with a friend, learning how to practise insight. On a festival day his mother thought of him and wept, seeing the sons of other families enjoying themselves with their wives. A courtesan offered to entice him back, and the mother promised that should she succeed she would make her Samudda’s wife and give her many gifts. The courtesan went, well attended, to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> and took lodgings in a house to which Samudda frequently came for alms. She saw that he was well seen to and showed herself to him, decked and adorned and wearing golden slippers. One day, slipping off her sandals at the door, she saluted him with clasped hands as he passed, and invited him in with seductive manner. Then the Thera<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">,</span> realizing that the heart of a worldling is unsteady, made then and there a supreme effort and attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship (Thag.vss.459-65; Thag­a.i.467f).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span>, Samudda accepted the invitation of the courtesan and went with her to the top floor of her seven-storied house (Dhp­a.iv.194ff.; cf. the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Piṇḍapātikatissa</span>). There she provided him with a seat and practised her wiles. Samudda suddenly realized what he was doing and was much distressed. The Buddha, seated in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, forty-five leagues away, saw what was happening and smiled. On being asked by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> why he smiled, he said: “A battle royal is being waged between Sundarasamudda and a courtesan, but Samudda will win.” So saying, he sent forth a ray of light to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samudda</span> and preached to him. At the end of the sermon Samudda became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>.
