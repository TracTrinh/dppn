# Saṅkhajātaka 01

## English source

Saṅkhajātaka 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkhabrāhmaṇajātaka</span> (Ja 442). The Bodhisatta was once born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Molinī</span> *nagara* (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>) as a very rich Brahmin, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkha</span>. He spent six thousand daily on almsgiving. He had a ship built, equipped and prepared to sail for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suvaṇṇabhūmi</span>. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>, seeing him with his divine eye, and foreseeing the danger in store for him, appeared before him on the way to the seaport. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkha</span> paid him all honour and presented him with his shoes and umbrella.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkha</span>’s ship sprang a leak on the seventh day. Taking with him one companion, he dived overboard and swam in the direction of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Molinī</span>. He swam thus for seven days, till <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇimekhalā</span>, seeing his plight, came to his rescue and offered him food. But this he refused, as he was keeping the fast. The goddess told him that his purity in worshipping the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> had been the cause of her coming to his aid and offered him a boon. He chose to be sent back to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Molinī</span>. The goddess provided him with a ship full of treasure, and he returned safely to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Molinī</span> with his attendant.

The story was related by way of thanks to a pious layman of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, who, having entertained the Buddha and his monks for seven days, presented shoes to the Buddha and to the members of his Saṅgha.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkha</span>’s attendant and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uppalavaṇṇā</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇimekhalā</span> (Ja.iv.15-22). The story is also called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkhabrāhmaṇajātaka</span>, e.g., Ja.iv.120.
