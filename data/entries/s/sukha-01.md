# Sukha 01

## English source

Sukha 01. A monk, generally known as Sukha *Sāmaṇera*. In his past life he had been <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhattabhatika</span> (*q.v.*). In his last life he was born in the house of a supporter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>. During her pregnancy, his mother gave alms to five hundred monks, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> at their head. When he was seven years old, he entered the Saṅgha under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, on which occasion his parents held a special almsgiving lasting for seven days.

Once, while going with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> for alms, he noticed several things, and like the novice <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍita</span> (*q.v.*) asked the Elder numerous questions. Then he expressed a wish to return to the monastery. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> agreed, and Sukha turned back saying, “Sir, when you bring my food, pray bring me food of one hundred flavours. If you cannot obtain it through your own merit, you can obtain it through mine.” So saying, he returned to his cell and meditated on the nature of the body. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s throne was heated, and he sent the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Four Regent Gods</span> to keep away all noise from Sukha’s cell. He also bade the Sun and Moon stand still. Sukha, helped by this silence, became an *Anāgāmī*.

Meanwhile, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> had gone to a house where he knew he could get the food desired by Sukha, and, having eaten there, returned with Sukha’s portion to the monastery. The Buddha, thinking that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>’s arrival might impede Sukha’s attainment of *Arahant*-ship, appeared near the gate of Sukha’s cell and stood guard. As he stood there, the Buddha asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> four questions. When the last question was answered, Sukha became an *Arahant*. Thereupon <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> opened the door and gave Sukha his food. Sukha ate it and washed the bowl. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Four Regent Gods</span> left their post, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> let go the rope of the door of the novice’s cell, and the Sun and Moon started once more on their course. Evening at once came on, and the Buddha, on being asked the reason, explained that it was a usual occurrence when they who possess merit engage in meditation. Dhp­a.iii.95ff.; op. the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍita</span>.
