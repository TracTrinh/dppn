# Suvaṇṇabhūmi

## English source

Suvaṇṇabhūmi. A country. At the end of the Third Council, the Theras <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span> visited this country in order to convert it to Buddhism. At that time a female deity of the sea was in the habit of eating every heir born to the king. The arrival of the Theras coincided with the birth of a prince. At first the people thought that the monks were the friends of the demon, but later the monks, being told the story, drove away the demon by their <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span> power and erected a bulwark round the country by reciting the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmajālasutta</span>. Sixty thousand people embraced the new faith, while three thousand five hundred young men and fifteen hundred girls of noble family entered the Saṅgha. Thenceforth all princes born into the royal family were called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇuttara</span> (Mhv.xii.6, 44f.; Dpv.viii.12; Vin-a.i.64).

There seems to have been regular trade between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bharukaccha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suvaṇṇabhūmi</span> (See, e.g., Ja.iii.188), and also between the latter and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> (Moliṇi), (Ja.iv.15), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span> (Ja.vi.34), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> (Pv-a.47), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span> (Pv-a.271). The distance between Ceylon and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suvaṇṇabhūmi</span> was seven hundred leagues, and, with a favourable wind, could be covered in seven days and nights (AN­a.i.265).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suvaṇṇabhūmi</span> is generally identified with Lower Burma, probably the Pagan and Moulmein districts. It probably included the coast from Rangoon to Singapore. The chief place in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suvaṇṇabhūmi</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhamma</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nagara</span> – i.e., Thaton – at the mouth of the Sittaung River (See Sās. Introd., p.4, and n.3). Fleet suggests (JRAS.1910, p.428), however, that it might be the district in Bengal called by Xuanzang “Ka-lo-na-su-fa-la-na” (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Karṇasuvarṇa</span>), or else the country along the river Son in Central India, a tributary of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span>, on the right bank of the river which is also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Hiraṇyavāha</span>. The probability is that there were two places of the same name, one originally in India itself and the other in Further India. cf. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunāparanta</span>.
