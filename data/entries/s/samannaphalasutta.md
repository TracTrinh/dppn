# Sāmaññaphalasutta

## English source

Sāmaññaphalasutta. The second <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghanikāya</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, accompanied by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span>, visits the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span>’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambavana</span> and questions him on the fruits of recluseship, wherefore men join the Buddha’s Saṅgha. The Buddha answers and includes in his answer his justification for the foundation of the Saṅgha, for the enunciation of the Vinaya, and the practical rules by which life in the Saṅgha is regulated.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> also contains a list of ordinary occupations followed by people in the Buddha’s day, which forms interesting reading.

In the introductory story, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> explains that he has already put the question to the founders of six other Orders: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūraṇa Kassapa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makkhali Gosāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajita Kesakambala</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pakudha Kaccāyana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sañjaya Belatthiputta</span>. But these teachers, instead of answering his questions, gave a general statement of their theories.

The summaries given here of their teachings are of great interest, because they form some sort of evidence, at least, as to the speculations favoured by them. By means of a counter-question, the Buddha finds from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> that even if a servant of his joined the Saṅgha, he would receive the king’s honour and respect. Then the Buddha proceeds to show, step by step, the fruits higher and nobler, which await the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">samaṇa</span>, immediate in their effect, culminating in the six-fold <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">abhiññā</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. The king is greatly impressed, takes refuge in the Buddha, and expresses his remorse for having killed his father. The Buddha utters no word of blame, but after the departure of the king, he informs the monks that if <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> had not been guilty of patricide he would have realized the first fruit of the Path (DN.i.47-86).

The Commentary adds (DN­a.i.238) that as a result of hearing this discourse, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> would, in the future, become a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Viditavisesa</span>. From this moment, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> was one of the Buddha’s most devoted followers. It is said that, after his father’s death, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> could never sleep at night until he had heard the Buddha, after which he enjoyed peaceful sleep.
