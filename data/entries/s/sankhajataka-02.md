# Saṅkhajātaka 02

## English source

Saṅkhajātaka 02. (Dhp-a 21.1). The Bodhisatta, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkha</span>, was once born as a Brahmin in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> and had a son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Susīma</span>. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Susīma</span> was about sixteen, he took leave of his father and went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> to study the Vedas. His teacher, who was a friend of his father’s, taught him all he knew, and then <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Susīma</span> went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span>, where lived some <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>. He entered the Saṅgha under them, attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship, and died while yet young. Having heard no news of his son for some time, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkha</span> was alarmed and went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> in search of him. There, after enquiry, he heard of his son’s death as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>, and was shown the shrine erected in his memory. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkha</span> weeded the grass round the shrine, sprinkled sand, watered it, scattered wild flowers round it, and raised aloft his robe as banner over it. He then planted his parasol over the top and departed.

The Buddha related the story to the monks at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, after his return from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, to explain the unparalleled honours he had received during the journey. Because he had uprooted the grass round <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Susīma</span>’s shrine, a road of eight leagues was prepared for him to journey comfortably; because he had spread sand, his route was also so spread; because he had scattered flowers, his route was covered with flowers; because he had sprinkled water, there was a shower in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> on his arrival; because he had raised a banner and set up a parasol, the whole <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cakkavāla</span> was gay with flags and parasols. Dhp­a.iii.445f.; Khp-a.198f. The story is not given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātakaṭṭhakatha</span>.
