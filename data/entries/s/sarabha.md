# Sarabha

## English source

Sarabha. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Paribbājaka</span> who joined the Saṅgha and soon after left it. He then went about proclaiming in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> that he knew the Dhamma and Vinaya of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyaputta</span> monks, and that was why he had left their Saṅgha. The Buddha, being told of this, visited the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Paribbājakārāma</span>, on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sappinikā</span>, and challenged <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabha</span> to repeat his statement. Three times the challenge was uttered, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabha</span> sat silent. The Buddha then declared to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paribbājakas</span> that no one could say that his claim to Awakening was unjustified, or that his Dhamma, if practised, did not lead to the destruction of suffering. After the Buddha’s departure, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paribbājakas</span> taunted and abused <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabha</span> (AN.i.185ff).

It is said (AN­a.i.412 f) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabha</span> joined the Saṅgha at the request of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paribbājakas</span>. They had failed to find any fault with the Buddha’s life, and thought that his power was due to an “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">āvattanīmāyā</span>,” conversion magic, which he and his disciples practised once a fortnight behind closed doors. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabha</span> agreed to find it out and learn it. He therefore went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span>, where he showed great humility to all the resident monks. An Elder, taking pity on him, ordained him. In due course he learned the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pātimokkha</span>, which, he realized, was what the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paribbājakas</span> took to be the Buddha’s “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">māyā</span>.” Having learned it, he went back to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paribbājakas</span>, taught it to them, and with them went about in the city boasting that he knew the Buddha’s teaching and had found it worthless.
