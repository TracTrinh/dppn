# Soṇa 04

## English source

Soṇa 04. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa Kuṭikaṇṇa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa Koṭikaṇṇa</span> A Thera<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">,</span> declared chief of those possessing clear utterance (AN.i.24). He was the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷī Kuraragharikā</span>, and was conceived before the Buddha appeared in the world (according to Thag­a.i.429, his father was a rich <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span>; no mention is made there of his mother).

A little while before the birth of the child <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷī</span> went to her parents’ house in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, and one day, as she was cooling herself, she heard a conversation between two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sātāgira</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemavata</span>. As she listened to their talk, her mind was filled with thoughts of the virtues of the Buddha, and she became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>. That same night the child was born and was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa</span>. His mother later returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuraraghara</span>. At that time <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span> lived near by and often visited her home. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa</span> was very attached to him, and was later ordained by him. Three years later he received the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">upasampadā</span>, and, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span>’s leave, visited the Buddha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷī</span> gave him a large carpet to spread in the Buddha’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhakuṭi</span>.

When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa</span> arrived at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhakuṭi</span>, he worshipped the Buddha, who asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> to find him a lodging. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, reading the Buddha’s thoughts, spread a rug in the Buddha’s chamber. Late at night <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa</span> went to bed, and, very early the next morning, the Buddha woke him and asked him to recite the Dhamma. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa</span> recited the whole of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṭṭhakavagga</span>, which he had learnt from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span>. At the end of the recital the Buddha applauded him and gave him a boon. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa</span> asked for the “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Vinayadharapañcamagaṇena upasampadā</span>, which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāna</span> had asked him to choose (this means permission to admit a monk into the Saṅgha with a chapter of only five monks, one of whom was versed in the Vinaya. For details of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa</span>’s visit to the Buddha, see Vin.i.194ff.; cf. Ud.v.6). Later he returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuraraghara</span> and visited his mother’s house. She had heard of the Buddha’s applause from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, and wished <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa</span> to recite the Dhamma just as he had done before the Buddha, and this he did.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa</span> had resolved to win this eminence. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span> he was a member of the Saṅgha and sewed a robe for a monk. Later he was a tailor of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> and mended a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>’s robe (Thag.vss.365-9; AN­a.i.133f.; Thag­a.i.429).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> says (Dhp­a.iv.103f) that, on the day when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa</span> recited the Dhamma in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuraraghara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷī</span> went to listen to him, leaving only one female slave in the house. Her house had seven walls and fortified gates and savage dogs on leash. Molten lead flowed round the walls at night, and in the night it proved a slippery surface, difficult to walk on. Nine hundred thieves had been awaiting a chance of breaking into the house, and this day they saw their opportunity. They stationed one of their number to watch <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷī</span> going to the monastery, and to kill her if she started homewards after the thieves entered her house. When they came her female servant ran to the monastery to tell her about it. But she would not be disturbed and sent her back. Again the servant went, and again she was sent back. When the thief, stationed near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷī</span>, saw her extraordinary piety, he was filled with remorse, and, at the end of the sermon, begged her forgiveness. All the nine hundred thieves joined the Saṅgha under Soṇa Kuṭikaṇṇa, and on the day they became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span> the Buddha appeared before them in a ray of light to encourage them.

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> Commentary (Ud­a.307), Soṇa was called Kuṭikaṇṇa because he wore ear ornaments worth one crore (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t18">koṭi</span>). It is said that he once went with a caravan to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>, and when the caravan stopped for the night he slept away from the rest of its members. The caravan started very early and nobody waked Soṇa. When he finally awoke, he ran along the road till he came to a large tree. There he saw an ugly man tearing off his own flesh and eating it. On enquiry, Soṇa learnt that he had been a wicked merchant of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhārukaccha</span>, who had been born as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Peta</span> because he had deceived his patrons. This revelation filled Soṇa with great misgivings, which were increased by the sight of two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Peta</span> boys with blood pouring out of their lips. They had been youths, also of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhārukaccha</span>, who had found fault with their mother for feeding an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> monk. When Soṇa returned from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span> he consulted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span> about these things, and resolved to enter the Saṅgha.

The Vinaya says (Vin.i.195f) that when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāna</span> wished to confer the higher ordination on Soṇa, it was three years before he could get together the necessary chapter of ten monks. This was because there were but few monks in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> and in the Southern Country; hence Soṇa’s request to the Buddha that he should allow five monks to officiate in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>.
