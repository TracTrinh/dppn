# Subha 02

## English source

Subha 02. A young man (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">māṇava</span>) called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Todeyyaputta</span>. He once visited the Buddha in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, asking him various questions. The interview is described in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhasutta</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). At the end of the discourse he declared himself the Buddha’s follower. While on his way back from the city, he met <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jāṇussoṇī</span>, and, on being asked what he thought of the Buddha, spoke of him in terms of the highest praise, saying that none but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>’s own peer could utter sufficient praise of him (MN.i.196f., 208f.; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jāṇussoṇī</span> addresses him as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhāradvāja</span>). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subha</span> is described (MN­a.ii.802; cf. MN.i.202) as the son of the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Todeyya</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tudigāma</span>. Elsewhere however, a different account is given of his conversion (DN­a.ii.384f.; cf. MN­a.ii.963f., which adds that the Buddha proved the identity of the dog by getting it to indicate the place where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Todeyya</span>’s treasure lay buried).

Subha’s father was a very rich merchant, chaplain to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, but a great miser. After death he was born as a dog in the same home. One day, when the Buddha was going his alms round in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tudigāma</span> near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, he arrived at Subha’s house. The dog saw the Buddha and barked, and the Buddha addressed it as “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Todeyya</span>.” The dog thereupon ran into the house and lay on a bed, from which no one could drive it away. When Subha asked the cause of the uproar, he was told the story. Thereupon he was very angry, saying that his father had been born in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahmaloka</span>, and, in order to refute the Buddha, he visited the monastery. This was the occasion for the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhasutta</span>. Soon after the Buddha’s death, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, was staying in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subha</span> sent a young man to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, with his respects and an invitation to his house. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, having taken medicine, did not go that day. But he went the next day, accompanied by a monk of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiya</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetaka</span>). Their conversation is recorded in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhasutta</span> (2) (DN.i.204f). See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Kammavibhaṅgasutta</span>, which too was preached to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subha</span>.
