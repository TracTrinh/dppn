# Saṅgārava

## English source

Saṅgārava. A very learned Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candalakappa</span>. One day he saw <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhānañjānī</span> trip up, and heard her exclaim three times, “Glory to the Buddha, the *Arahant*, the all-enlightened.” He blamed her for thus extolling a shavelling monk, but when she told him of the Buddha’s marvellous qualities, he felt a desire to see him. Some time after, the Buddha went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candalakappa</span> and stayed in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Todeyya</span>’s Mango grove. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhānañjānī</span> told Saṅgārava that he was there, Saṅgārava visited him and questioned him on his views on Brahmins. The Buddha said he had great regard for Brahmins who had here and now won the goal, having discovered unaided a doctrine before unknown. He himself was one of these. He then proceeds to describe how he came to leave the household life and how, in due course, he won <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>.

Saṅgārava asks further whether there are any gods. The Buddha answers that of that there is no doubt; the whole world is in agreement on that point. Saṅgārava is pleased and accepts the Buddha as his teacher (MN.ii.209ff). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (MN­a.ii.808) that Saṅgārava was the youngest of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhāradvājas</span> (*q.v.*), brothers of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhānañjānī</span>’s husband.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta</span> (SN.i.182f) mentions a Saṅgārava who is perhaps distinct from the above. He lived in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> and was a “bath ritualist,” believing in purification by water, bathing morning and evening. The Buddha, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>’s request, visited his house and preached to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span>, after which he became the Buddha’s follower. The Commentary (SN­a.i.207) explains that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> and Saṅgārava had, as laymen, been friends, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> was anxious to prevent “this wretch (*varāko*) who, for all our friendship has contracted wrong views, from becoming a hell-filler; moreover he has a circle of friends, and hundreds may follow if he is converted.”

Another <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta</span> passage (SN.v.121f.; see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅgāravasutta</span> 3) contains a *Sutta* in which the Brahmin Saṅgārava visits the Buddha and asks him why he can remember certain mantras with great ease and others not at all. It may be this same Brahmin who is mentioned several times also in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttara</span>. AN.i.168 f; iii.230f.; v. 232, 252. For details see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅgāravasutta</span> (4-7).

The Commentary states (AN­a.i.396) that he was an overseer in charge of the repair of the dilapidated buildings in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> (*Rājagahanagare jiṇṇapaṭisaṅkharaṇakārako āyuttakabrāhmaṇo*).
