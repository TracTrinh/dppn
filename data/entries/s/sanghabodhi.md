# Saṅghabodhi

## English source

Saṅghabodhi. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lambakaṇṇa</span>, king of Ceylon (307-9 CE.), generally called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirisaṅghabodhi</span>. He succeeded <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghatissa</span> and set up a *salākā*-house in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>. He was a very good king, and made rain fall by virtue of his goodness. He quelled the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rattakkhi</span>, who devastated his territory. When his treasurer, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Goṭhakābhaya</span>, rose in revolt, he abdicated in his favour and became an ascetic. Later, he gave his head in gratitude to a poor man who gave him a meal, so that the man could win from the king the price which had been set on his head. (Mhv.xxxvi.73ff.; Dpv.xxii.53f).

The legend of the king surrendering his head is famous in Ceylon, and forms the theme of a Pāli Chronicle, the *Hatthavanagallavihāravaṁsa*.
