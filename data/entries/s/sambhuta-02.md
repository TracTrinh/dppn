# Sambhūta 02

## English source

Sambhūta 02. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambhūta Thera</span>. A Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> who, with his friends, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhūmija</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jeyyasena</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhirādhana</span>, entered the Saṅgha. Because he stayed continuously in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sītavana</span>, meditating on the nature of the body, he came to be called “*Sītavaniya*.” In due course he won *Arahant*-ship, and the verses, declaring his *aññā*, are included in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> (Thag.vs.6).

It is said (Thag­a.i.46) that when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambhūta</span> was meditating, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> passing that way, saw him and worshipped him, and left two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> to keep guard and to tell <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambhūta</span> of his visit. When the Thera had finished his meditations, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> gave him <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span>’s message offering him protection. But he refused their protection saying that the mindfulness taught by the Buddha was sufficient guard. On his return journey, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> again visited him, and, realizing from the appearance of the Elder that he had achieved his goal, went to the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> and announced to him <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambhūta</span>’s attainment.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambhūta</span> had been a householder in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atthadassī Buddha</span>, and conveyed the Buddha and a large company across a river.

He is probably identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Taraṇiya Thera</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>. Ap.i.204f.; see also Vibh­a.306 and SN­a.iii.201, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambhūta</span> is given as an example of one who developed *lokuttaradhamma* by developing the heart (*cittaṁ dhuraṁ katvā*).
