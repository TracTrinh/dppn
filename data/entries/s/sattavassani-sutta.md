# Sattavassāni Sutta

## English source

Sattavassāni Sutta. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> approaches the Buddha under the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajapālanigrodha</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span>, and engages him in conversation. He asks the Buddha to go about among men and make friends and be happy, satisfied with having discovered the path of immortality for himself without feeling it necessary to teach others. But the Buddha refuses his request, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> confesses that he has no longer any power over him; he is like a crab whose claws have been broken one by one. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> adds that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> had been awaiting an opportunity to find a flaw in the Buddha for seven years. The Commentary adds: six before the Awakening and one after. He retires discomfited and sits not far away from the Buddha, brooding, scratching the earth with a stick. SN.i.122ff.
