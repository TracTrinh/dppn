# Saṁyuttanikāya

## English source

Saṁyuttanikāya, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttāgama</span>. One of the five divisions of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttapiṭaka</span>. It consists of 7,762 <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span> (DN­a.i.17; Gv.56), and, at the First Council, was given in charge of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> and his pupillary succession (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nissitaka</span>) (DN­a.i.15).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nikāya</span> is divided into five main Vaggas and fifty-six sections, called Saṁyuttas, each Saṁyutta being again subdivided into minor Vaggas or chapters.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span>, wrote a Commentary on the Saṁyutta, called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāraṭṭhappakāsinī</span>.

The Saṁyuttanikāya is quoted in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span>, e.g., pp. 137, 242, 377, 379; see also Vin.ii.306, where an Uposathasaṁyutta is mentioned, but what is evidently meant is the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uposathakkhandhaka</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kittisirirājasīha</span>, king of Ceylon, had the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> copied by scribes (Cv.xcix.33). One of the Saṁyuttas, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anamatagga</span>, was preached by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rakkhita</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vanavāsa</span> (Mhv.xii.32) and by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> in Ceylon (Mhv.xv.186), soon after their respective arrivals in these countries, at the conclusion of the Third Council. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nikāya</span> has been translated into Burmese. Bode, 92.
