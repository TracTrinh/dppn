# Susunāga

## English source

Susunāga. King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> and father of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷāsoka</span>. He reigned for eighteen years (Mhv.iv.6; but see Dpv.v.98, where his reign is given as ten years, in the eighth year of which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dasaka</span> Thera died. Cf. Vin-a.i.33.).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsaṭīkā</span> (Mv-ṭ.155f), he was the son of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span> *rājā* and a courtezan. When he was born he looked like a lump of flesh, and his mother gave orders that he should be put in a vessel and cast on the rubbish heap. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>-king of the city kept guard over the vessel, but when the people crowded round him and said “*Su-sū*,” he left it and went away. In the crowd was a minister’s son, and, when the vessel was opened and the child was discovered, he decided to adopt him. In due course the child grew up, and when the infuriated populace deposed the parricide <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadāsaka</span>, they placed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Susunāga</span> on the throne. He was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Susunāga</span> because he was guarded by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> who disappeared when the people said “*Su-sū*.”
