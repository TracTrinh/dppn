# Sumanā 06

## English source

Sumanā 06. called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanārājakumārī</span>. She was the daughter of the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> and sister of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>. She is included among the eminent *upāsikās* (AN.iv.347). She once visited the Buddha, with five hundred royal maidens in five hundred royal chariots, and questioned him regarding the efficacy of giving (See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanārājakumārīsutta</span>, AN.iii.32f).

The Commentary explains (AN­a.ii.593f) that these five hundred companions were born on the same day as herself. She was seven years old when the Buddha paid his first visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, and she was present at the dedication of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> with her five hundred companions, carrying vases, flowers, etc., as offering to the Buddha. After the Buddha’s sermon she became a *Sotāpanna*.

It is said that, in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span>, she belonged to a *seṭṭhi* family, her father being dead. When the people, almost at the point of the sword, obtained the king’s permission to entertain the Buddha and his monks, it was the *senāpati*’s privilege to invite the Buddha to his house on the first day. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanā</span> came back from playing, she found her mother in tears, and when asked the reason, her mother replied, “If your father had been alive, ours would have been the privilege of entertaining the Buddha today.” <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanā</span> comforted her by saying that that honour should yet be theirs. She filled a golden bowl with richly flavoured milk-rice, covering it with another bowl. She then wrapped both vessels all round with jasmine flowers and left the house with her slaves. On the way to the *senāpati*’s house she was stopped by his men, but she coaxed them to let her pass, and, as the Buddha approached, saying that she wished to offer him a jasmine garland, she put the two vessels into his alms bowl. She then made the resolve that in every subsequent birth she should be named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanā</span> and that her body should be like a garland of jasmine. When the Buddha arrived in the *senāpati*’s house and was served first with soup, he covered his bowl saying that he had already been given his food. At the end of the meal the *senāpati* made enquiries, and, full of admiration for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanā</span>’s courage, invited her to his house and made her his chief consort. Ever after that she was known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanā</span>, and, wherever she was born, a shower of jasmine flowers fell knee deep on the day of her birth.

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Therīgāthā</span> Commentary (Thīg­a.22f), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanā</span> joined the Saṅgha in her old age. She was present when the Buddha preached to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, the discoursebeginning with, “There are four young creatures, Sire, who may not be disregarded,” and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> was established in the Refuges and the Precepts (SN.i.68-70). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanā</span> wished to leave the world, but put off doing so that she might look after her grandmother as long as she lived.

After the grandmother’s death, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanā</span> went with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> to the *vihāra*, taking such things as rugs and carpets, which she presented to the Saṅgha. The Buddha preached to her and to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, and she became an *Anāgāmī*. She then sought ordination, and, at the conclusion of the stanzas (Thīg.vs.16) preached to her by the Buddha, attained *Arahant*-ship.
