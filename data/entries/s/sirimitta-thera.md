# Sirimitta Thera

## English source

Sirimitta Thera. He belonged to a rich landowner’s family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> and was the nephew of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirigutta</span>. He saw the Buddha subdue the elephant, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanapāla</span>, and, much impressed, entered the Saṅgha, becoming an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> in due course. One day, rising from his seat to recite the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pātimokkha</span>, he took a painted fan and, reseating himself, he spoke eight verses in admonition of the monks and by way of confessing his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">aññā</span> (cf. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khujjuttarā</span>, Dhp­a.i.209). Thag.vss.502-9; Thag­a.i.488f.
