# Sussondījātaka

## English source

Sussondījātaka (Ja 360). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tamba</span> was a king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> and his wife was the beautiful <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sussondī</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadīpa</span> was then known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Seruma</span>, and the Bodhisatta was a young <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Garuḍa</span> living there. He used to go in disguise to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> and play at dice with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tamba</span>. The queen heard of his beauty and contrived to see him, and they fell in love with each other. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Garuḍa</span>, by his power, raised a storm in the city and covered it with darkness, under cover of which he carried off <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sussondī</span>. The king was filled with grief, not knowing what had happened to his queen, as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Garuḍa</span> continued to play at dice with him. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tamba</span> therefore sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sagga</span>, a minstrel, to search for her. In the course of his wanderings, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sagga</span> came to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhārukaccha</span> and took ship for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suvaṇṇabhūmi</span>. In the middle of the ocean the sailors asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sagga</span> to play for them, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sagga</span> told them that his music would excite the big fish and trouble would ensue. The sailors, however, insisting, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sagga</span> played, and the fish, maddened by the sound, splashed about, and the ship broke in two under the leap of a sea-monster. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sagga</span> lay on a plank, which drifted to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadīpa</span>. There he saw and was recognized by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sussondī</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sussondī</span> took him home, and, keeping him hidden from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Garuḍa</span>, enjoyed herself with him when the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Garuḍa</span> was away playing at dice. Six weeks later a ship, with merchants for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, touched at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadīpa</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sagga</span> returned home. He found <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tamba</span> playing at dice with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Garuḍa</span> and recounted his adventures in song. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Garuḍa</span> heard the song and understood the references. Filled with remorse that he had not been able to keep his wife, he brought her back to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tamba</span>.

The story was related in reference to a love-sick monk. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tamba</span>. Ja.iii.187-90.
