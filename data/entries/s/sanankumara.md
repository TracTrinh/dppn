# Sanaṅkumāra

## English source

Sanaṅkumāra. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Brahma</span>. In the *Nikāyas* he is mentioned as the author of a famous verse, there quoted: *Khattiyo seṭṭho jane tasmiṁ ye gottapaṭisārino, vijjācaraṇasampanno so seṭṭho devamānuse*, among people who trust in lineage, the *khattiya* is considered supreme, but one accomplished in knowledge and conduct is truly supreme among gods and humans (DN.i.121; MN.i.358; SN.i.153; AN.v.327).

In one place (SN.ii.284) the verse is attributed to the Buddha, thus endowing it with the authoritativeness of a pronouncement by the Buddha himself. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sanaṅkumāra</span> is represented as a very devout follower of the Buddha.

In a *Sutta* of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyutta</span> (SN.i.153), he is spoken of as visiting the Buddha on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sappinī</span>, and it was during this visit that the above verse was spoken. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sanaṅkumāra</span> was present at the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Samayasutta</span> (DN.ii.261).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janavasabhasutta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janavasabha</span> describes to the Buddha an occasion on which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sanaṅkumāra</span> attended an assembly of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, presided over by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Four Regent Gods</span>. There was suddenly a vast radiance, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> knew of the approach of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sanaṅkumāra</span>. As the usual appearance of the *Brahma* is not sufficiently materialized for him to be perceived by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, he is forced to appear as a relatively gross personality which he specially creates. As he arrives, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> sit in their places with clasped hands waiting for him to choose his seat. Then <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sanaṅkumāra</span> takes on the form of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span> (because all <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> like <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span>, says the Commentary, DN­a.ii.640) and sits, above the assembly, cross-legged, in the air. So seated, he expresses his satisfaction that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> and all the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> should honour and follow the Buddha. His voice has all the eight characteristics of a *Brahma*’s voice (these are given at DN.ii.211). He then proceeds to create thirty-three shapes of himself, each sitting on the divan of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> *Deva*, and addresses the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, speaking of the advantages of taking refuge in the Buddha, the Dhamma and the Saṅgha. Each *Deva* fancies that only the shape sitting on his own divan has spoken and that the others are silent. Then <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sanaṅkumāra</span> goes to the end of the Hall, and, seated on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s throne, addresses the whole assembly on the four ways of *iddhi*; on the three avenues leading to Bliss, as manifested by the Buddha; on the four *satipaṭṭhāna*s, and the seven *samādhiparikkhārā*. He declares that more than twenty-four lakhs of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> disciples, having followed the teachings of the Buddha, have been born in the *Deva* worlds. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sanaṅkumāra</span> has finished his address, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> wonders if there have been Buddhas in the past and will be in the future. The *Brahma* reads his thoughts and says there certainly were and will be.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sanaṅkumāra</span> means “ever young.” <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says that, in his former birth, he practised *jhānas* while yet a boy with his hair tied in five knots (*pañcacūḷakakumārakāle*), and was reborn in the *Brahma* world with the *jhāna* intact (MN­a.ii.584; cf. SN­a.i.171). He liked the guise of youth and continued in the same, hence the name. Rhys Davids sees in the legend of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sanaṅkumāra</span> the Indian counterpart of the European legend of Galahad (Dial.ii.292, n.3; cf.i.121, n.1). The oldest mention of it is in the *Chāṇḍogya Upaniṣad* (Chap. VII), where the ideal, yet saintly knight, teaches a typical Brahmin the highest truths. In the *Mahā Bhārata* (iii.185, Bombay Edition) he expresses a sentiment very similar to that expressed in the stanza quoted above. In mediaeval literature he is said to have been one of five or seven mind-born sons of *Brahma* who remained pure and innocent. A later and debased Jaina version of the legend tells in detail of the love adventures and wives of this knight, with a few words at the end on his conversion to the saintly life. See JRAS.1894, p. 344; 1897, p.585 f; *Revue de Histoire des Religions*, vol.xxxi.pp.29ff.
