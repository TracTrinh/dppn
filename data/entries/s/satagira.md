# Sātāgira

## English source

Sātāgira. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>. He and his friend, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemavata</span>, were two of the twenty-eight leaders of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span>. They had both been monks in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>, but had been guilty of deciding wrongly in the dispute which arose between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammavādī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Adhammavādī</span>, hence their birth as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span>, Sātāgira in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sātapabbata</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemavata</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span>. They recognized each other at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> assembly in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhagalavatī</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pabbata</span>, and promised to inform each other if, in their lives, they came across anything of interest.

When the Buddha was born and when he preached his first sermon, Sātāgira was present in the assembly, but, because he was constantly looking about to see if <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemavata</span> was there, he could not concentrate his mind on the Buddha’s teaching. When the sun set and the Buddha was still preaching, he went with five hundred of his followers to fetch <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemavata</span>. At <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> they met <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemavata</span>, who was on the way to invite Sātāgira to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span>, which was covered with such flowers as had never before been seen. Sātāgira explains that the reason for this miracle is the appearance of the Buddha in the world, and, in answer to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemavata</span>’s questions, declares the greatness of the Buddha.

Their conversation is found in the introductory <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gāthā</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemavatasutta</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (Snp­a.i.199) that, according to some, this meeting took place, not on the occasion of the first sermon, but later, when the Buddha was living in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotāmaka</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāli Kuraragharikā</span>, as she sat by her window cooling herself, heard the conversation of the two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span>, and her mind being filled with devotion to the Buddha, as she heard his wonderful qualities being enumerated she attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpatti</span>.

When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemavata</span> is satisfied, from Sātāgira’s description, that the Buddha is really the Awakened One, he decides to go to him with Sātāgira. Together they go with their followers to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> in the middle watch of the night, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemavata</span> questions the Buddha about his teaching. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemavata</span> is, by nature, powerful and wise and filled with respect for the good, and the Buddha’s marvellous exposition of the Dhamma fills him with great joy. He sings the Buddha’s praises in five stanzas, and, after taking leave of him and of Sātāgira, returns home with the promise that he will wander from place to place carrying the joyful news of the Buddha and his Dhamma among all beings. This story is given in the Commentary to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemavatasutta</span>; Snp­a.i.194-216; cf. AN­a.i.134f.

Later, when journeying through the air in various conveyances, on their way to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> assembly, Sātāgira and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemavata</span> and their followers were about to pass over <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span>’s hermitage; but because the Buddha was then inside, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> found that their conveyances remained stationary as no one could pass over the head of the Buddha. When they thus discovered his presence, they alighted and made obeisance to the Buddha, and congratulated <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavaka</span> on his extreme good fortune in having an opportunity of meeting and listening to the Buddha. AN­a.i.221; a similar story is related (Ud­a.64) about them when they passed the abode of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajakalāpaka</span>.

Sātāgira is mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āṭānāṭiyasutta</span> (DN.iii.204) as one of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> to be invoked in time of need by the Buddha’s followers. He is identified with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhisajātaka</span>. Ja 488, Ja.iv.314.
