# Sīvalī 02

## English source

Sīvalī 02. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalī Thera</span>. He was the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppavāsā</span>, daughter of the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiya</span>. For seven years and seven days he lay in her womb, and for seven days she was in labour and was unable to bring forth the child. She said to her husband: “Before I die I will make a gift,” and sent a gift by him to the Buddha. He accepted the gift and pronounced blessing on her. She was immediately delivered of a son. When her husband returned, she asked him to show hospitality to the Buddha and his monks for seven days.

From the time of his birth, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalī</span> could do anything. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> talked with him on the day of his birth and ordained him with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppavāsā</span>’s permission. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalī</span> became a *Sotāpanna* in the Tonsure hall when his first lock of hair was cut, and a *Sakadāgāmī* with the second. Some say that after his ordination he left home on the same day and lived in a secluded hut, meditating on the delays in his birth, and thus, winning insight, attained *Arahant*-ship.

In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>’s time he made the resolve to be pre-eminent among recipients of gifts, like <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudassana</span>, disciple of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span>. To this end he gave alms for seven days to the Buddha and his monks.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span> he was a householder near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhumatī</span>. The people gave alms to the Buddha and the Saṅgha in competition with the king, and when they were in need of honey, curds and sugar, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalī</span> gave enough of these for sixty-eight thousand monks.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atthadassī Buddha</span> he was a king, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Varuṇa</span>, and when the Buddha died, he made great offerings to the Bodhi-tree, dying under it later. Then he was born in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nimmānaratī</span> world.

Thirty-four times he was king of men, under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subāhu</span> (Thag.vs.60; Thag­a.i.135). According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> account his father in his last birth was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī Mahāli</span> (Ap.ii.492f).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asātarūpajātaka</span> (Ja 100) gives the reason for the delay in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalī</span>’s birth. Cf.Ap.ii.494, vs.29f. The story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalī</span> is given also at Ud.ii.8; AN­a.i.130f.; Dhp­a.iv.192f.; ii.196; Ja.i.408f. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> is very similar to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (iv.192f.). Both <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> say that a lay supporter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> postponed his entertainment of the Buddha (who requested him to do so) to enable the Buddha to accept <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppavāsā</span>’s invitation after the birth of the child. Other accounts omit this. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> says nothing about <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalī</span>’s retirement from the world. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> account of this differs from the others.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalī</span> was declared by the Buddha (AN.i.24) pre-eminent among recipients of gifts. It is said (Thag­a.i.138; Ap.ii.495; AN­a.i.139) that when the Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khadiravaniya Revata</span>, he took <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalī</span> with him because the road was difficult and provisions scarce. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalī</span> went to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span> with five hundred others, to test his good luck. The gods provided them with everything. On <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhamādana</span> a *Deva*, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadatta</span>, entertained them for seven days on milk-rice.
