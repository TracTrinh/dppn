# Sādhīnajātaka

## English source

Sādhīnajātaka (Ja 494). The Bodhisatta was once born as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sādhīna</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span>. He built six alms halls and spent daily six hundred thousand pieces on alms. He lived a good life, and his subjects followed his example. In the assembly of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> his praises were spoken, and various <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> wished to see him. So <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātali</span>, with his chariot, to fetch <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sādhīna</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>. When he arrived, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> gave him half his kingdom and his luxuries. For seven hundred years, in human reckoning, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sādhīna</span> ruled in heaven, and then he became dissatisfied and returned to his royal park on earth. The parkkeeper brought news of his arrival to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span>, the reigning king, seventh in direct descent from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sādhīna</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span> arrived, paid homage to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sādhīna</span>, and offered him the kingdom. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sādhīna</span> refused it, saying that all he wished was to distribute alms for seven days. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span> arranged a vast largesse for distribution. For seven days <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sādhīna</span> gave alms, and on the seventh day he died and was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>. The story was related to lay disciples to show them the importance of keeping the fast day.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> (Ja.iv.355-60).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sādhīna</span> was one of the four human beings who went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> while in their human body. Mil.115, 271; MN­a.ii.738. The others being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Guttila</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mandhātā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nimi</span>.
