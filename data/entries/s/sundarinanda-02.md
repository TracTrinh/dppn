# Sundarīnandā 02

## English source

Sundarīnandā 02. A Therī. She was the daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Pajāpatī</span> and sister of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda Thera</span>. Seeing that most of her kinsmen had joined the Saṅgha, she too became a nun, not from faith, but from love of her kin. Being intoxicated with her own beauty, she did not go to see the Buddha lest he should rebuke her. The rest of her story is very similar to that of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhirūpanandā</span>. The Buddha preached to her and she became a *Sotāpanna*. He then gave her a topic of meditation, and she, developing insight, became an *Arahant*. Later she was declared foremost among nuns in power of meditation, an eminence which she had resolved to obtain in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>. Thag.vs.82-6; Thīg­a.80f.; Ap.ii.572f; AN.i.25; AN­a.i.198f.

She seems to have been called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rūpanandā</span> (AN­a.i.198) too; there seems to have been some confusion in the legends of the different *Therīs* named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandā</span>.
