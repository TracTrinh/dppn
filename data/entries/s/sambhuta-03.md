# Sambhūta 03

## English source

Sambhūta 03. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambhūta Thera</span>. He belonged to a family of clansmen and joined the Saṅgha under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, after the Buddha’s death, attaining *Arahant*-ship in due course.

He lived in the bliss of emancipation, till one century after the Buddha’s death, and, when the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjiputtaka</span> heresy arose, his help was sought by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasa Kākaṇḍakaputta</span>. At that time he lived on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ahogaṅgapabbata</span> and was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sānavāsī</span> because he wore a hempen robe. At the assembly of the *Arahants* held on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ahogaṅgapabbata</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambhūta</span> suggested that they should seek the support of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soreyya Revata</span>. Together they went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabbakāmī</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambhūta</span> questioned him regarding the “Ten Points.” <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambhūta</span> was one of the monks appointed to the committee to discuss the points raised, and when they were declared heretical, he joined in the holding of the Second Council. Vin.ii.298 f., 303ff.; Thag­a.i.390 f.; Mhv.iv.18, 57; Dpv.iv.49; v.22; Vin-a.i.34f. A series of verses uttered by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambhūta</span>, moved by righteous emotion at the proposed perversion of the Dhamma and Vinaya by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjiputtakas</span>, is included in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> (Thag.vss.291-4).

In the past, during a period when there were no Buddhas in the world, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambhūta</span> was a *Kinnara* on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candabhāgā</span>, and seeing a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>, he worshipped him and offered him *ajjuna*-flowers.

He is evidently identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajjunapupphiya</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>. Ap.i.450.
