# Dhammadinnā 03

## English source

Dhammadinnā 03. An eminent Therī, ranked foremost among nuns who possessed the gift of preaching (AN.i.25). She was the wife of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākha</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, and when he, having heard the Buddha preach, became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmī</span>, she left the world with the consent of her husband who sent her to the nunnery in a golden palanquin (MN­a.i.515 says this was provided by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>). Dwelling in solitude, she soon attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship with the four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">paṭisambhidā</span> (see Thīg.v.12 for a stanza uttered by her). She later returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> to worship the Buddha, and there <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākha</span> asked her questions on the Dhamma, which she answered “as easily as one might cut a lotus-stalk with a knife.” The questions and answers are given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Vedallasutta</span> (MN.i.299ff). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākha</span> reported this interview to the Buddha, who praised her great wisdom and commended her eloquence.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> she was a servant, and one day saw the Buddha’s disciple, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujāta</span>, begging alms and gave him a curry (Thīg­a. p.15 says <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujāta</span> had just risen from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">samādhi</span>). Her master, seeing this, made her his daughter-in-law. Later, while on a visit to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span>, she saw a nun declared to be chief of preachers and wished for similar eminence.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phussa Buddha</span> she obtained merit by giving to the Buddha’s half-brother double the gift prescribed by her husband (details of this are given in Pv-a., p.21).

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> she was born as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhammā</span>, the sixth of the seven daughters of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kikī</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>; for twenty thousand years she lived in celibacy (her story is given in Thīg­a.15ff; Ap.ii.567f; AN­a.i.196f; MN­a.i.515ff; Dhp­a.iv.229ff).

Dhammadinnā was the teacher of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sukhā</span> (Thīg­a.58).
