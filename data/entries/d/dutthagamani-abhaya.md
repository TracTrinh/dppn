# Duṭṭhagāmaṇī Abhaya

## English source

Duṭṭhagāmaṇī Abhaya. King of Ceylon (101-77 B.C.) He was the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kākavaṇṇatissa</span> (ruler of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Gāma</span>) and of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vihāradevī</span>, and was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gāmaṇi Abhaya</span>. The antenatal cravings of his mother showed that he would be a great warrior, and his father gathered at his court the most famous warriors of the land skilled in various ways. Chief among them were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandhimitta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sūranimila</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Soṇa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Goṭha-imbara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theraputtābhaya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bharaṇa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷusumana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khañjadeva</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phussadeva</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Labhiya Vasabha</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span> early showed signs of an adventurous disposition, and resented the confined limits of his father’s kingdom, bounded on the north by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vālukanadī</span>, on the further bank of which lay the Sinhalese country ruled by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Damiḷas</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span> was constantly refused permission by his father to fight the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Damiḷas</span> and fled in anger to the hills, whence he sent his royal father a woman’s garment, to indicate that he was no man. This earned for him the nickname of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭha</span>, which always stuck to him. At his father’s death he had to fight with his brother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> (afterwards <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saddhātissa</span>) for the possession of the throne. He was first defeated at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷaṅganiyapiṭṭhi</span>, but later he was victorious, and the Saṅgha brought about a reconciliation between the brothers. When fully prepared, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> marched against the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Damiḷa</span> king, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Eḷāra</span>. He rode his state elephant, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇḍula</span>, born on the same day as himself. He commenced operations at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahiyaṅgaṇa</span>, capturing fort after fort, manned by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Eḷāra</span>’s followers, and fought his way down to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vālukanadī</span>, where he pitched his camp at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kandhāvārapiṭṭhi</span>, near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijitapura</span>, where were concentrated the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Damiḷas</span>. After a siege of four months <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijitapura</span> fell, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> advanced through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Girilaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahelanagara</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsapabbata</span> near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>, the capital. (Mhv.xxv.75. It is said that in the course of his journey from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Gāma</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> he captured thirty-two fortresses manned by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Damiḷas</span>). There he waited for the onset of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Eḷāra</span> and, in the battle that ensued, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Eḷāra</span> was defeated and fled towards the capital, but he was pursued by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> and slain by him in single combat close to the southern gate of the city. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Eḷāra</span>’s body was burnt with royal honours, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> built a tomb over the ashes and decreed that no music should be played by people passing it, a decree that was for long honoured. This act of chivalry, so much in contrast with the usual conduct of victors, earned for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> great honour. Later, he defeated reinforcements from India under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhalluka</span>, nephew of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Eḷāra</span>, and thus became sole monarch of Laṅkā.

On the seventh day after his final victory, he celebrated a water festival at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissavāpi</span> and, at its conclusion, built the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maricavaṭṭhithūpa</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) on the spot where his spear, containing the relic of the Buddha, given by the monks at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissamahārāma</span>, remained firmly embedded, no one being able to remove it. From now onwards, consoled by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piyaṅgudīpa</span>, who absolved him from blame for the slaughter of his enemies, he began his great works of piety, after having distributed largesse to his generals and soldiers. He first built the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) of nine stories, resembling the palace of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bīraṇī</span>, the plan of which was brought to him from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span>. He then began his greatest achievement, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span>, erected on a site visited by the Buddha during his third visit to Ceylon. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, led by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, provided the necessary materials, discovered in various parts of the island, and he began work immediately, on the full-moon day of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesākha</span>. Great celebrations marked the inauguration of the mighty task, plans of various builders were inspected before the final choice and no free work was allowed to be done. After the relics, obtained by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇuttara</span> from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>-world, had been enshrined in unparalleled splendour and with great feasting, but before the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t18">chatta</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span> and the plaster work could be finished, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> fell ill. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saddhātissa</span> was summoned from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghavāpi</span>, and he covered the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span> with white cloth and crowned it with a spire of bamboo, that the king, before his death, might visualize his great work in its complete form. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theraputtābhaya</span>, a former general, now become an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>, and living in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañjalipabbata</span>, was at the king’s side at the time of his death and consoled him with reminders of the great merit he had accumulated during his life. A record of the king’s good deeds was read by his secretary, from which it would appear that the king had erected ninety-nine other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāras</span>, besides the buildings already mentioned. He had once tried to preach in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span>, but was so overcome by nervousness that, realizing how difficult was the task of the preacher, he ordered special benefactions for those who preached the Dhamma. Two gifts made by him are recorded as of very special merit – one was the sale of his special earrings to procure food for five Theras during the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Akkhakkhāyika</span> famine, the other was his gift of food during his flight from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷaṅganiyapiṭṭhi</span> (for details see Mhv.xxxii.49ff; also AN­a.i.365f). He was starving, and his minister <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> procured a meal for him, but as he never ate without offering some of the food to the monks, he wished for a monk to appear before him. When a Thera did so appear, he gave him all he had. He was told later, on his death bed, by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theraputtābhaya</span>, that this food was divided among many thousands of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span> so that the merits of the donor might increase manifold.

It is said that after death <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> was born in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span>-world, there to await the appearance of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Metteyya Buddha</span>. He will then become the chief disciple of that Buddha, and his parents will be the parents of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Metteyya</span>. Before his birth, as the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kākavaṇṇatissa</span>, he was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sāmaṇera</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṭapabbata</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span>. He fell ill through his hard work on behalf of the Saṅgha at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ākāsacetiya</span> near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittalapabbata</span>, and as he lay dying in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Silāpassayapariveṇa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vihāradevī</span> visited him at the suggestion of an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> Thera<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t18">,</span> and after much difficulty persuaded him to be reborn in this world as her son (these particulars relating to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> are summarised from Mhv. chaps.xxii–xxxii; Dpv.xviii.53; xix.1ff; Vin-a.i.102).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> is regarded as the hero of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsa</span> epic. His son was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāliya</span>, who, however, did not succeed him, preferring to marry a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Caṇḍāla</span> maiden, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asokamālā</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span>’s successor, therefore, was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saddhātissa</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp­a.iv.50) mentions a minister of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lakuṇṭaka Atimbara</span>, whose wife was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanā</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> lived to the age of sixty-eight (Mhv.xxiv.47).

Once, after his conquest of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Damiḷas</span>, he was unable to sleep for a whole month, then, at the suggestion of the monks, he took the fast of the eight vows and eight monks chanted to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittayamaka</span>. He fell asleep during the chanting (DN­a.ii.640).
