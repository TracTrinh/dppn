# Dakkhiṇāpatha

## English source

Dakkhiṇāpatha (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇapatha</span>). In the old Pāli literature the name Dakkhiṇāpatha would seem to indicate only a remote settlement or colony on the banks of the upper <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godhāvarī</span>. Thus, we are told that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span> had his hermitage in Dakkhiṇāpatha territory, midway between the kingdoms of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aḷaka</span> (Snp., vs.976). Elsewhere the name is coupled with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> as Avantidakkhiṇāpatha and seems to refer, but more vaguely, to the same limited district. Vin.i.195, 196; ii.298. In Ja.v.133, however, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span> is spoken of as a part of Dakkhiṇāpatha (*Dakkhiṇūpathe Avantiraṭṭha*), but see Ja.iii.463, where Avantidakkhiṇāpatha is spoken of.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span> Commentary (ii.580) seems to explain Dakkhiṇāpatha as the road leading to the Dakkhiṇajanapada, while the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumaṅgalavilāsinī</span> (DN­a.i.265) takes Dakkhiṇāpatha to be synonymous with Dakkhiṇajanapada and says that it was the district (*janapada*) south of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span> (*Gaṅgāya dakkhiṇato pākatajanapadaṁ*).

It is clear that, in the earlier literature at any rate, the word did not mean the whole country comprised in the modern word Dekkhan. It is possible that Dakkhiṇāpatha was originally the name of the road which led southwards – the Aryan settlement at the end of the road, on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godhāvarī</span> being also called by the same name – and that later the road lent its name to the whole region through which it passed (for a detailed description see Law: *Geography of Early Buddhism*, pp.60ff). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Petavatthu</span> Commentary (Pv-a., p.133) the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Damiḷa</span> country (*Damiḷavisaya*) is included in the Dakkhiṇāpatha.

The Dakkhiṇāpatha is famous in literature as the birthplace of strong bullocks (Dhs-a.141; NidA.16; Dhp­a.iii.248, etc.). It held also a large number of ascetics (DN­a.i.265), and in the “southern districts” (*Dakkhiṇesu janapadesu*) people celebrated a feast called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dharaṇa</span> (AN.v.216). See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dharaṇasutta</span>.
