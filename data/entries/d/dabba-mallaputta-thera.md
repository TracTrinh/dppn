# Dabba Mallaputta Thera

## English source

Dabba Mallaputta Thera, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba Mallaputtatissa</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thera</span>. An <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. He was born at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anupiya</span> in a family of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>, says the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>). As a child of seven he saw <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> who was visiting in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malla</span> country, and he asked his grandmother (his mother having died at his birth) if he might join the Saṅgha under the Buddha. She brought him to the Buddha and the boy became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> in the Tonsure-hall. He returned with the Buddha to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> where, with the Buddha’s sanction, and wishing to be of service to the Saṅgha, he took upon himself the task of appointing night’s-lodgings to travelling monks and of directing them to meals. He performed his duties most diligently and with great intelligence, and his fame spread far and wide. Monks coming from afar, wishing to witness his skill, would deliberately arrive late and ask for lodgings in some place remote from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>; Dabba would “burst into flame” and walk ahead of them, with his finger burning to light them on the way. It was the sight of Dabba on one of these journeys which led to a slave-woman, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇā</span>, being visited by the Buddha, resulting in her becoming a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span> (Dhp­a.iii.321ff).

It once happened that meals were allotted by Dabba to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mettiya Bhummajakā</span> at the house of a rich man, who, discovering their identity, gave orders that they were to be fed anyhow. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mettiya Bhummajakā</span> were greatly offended, and believing that Dabba had intended to slight them, induced one of their partisans, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mettiyā</span>, to accuse Dabba of having seduced her. The charge was investigated, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mettiyā</span> was expelled, and Dabba’s fame increased (Thag.v.5; Vin.ii.74ff; iii.158f, 166f, iv.37f; Vin-a.iii.598f). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mettiya Bhummajakā</span> persuaded the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaḍḍha</span>, to make a similar charge against Dabba regarding his wife (Vin.ii.124f). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Taṇḍulanālijātaka</span> (Ja 5, Ja.i.123f) mentions another dispute, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lāḷudāyi</span> charges Dabba with not performing his duties conscientiously. Thereupon <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lāḷudāyi</span> was appointed to the task, but proved a failure.

Dabba was given the rank of chief of those who appointed lodgings (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">senāsanapaññāpakānaṁ</span>) and was given the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">upasampadā</span> ordination when only seven years old (AN.i.24). He was called Dabba because he was said to be born of his mother while she was being burnt in the funeral pyre; when the flames were extinguished, the child was found lying on one of the posts of the pyre (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dabbatthambhe</span>) (Thag­a.i.41; AN­a.i.152f).

He was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhiputta</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsavatī</span> in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>, and it was then that he conceived the desire for the rank of chief apportioner of lodgings. One hundred and three times he became king among <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> and one hundred and five times king of men. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span> he spoke calumny about an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> Thera<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t18">,</span> hence the conspiracy against him by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mettiya Bhummajakā</span>. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> he, with six others, went to the top of a hill, determined not to return till they had accomplished their purpose, but five of them died before this came to pass. The other four were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sabhiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāhiya</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumāra Kassapa</span> (Dhp­a.ii.212; Thag­a.i.44ff; Ap.ii.471f; Ud­a.81; Vin-a.ii.578f).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba</span> evidently died young. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udāna</span> contains an account of his death (Ud.viii.9; Ud­a.431f). One day, returning from his alms rounds in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, he saw that he had but a short while yet to live. He went, therefore, to the Buddha and, with his leave, showed various <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span>-powers and passed away.
