# Dīghāvusutta

## English source

Dīghāvusutta. Records the visit of the Buddha to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghāvu</span> (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dighāvu</span> 2). The Buddha exhorts him to practise the six conditions which are constituent parts of knowledge (*cha vijjābhāgiyadhamme*). These are:

- contemplation of impermanence in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t23">saṅkhāras</span>,
- consciousness of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t23">dukkha</span> in impermanence,
- of there being no-self in what is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t23">dukkha</span>,
- consciousness of abandoning, of dispersion, and of cessation. SN.v.344f.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghāsana</span>. A monastery in Ceylon, in which lived <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma Thera</span> (Cv.xxxix.42). Geiger thinks (Cv.Trs.i.48, n.1) that Dīghāsana is very probably a wrong reading for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghasanda</span>.
