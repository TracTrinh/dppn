# Dhaniya 01

## English source

Dhaniya 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanika</span>. A herdsman living on the bank of the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahī</span>. He was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhiputta</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammakoṇḍa</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pabbataraṭṭha</span>, which belonged to the kingdom of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span>. He had thirty thousand oxen and twenty thousand cows. He had seven sons and seven daughters and numerous retainers. In the dry season he lived on an island formed by the two forks of the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahī</span>, and in the rainy season in a house on the upland. One day, while he was in this house, having finished his preparations for the approach of the rains, he sat meditating on his comfortable circumstances and broke forth into song in token of his happiness. The Buddha heard the song at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, seven hundred leagues away, and having travelled through the air, stood over <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhaniya</span>’s dwelling. As <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhaniya</span> proceeded with his song, the Buddha added a verse to each one of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhaniya</span>’s. At the end of this song <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhaniya</span>, his wife, and two of his daughters, became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpannas</span>. The Buddha then revealed himself and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhaniya</span> and his wife entered the Saṅgha. Later they became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span> and the cowherds erected for their use a monastery, which came to be called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gokulaṅkavihāra</span>. Snp.vv.18ff; Snp­a.i.26ff.
