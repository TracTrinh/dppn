# Devadaha 01

## English source

Devadaha 01. A township (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nigama</span>) of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span>. The Buddha stayed there during his tours and preached to the monks on various topics (SN.iii.5f; iv.124f; MN.ii.214). According to the Commentaries (Ja.i.52; Bv­a.226; MN­a.ii.924, 1021, etc; Thīg­a.182) it was the city of the birth of the Buddha’s mother and of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī Gotamī</span> and of their companions, who married the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lumbinīvana</span>, where the Buddha was born, was near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadaha</span>. The name was originally that of a lake, so-called either because kings held their sports in it (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">devā vuccanti rājāno tesaṁ maṅgaladaho</span>), or because it came into existence without human intervention, hence divine (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sayañjāto vā so daho, tasmā pi Devadaho</span>). The name was later transferred to the village near by. SN­a.ii.186; also MN­a.ii.810. According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Dulva</span> (Rockhill, p.12), the city was founded by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, when they grew very numerous. The spot was pointed out by a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span>, hence its name.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppabuddha</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadaha</span> was a contemporary of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> (p.14).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadaha</span> was the residence of Devadahasakka (Mhv.ii.17; Mhv­ṭ.87) and of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pakkha Thera</span> (Thag­a.i.114).
