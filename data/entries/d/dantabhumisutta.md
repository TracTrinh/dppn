# Dantabhumisutta

## English source

Dantabhumisutta. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayasena</span> visits the novice <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aciravata</span> at the Forest Hut in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span> and asks him to explain how a monk whose life is strenuous and purged of self can find peace in his heart.

At the end of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aciravata</span>’s discourse, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayasena</span> leaves him, complaining that the matter is not at all clear to him.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aciravata</span> mentions the matter to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>, who says that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayasena</span> cannot understand such matters, being too fond of pleasure and undisciplined.

He illustrates his meaning by various examples, one being a description of the catching and taming of a wild elephant. MN.iii.128ff.
