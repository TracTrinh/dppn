# Dīghavāpi

## English source

Dīghavāpi. A tank and a district in Ceylon. When the Buddha went to Ceylon he visited the village, and on the spot where he sat in meditation a *cetiya* was later erected (Mhv.i.78; Dpv.ii.60; Vin-a.i.89). It seems to have been the central post in the country lying between the Tamil kingdom and the province of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohaṇa</span>. Thus we find <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span>’s brother, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span>, occupying it by the order of his father. Later, on the death of his father, he retired to Dīghavāpi with his mother and the elephant <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇḍula</span> (Mhv.xxiv.2, 14f, 48). When he made peace with his brother, he was again sent there to look after the district.

After the conquest of the Tamils, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> was again in charge of Dīghavāpi, for we find him being sent for from there at the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span>’s death (Mhv.xxxii.2). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> (afterwards called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saddhātissa</span>) founded the Dīghavāpi *vihāra*, in connection with which he built a *cetiya*, to which he made valuable offerings (Mhv.xxxiii.9, 14). We hear of Dīghavāpi in connection with the campaigns of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu I</span>. (Cv.lxxiv.89; 98, 110, 180; lxxv.1, 10). Many years later <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājasīha II</span>. gave the district round Dīghavāpi to the settlers who came from Holland (Cv.xcvi.25, 28; for its identification see Cv.Trs.ii.30, n.1). The village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Muni</span>, residence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanā</span>, wife of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lakuṇṭaka Atimbara</span>, was in Dīghavāpi (Dhp­a.iv.50). Dīghavāpi was nine leagues from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissamahārāma</span> (AN­a.i.386).

For a story connected with the *cetiya* see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhajaggaparitta</span>.
