# Duṭṭhatthaka

## English source

Duṭṭhatthaka. The third <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṭṭhakavagga</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span> (Snp.vs.780-87). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> was preached in reference to the calumny raised against the Buddha by the heretics who used the female ascetic <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sundarī</span> to further their ends (Snp­a.ii.518f). Commentarial explanation of this is included in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Niddesa</span> (MNidd.i.62ff). The burden of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> is that he who praises his own virtue and is dependent upon varying dogmas of philosophy is constantly censured. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">muni</span> is one who has shaken off all systems of philosophy.
