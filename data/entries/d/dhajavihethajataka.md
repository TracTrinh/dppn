# Dhajaviheṭhajātaka

## English source

Dhajaviheṭhajātaka (Ja 391). A wizard was wont to come at midnight in order to corrupt the queen of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. She complained to the king and, at his request, she set the mark on her hand with vermillion on his back. By day the man was an ascetic, and when he found that he was discovered he fled through the air. The king thereupon suspected all ascetics and ordered them all to leave the kingdom. The king became a heretic. The Bodhisatta who was born as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, seeing all this, came to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> with an old <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> and stood close to the palace, showing him great reverence. When the king came out <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> revealed his identity, telling him that even the ruler of the gods honoured pious men. The king saw his error and mended his ways.

The origin of the story is given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaṇhajātaka</span> (Ja 469). The king is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>. Ja.iii.303-7. More or less the same story is given at greater length and with several variations in detail in both the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Haṁsajātaka</span> (Ja 533) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Haṁsajātaka</span> (Ja 534).
