# Dīghanakha

## English source

Dīghanakha. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Paribbājaka</span>, nephew (sister’s son) of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>.

He visited the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sūkarakhatalena</span> and the Buddha preached to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghanakhasutta</span>, at the end of which he became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> he is addressed as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggivessana</span> (MN.i.497f; Thag­a.ii.95; Dhp­a.i.79).

He was originally an Annihilationist (MN­a.ii.477). Perhaps he is the heretic Brahmin, nephew of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, who was admonished by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> because he expressed great repugnance at the sight of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>. Thag.1108ff; Thag­a.ii.180.
