# Dhātusena 02

## English source

Dhātusena 02. King of Ceylon (460-78 CE.). He was the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dāṭhānāma</span> and brother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Silātissabodhi</span>. He seems to have had another brother, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumārasena</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhātusena</span> belonged to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moriyavaṁsa</span>. He became a monk under his uncle, the incumbent of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Dīghasaṇḍasenāpatipariveṇa</span>, and remained with him till preparations for his campaign were made. Then he returned to the lay life, killed the Pāṇḍyan, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tiritara</span>, then reigning at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>, and became king. His chief work was the construction of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kālavāpi</span>, which he carried out by damming the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Goṇanadī</span>. He built eighteen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāras</span> and eighteen tanks. He showed great favour to the monks and did many works of piety. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambatthalavihāra</span> he gave to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammarucikas</span>.

He had two sons – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> rebelled against his father at the instigation of his brother-in-law-who had been punished for ill-treating the king’s daughter – and seized <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhātusena</span> in the hope of securing his treasure. The king asked to be taken to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kālavāpi</span>, saw the Thera who had been his teacher, and announced that his whole treasure was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kālavāpi</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> then had him buried alive.

Among <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhātusena</span>’s gifts is mentioned that of one thousand gold pieces spent by him for an interpretation (?) of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīpavaṁsa</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dīpetuṁ Dīpavaṁsaṁ</span>). Cv.xxxviii.14f, 30ff.
