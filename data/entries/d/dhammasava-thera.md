# Dhammasava Thera

## English source

Dhammasava Thera. An <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. He was a Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> who, having heard the Buddha preach at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇāgiri</span>, entered the Saṅgha. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> he was a Brahmin ascetic named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suvaccha</span>, versed in the three Vedas. The Buddha appeared before him, and he paid him homage by scattering <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nāga</span>-flowers in his path. Thirty-one <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> ago he was a king named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Ratha</span> (Thag.107; Thag­a.i.214f). He is probably identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgapupphiya</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>. Ap.i.179.
