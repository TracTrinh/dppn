# Dhammika 01

## English source

Dhammika 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammika Thera</span>. A Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> who was converted at the presentation of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> and entered a village *vihāra*. As he became irritated when monks visited the *vihāra* they desisted, and he became sole master of the *vihāra*. When this was reported to the Buddha by a layman, the Buddha sent for him and preached to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rukkhadhammajātaka</span> (Ja 74), showing that in the past, too, he had been guilty of similar conduct. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammika</span> concentrated on the verses of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> and, developing insight, became an *Arahant*.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sikhī Buddha</span> he had been a hunter and had listened to the Buddha preaching to an assembly of the gods in a forest. Thag.303-6; Thag­a.i.396ff.

According to AN.iii.366ff. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammika</span> had to leave seven lodgings, one after the other, because the lay supporters of the lodgings could not tolerate his insulting ways. He therefore sought the Buddha and complained to him. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rukkhadhammajātaka</span> mentioned here is evidently not the story of the same name mentioned in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka Commentary</span> (i.327ff.). The story is given in full in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (*loc.cit.*). There the Buddha is said to have related to him stories of several past teachers, showing the evil effects of reviling others.

He may be identical with Ghosasaññaka of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (Ap.ii.451).
