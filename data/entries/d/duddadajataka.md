# Duddadajātaka

## English source

Duddadajātaka. *v.l.* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duddajātaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dudadajātaka</span> (Ja 180). Once the Bodhisatta was a Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span> and, after being educated in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span>, became an ascetic in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span>. When he and his fellow ascetics visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> for salt and seasoning, the people gathered together and gave them food. The story was told in reference to two young men who made a collection in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> to feed the Buddha and his monks (Ja.ii.85f).
