# Devadatta

## English source

Devadatta. Son of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan Suppabuddha</span> (maternal uncle of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>) and his wife <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amitā</span>. He had a sister <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddakaccānā</span>, who married Prince <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siddhattha</span>. Mhv.ii.22; Mhv­ṭ.136; Dhp­a.iii.44. The *Dulva* (Rockhill, p.13) calls him the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amitodana</span> and brother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>. This is supported by *Mahā Vastu* (Mvu.ii.69), which says that after the Buddha’s renunciation, Devadatta tried to tempt <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddakaccānā</span>.

In one passage in the Vinaya (ii.189), Devadatta is spoken of as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godhiputta</span>. Does this mean that his mother’s name was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godhī</span>? The Sanskrit books (e.g., *Mahā Vastu*) give several stories of his youth which show his malice. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siddhattha</span> was about to show his skill in the arts, a white elephant was being brought for him, and Devadatta, out of envy, killed it. The carcase blocked the city gates till <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siddhattha</span> threw it outside. The Pāli Commentaries (e.g., SN­a.i.62) say that Devadatta had the strength of five elephants. On another occasion he quarrelled with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siddhattha</span>, who protested against his shooting a goose.

When the Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> after the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span> and preached to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span>, Devadatta was converted together with his friends <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhagu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbila</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span>, and their barber, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli</span>, and he sought the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anupiyā</span> and entered the Saṅgha (Vin.ii.182). During the rainy season that followed, Devadatta acquired the power of *iddhi* possible to those who are yet of the world (*puthujjanika-iddhi*) (Vin.ii.183; for particulars see Rockhill, p.85). For some time he seems to have enjoyed great honour in the Saṅgha, and in one passage he is mentioned in a list of eleven of the chief Elders of all of whom the Buddha speaks in praise.(Ud.i.5. Again in Vin.ii.189 <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> is mentioned as having gone about <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> singing Devadatta’s praises; see also Dhp­a.i.64f). Devadatta was later suspected of evil wishes (e.g., SN.ii.156).

About eight years before the Buddha’s death, Devadatta, eager for gain and favour and jealous of the Buddha’s fame, attempted to win over <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>.

The following account is summarised from various passages in the books, chiefly Vin.ii.184ff; iii.171f; 174f; iv.71; Dhp­a.i.112ff; iii.154; AN.iii.123, 402; ii.73; iv.160; Ja.i.113, 142, 185, 490; iv.37, 158; v.333ff; vi.129f., etc.

He assumed the form of a child having a girdle of snakes, and suddenly appeared on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>’s lap, frightening him. He then resumed his own form, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, much impressed, paid him great honour and, it is said, visited him morning and evening with five hundred chariots and sent him daily five hundred dishes of food (According to Ja.i.186, 508, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> built for him a monastery at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayāsīsa</span> and sent him, daily, five hundred pots of three-year-flavoured rice and the choicest dishes. These meals were so tempting that some of the Buddha’s followers would go there to eat them and return stealthily).

This encouraged Devadatta in his schemes, and he conceived the idea of taking the Buddha’s place as leader of the Saṅgha. As soon as this thought occurred to him, his *iddhi*-power disappeared.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyan</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakudha</span>, follower of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>, reborn as a *manomayakāyikadeva*, divined Devadatta’s plan and informed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>. The latter repeated the matter to the Buddha, but the Buddha said it was unnecessary to discuss it as Devadatta would ultimately betray himself.

Sometime later, Devadatta went to the Buddha and suggested that the leadership of the Saṅgha should be handed over to him in view of the Buddha’s approaching old age. The Buddha scorned the suggestion, saying, “Not even to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> would I hand over the Saṅgha, and would I then to thee, vile one, to be vomited like spittle?” (Vin.ii.188. This incident is referred to in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayarājakumārasutta</span>, MN.i.393). Devadatta showed great resentment and vowed vengeance. Thereupon, at the Buddha’s suggestion, a proclamation was issued to the Saṅgha that in anything done by Devadatta in the name of the Buddha, the Dhamma and the Saṅgha, none but Devadatta was to be recognised.

It was at this time that Devadatta incited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> to kill his father, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, while he himself prepared to kill the Buddha (the Ap.ii.300f explains that all these plans of Devadatta to harm the Buddha were the result of the Buddha’s previous evil deeds).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> agreed, and provided Devadatta with royal archers to shoot the Buddha. These were placed on different paths, one on one path, two on another, and so on up to sixteen, and the plan was so laid that not one of them would survive to tell the tale. But when the Buddha approached the first man, he was terrified by the Buddha’s majesty, and his body became stiff. The Buddha spoke kindly to him, and the man, throwing away his weapons, confessed his intended crime. The Buddha thereupon preached to him and, having converted him, sent him back by a different path. The other groups of archers, tired of waiting, gave up the vigil and went away one after the other. The different groups were led to the Buddha by his *iddhi*-power, and he preached to them and converted them. The first man returned to Devadatta saying that he was unable to kill the Buddha because of his great *iddhi*-power.

Devadatta then decided to kill the Buddha himself. One day, when the Buddha was walking on the slopes of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span>, he hurled down on him a great rock. Two peaks sprang up from the ground, thereby arresting its rushing advance, but a splinter struck the Buddha’s foot, causing the blood to flow. Being in great pain, he was carried to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maddakucchi</span>, and from there to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvakambavana</span>, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span> attended him. After this event, the monks wished the Buddha to have a guard, but this he refused, saying that it was impossible for anyone to deprive a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tathāgata</span> of his life.

Devadatta’s next attempt on the Buddha’s life was to persuade elephant-keepers to let loose a fierce elephant, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālāgiri</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanapāla</span>), drunk with toddy, on to the road by which the Buddha would pass. The news spread rapidly, and the Buddha was warned, but refused to turn back. As the elephant advanced he pervaded it with love, and thus completely subdued it.

This outrage made Devadatta very unpopular, and even <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> was compelled by the force of public opinion to withdraw his patronage from Devadatta, whose gain and honour decreased (Vin-a.iv.811. At this time, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span> was very useful to Devadatta, Ja. ii.438). Thereupon he decided, with the help of several others, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭamorakatissa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khaṇḍadeviyāputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samuddadatta</span>, to bring about a schism in the Saṅgha. These five went accordingly to the Buddha and asked for the imposition of five rules on all members of the Saṅgha:

- (1) That monks should dwell all their lives in the forest,
- (2) That they should accept no invitations to meals, but live entirely on alms obtained by begging,
- (3) That they should wear only robes made of discarded rags and accept no robes from the laity,
- (4) That they should dwell at the foot of a tree and not under a roof,
- (5) That they should abstain completely from fish and flesh.

The Buddha’s reply was that those who felt so inclined could follow these rules – except that of sleeping under a tree during the rainy season – but he refused to make the rules obligatory. This refusal delighted Devadatta, who went about with his party, declaring that the Buddha was prone to luxury and abundance. He was believed by the foolish, and in spite of the Buddha’s warning against the dire sin of causing schism in the Saṅgha, Devadatta informed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> of his intention of holding an *uposatha* meeting without the Buddha, and, having persuaded five hundred newly ordained monks from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> to join him, he went out to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayāsīsa</span>. On this occasion he tried to imitate the Buddha, keeping two chief disciples beside him (Dhp­a.i.122). Three *Suttas*, the two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadattasutta</span>s, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāsāropamasutta</span>, were preached after this event.

Among the followers of Devadatta were also some nuns, chief of whom was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thullanandā</span>, who never tired of singing his praises (Vin.iv.66, 335). The mother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumāra Kassapa</span> (*q.v.*), also, first entered the Saṅgha under Devadatta, but when he denounced her, following the discovery of her pregnancy, she sought refuge with the Buddha. Some of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span>, too, seem to have preferred Devadatta to the Buddha – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Daṇḍapāṇī</span> (MN­a.i.298).

The Buddha sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayāsīsa</span> to bring back the deluded ones. Devadatta, believing that they had come to join him, rejoiced, and, in spite of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span>’s warning, welcomed them. That night he preached very late to the monks, and, wishing for rest, asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> to address the assembly. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> preached to such effect that they persuaded the five hundred monks to return with them. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span> kicked Devadatta on the chest to awaken him and tell him the news. When Devadatta discovered what had happened, hot blood came from his mouth, and for nine months he lay grievously ill (the Vinaya account omits the kicking, but it is mentioned in Dhp­a.i.143 and in Ja.i.491).

As his end drew near, he wished to see the Buddha, though the latter had declared that it would not be possible in this life. Devadatta, however, started the journey on a litter, but on reaching <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, he stopped the litter on the banks of the pond and stepped out to wash. The earth opened and he was swallowed up in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avīci</span>, where, after suffering for one hundred thousand *kappas*, he would be reborn as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atthissara</span> (the *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka* (chap.xi.) says he will be a Buddha named *Devarāja*). It is said that at the moment of being swallowed by the earth, Devadatta uttered a stanza in which he declared that he had no refuge other than the Buddha (Dhp­a.i.147; see also Mil.108). It is this last act of Devadatta’s which the Buddha had in view when he agreed to ordain Devadatta (he was one of five people who were swallowed by the earth in the Buddha’s time. Mil.101).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> contains a graphic account of the tortures of Devadatta in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avīci</span> (Dhp­a.i.147; also Paṭis-a.79. His body in hell is one hundred leagues long). In previous births, also, he had been swallowed by the earth, as King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalābu</span> and as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Patāpa</span>. When the people heard of Devadatta’s death, they held a great festival, as they had done of yore at the death of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṅgala</span>, who was an incarnation of Devadatta (Dhp­a.i.126f).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka Commentary</span> contains numerous stories showing that Devadatta’s enmity towards the Buddha was not confined to this life. It had existed during many *kappas*, and though sometimes he was foiled in his attempts to harm the Bodhisatta, in many cases he succeeded in working his will. The beginning of this enmity, which increased with time, is described in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Serivāṇijajātaka</span> (Ja 3).

One of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milinda</span> dilemmas (200ff) is as follows: “Why should Devadatta, who was so wicked, have been, time after time, superior in power to the Bodhisatta?” A list of such instances is given. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgasena</span>’s reply is that Devadatta did several good deeds, such as protecting the poor, building bridges, etc.
