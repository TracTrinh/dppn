# Devadatta

## English source

Devadatta. Son of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan Suppabuddha</span> (maternal uncle of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>) and his wife <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amitā</span>. He had a sister <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddakaccānā</span>, who married Prince <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siddhattha</span>. Mhv.ii.22; Mhv­ṭ.136; Dhp­a.iii.44. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Dulva</span> (Rockhill, p.13) calls him the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amitodana</span> and brother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>. This is supported by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Vastu</span> (Mvu.ii.69), which says that after the Buddha’s renunciation, Devadatta tried to tempt <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddakaccānā</span>.

In one passage in the Vinaya (ii.189), Devadatta is spoken of as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godhiputta</span>. Does this mean that his mother’s name was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godhī</span>? The Sanskrit books (e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Vastu</span>) give several stories of his youth which show his malice. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siddhattha</span> was about to show his skill in the arts, a white elephant was being brought for him, and Devadatta, out of envy, killed it. The carcase blocked the city gates till <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siddhattha</span> threw it outside. The Pāli Commentaries (e.g., SN­a.i.62) say that Devadatta had the strength of five elephants. On another occasion he quarrelled with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siddhattha</span>, who protested against his shooting a goose.

When the Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> after the Awakening and preached to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span>, Devadatta was converted together with his friends <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhagu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimbila</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span>, and their barber, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāli</span>, and he sought the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anupiyā</span> and entered the Saṅgha (Vin.ii.182). During the rainy season that followed, Devadatta acquired the power of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span> possible to those who are yet of the world (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">puthujjanika-iddhi</span>) (Vin.ii.183; for particulars see Rockhill, p.85). For some time he seems to have enjoyed great honour in the Saṅgha, and in one passage he is mentioned in a list of eleven of the chief Elders of all of whom the Buddha speaks in praise.(Ud.i.5. Again in Vin.ii.189 <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> is mentioned as having gone about <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> singing Devadatta’s praises; see also Dhp­a.i.64f). Devadatta was later suspected of evil wishes (e.g., SN.ii.156).

About eight years before the Buddha’s death, Devadatta, eager for gain and favour and jealous of the Buddha’s fame, attempted to win over <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>.

The following account is summarised from various passages in the books, chiefly Vin.ii.184ff; iii.171f; 174f; iv.71; Dhp­a.i.112ff; iii.154; AN.iii.123, 402; ii.73; iv.160; Ja.i.113, 142, 185, 490; iv.37, 158; v.333ff; vi.129f., etc.

He assumed the form of a child having a girdle of snakes, and suddenly appeared on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>’s lap, frightening him. He then resumed his own form, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, much impressed, paid him great honour and, it is said, visited him morning and evening with five hundred chariots and sent him daily five hundred dishes of food (According to Ja.i.186, 508, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> built for him a monastery at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayāsīsa</span> and sent him, daily, five hundred pots of three-year-flavoured rice and the choicest dishes. These meals were so tempting that some of the Buddha’s followers would go there to eat them and return stealthily).

This encouraged Devadatta in his schemes, and he conceived the idea of taking the Buddha’s place as leader of the Saṅgha. As soon as this thought occurred to him, his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span>-power disappeared.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyan</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakudha</span>, follower of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>, reborn as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t18">manomayakāyikadeva</span>, divined Devadatta’s plan and informed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>. The latter repeated the matter to the Buddha, but the Buddha said it was unnecessary to discuss it as Devadatta would ultimately betray himself.

Sometime later, Devadatta went to the Buddha and suggested that the leadership of the Saṅgha should be handed over to him in view of the Buddha’s approaching old age. The Buddha scorned the suggestion, saying, “Not even to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> would I hand over the Saṅgha, and would I then to thee, vile one, to be vomited like spittle?” (Vin.ii.188. This incident is referred to in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayarājakumārasutta</span>, MN.i.393). Devadatta showed great resentment and vowed vengeance. Thereupon, at the Buddha’s suggestion, a proclamation was issued to the Saṅgha that in anything done by Devadatta in the name of the Buddha, the Dhamma and the Saṅgha, none but Devadatta was to be recognised.

It was at this time that Devadatta incited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> to kill his father, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, while he himself prepared to kill the Buddha (the Ap.ii.300f explains that all these plans of Devadatta to harm the Buddha were the result of the Buddha’s previous evil deeds).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> agreed, and provided Devadatta with royal archers to shoot the Buddha. These were placed on different paths, one on one path, two on another, and so on up to sixteen, and the plan was so laid that not one of them would survive to tell the tale. But when the Buddha approached the first man, he was terrified by the Buddha’s majesty, and his body became stiff. The Buddha spoke kindly to him, and the man, throwing away his weapons, confessed his intended crime. The Buddha thereupon preached to him and, having converted him, sent him back by a different path. The other groups of archers, tired of waiting, gave up the vigil and went away one after the other. The different groups were led to the Buddha by his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span>-power, and he preached to them and converted them. The first man returned to Devadatta saying that he was unable to kill the Buddha because of his great <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span>-power.

Devadatta then decided to kill the Buddha himself. One day, when the Buddha was walking on the slopes of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span>, he hurled down on him a great rock. Two peaks sprang up from the ground, thereby arresting its rushing advance, but a splinter struck the Buddha’s foot, causing the blood to flow. Being in great pain, he was carried to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maddakucchi</span>, and from there to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvakambavana</span>, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span> attended him. After this event, the monks wished the Buddha to have a guard, but this he refused, saying that it was impossible for anyone to deprive a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tathāgata</span> of his life.

Devadatta’s next attempt on the Buddha’s life was to persuade elephant-keepers to let loose a fierce elephant, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālāgiri</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanapāla</span>), drunk with toddy, on to the road by which the Buddha would pass. The news spread rapidly, and the Buddha was warned, but refused to turn back. As the elephant advanced he pervaded it with love, and thus completely subdued it.

This outrage made Devadatta very unpopular, and even <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> was compelled by the force of public opinion to withdraw his patronage from Devadatta, whose gain and honour decreased (Vin-a.iv.811. At this time, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span> was very useful to Devadatta, Ja. ii.438). Thereupon he decided, with the help of several others, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭamorakatissa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khaṇḍadeviyāputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samuddadatta</span>, to bring about a schism in the Saṅgha.
