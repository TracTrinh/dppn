# Devā 01

## English source

Devā 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Deva</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>. A class of beings. As a title the word *Deva* is attributed to any being regarded, in certain respects, as being above the human level. Thus it is used for a king. In a late classification (CNid.307; Khp­a.123, etc.) there are three kinds of Devā: *sammutidevā* (conventional gods – e.g., kings and princes); *visuddhidevā* (beings who are divine by the purity of their great religious merit – *Arahants* and Buddhas); and *uppattidevā* (beings who are born divine). Under the third category various groups are enumerated, the commonest number being seven: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cātummahārājikā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yāmā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusitā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nimmānaratī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paranimmitavasavattī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmakāyikā</span> (e.g., DN.i.216; AN.i.210, etc.). The longest list is that of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhimanikāya</span> (i.289; iii.100. The *Divyāvadāna* p.266 contains a list of twenty-two), which contains the names of twenty-five groups. The popular etymology of the word connects it with the root *div* in the sense of playing, sporting, or amusing oneself, sometimes also of shining: *dibbantī ti devā, pañcahi kāmaguṇehi kīlanti, attano vā siriyā jotantī ti attho* (Khp­a.123). The word implies possession of splendour and power of moving at will, beauty, goodness and effulgence of body, and, as such, is opposed to the dark powers of mischief and destruction – such as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span>, *Petas* and *Nerayikas*. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> are generally regarded as sharing kinship and continuity of life with humans; all <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> have been men and may again be born among men. They take interest in the doings of men, especially the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cātummahārājikā</span> and the gods of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>. They come to earth to worship the Buddha and to show reverence to good men. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> (*q.v.*) is usually spoken of as chief of the gods – *devānaṁ indo*. All <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> are themselves in *Saṁsāra*, needing salvation. They are subject to death, their life-spans varying according to the merit of each individual *Deva*. They are born in the full flower of youth and are free from illness till the moment of their death. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> die from one of the following causes: exhaustion of life, merit or food; failing, through forgetfulness to eat; and jealousy at the glory of another, which leads to anger (Dhp­a.l.173. For other particulars regarding <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> see the article in the PTSPED). When a *Deva* is about to die five signs appear on him: his clothes get soiled, flowers worn by him fade, sweat exudes from his armpits, his body loses its colour and he becomes restless on his seat. DN­a.ii.427f; Dhs-a, 33, etc.
