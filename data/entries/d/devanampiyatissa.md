# Devānampiyatissa

## English source

Devānampiyatissa. King of Ceylon (247-207 B.C.). He was the second son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Muṭasīva</span>. It is said that on the day of his coronation many wonderful treasures miraculously appeared, some of which he resolved to send as tokens of esteem to his contemporary <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammāsoka</span> of India, with whom he had long been on terms of friendship. An embassy, led by his nephew <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāriṭṭha</span>, was despatched to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span>, and the emperor showed the ambassadors every mark of honour. He sent back with them all the requisites for a coronation, with instructions to celebrate the inauguration of the Sinhalese king, whom he invited to embrace Buddhism. On the return of the embassy, the king was solemnly crowned a second time. This confirmation of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span>’s sovereignty under the aegis of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> may have been due either to the commanding position of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> or for the strengthening of family connections. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moriyan</span> (a branch of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span>) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span> had <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> blood.

The chief event in the reign of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span> was the arrival of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> in Ceylon. He arrived at the head of a mission in the year of the king’s second coronation. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> met the king hunting on the full-moon day of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Jeṭṭha</span>. The king welcomed him with great honour and speedily embraced the new religion, to which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> had already drawn his attention.

His conversion was the direct result of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span>’s preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Hatthipadopamasutta</span>. His earlier religion is not known, it may have been Jainism. His example was followed by a large number of his subjects, many of whom entered the Saṅgha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span> dedicated to their use the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandana</span> park and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Meghavana</span>, which he himself had laid out a little earlier. In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Meghavana</span> he built the famous <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> which, for many centuries, remained the centre of the orthodox religion in Ceylon. The dedication of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> took place in the 236th year after the death of the Buddha. The king’s next pious work was the erection of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyapabbata</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> and he, later, built the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thūpārāma</span>, containing the Buddha’s right collar-bone.

When the women of the palace, led by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anulā</span>, wife of the sub-king, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāga</span>, expressed a desire to become nuns, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span> sent another embassy to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> asking him to send <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghamittā</span>, together with the right branch of the sacred Bodhi-tree. This branch miraculously severed itself from the parent tree and, together with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghamittā</span> and eleven others, was conveyed down the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span> and arrived in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambukola</span>, where it was received with all honour by Devānampiyatissa. From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambukola</span> it was taken in procession to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>, where it was planted in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Meghavana</span>, the king instituting in its honour a festival, which was observed for many centuries. For the use of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghamittā</span> and the nuns the king erected various buildings, the chief of which was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthāḷhakavihāra</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upāsikāvihāra</span> with its twelve mansions (this account is summarised from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsa</span> (chaps.xi., xiii.–xx.); also Dpv.xi.14ff; xii.7; xvii.92).

Among other works of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span> we are told of the building of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Issarasamaṇavihāra</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessagiri</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāras</span>, the refectory called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Pāli</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambukolavihāra</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadīpa</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa Mahā Vihāra</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pācīnārāma</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭhamathūpa</span>. He also built the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissavāpi</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> (the Cūḷavaṁsa (xxxvii.94) mentions also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammacakka</span> as having been built by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span>. It later became the Temple of the Tooth at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> survived him by eight years. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span> seems to have died without issue, for he was succeeded by four of his brothers.
