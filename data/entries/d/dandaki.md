# Daṇḍakī

## English source

Daṇḍakī. King of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumbhavatī</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaliṅga</span> (MN.i.378; MN­a.ii.599ff; Ja.iii.463; v.133ff; 267; Mvu.iii.363ff).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kisavaccha</span>, pupil of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhaṅga</span>, desiring solitude, lived in the royal park near the city, and was ill-treated by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Daṇḍakī</span> and his army while on their way to quell a rebellion, they being under the impression that insult inflicted on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kisavaccha</span> would bring them luck.

As a result the gods were greatly incensed and destroyed the king and his country, only three people escaping death: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kisavaccha</span>, the commander-in-chief, who was a pious follower of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kisavaccha</span>, and a man named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāma</span>, who had come from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumbhavatī</span>. The last named was saved from destruction owing to his care for his parents. The forest which grew on the desolate land came to be called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Daṇḍakārañña</span>.
