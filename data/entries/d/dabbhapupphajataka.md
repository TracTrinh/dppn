# Dabbhapupphajātaka

## English source

Dabbhapupphajātaka (Ja 400). There was once a jackal called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māyāvī</span>. His wife had a longing to eat fresh fish, and while he was searching for it he saw two otters, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anutīracārī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gambhīracārī</span>, disputing as to the division of a *rohita* fish which they had caught between them. They appealed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māyāvī</span> to arbitrate, and he gave one the head, the other the tail, while he kept the middle portion for himself!

The story was related in reference to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upananda</span>, who is identified with the jackal. Two old monks had received as a gift two coarse cloaks and one fine blanket, and they appealed to him to divide the gifts. He gave them each a cloak and kept the blanket for himself. Ja.iii.332ff; the story is quoted at Dhp­a.iii.139ff.
