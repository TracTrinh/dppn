# Dakkhiṇāgiri

## English source

Dakkhiṇāgiri (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">v.l.</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇagiri</span>). A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">janapada</span> (district) in India, the capital of which was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>, and over which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> ruled as Viceroy. It also contained the city of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vedisā</span> (Vin-a.i.70; Mhv.xiii.5).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇāgiri</span> lay to the south of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, beyond the hills that surrounded the city – hence its name (Snp­a.i.136; MN­a.ii.795; SN­a.i.188). In the district was the Brahmin village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekanāḷā</span> (Snp., p.13). The road from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> lay through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇāgiri</span>, and the Buddha traversed it in the course of his periodical tours through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, residing in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇāgiri</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekanāḷā</span> (SN.i.172; SN­a.ii.133; Vin.i.80). It was during one of these tours that he converted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kasī Bhāradvāja</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammasava</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) and his father. On another of these occasions the Buddha saw the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadhakhetta</span>, which gave him the idea of designing the robe of a monk to resemble a field (Vin.i.287). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> is also said to have travelled through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇāgiri</span>, gathering a large number of young men into the Saṅgha, who, however, do not appear to have been very serious in their intentions, as their behaviour earned for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> the censure of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> (SN.ii.217f). Later, we find <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇa</span> with a large following in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇāgiri</span> refusing to join in the findings of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> Council, and preferring to follow the Dhamma according to his own lights (Vin.ii.289).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇāgiri</span> was the residence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandamātā</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷukaṇṭakī</span> and she was visited both by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> during a tour in the district (AN.iv.64). In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇāgiri</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> heard of the lack of zeal of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhānañjāni</span> (MN.ii.185; see Ja.i.224 for another incident connected with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>’s tour). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Arāmadūsajātaka</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) was preached in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇāgiri</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇāgiri</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> was, for a long time, a great monastic centre, and at the foundation of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> there were present from there forty thousand monks led by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Saṅgharakkhita</span>. Mhv.xxix.35.
