# Māṇābharaṇa 02

## English source

Māṇābharaṇa 02. Son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirivallabha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sugalā</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Līlāvatī</span> was his sister (Cv.lxii.2). He married <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mittā</span>, daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māṇābharaṇa I</span>., and also her sister, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pabhāvatī</span>, and by the latter he had a son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kittisirimegha</span> (Cv.lxiv. 19, 23, 24). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māṇābharaṇa</span> reigned in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohaṇa</span> as an independent king (Cv.lxvii.95). When the throne was captured by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māṇābharaṇa</span> tried several times to wrest it from him, but, failing in these attempts, made an alliance with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhu</span> through the intervention of the monks; later, however, finding <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu</span> growing in power, he went over to him (Cv.lxx.179ff). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhu</span> was captured and detained at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulatthinagara</span>, the soldiers started to pillage the city, despite the orders of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu</span>. The people were enraged and invited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māṇābharaṇa</span> to come. On his arrival at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulatthipura</span>, he captured <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhu</span> and threw him into a dungeon, seized all the treasures, including the Tooth Relic and Alms bowl, and took counsel with his mother to kill <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhu</span>. On hearing of this, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu</span> sent his forces against <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māṇābharaṇa</span> and defeated the latter’s followers at various places. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māṇābharaṇa</span> then fled to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohaṇa</span>, taking with him some of the treasures (Cv.lxx.255ff). From there he again tried to ally himself with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajabāhu</span>; but the latter did not so desire, though his ministers were in favor of it. Relying on their support, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māṇābharaṇa</span> advanced from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohaṇa</span>. He was, however, severely defeated at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūnagāma</span> and other places and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu</span>’s forces assailed him from all sides. The campaign brought varying success to the opposing armies, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māṇābharaṇa</span> proved a skilful warrior. He was helped by various chieftains and fought bitterly and valiantly to the end (for details see Cv.lxxii.148-309), but, as he lay dying, he summoned his children and ministers and counseled them to join <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu</span>. Even after his death his queen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sugalā</span> encouraged intrigues against <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu</span>. Cv.lxxiv.29ff.
