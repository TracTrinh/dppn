# Mahā Samayasutta 01

## English source

Mahā Samayasutta 01. Preached at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vana</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> was staying with five hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span>. The gods of the ten thousand world systems had come to visit the Buddha and the monks, and were joined by four gods from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhāvāsā</span>, who saluted the Buddha in verse. The Buddha then addressed the monks and recited this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> (DN.ii.253-62). It is possible to divide the discourse into three parts.

The first contains a list of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> and other beings present to worship the Buddha. It is a long list of strange names given in verses, mostly doggerel. Many of the beings mentioned are to us now mere names, with no special information attached. Most of them were probably local deities, the personification of natural phenomena, guardian spirits, fairies, harpies, naiads, dryads, and many others, who are here represented as adherents of the Buddha, come to do him honour. It is noteworthy that even the most important gods – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Varuṇa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahma</span> – are only incidentally mentioned, added to the list, as it were, without special distinction. A similar list of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> is found in the Mahā Vastu (Mvu.i.245; iii.68, 77); the addition of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siva</span> to this list is significant. The list of gods given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Samaya</span> should be compared with that given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āṭānāṭiyasutta</span>.

The second part of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> is the framework of the words attributed to the Buddha, introducing the list of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, giving the Buddha’s warning to the monks to beware of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>’s declaration that he had no power over them as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span> are free from fear.

The third part of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> may be called the prologue, the verses of greeting spoken by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhāvāsā</span> (Rhys Davids, Dial.ii.282f). This prologue is elsewhere preserved as a separate episode (SN.i.27).

The Commentaries give long accounts of the preaching of the Mahā Samaya (e.g., Snp­a. 357ff.; DN­a.ii.672ff). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span> mentioned are <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyan</span> youths, representatives of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyan</span> families, sent to join the Saṅgha as a sign of gratitude to the Buddha for having averted the quarrel between the two families regarding the water of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohiṇī</span>. On that occasion the Buddha had preached to the opposing armies the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Attadaṇḍasutta</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phandana</span> (Ja 475), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Laṭukika</span> (Ja 357) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṭṭakajātakas</span> (Ja 35) (DN­a.ii.674 adds the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭhavi-uddriyana</span> (Ja 322) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rukkhadhammajātakas</span> (Ja 74)). He then related the story of their origin, showing that both families were descended from a common stock. When the quarrel was thus settled, two hundred and fifty young men from each family entered the Saṅgha and the Buddha dwelt with them in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vana</span>. But the wives of the men tried to entice them back; the Buddha, therefore, took them to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span>, where he preached to them the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇālajātaka</span> (Ja 536) on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṇāla</span> Lake. At the conclusion of the sermon they attained to various fruits of the Path, from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmīphala</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpatti</span>. He then returned with them to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vana</span>, where they developed insight and became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span>. They assembled to pay homage to their teacher on the evening of the full moon day of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Jeṭṭhamāsa</span> (in June) and to the assembly came the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> of the ten thousand world systems. The Buddha told the monks the names of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> present (as given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Samaya</span>), and, surveying the assembly, saw that it consisted of two kinds of beings, one capable of benefiting by his teaching (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t18">bhabbā</span>), and the other not so capable (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t18">abhabbā</span>). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t18">bhabbā</span>, he saw, could be divided into six groups – the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t18">rāgacaritā</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dosacaritā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t18">mohacaritā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vitakkacaritā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t18">saddhācaritā</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t18">buddhicaritā</span>, according to temperament. To these, respectively, he preached six <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span>, calculated to benefit each separate class, and, in order that each <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> might take the form of question and answer, he created a Buddha form to ask questions, while he himself answered them. The six <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span>, so preached, were the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Purābheda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalahavivāda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Vyūha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vyūha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tuvaṭaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sammāparibbājaniya</span>. Countless numbers of beings realized the Truth (AN­a.i.173, 320; Mil. 20, 350; Snp­a.i.174).

The preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Samayasutta</span> was among the incidents of the Buddha’s life sculptured in the Relic Chamber of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span>. Mhv.xxx.83.
