# Moggaliputtatissa Thera

## English source

Moggaliputtatissa Thera. President of the Third Council. In his penultimate birth he was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span>, called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span>, and consented to be born in the world of men at the urgent request of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span> who held the Second Council, in order to prevent the downfall of the Buddha’s religion. He was born in the home of the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggali</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siggava</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍavajji</span> had been entrusted with the task of converting him. From the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span>’s birth, therefore, for seven years, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siggava</span> went daily to the house of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggali</span>, but not even one word of welcome did he receive. In the eighth year someone said to him, “Go further on.” As he went out he met <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggali</span>, and, on being asked whether he had received anything at his house, he said he had. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggali</span> inquired at home and the next day charged <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siggava</span> with lying. But hearing <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siggava</span>’s explanation, he was greatly pleased and thereafter constantly offered <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siggava</span> hospitality at his house. One day, young <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span>, who was thoroughly proficient in the Vedas, was much annoyed at finding <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siggava</span> occupying his seat and spoke to him harshly. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siggava</span> started to talk to him and asked him a question from the Cittayamaka. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> could not answer it, and, in order to learn the Buddha’s teachings, he entered the Saṅgha under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siggava</span>, becoming a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span> soon after. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siggava</span> instructed him in the Vinaya and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍavajji</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttapiṭaka</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhammapiṭaka</span>. In due course he attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship and became the acknowledged leader of the monks at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span> (Mhv.v.95ff., 131ff.; Dpv.v.55ff.; Vin-a.i.35-41).

At the festival of dedication of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asokārāma</span> and the other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāras</span> built by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggaliputtatissa</span> informed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>, in answer to a question, that one becomes a kinsman of the Buddha’s religion only by letting one’s son or daughter enter the Saṅgha. Acting on this suggestion, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> had both his children ordained. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggaliputta</span> acted as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span>’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t18">upajjhāya</span> (Mhv.v.191ff.; Vin-a.i.50f). Later, because of the great gains which accrued to the monks through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s patronage of the Buddha’s religion, the Saṅgha became corrupt, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggaliputta</span> committed the monks to the charge of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span>, and, for seven years, lived in solitary retreat on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ahogaṅgā</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pabbata</span>. From there <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> sent for him to solve his doubts as to what measure of sin belonged to him owing to the murder of the monks by his minister. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggaliputta</span> would not come until persuaded that his services were needed to befriend the religion. He traveled by boat to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span>, and was met at the landing place by the king who helped him out by supporting him on his arm.

According to Vin-a.i.58, the king had a dream on the preceding night which the soothsayers interpreted to mean that a great ascetic (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t18">samaṇanāga</span>) would touch his right hand. As the Thera touched the king’s hand the attendants were about to kill him, for to do this was a crime punishable by death. The king, however, restrained them. The Thera took the king’s hand as a sign that he accepted him as pupil.

The king, having led him to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rativaḍḍhana</span> Park, begged him to perform a miracle. This the Thera consented to do and made the earth quake in a single region. To convince the king that the murder of the monks involved no guilt for himself, the Thera preached to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tittirajātaka</span>. Within a week, with the aid of two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span>, the king had all the monks gathered together and held an assembly at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asokārāma</span>. In the presence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggaliputta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> questioned the monks on their various doctrines, and all those holding heretical views were expelled from the Saṅgha, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggaliputta</span> decreeing that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vibhajjavāda</span> alone contained the teaching of the Buddha. Later, in association with one thousand <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggaliputta</span> held the Third Council at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asokārāma</span>, with himself as President, and compiled the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kathāvatthu</span>, in refutation of false views. This was in the seventeenth year of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s reign and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggaliputta</span> was seventy-two years old (Mhv.v.231-81; Dpv.vii.16ff., 39ff.; Vin-a.i.57ff). At the conclusion of the Council in nine months, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggaliputta</span> made arrangements, in the month of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kattika</span>, for monks to go to the countries adjacent to India for the propagation of the religion (for a list of these, see Mhv.xii.1ff.; Dpv.viii.1ff.; Vin-a.i.63ff). Later, when the request came from Ceylon for a branch of the Bodhi-tree, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> consulted the Elder as to how this could be carried out, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggaliputta</span> told him of the five resolves made by the Buddha on his death bed (Mhv.xviii.21ff).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīpavaṁsa</span> says (Dpv. 68ff., 82, 94, 96, 100f. 107f.; also vii.23ff) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggaliputtatissa</span>’s ordination was in the second year of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candagutta</span>’s reign, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siggava</span> was sixty-four years old. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candagutta</span> reigned for twenty-four years, and was followed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Biṇḍusāra</span>, who reigned for thirty-seven years, and he was succeeded by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>. In the sixth year of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s reign, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggaliputta</span> was sixty-six years old, and it was then that he ordained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span>. He was eighty years old at the time of his death and had been leader of the Saṅgha for sixty-eight years. He died in the twenty-sixth year of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s reign.
