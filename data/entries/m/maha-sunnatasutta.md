# Mahā Suññatāsutta

## English source

Mahā Suññatāsutta. Preached at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>. The Buddha was staying in the cell erected by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷa Khemaka</span>, and near by, in the cell erected by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭāya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, with many monks, was making robes. The Buddha knew from the number of pallets outside the cells of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷa Khemaka</span> that many monks were in residence there. He therefore addressed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, telling him of the joys of tranquility and solitude. A monk should dwell apart and concentrate his heart internally and thus develop the four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jhānas</span>. He then knows that, whatever his posture, he will be free from evil dispositions; his speech will be free from faults, his thoughts pure. A monk should always search his heart to discover if he has any traffic with pleasures of sense. A disciple should follow his master’s footsteps, not in order to obtain interpretations of canonical law, but solely to hear words which conduce to passionlessness, illumination, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nibbāna</span> (MN.iii.109-18).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> is also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṇabheda</span>, because it tends to break up crowds. Once, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vālikapiṭṭhivihāra</span>, Ābhidhammika <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span> recited this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> with several others, and, understanding its import, dwelt apart and attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship during the rains. MN­a.ii.907.
