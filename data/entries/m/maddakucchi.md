# Maddakucchi

## English source

Maddakucchi. A park near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, at the foot of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span>. It was a preserve (*migadāya*) where deer and game could dwell in safety. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>, wishing to kill the Buddha, hurled a rock down <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span>, it was stopped midway by another rock, but a splinter from it fell on the Buddha’s foot, wounding it severely. As the Buddha suffered much from loss of blood, the monks took him on a litter to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maddakucchi</span>, and from there to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span> *ambavana*, where he was treated by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span> (Vin.ii.193f.; Dhp­a.ii.164ff.; Ja.iv.430; Mil.179). It is said (SN.i.27f) that seven hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Satullapa</span> group visited the Buddha there and told him of their great admiration for his qualities. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> tried to stir up discontent in the Buddha, but had to retire discomfited (SN.i.110; this visit of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> is referred to at DN.ii.116).

According to the Commentaries (e.g., SN.A.i.61; cp. Ja.iii.121f), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maddakucchi</span> was so-called because it was there that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>’s queen, mother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, tried to bring about an abortion when she was told by soothsayers that the child in her womb was destined to bring about <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>’s death. She went into the park unknown to the king and violently massaged her womb, but without success. The king heard of this and forbade her to visit the park.

Once when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kappina</span> was at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maddakucchi</span>, doubts arose in his mind as to the necessity of joining the assembly of monks for the holding of *uposatha*, he himself being pure. The Buddha read his thoughts, appeared before him, and urged upon him the necessity of so doing (Vin.i.105).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maddakucchi</span> was difficult of access; monks who came from afar late at night, wishing to put <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba Mallaputta</span>’s powers to the test, would often ask him to provide lodging there for them. Vin.ii.76; iii.159.
