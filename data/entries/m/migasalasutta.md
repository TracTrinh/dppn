# Migasālāsutta

## English source

Migasālāsutta. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> visits <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migasālā</span> in her home and is questioned as to why both <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūraṇa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span>, the former of whom was a *brahmacārī* and the latter not, should both have been born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span>, as *Sakadāgāmīs*. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> offers no explanation, but consults the Buddha, who declares that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migasālā</span> is but a foolish, frail, motherly body with none but mother wit; how, then, could she understand the diversity in the person of man (*purisapuggalaparopariyañāṇa*)? The Buddha then goes on to divide men into six classes according to their capabilities and attainments. It is not possible for anyone, save a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tathāgata</span>, to measure persons. AN.iii.347ff.; v. 137ff.
