# Mahā Saṅghikā

## English source

Mahā Saṅghikā, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sangītikā</span>. One of the Buddhist schools which separated out from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theravādins</span> at the Second Council. The members rejected the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parivāra</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭisambhidāmagga</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Niddesa</span> and some portions of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātakas</span> (Kv-a.p.4; Dpv.v.32ff).

The school was so-called owing to the great number of its followers, which made a great assembly or “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Sangīti</span>.” They were counted among the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anātmavādīs</span>, and later gave rise to the following schools: the Mahā Saṅghika, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pubbasela</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparasela</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagiriyā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemavatas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyavādins</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkantivādins</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gokulikas</span>. Originally they had only two divisions the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekabbohārikas</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gokulikas</span> (Rockhill, op.cit., 182ff).

Their separation from the orthodox school was brought about by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjiputta</span> monks, and was probably due to difference of opinion on the ten points (for these see Vin.ii.294f) held by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjiputta</span> monks.
