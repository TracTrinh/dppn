# Mahā Saṅghikā

## English source

Mahā Saṅghikā, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sangītikā</span>. One of the Buddhist schools which separated out from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theravādins</span> at the Second Council. The members rejected the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parivāra</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭisambhidāmagga</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Niddesa</span> and some portions of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātakas</span> (Kv-a.p.4; Dpv.v.32ff).

The school was so-called owing to the great number of its followers, which made a great assembly or “*Mahā Sangīti*.” They were counted among the *Anātmavādīs*, and later gave rise to the following schools: the Mahā Saṅghika, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pubbasela</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparasela</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagiriyā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemavatas</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyavādins</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkantivādins</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gokulikas</span>. Originally they had only two divisions the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekabbohārikas</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gokulikas</span> (Rockhill, op.cit., 182ff).

Their separation from the orthodox school was brought about by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjiputta</span> monks, and was probably due to difference of opinion on the ten points (for these see Vin.ii.294f) held by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjiputta</span> monks. According to Northern sources, however, the split occurred on the five points raised by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Deva</span>:

- (1) An <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Arahant</span> may commit a sin under unconscious temptation;
- (2) one may be an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Arahant</span> and unconscious of the fact;
- (3) an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Arahant</span> may have doubts on matters of doctrine;
- (4) one cannot attain <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t23">Arahant</span>-ship without the help of a teacher;
- (5) the “Noble Way” may begin with some such exclamation as “How sad!” uttered during meditation (JRAS. 1910, p. 416; cf. Mhv­ṭ 173).

These articles of faith are found in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kathāvatthu</span>, attributed to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pubbaselas</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparaselas</span>, opponents of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Saṅghika</span> school (173ff., 187ff., 194, 197).

According to Xuanzang (Beal-Xuan.ii.164), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Saṅghikas</span> divided their canon into five parts: *Sūtra*, *Vinaya*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span>, *Miscellaneous* and *Dhāraṇī*. Faxian took from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span> to China a complete transcript of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Saṅghika</span> Vinaya. (Giles, p. 64, Nañjio’s Catalogue mentions a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Saṅghika</span> Vinaya and a Mahā Saṅgha Bhikṣuṇī Vinaya in Chinese translations, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Coḷa</span>. 247, 253. Ms. No.543).

The best known work of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Saṅghikas</span> is the Mahā Vastu. Their headquarters in Ceylon were in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagirivihāra</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sena I</span>. is said to have built the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vīraṅkurārāma</span> for their use. Cv.1.68.
