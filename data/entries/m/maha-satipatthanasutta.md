# Mahā Satipaṭṭhānasutta

## English source

Mahā Satipaṭṭhānasutta. Preached at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kammāssadamma</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuru</span> country. The Buddha tells the monks that the one and only path leading to *Nibbāna* is that of the Four Bases of Mindfulness. These, in brief, are the four ways of directing the mind to the impurities and the impermanency of body: (1) *kāya*, physical structure and activities; (2) *vedanā*, the emotional nature, first as bare feeling, then as having ethical implications; (3) *citta*, conscious life or intelligence, considered under ethical aspects; (4) *Dhamma*, considered under the five hindrances; the five groups (*khandhas*), the six spheres of sense, the seven *bojjhaṅgas*, and the four Ariyan Truths. DN.ii.290-315.

The *Sutta* is considered as one of the most important in the Buddhist Canon. It has been translated into various languages, and several commentaries on it are in existence. Its mere recital is said to ward off dangers and to bring happiness, and it is the desire of every Buddhist that he shall die with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Satipaṭṭhānasutta</span> on his lips, or, at least, with the sound of it in his ears.

The materials found in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Satipaṭṭhānasutta</span> are found also in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhimanikāya</span>, broken up into two portions, each representing a separate discourse – the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Satipaṭṭhānasutta</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saccavibhaṅgasutta</span>.
