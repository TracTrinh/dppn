# Mantidatta Thera

## English source

Mantidatta Thera. He was once an officer of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, but later left the world. A conversation between him and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanuggahatissa</span> it was which, overheard by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>’s spies, led to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> changing his tactics and defeating <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>. Ja.iv.343; but see Ja.ii.403, where Dhanuggatissa’s companion is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Utta</span>, which is probably a wrong reading of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Datta</span>.

In the conversation <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mantidatta</span> is addressed as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Datta</span>, which was evidently his personal name, the prefix <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manti</span> being given him as the king’s minister and to distinguish him from other Dattas.
