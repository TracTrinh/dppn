# Mattakuṇḍali

## English source

Mattakuṇḍali, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Matthakuṇḍali</span>. The only son of the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Adinnapubbaka</span>. His father loved him dearly, but was a great miser, and made for him, with his own hands, a pair of burnished ear rings in order to save the goldsmith’s fee; the boy thus came to be called Mattakuṇḍali (burnished ear rings). When he was sixteen he had an attack of jaundice. His father refused to call in a physician, and prescribed for him himself till the boy was beyond all cure. Then he carried him out and laid him on the terrace, lest those who came to prepare for the funeral should see his wealth.

The Buddha saw <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mattakuṇḍali</span> as he lay dying, and, out of compassion, came to the door of his father’s house. Too weak to do anything else, the boy conjured up devout faith in the Buddha; he died soon after, and was born among the gods in a golden mansion thirty leagues in extent. When he surveyed his past birth, he saw his father in the charnel ground, weeping and lamenting and preparing to cremate his body. Assuming the form of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mattakuṇḍali</span>, he went himself to the charnel ground and, standing near, started to weep. When questioned by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Adinnapubbaka</span>, he said he wanted the moon, and in the course of conversation he revealed his identity and censured his father. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Adinnapubbaka</span> invited the Buddha to a meal the next day, and, when it was over, asked if it were possible to attain to heaven by a mere act of faith. In order to convince him, the Buddha made <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mattakuṇḍali</span> appear before him and confirm his statement that this was so. At the conclusion of the Buddha’s sermon, both <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Adinnapubbaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mattakuṇḍali</span> became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpannas</span>, and eighty thousand persons realized the Truth (Mil.350).

Dhp­a.i.20ff.; Vv.vii.9; Vv-a.322ff.; Pv.ii.5; Pv-a.92; the stanzas found in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mattakuṇḍali</span>’s story occur also in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mattakuṇḍalijātaka</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), but the introductory story is quite different.
