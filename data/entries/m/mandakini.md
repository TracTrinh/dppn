# Mandākinī

## English source

Mandākinī. One of the seven great lakes of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span>. Their names are given at Ja.v.415; AN.iv.101; Snp­a.ii.407; DN­a.i.164; Ud­a.300; AN­a.ii.759. At Vsm.416, the name <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tiyaggalā</span> is substituted for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mandākinī</span>.

It is in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chaddanta</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vana</span> and is fifty leagues in extent, of which twenty-five leagues is of crystal water, free from moss or weeds. For the next twenty-five leagues, the water is but waist deep and is covered with white lotus, spreading for half a league around the lake; beyond that are red lotus, red lilies, etc., rice fields, fruit trees, a grove of sugar cane – each cane being as big as a palm tree – banana, jak, mango, rose-apple, etc.

On the bank of the lake is a spot where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>’s generally live; but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aññā Koṇḍañña</span> lived there for twelve years attended by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chaddanta</span>, the elephant and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadatta</span>, a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Devaputta</span>. They ministered to all his needs, and he only left there to take leave of the Buddha before his death. He then returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mandākinī</span>, where he died and was cremated, his relics being later deposited at the gateway of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span>, where a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span> was erected over them. SN­a.i.217ff.; but see Thag­a.ii.3, where he is said to have lived on the bank of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chaddanta</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">daha</span>; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mandākinī</span> may have been another name for the same lake.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mandākinī</span> Lake never grows hot and dries up only at the end of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappa</span>. Snp­a.ii.407.
