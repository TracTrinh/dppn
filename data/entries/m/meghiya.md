# Meghiya

## English source

Meghiya. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meghiya Thera</span>. He belonged to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, and having joined the Saṅgha, was for some time the personal attendant of the Buddha. Once, when the Buddha was staying with him at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cālikā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meghiya</span> went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jantugāma</span> for alms, and, on his return, was much attracted by a mango grove on the banks of the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kimikālā</span> (this was in the thirteenth year after the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>, Bv­a.3). He asked the Buddha’s permission to dwell there in meditation. Twice the Buddha refused, but, on his third request, let him go. There, however, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meghiya</span> was consumed by evil thoughts and returned to the Buddha. The Buddha preached to him on the five things which make the heart ripe for emancipation – good friends, virtuous life, profitable talks, zealous exertion, insight – and admonished him. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meghiya</span> thereupon attained *Arahant*-ship. AN.iv.354ff.; Ud.iv.1; Thag.66; AN­a.ii.794; Dhp­a.i.289 says, however, that at the end of the Buddha’s sermon <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meghiya</span> became a *Sotāpanna*.

Ninety-one *kappas* ago, on the death of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span>, there was a great earthquake. The people were very frightened, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessavaṇa</span> explained to them the reason for it and dispelled their fears. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meghiya</span> was then a householder, and having thus heard of the Buddha’s qualities, was filled with joy. Fourteen *kappas* ago he was a king named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samita</span> (Ud­a.217ff.; Thag­a.i.149f). He is evidently to be identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhasaññaka</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>. Ap.i.151f.
