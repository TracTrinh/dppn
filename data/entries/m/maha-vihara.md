# Mahā Vihāra

## English source

Mahā Vihāra. The great monastery at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>, for many centuries the chief seat of Buddhism in Ceylon. It was founded by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span>, on the counsel of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span>, and included the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Meghavana</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Meghavanārāma</span> henceforth came to be included in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>. The boundary of the *vihāra* was marked out by the king ploughing a circular furrow starting from near the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṅgalatittha</span> on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kadambanadī</span> and ending again at the river (Mhv.xv.188ff.; Mhv­ṭ.361; Mbv. 135, 136 says that the ford on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kadambanadī</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāsāṇatittha</span>).

A list is given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Bodhivaṁsa</span> (pp. 135f) of the places through which the *sīmā* (boundary) of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> passed – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāsāṇatittha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuddavāṭakapāsāṇa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumbhakāra-āvāta</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nīpa</span> tree, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakudhapāli</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Aṅgaṇa</span> tree, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khujjamātula</span> tree, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Marutta</span> *pokkharaṇī*, the northern gate of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijayārāma</span> park, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gajakumbhakapāsāṇa</span>, then passing <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avaṭṭimajjha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bālakapāsāṇa</span> on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayavāpi</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Susāna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghapāsāṇa</span>, the left side of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍālagāma</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nīcasusāna</span> to the left of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kammāradeva</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīmānigrodha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvaṅgaṇa</span>, round the hermitages of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotiya Giri</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kumbhaṇḍa</span>, to the right of the various hermitages of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paribbājakas</span>, by Hiyagalla, along the shrine of the Brahmin Dīyavāsa, through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Telumapāli</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tālacatukka</span>, to the right of the stables (*assamaṇḍala*), on to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sasakapāsāṇa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Marumbatittha</span>. It then proceeded up the river to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīhasinānatittha</span>, on to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāsāṇatittha</span>, ending at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuddavāṭakapāsāṇa</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> contained thirty-two *Mālakas* (Mhv.xv.214) and had numerous buildings attached to it, apart from sacred shrines, such as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Bodhi</span>-tree, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thūpārāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span>, etc. In its early period, the precincts of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> contained other buildings besides those dedicated to the service of Buddhism e.g., the hermitages of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paribbājakas</span> (as mentioned above) and the shrine of the guardian deity of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> (Mhv.xxv.87). In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṭṭagāmaṇī</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> monks divided into two factions, and one party occupied <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span>, built by the king (Mhv.xxxiii.97f). At first the differences between these two factions were trivial, but, as time went on, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> grew in power and riches and proved a formidable rival to the older monastery.

From time to time various kings and nobles made additions and restorations to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>. Thus <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vasabha</span> (Mhv.xxxxv.88) built a row of cells, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhātikatissa</span> erected a boundary wall (Mhv.xxxvi.2), while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaniṭṭhatissa</span> removed the boundary wall and constructed the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkuṭagiri</span> *pariveṇa*, twelve large *pāsādas*, a refectory, and a road leading from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇavihāra</span> (Mhv.xxxvi.10f). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vohārikatissa</span> appointed a monthly gift of a thousand to the monks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> (Mhv.xxxvi.32), while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirisaṅghabodhi</span> built a *salāka* house (Mhv.xxxvi.74). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Goṭhābhaya</span> erected a stone pavilion and made a *padhānabhūmi* to the west of the *vihāra* (Mhv.xxxvi.102,106).

Towards the latter part of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Goṭhābhaya</span>’s reign, a dispute arose between the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span> on matters of doctrine, and sixty monks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span>, who had adopted the *Vetulyavāda*, were banished. They obtained the assistance of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Coḷa</span> monk, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghatissa</span>, and at a solemn assembly of the monks concerned, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thūpārāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghamitta</span> expounded his heretical doctrine, refuting the opposition of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> monks, and succeeded in winning over the king, who was present, in spite of the efforts of his uncle, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Goṭhābhaya</span> Thera*,* to bring him round to the orthodox party. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghamitta</span> became tutor to the king’s sons, and when one of these, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sena</span>, became king, he prompted him to destroy the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>. A royal decree was issued forbidding the giving of alms to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>. The monks thereupon left the monastery, and for nine years it remained deserted. Many of the buildings were destroyed, and various possessions belonging to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> were removed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span>; but the people, led by the king’s minister and friend, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meghavaṇṇābhaya</span>, revolted against the impious deeds of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sena</span> and his admirers, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghamitta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa</span>, and the king was forced to yield. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghamitta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa</span> were slain by one of the queens, and the king, with the help of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meghavaṇṇābhaya</span>, rebuilt several *pariveṇas* and restored some of the possessions, which had been removed. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sena</span>’s allegiance to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> teaching was not lasting; acting on the advice of a monk named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span>, he built the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavanavihāra</span> in the grounds of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>, against the wish of the monks there; the latter left again for nine months as a sign of protest against the king’s attempts to remove the boundary of the *vihāra*. This attempt, however, he was forced to abandon (Mhv.xxxvi.110f.; xxxvii.1-37).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sena</span>’s son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimeghavaṇṇa</span>, on coming to the throne, exerted himself to undo the damage which had been wrought by his father. He rebuilt the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohapāsāda</span> and restored all the demolished *pariveṇas*, together with their endowments (Cv.xxxvii.54ff). Mahā Vihāra had, by now, become famous as a seat of learning; it was the centre of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theravāda</span> Buddhism, and was the repository of various Commentaries, of which the chief were the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīhalaṭṭhakathā</span> on the Pāli Canon. Thither, therefore, came scholars from various countries, among them <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (q.,v.), who resided in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganthākara</span> *pariveṇa* and compiled his Pāli Commentaries (Cv.xxxvii.215ff).

When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhātusena</span> became king he had the walls of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> painted with various ornamental designs (Cv.xxxviii.43). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammarucikas</span> seem to have been favourites of this king and to have occupied the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>, later moving to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambatthala</span> *vihāra* (Cv.xxxviii.75). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāga</span> instituted a permanent distribution of soup to the inhabitants of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> (Cv.xli.99) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jeṭṭhatissa III</span>. planted another Bodhi-tree there, called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Metta</span> (Cv.xliv.96).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udaya I</span>. built a new *salāka* hall (Cv.xlix.14). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggabodhi IX</span>. discontinued the habit of the monks of the smaller *vihāras* surrounding <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> from coming to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> for their supply of medicines and made other arrangements for their distribution (Cv.xlix.88). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sena I</span>. and his queen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghā</span> erected and endowed the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghasena</span> *pariveṇa* (Cv.l.70), while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa IV</span>. built the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="119" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samuddagiri</span> *pariveṇa* and gave it for the use of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="120" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṁsukūlikas</span>, while for the forest dwelling monks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="121" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> he built forest dwellings (Cv.lii.21f.; Cv.Trs.i.163, n.8). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="122" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>’s kinsman, the general <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="123" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rakkha</span>, built a *vihāra* in the village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="124" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Savāraka</span> and gave it to the incumbents of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="125" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>, to be used as a *padhānaghara*, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="126" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Lekhasena</span> built, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="127" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> itself, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="128" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Lekhapabbata</span> (Cv.lii.31ff). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="129" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udaya IV</span>. gave a diadem of jewels to the Buddha image in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="130" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>, while his wife <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="131" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vidurā</span> added to it a network of rays made of precious stones (Cv.liii.49ff).

During the invasions of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="132" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Coḷas</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="133" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍus</span> from South India, and owing to the consequent confusion prevailing in the country, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="134" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> seems to have been neglected. Many of the buildings were destroyed and their priceless possessions plundered. Discipline among the monks became slack and there were many dissensions. Later, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="135" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu I</span>. had restored peace, he wished to purify the religion, but met with great opposition, and it was only after strenuous efforts that he brought about a reconciliation between the different parties (Cv.lxxviii.11ff).

It is said that the king could not find one single pure member of the Saṅgha (Cv.lxxviii.25). He, therefore, held a special ordination ceremony, admitting many monks into the Saṅgha.

After the removal of the capital from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="136" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="137" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulatthipura</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="138" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> lost its importance; the centre of activity was now at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="139" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulatthipura</span>, and later, at other capitals, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="140" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> fell into neglect and decay, from which it has never recovered.
