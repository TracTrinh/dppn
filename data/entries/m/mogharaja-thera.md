# Mogharāja Thera

## English source

Mogharāja Thera. He belonged to a Brahmin family and studied under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span> as an ascetic. He was one of the sixteen pupils sent by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bāvarī</span> to the Buddha. When Mogharāja had asked his question of the Buddha and had received the answer, he attained *Arahant*-ship. He then attained distinction by wearing rough cloth which had been thrown away by caravaners, tailors, and dyers, and the Buddha declared him foremost among wearers of rough clothing (See also AN.i.25). Later, through want of care and former *kamma*, pimples and the like broke out over his body. Judging that his lodging was infected, he spread a couch of straw in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> field and lived there even during the winter. When the Buddha asked him how he fared in the cold, he replied that he was extremely happy (Thag.207f).

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>, Mogharāja first resolved to win the eminence which was his. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atthadassī Buddha</span> he was a Brahmin teacher, and one day, while teaching his students, he saw the Buddha, and having worshipped him with great solemnity, he uttered six verses in his praise and offered him a gift of honey. Later, after a sojourn in the *Deva* worlds, he became a minister of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭṭhavāhana</span>, and was sent by him, with one thousand others, to visit <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>. He heard the Buddha preach, entered the order, and lived the life of a monk for twenty thousand years (Thag­a.i.181ff.; Snp. vs. 1006). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyuttanikāya</span> contains a stanza spoken by Mogharāja and the Buddha’s answer thereto (SN.i.23).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> explains (SN­a.i.49f) that Mogharāja was present during the discussion of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasūra</span> *paribbājaka* (*q.v.*) with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>. At the end of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>’s explanation, Mogharāja wished to settle the matter and uttered this stanza.

Mogharāja is given as an example of one who attained *Arahant*-ship by the development of investigation (*vīmaṁsaṁ dhuraṁ katvā*) (SN­a.iii.201).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> contains two sets of verses in reference to Mogharāja. They seem to be parts of the same <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> which have become separated. The first set (Ap.i.87f) gives an account of the meeting of Mogharāja with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Atthadassī Buddha</span> (see above) and includes the verses uttered by Mogharāja in praise of the Buddha. The second set (Ap.ii.486f) contains an account of his meeting with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> and the resolves he made before him. It further mentions that, for one thousand years, in a later birth, Mogharāja suffered in hell, and that for five hundred births he suffered from skin diseases. This was because he had lighted a fire in the Buddha’s cloister and had made the floor black. In his last birth, too, he suffered from a *kuṭṭharoga* and could not sleep at night, hence his name (*mogharajjasukhaṁ yasmā Mogharājā tato ahaṁ*). These verses also include the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mogharājamāṇavapucchā</span>.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span> (p. 412) appears a stanza attributed to Mogharāja, but not found in the stanzas mentioned in connection with him either in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span> or in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span>. See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mogharājamāṇavapucchā</span>.
