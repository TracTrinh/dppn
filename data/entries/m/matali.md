# Mātali

## English source

Mātali. The name given to the chariot driver (*saṅgāhaka*) of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātali</span> of the present age had a son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sikhaṇḍhi</span>, with whom <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā Suriyavaccasā</span>, daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Timbarū</span>, was in love; but later she transferred her affections to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcasikha</span> (DN.ii.268). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātali</span> is <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s constant companion and accompanies him everywhere, more as a confidant than as a servant. See, e.g., the conversation reported at SN.i.221, 224, 234ff.; and Vv.iv.9.

Thus, he was by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s side in the war against the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asuras</span> and drove his chariot when he fled with his bride <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujātā</span>. The chariot is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vejayanta</span> *ratha* and is drawn by one thousand Sindh horses (Dhp­a.i.279f.; Ja.i.202f). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātali</span> often accompanied <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> on his journeys to the world of men, changing his form e.g., to that of a fish in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Dhanuggahajātaka</span> (Ja 374), a Brahmin in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Biḷārikosiyajātaka</span> (Ja 450) and in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhābhojanajātaka</span> (Ja 535), and a big black dog in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaṇhajātaka</span> (Ja 469). On several occasions he was sent by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> to fetch human beings to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Guttila</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nimi</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makhādeva</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sādhīna</span> and he proved an excellent guide, pointing out to the visitors the places of interest passed on the way.

When the Buddha descended from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, after preaching there the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span>, he was accompanied, on the left, by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātali</span>, offering celestial scents, garlands and flowers (Dhp­a.iii.226). Both in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Biḷārikosiyajātaka</span> (Ja 450) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhābhojanajātaka</span> (Ja 535, *q.v.*) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātali</span> is spoken of as the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suriya</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> is said to have been <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātali</span> during several lives (Ja.i.206; iv.180; v. 412; vi.129); so also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> (Ja.iv.69). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Samayasutta</span> (DN.ii.258), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātali</span> is described as a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhabba</span> chief, while in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āṭānāṭiyasutta</span> (DN.iii.204) he is mentioned among the chief <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> to be invoked by followers of the Buddha in time of need.
