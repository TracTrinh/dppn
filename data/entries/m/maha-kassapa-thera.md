# Mahā Kassapa Thera

## English source

Mahā Kassapa Thera. One of the Buddha’s most eminent disciples, chief among those who upheld asceticism (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dhutavādānaṁ</span>) (AN.i.23). He was born in the Brahmin village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Tittha</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, and was the son of the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapila</span>, his mother being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanādevī</span>; he himself was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pippali</span> at Ap.ii.583, vs. 56; but there his father is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosiyagotta</span>.

When he grew up he refused to marry in spite of the wishes of his parents; but in the end, to escape from their importunities, he agreed to marry if a wife could be found resembling a statue, which he had made. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā Kāpilānī</span> was found at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgala</span> to fulfil these conditions, and though the young people wrote to each other suggesting that somebody else should be found as a match for each, their letters were intercepted and they were married. By mutual consent, however, the marriage was not consummated, the two spending the night separated by a chain of flowers. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pippali</span> had immense wealth; he used twelve measures of perfumed powder daily, each measure a Magadha <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nāḷi</span>, for his person alone. He had sixty lakes with water works attached, and his workmen occupied fourteen villages, each as large as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>.

One day he went to a field, which was being ploughed and saw the birds eating the worms turned up by the plough. On being told that the sin therein was his, he decided to renounce all his possessions.

At the same time, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā</span> had been watching the crows eating the little insects, which ran about among the seamsum seeds that had been put out to dry, and when her attendant women told her that hers would be the sin for their loss of life, she also determined to renounce the world.

The husband and wife, finding that they were of one accord, took yellow raiments from their wardrobe, cut off each other’s hair, took bowls in their hands, and passed out through their weeping servants, to all of whom they granted their freedom, and departed together, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pippali</span> walking in front. But soon they agreed that it was not seemly they should walk thus together, as each must prove a hindrance to the other. And so, at the cross roads, he took the right and she the left and the earth trembled to see such virtue.

The Buddha, sitting in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhakuṭi</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span>, knew what the earthquake signified, and having walked three <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gāvutas</span>, sat down at the foot of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bahuputtaka</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nigrodha</span>, between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālandā</span>, resplendent in all the glory of a Buddha (this journey of the Buddha is often referred to – e.g., MN­a.i.347, 357). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pippali</span> saw the Buddha, and recognising him at once as his teacher, prostrated himself before him (he was henceforth called Mahā Kassapa, no explanation is to be found anywhere as to why he is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>; it was probably his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gotta</span> name, but see Ap.ii.583, vs.56 where his father is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosiyagotta</span>). The Buddha told him to be seated, and, in three homilies, gave him his ordination.

The three homilies are given at SN.ii.220, “Thus Kassapa must thou train thyself: (1) ‘There shall be a lively sense of fear and regard (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">hirotappa</span>) towards all monks, seniors, novices, and those of middle status.’ (2) ‘Whatever doctrine I shall hear bearing upon what is good, to all that I will hearken with attentive ear, digesting it, pondering it, gathering it all up with my will.’ (3) ‘Happy mindfulness with respect to the body shall not be neglected by me.’”

Together they returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, Kassapa, who bore on his body seven of the thirty-two marks of a Great Being, following the Buddha. On the way, the Buddha desired to sit at the foot of a tree by the roadside, and Kassapa folded for him his outer robe (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pilotikasaṅghāṭi</span>) as a seat. The Buddha sat on it and, feeling it with his hand, praised its softness. Kassapa asked him to accept it. “And what would you wear?” inquired the Buddha. Kassapa then begged that he might be given the rag robe worn by the Buddha. “It is faded with use,” said the Buddha, but Kassapa said he would prize it above the whole world and the robes were exchanged (the robe which Kassapa exchanged with the Buddha was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇā</span>’s cloak. See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇā</span> 6).

This incident Kassapa always recalled with pride, e.g. SN.ii.221. It is said that the Buddha paid him this great honour because he knew that Kassapa would hold a recital after his death, and thus help in the perpetuation of his religion, SN­a.ii.130. The earth quaked again in recognition of Kassapa’s virtues, for no ordinary being would have been fit to wear the Buddha’s cast-off robe. Kassapa, conscious of the great honour, took upon himself the thirteen austere vows (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dhutaguṇā</span>) and, after eight days, became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>.

In the past Kassapa and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā</span> had been husband and wife and companions in good works in many births. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> was a very rich householder named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vedeha</span> and married to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā</span>, and very devoted to the Buddha. One day he heard the Buddha’s third disciple in rank (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nisabha</span>) being awarded the place of pre-eminence among those who observed austere practices, and registered a wish for a similar honour for himself in the future. He learnt from the Buddha of the qualities in which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nisabha</span> excelled the Buddha himself, and determined to obtain them. With this end in view, during birth after birth, he expended all his energies in goods deeds. Ninety-one <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span> ago; in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span>, he was the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekasāṭaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā</span> was his wife. In the interval between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇāgamana Buddha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> he was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhiputta</span>. He married <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā</span>, but because of an evil deed she had done in the past (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā Kāpilānī</span>), she became unattractive to him and he left her, taking her as wife again when she became attractive. Having seen from what had happened to his wife how great was the power of the Buddhas, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhiputta</span> wrapped <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>’s golden <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span> with costly robes and decked it with golden lotuses, each the size of a cartwheel.

The Therī <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (Ap.ii.582. vs. 47-51) gives an account of two more of his lives, one as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumitta</span> and the other as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyaputta</span>, in both of which he and his wife ministered to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>.

In the next birth he was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, and, because he had given robes in past lives, he had thirty-two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kapparukkhas</span>, which provided him and all the people of his kingdom with garments. At the suggestion of his queen, he made preparations to feed holy men, and five hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>, sons of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumā</span>, came to accept his gift. In that life, too, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span> and his queen renounced the world and became ascetics, and having developed the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jhānas</span>, were reborn in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> world.

This account of Kassapa’s last life and his previous life is compiled from AN­a.i.92ff.; SN­a.ii.135ff.; Thag­a.ii.134ff.; Ap.ii.578ff. Ap.i.33ff. gives other particulars – that he made offerings at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span>’s funeral pyre and that he was once a king named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ubbiddha</span> in the city of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rammaka</span>; see also Ap­a.i.209f.

Kassapa was not present at the death of the Buddha; as he was journeying from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusināra</span> he met an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ājīvaka</span> carrying in his hand a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t18">mandārava</span>-flower picked up by him from among those which had rained from heaven in honour of the Buddha, and it was he who told <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> the news. It was then the seventh day after the Buddha’s death, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> had been trying in vain to set fire to his pyre. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> Theras, who were present, declared that it could not be kindled until <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> and his five hundred companions had saluted the Buddha’s feet. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> then arrived and walked three times round the pyre with bared shoulder, and it is said the Buddha’s feet became visible from out of the pyre in order that he might worship them. He was followed by his five hundred colleagues, and when they had all worshipped the feet disappeared and the pyre kindled of itself (DN.ii.163f).

It is said that the relics of the Buddha which fell to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>’s share were taken to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> by Kassapa, in view of that which would happen in the future (Mhv.xxxi.20f.; see also Vsm.430). At <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span> (on the announcement of the Buddha’s death), Kassapa had heard the words of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Subhadda</span>, who, in his old age, had joined the Saṅgha, that they were “well rid of the great <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t18">samaṇa</span> and could now do as they liked.” This remark it was which had suggested to Kassapa’s mind the desirability of holding a Recital of the Buddha’s teachings. He announced his intention to the assembled monks, and, as the senior among them and as having been considered by the Buddha himself to be fit for such a task, he was asked to make all necessary arrangements (e.g., DN­a.i.3). In accordance with his wishes, all the monks, other than the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span> chosen for the Recital, left <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> during the rainy season. The five hundred who were selected met in Council under the presidency of Kassapa and recited the Dhamma and the Vinaya (DN­a.i.3f.; 5ff.; Vin-a.i.4.ff.; Mhv.iii.3ff). This recital is called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Therasaṅgīti</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theravāda</span>.

The books contain numerous references to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> – he is classed with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kappina</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> for his great <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span>-powers, e.g., SN.i.114; but his range of knowledge was limited; there were certain things which even Kassapa did not know (Dhp­a.i.258).

The Buddha regarded him as equal to himself in exhorting the monks to lead the active and zealous lives (SN.ii.205), and constantly held him up as an example to others in his great contentment (SN.ii.194f) and his ability to win over families by his preaching. The Buddha compares him to the moon (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t18">candopama</span>), unobtrusive; his heart was free from bondage, and he always taught others out of a feeling of compassion. SN.ii.197ff. Kassapa’s freedom from any kind of attachment was, as the Buddha pointed out to the monks, due to the earnest wish he had made for that attainment in the past, “He has no attachment to requisites or households or monasteries or cells; but is like a royal goose which goes down into a lake and swims there, while the water does not adhere to his body” (Dhp­a.ii.169f.).

The Buddha also thought him equal to himself in his power of attaining the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jhānas</span> and abiding therein (SN.ii.210ff).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> was willing to help monks along their way, and several instances are given of his exhortations to them (e.g., Thag.vss.1051-57, 1072-81, and his long sermon at AN.v.161ff); but he was evidently sensitive to criticism, and would not address them unless he felt them to be tractable and deferential to instruction, e.g., SN.ii.203ff.; and at 219, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thullanandā</span> finds fault with him for blaming <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> had good reason for not wishing to address recalcitrant monks. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṭidūsakajātaka</span> (Ja 321) relates how one of his disciples, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uluṅka Saddaka</span>, angered by some admonition from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>, burnt the latter’s grass hut while he was away on his alms round (Ja.iii.71f.).

He was very reluctant to preach to the nuns, but on one occasion he allowed himself to be persuaded by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, and accompanied by him he visited the nunnery and preached to the nuns. He was probably not popular among them, for, at the end of his discourse, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thullatissā</span> openly reviled him for what she called his impertinence in having dared to preach in the presence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, “as if the needle pedlar were to sell a needle to the needle maker.” (SN.ii.215f) Kassapa loved <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> dearly, and was delighted when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship in time to attend the First Recital, and when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> appeared before the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahants</span>, it was Kassapa who led the applause (DN­a.i.10f). But Kassapa was very jealous of the good name of the Saṅgha, and we find him (SN.ii.218f) blaming <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> for admitting into the Saṅgha new members incapable of observing its discipline and of going about with them in large numbers, exposing the Saṅgha to the criticism of the public. “A corn trampler art thou, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>,” he says, “a despoiler of families, thy following is breaking up, thy youngsters are melting away,” and ends up with “The boy, methinks, does not know his own measure.” <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, annoyed at being called “boy,” protests “Surely my head is growing grey hairs, your reverence.” This incident, says the Commentary took place after the Buddha’s death, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, as a new <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> and with all the honour of his intimacy with the Buddha, whose bowl and robe he now possessed, had become a notable personage. SN­a.ii.133; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> regarded Kassapa in some sort of way as a teacher, and held him in great respect, not daring to mention even his name, lest it should imply disrespect (see Vin.i.92f.).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thullanandā</span> heard Kassapa censuring <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> and raised her voice in protest, “What now? Does Kassapa, once a heretic, deem that he can chide the learned sage <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>?” Kassapa was hurt by her words, and complained to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> that such things should be said of him who had been singled out by the Buddha for special honour.

Kassapa viewed with concern the growing laxity among members of the Saṅgha with regard to the observance of rules, even in the very lifetime of the Buddha, and the falling off in the number of those attaining <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship, and we find him consulting the Buddha as to what should be done. SN.ii.224f. At the First Council, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> stated that the Buddha had given leave for the monks to do away with the minor rules of the Saṅgha, Kassapa was opposed to any such step, lest it should lead to slackness among the monks and contempt from the laity (Vin.ii.287f.).

Kassapa himself did his utmost to lead an exemplary life, dwelling in the forest, subsisting solely on alms, wearing rag robes, always content with little, holding himself aloof from society, ever strenuous and energetic. See also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="119" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Gosiṅgasutta</span> (MN.i.214), where Kassapa declares his belief in the need for these observances; that his example was profitable to others is proved by the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="120" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Somamitta</span> who, finding his own teacher <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="121" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vimala</span> given up to laziness, sought Kassapa and attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="122" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship under his guidance.

When asked why he led such a life, he replied that it was not only for his own happiness but also out of compassion for those who came after him, that they might attain to the same end. Even when he was old and the Buddha himself had asked him to give up his coarse rag robe and to dwell near him, he begged to be excused. SN.ii.202f; but see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="123" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotidāsa</span>, who is said to have built a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="124" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="125" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span>, and entertained him.

Once, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="126" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> lay grievously ill at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="127" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pipphaliguhā</span>, the Buddha visited him and reminded him of the seven <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="128" data-dppn-term-class="t18">bojjhaṅgas</span> which he had practised (SN.v.78). The knowledge that he had profited by the Master’s teaching, we are told (SN­a.iii.128), calmed his blood and purified his system, and the sickness fell away from him “like a drop of water from a lotus leaf.”

He disdained being waited upon by anybody, even by a goddess such as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="129" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lājā</span>, lest he should set a bad example (Dhp­a.iii.6ff). Owing to his great saintliness, even the gods vied with each other to give alms to Kassapa. Once when he had risen from a jhāna lasting seven days, five hundred nymphs, wives of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="130" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, appeared before him; but, snapping his fingers, he asked them to depart, saying that he bestowed his favours only on the poor.

The story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="131" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷavilaṅgika</span> is an example of Kassapa’s compassion for the poor. Once, after a seven days’ jhāna, he went to the house of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="132" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷavilaṅgika</span> and received alms from his wife, which he gave to the Buddha for their greater benefit. The Buddha took a portion of this and gave the rest to five hundred monks. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="133" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷavilaṅgika</span>, received only a mouthful of the food left. The Buddha said that as a result he would be a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="134" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span> within seven days. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="135" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷavilaṅgika</span> told this to his wife. It happened that a few days later the king saw a man impaled alive in the place of execution; the man begged him for some food, which he agreed to send. At night, when eating, the king remembered his promise, but could find no one bold enough to go to the cemetery. On the offer of one thousand pieces, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="136" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷavilaṅgika</span>’s wife agreed to go in the guise of a man. On the way she was stopped by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="137" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="138" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghataphala</span>, who, however, later released her and gave her treasure, as did also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="139" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>’s father-in-law, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="140" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="141" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span>. The man ate the food and, when wiping his mouth, recognised her as a woman and caught hold of her hair. But she cut off her hair, and proved to the satisfaction of the king that her mission had been accomplished. She then recovered the treasure given her by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="142" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="143" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span>; when the king discovered her wealth, she and her husband were raised to the rank of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="144" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span> (MN­a.ii.812ff.).

When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="145" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> heard of this, he disguised himself as a weaver worn with age, and accompanied by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="146" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujātā</span>, transformed into an old woman, appeared in a weaver’s hut along the lane where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="147" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> was begging. The ruse succeeded and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="148" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> accepted their alms; but, later, be discovered the truth and chided <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="149" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="150" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> begged forgiveness, and, on being assured that in spite of his deception the almsgiving would bring him merit, he flew into the air shouting, “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="151" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Aho dānaṁ, mahā danaṁ, Kassapassa patiṭṭhitaṁ</span>.” The Buddha heard this and sympathised with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="152" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> in his great joy (Dhp­a.i.423ff.; cp. Ud.iii.7).

But on one occasion so great was the importunity with which the monks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="153" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavī</span> had wearied the people, that even <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="154" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> failed to get alms from them (Ja.ii.282). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="155" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visuddhimagga</span> (p.403) relates a story of how once, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="156" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> was begging for alms in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="157" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, in the company of the Buddha, on a festival day, five hundred maidens were going to the festival carrying cakes, “round like the moon.” They saw the Buddha but passed him by, and gave their cakes to Kassapa. The Elder made all the cakes fill just his single bowl and offered it to the Buddha (this is probably the incident referred to at Vsm.68).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="158" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> seems to have held Kassapa in great esteem, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="159" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapasaṁyutta</span> contains two discussions between them: one on the necessity for zeal and ardour in the attainment of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="160" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nibbāna</span> (SN.ii.195f), and the other on the existence of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="161" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tathāgata</span> after death (SN.ii.222f). This regard was mutual, for when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="162" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> saw the great honour paid to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="163" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="164" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> he rejoiced greatly and broke forth into song (Thag.vs.1082-5).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="165" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> lived to be very old, and, when he died, had not lain on a bed for one hundred and twenty years. DN­a.ii.413; AN­a.ii.596; he was one hundred and twenty at the time of the First Recital (SN­a.ii.130). According, to northern sources, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="166" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa</span> did not die; he dwells in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="167" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkuṭagiri</span> Mountains, wrapt in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="168" data-dppn-term-class="t18">samādhi</span>, awaiting the arrival of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="169" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Metteyya Buddha</span> (Beal-Xuan, op.cit., ii.142f.). A tooth of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="170" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> was enshrined in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="171" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhīmatitthavihāra</span> in Ceylon (Cv.lxxxv.81).
