# Mahā Ariyavaṁsasutta

## English source

Mahā Ariyavaṁsasutta. On the four Ariyan lineages, reckoned as ancient and pure, and held in esteem by discerning recluses and Brahmins of all times. A monk is content with any kind of robe; he does not, for the sake of robes, resort to unseemly conduct; he is free from either selfishness or greed with regard to robes; neither does he exalt himself because of his contentment. So it is with other requisites. He also delights in abandoning and in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">bhāvanā</span>. A monk possessed of these four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ariyavaṁsā</span> verily becomes a sage, praised by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brahma</span> himself (AN.ii.27ff).

This <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> was evidently a favourite topic for a sermon (AN­a.i.385, 386). The Commentary explains (AN­a.ii.494) how, for instance, anyone who preaches on the first three <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ariyavaṁsā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">catupaccayasantosa</span>) could bring the whole <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vinayapiṭaka</span> to bear on the discussion, while a discussion on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">bhāvanārāma­ariyavaṁsa</span> could include the two other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Piṭakas</span>, chiefly the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nekkhammapāli</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭisambhidāmagga</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dasuttarasutta</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghanikāya</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Satipaṭṭhānasutta</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhimanikāya</span>, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Niddesapariyāya</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span>.

The full name of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> seems to have been <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Catupaccayasantosabhāvanārāmamahā-ariyavaṁsasutta</span> (AN­a.i.385). It was also probably called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṁsasutta</span> for short.

It is probably this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Ariyavaṁsasutta</span> which was held in such high esteem by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vohārakatissa</span>, that he ordered almsgiving throughout Ceylon whenever the “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ariyavaṁsa</span>” was read (Mhv.xxxvi.38; but see Mhv.Trs.258, n.6). It is said that people would journey five <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojanas</span> to hear a monk preach the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariyavaṁsa</span> (e.g., AN­a.i.386), and mention is made of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Ariyavaṁsabhāṇakā</span>, who, judging from the stories of them (e.g., SN­a.iii.151), were extremely able and eloquent preachers.
