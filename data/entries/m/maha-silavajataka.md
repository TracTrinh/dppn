# Mahā Sīlavajātaka

## English source

Mahā Sīlavajātaka (Ja 51). The Bodhisatta was once king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sīlava</span>. He built six almonries and ruled in all goodness. One of his ministers, having intrigued with a member of his harem, was expelled and took service under the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>. He caused several bands of ruffians to invade the territory of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sīlava</span> at different times. When they were caught and brought before <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sīlava</span>, the latter gave them money and sent them away, telling them to act differently in the future. In this way the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> was easily persuaded by his minister that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sīlava</span>’s kingdom could easily be captured. He therefore set out with an army, and as the people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sīlava</span> were allowed to offer no resistance, the king and his ministers were captured alive and buried up to their necks in the cemetery. In the night, when jackals approached to eat them, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sīlava</span> fastened his teeth in the neck of the jackal that came to him. The jackal started howling and his companions fled. In his struggles to get free, the jackal loosened the earth round <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sīlava</span>, who managed to free himself and then his companions.

In the cemetery two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkhas</span> were having a dispute about a dead body, and they asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sīlava</span> to settle it. But he wished first to bathe, and they fetched him water and perfumes and food from the usurper’s table in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> and also his sword of state. With this he cut the body in half, giving half to each <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>, and, with their aid, he entered the usurper’s room where he slept. When the latter showed signs of terror, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sīlava</span> told him of what had happened and granted him forgiveness. His kingdom was then restored, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sīlava</span> exhorted his subjects on the virtues of perseverance. The story was related to a backsliding monk. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> is identified with the treacherous minister of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span>. Ja.i.261-8.
