# Māyā

## English source

Māyā, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Māyā</span>. The mother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> (DN.ii.52; see Thomas: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Life and Legend of the Buddha</span>, 25). Her father was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan Añjana</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadaha</span>, son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadahasakka</span>, and her mother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasodharā</span>, daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayasena</span>. (Mhv.ii.17ff.; elsewhere her father is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Suppabuddha</span> (Thīg­a.141), while the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (ii.538) gives the name of her mother as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sulakkhaṇā</span>).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Daṇḍapāṇī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppabuddha</span> were her brothers, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Pajāpatī</span> her sister. Both the sisters were married to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> in their youth, but it was not till <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māyā</span> was over 50 years old that the Buddha was born (Vibh-a 278). She had all the qualities necessary for one who was to bear the exalted rank of being the mother of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>: she was not too passionate, she did not take intoxicants, she had practiced the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pāramī</span> for one hundred thousand <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappas</span>, and had not, since her birth, violated the five <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sīlā</span>. On the day of her conception she kept her fast, and in her sleep that night she had the following dream: the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cattāro Mahā Rājāno</span> gods took her in her bed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span> and placed her under a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sāla</span> tree on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manosilātala</span>. Then their wives came and bathed her in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anotatta Lake</span> and clad her in divine robes. They then led her into a golden palace and laid her on a divine couch; there the Bodhisatta, in the form of a white elephant, holding a white lotus in his gleaming trunk, entered into her right side. This was on the day of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Uttarāsāḷha nakkhatta</span>, after a festival lasting seven days, in which she had already taken part.

From the day of her conception she was guarded by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Four Regent Gods</span>; she felt no desire for men, and the child in her womb could be seen from outside. At the end of the tenth month she wished to return to her people in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadaha</span>, but, on her way thither, she stopped at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sāla</span> grove in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lumbinī</span> and there her child was born as she stood holding on to the branch of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sāla</span> tree (Ja.i.49ff). Seven days later <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māyā</span> died and was reborn as a male in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> world, under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māyā</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Devaputta</span> (Thag.vss.533f.; Thag­a.i.502).

The Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> immediately after the performance of the Twin Miracle at the foot of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṇḍamba</span> tree, on the full moon day of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Āsāḷha</span>, and there, during the three months of the rainy season, the Buddha stayed, preaching the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhammapiṭaka</span> to his mother (who came there to listen to him), seated on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍukambalasilāsana</span>, at the foot of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāricchattaka</span> tree (it is said that, during this time, at certain intervals, the Buddha would return to earth, leaving a seated image of himself in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> to continue the preaching while he attended to his bodily needs, begging alms in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> and eating his food on the banks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anotatta</span>, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> waited on him and learnt of what he had been preaching to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>. Dhs-a.i.15; Dhp­a.iii.216f)

The Commentaries (Ud­a.276f) state the view, held by some, that had <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māyā</span> been alive the Buddha would not have shown such reluctance to bestow ordination on women. This view, says <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapāla</span> is erroneous. It would have made no difference, for it is the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dhammatā</span> of all Buddhas that women shall be ordained, but subject to certain important restrictions. The mothers of all Buddhas die very soon after the birth of their son, because no other child is fit to be conceived in the same womb as a Buddha.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māyā</span> is mentioned in several Jātakas as the mother of the Bodhisatta – e.g., in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Alīnacitta</span> (Ja 156), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṭṭhahāri</span> (Ja 7), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurudhamma</span> (Ja 276), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> (Ja 428), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khaṇḍahāla</span> (Ja 542), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dasaratha</span> (Ja 461), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhanāgāra</span> (Ja 201), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Ummagga</span> (Ja 546), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātuposaka</span> (Ja 455), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span> (Ja 547), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Susīma</span> (Ja 411) the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Somanassa</span> (Ja 505), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthipāla</span> (Ja 509).
