# Mahā Dhammapālajātaka

## English source

Mahā Dhammapālajātaka (Ja 447). In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapāla</span>, a village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span>, there lived a family whose head was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Dhammapāla</span>. The Bodhisatta was his son, and was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapāla</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kumāra</span>. He went to study at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span>. There the teacher’s eldest son died, but among all the lamentations it was noticed that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapāla</span> did not weep. When questioned by his fellows as to how he could refrain, he answered that as it was impossible for anybody young to die, he did not believe his friend was dead. The teacher asked him about this, and found that in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapāla</span>’s family no one died young. Wishing to know if this were true, he left <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> and went to the home of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapāla</span>, carrying with him the bones of a goat. After his welcome had subsided, he announced to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapāla</span> that his son was dead, and begged him not to grieve. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapāla</span> clapped his hands and laughed, saying that such a thing could never be as no member of their family ever died young. He then told the Brahmin, in answer to his query, that they owed their longevity to the fact that they lived good lives.

The story was related to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span>, who told the Buddha how, when the Bodhisatta was practising severe penances, some gods came to him (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span>) and said that he was dead. But he refused to believe them. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Dhammapāla</span> and the teacher <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> (Ja.iv.50-55). At the conclusion of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Pajāpatī Gotamī</span> a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>. Dhp­a.i.99; Ja.i.92.
