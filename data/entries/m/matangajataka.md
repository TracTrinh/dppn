# Mātaṅgajātaka

## English source

Mātaṅgajātaka (Ja 497). The Bodhisatta was once born in a *Caṇḍāla* village outside <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> and was named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span>. One day, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Diṭṭhamaṅgalikā</span>, the daughter of a rich merchant, was on her way to the park with a group of friends, she saw <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span> coming towards the city, and thinking the sight inauspicious, washed her eyes with perfumed water and turned back home. Her companions, annoyed at being deprived of their fun, beat <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span> and left him senseless. On recovering consciousness, he determined to get <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Diṭṭhamaṅgalikā</span> as wife and lay down outside her father’s house refusing to move. Seven days he lay thus until her relations, fearing the ignominy of having a *Caṇḍāla* die at their door, gave <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Diṭṭhamaṅgalikā</span> to him as wife.

Knowing her pride to be quelled by this act, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span> decided to bring her great honour. He, therefore, retired into the forest and in seven days, won supernatural power. On his return he told her to proclaim abroad that her husband was not a *Caṇḍāla* but *Mahā Brahmā*, and that seven days later, on the night of the full moon, he would come to her, breaking through the moon’s disk. She did as he said and so it happened. The people thenceforth honoured her as a goddess; the water in which she washed her feet was used for the coronation of kings, and in one single day she received eighteen crores from those who were allowed the privilege of saluting her. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span> touched her navel with his thumb, and, knowing that she had conceived a son, admonished her to be vigilant and returned to the moon.

The son was born in the pavilion, which the people had constructed for the use of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Diṭṭhamaṅgalikā</span>, and was therefore called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇḍavya</span>. At the age of sixteen he knew all the Vedas and fed sixteen thousand Brahmins daily. On a feast day <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span> came to him, thinking to turn him from his wrong doctrines, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇḍavya</span> failed to recognize him and had him cast out by his servants, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaṇḍakucchi</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upajjhāya</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upajotiya</span>. The gods of the city thereupon grew angry and twisted the necks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇḍavya</span> and all the Brahmins so that their eyes looked over their shoulders. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Diṭṭhamaṅgalikā</span> heard of this she sought <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span>, who had left his footsteps so that she might know where he was. He asked her to sprinkle on the Brahmins water in which were dissolved the leavings of his food; to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇḍavya</span> himself was given some of the food. On recovering and seeing the plight of the Brahmins, he realized his error. The Brahmins recovered, but were shunned by their colleagues; they left the country and went to live in the kingdom of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mejjha</span>.

On the bank of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vettavatī</span> lived a Brahmin called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātimanta</span>, very proud of his birth. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span> went thither to humble the pride of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātimanta</span> and lived higher upstream. One day he nibbled a tooth stick and threw it into the river, where, lower down, it got entangled in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātimanta</span>’s hair. He was greatly annoyed and went upstream, where he found <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span> and told him that, if he stayed there any longer, at the end of seven days his head would split into seven pieces. On the seventh day <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span> stopped the sun from rising. On discovering the cause, the people dragged <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātimanta</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span> and made him ask forgiveness, falling at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span>’s feet. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātimanta</span>’s head was covered with a lump of clay, which was immersed in the water as the sun rose.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span> then went to the kingdom of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mejjha</span>, where the exiled Brahmins reported against him to the king, saying that he was a juggler and a mountebank. The king’s messengers surprised <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span> as he was eating his food beside a well, and cut off his head. He was born in the *Brahma* world. The gods were angry and wiped out the whole kingdom of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mejjha</span> by pouring on it torrents of hot ashes. Before his meeting with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Diṭṭhamaṅgalikā</span> the Bodhisatta was a mongoose tamer (*koṇḍadamaka*). But in Snp­a.i.186, he is called a *sopākajīvika*.

The story was told in reference to the attempt of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> (q.i) to torture <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piṇḍola Bhāradvāja</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇḍavya</span>. Ja.iv.375-90; the story is found also at Snp­a.i.184-93, with alterations in certain details, e.g., for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vettavatī</span> we have <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhumatī</span>; see also Mil.123ff.
