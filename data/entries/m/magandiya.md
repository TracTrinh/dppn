# Māgandiyā

## English source

Māgandiyā. Daughter of the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiya</span>. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span> rejected her father’s offer of marriage with her, her parents joined the Saṅgha, giving her in charge of her uncle, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Māgandiya</span>. The latter took her to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>, who made her his chief consort, giving her five hundred ladies in waiting. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span> was incensed against the Buddha for having called her a “vessel of filth,” and, when he came to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span>, she planned her revenge. Having discovered that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span>’s other queen, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>, and her companions were in the habit of watching for the Buddha through windows in the walls of their rooms, she told the king that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> and her friends were conspiring to kill him (Udena). For some time the king refused to believe this, but when the holes were shown to him, he had them closed up and the windows built higher.

This plan having failed, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span> hired a slave to revile and abuse the Buddha in the streets. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> suggested to the Buddha that they should go elsewhere. The Buddha answered, “I am like the elephant who has entered the fray, I must endure the darts that come upon me. After seven days the abuse ceased. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span> then persuaded her uncle to send eight live cocks to the palace and sent a page with them to the king’s drinking place. When the king asked what should be done with them, she suggested that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> and her friends should be asked to cook them for him. This the king agreed to do, but the women refused to deprive an animal of its life. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span> said they should be tested, and sent word by the page that the cocks were to be cooked for the Buddha. The page was bribed to change the live cocks for dead ones on the way, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> and her companions then cooked them and sent them to the Buddha. But even then the king, though not knowing of the exchange, would not be convinced of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>’s disloyalty.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span> then obtained a snake from her uncle with its fangs removed. This she inserted in the shell of the flute which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> carried about, closing the hole with a bunch of flowers. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> was in the habit of spending a week in turn with each of his three consorts. When he announced his intention of going to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span> begged of him not to go, saying she had had a dream and feared for his safety. But the king went and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span> went with him. As he lay asleep with the lute under his pillow she pulled out the bunch of flowers, and the snake lay coiled on his pillow. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span> screamed and accused <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> of designs on the king’s life. This time <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> believed her, and placing <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> and her friends in a line one behind the other, he sent for his bow, which could only be strung by one thousand men, and shot an arrow at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>’s breast. But by the power of her goodness the arrow failed to pierce her. Convinced of her innocence, the king pleaded for her forgiveness and gave her a boon. She chose that the Buddha should be invited to come to the palace every day, but the Buddha would not accept the invitation and sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> in his place.

Once more <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span> conspired with her uncle against <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>. They had all the pillars of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span>’s house wrapt in cloth, soaked in oil, and, when she and her women were inside, the house was set fire to. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāmāvatī</span> saw the flames spreading and exhorted her women to be self-possessed, and they attained to various fruits of the Path. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udena</span> questioned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span> very carefully, and became convinced of her share and that of her uncle in the crime. He then sent for all <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiya</span>’s relations saying that he wished to reward them. He buried them waist-deep in the palace grounds and covered them with straw; the straw was then set fire to, and when it was burnt he had their bodies ploughed with an iron plough. Pieces of flesh were ripped from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span>’s body, fried like cakes in oil, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span> was then forced to eat them. Dhp­a.i.201f., 210ff.; Ud­a.383f.; cf. Divy., 515ff., where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgandiyā</span> is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anūpamā</span>.
