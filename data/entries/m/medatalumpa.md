# Medataḷumpa

## English source

Medataḷumpa. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> village three leagues from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Naṅgaraka</span> (MN.ii.119). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> when staying there with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha Kārāyaṇa</span>, heard that the Buddha was there and visited him. On this occasion was preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammacetiyasutta</span> (*q.v.*). This was the last time that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> saw the Buddha.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span>, however, in its record (Dhp­a.i.356) of the king’s visit, calls the place <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uḷumpa</span>. On the other hand, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhima Commentary</span> (MN­a.ii.753) confirms the reading <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Medataḷumpa</span>, and says that it was so-called because *medavaṇṇa*-stones were visible there on the surface of the earth (*medavaṇṇā pāsāṇā kirettha ussannā ahesuṁ, tasmā Medataḷumpan-ti sankhaṁ gataṁ*).
