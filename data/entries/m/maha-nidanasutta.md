# Mahā Nidānasutta

## English source

Mahā Nidānasutta. The fifteenth <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghanikāya</span>, preached to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kammāssadamma</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuru</span> country. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> says that the doctrine of events as arising from causes is quite clear to him; that it only appears deep. “Say not so,” warns the Buddha, and proceeds to give a detailed exposition of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Paṭiccasamuppāda</span>, or Chain of Causation, as it comes later to be called, explaining how each link in the chain of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Saṁsāra</span> is both the effect of one factor and the cause of another. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> also discusses the idea of “soul,” the seven resting places of cognition (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">viññāṇaṭṭhiti</span>), the two spheres (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">āyatana</span>), and the eight kinds of deliverance (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vimokkha</span>). DN.ii.55-71; Thomas: cp. Cit.,197.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemā</span> had heard the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nidānasutta</span> in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> (Ap.ii.546, vs.34). On hearing it again, preached by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>, she revived her memory and became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. Ap.ii.549, vs.72.
