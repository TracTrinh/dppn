# Malaya Mahā Deva Thera

## English source

Malaya Mahā Deva Thera (*v.l.* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maliya Mahā Deva Thera</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malliyadeva Thera</span>). An *Arahant*. During the *Akkhakkhāyika* famine, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> provided him and four others with a dish of sour millet gruel, which was purchased with the proceeds of the sale of the king’s earrings (Mhv.xxxii.30). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Deva</span> took his portion to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanakūṭa</span> and shared it with nine hundred others (Mhv.xxxii.49). He was also among the eight *Arahants* who accepted a meal of pork from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāliya</span> in his previous birth as a blacksmith (Mhv­ṭ.606). He was probably so-called because he lived at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṭapabbata</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malaya</span> country. Mhv­ṭ.606 he is called *Koṭapabbatavāsika*.

It is said that for three years after his ordination <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Deva</span> lived in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇḍalārāmaka</span> *vihāra* (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Deva</span> called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maliyadeva</span> in the context (AN­a.i.22), but further on in the same passage (p.23) he is addressed as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Deva</span>). One day, while going for alms in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kallagāma</span>, near by, he was invited by an *upāsikā* to her dwelling, where she gave him a meal, and, regarding him as a son, invited him to take all his meals at her home. The invitation was accepted, and each day, after the meal, he would return thanks with the words “May you be happy and free from sorrow” (*sukhaṁ hotu, dukkhā mucca*). At the end of the rainy season he became an *Arahant*, and the chief incumbent of the *Vihāra* entrusted him with the task of preaching to the assembled people on the *Pavāraṇa* Day. The young novices informed the *upāsikā* that her “son” would preach that day, but she, thinking they were making fun of her, said that not everyone could preach. But they persuaded her to go to the *vihāra*, and, when the turn of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maliyadeva</span> came, he preached all through the night. At dawn he stopped, and the *upāsikā* became a *Sotāpanna*.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maliyadeva</span> once preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chachakkasutta</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohāpāsāda</span>, and sixty monks, who listened to him, became *Arahants*. He also preached the same *Sutta* in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Maṇḍapa</span>, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyapabbata</span>, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyavaṁsa</span> *vihāra*, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuṭāli</span> *vihāra*, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Antarasobbha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mutiṅgana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vātakapabbata</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pācīnagharaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghavāpī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lokaṇḍara</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gameṇḍavāḷa</span>, and, at each place, sixty monks attained *Arahant*-ship. At <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittalapabbata</span> he saw a monk of over sixty preparing to bathe at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuruvakatittha</span>, and asked permission to bathe him. The Elder, discovering from his conversation that he was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maliyadeva</span>, agreed to let him do so, though, he said, no one had ever touched his body during sixty years. Later in the day, the Elder begged <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maliyadeva</span> to preach to him, and this he did. Sixty monks, all over sixty, were among the audience, and at the conclusion of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chachakkasutta</span> they all became *Arahants*. The same thing happened at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa Mahā Vihāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalyāṇi</span> *vihāra*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgamahā</span> *vihara*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalacchagāma</span>, and at other places, sixty in all (MN­a.ii.1024f).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malaya Mahā Deva</span> was among those various large groups who renounced the world in the company of the Bodhisatta: the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuddāla</span> *samāgama*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mūgapakkha</span> *samāgama*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Sutasoma</span> *samāgama*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ayogharapaṇḍita</span> *samāgama* and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hatthipāla</span> *samāgama* (Ja.iv.490; also vi.30, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Maliyadeva</span> is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷavelavāsī</span>). It is said (Vsm.241) that two monks once asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malaya Mahā Deva</span> for a subject of meditation, and that he gave them the formula of the thirty-two parts of the body. Though versed in the three *Nikāyas*, the monks could not become *Sotāpannas* until they had recited the formula for a period of four months.
