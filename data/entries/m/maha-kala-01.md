# Mahā Kāḷa 01

## English source

Mahā Kāḷa 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kāḷa Thera</span>. He belonged to a merchant family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setavyā</span>, and, while on a journey to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> with five hundred carts, he heard the Buddha preach at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> and entered the Saṅgha. He lived in the charnel field meditating, and, one day, the crematrix <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷā</span>, noticing him, arranged the limbs of a recently cremated body near the Thera that he might gaze at them. With these as a topic of meditation, he soon became an *Arahant*. Thag.vss.151f.; his story is given in much greater detail at Dhp­a.i.66ff.; there he is said to have been the eldest of three brothers, of whom the others were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhima Kāḷa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Kāḷa</span>.

He went with the latter to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, where both of them joined the Saṅgha. After becoming an *Arahant*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kāḷa</span> went with the Buddha to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Setavyā</span> and dwelt in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siṁsapā</span> grove, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Kāḷa</span> accompanying him. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Kāḷa</span>’s wives invited the Buddha and the other monks to a meal, and he himself went on earlier to make arrangements. His wives disrobed him. At the end of the meal, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kāḷa</span> was left behind by the Buddha to make the thanksgiving. His eight wives surrounded him and stripped him of his robes, but, knowing their intention, he disappeared through the air.

Ninety-one *kappas* ago, while wandering near the mountain <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Urugaṇa</span>, he saw the rag robe of an ascetic and offered three *kiṅkiṇika*-flowers in its honour (Thag­a.i.271f). He is probably identical with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṁsukūlapūjaka Thera</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span>. Ap.ii.434; but see Thag­a.i.79, where the same <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> verses are quoted.
