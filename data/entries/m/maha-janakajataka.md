# Mahā Janakajātaka

## English source

Mahā Janakajātaka (Ja 539). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janaka</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span>, had two sons, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariṭṭhajanaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Polajanaka</span>. On his death, the elder came to the throne and made his brother viceroy, but, later, suspecting him of treachery, had him put in chains. Polajanaka escaped, and, when he had completed his preparations, laid siege to the city, killed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariṭṭhajanaka</span>, and seized the throne. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariṭṭhajanaka</span>’s wife escaped in disguise, taking with her a lot of treasures. She was pregnant, and as her child was the Bodhisatta, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s throne was heated, and he appeared before her as a charioteer and took her to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷa Campā</span>. There she was adopted by an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udicca</span> Brahmin as his sister and the child was born. When he played with other boys they mocked at him, calling him the widow’s son. He asked his mother what this meant, but she put him off with evasive answers until one day he bit her on the breast and insisted on being told the truth. When he was sixteen, she gave him half the treasures, and he embarked on a ship going to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suvaṇṇabhūmi</span> for trade. The ship was wrecked in mid-ocean, but nothing daunted, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janaka</span> (as the boy was called) swam valiantly for seven days, till <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇimekhalā</span>, goddess of the sea, admiring his courage, rescued him and placed him in the mango grove in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span>.

Meanwhile <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Polajanaka</span> had died and left orders that the throne should go to one who could find favour in the eyes of his daughter, should know which is the head of a square bed, could string the bow that required the strength of one thousand men, and could draw out the sixteen great treasures. No one seemed forthcoming who was able to fulfil these conditions; the ministers thereupon decked the state chariot with the five insignia of royalty and sent it out, accompanied by music. The car left the city gates, and the horses went to the mango grove and stopped at the spot where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janaka</span> lay asleep. The chaplain, seeing the auspicious marks on his feet, awoke him, and explaining to him his mission, crowned him king. When he entered the palace, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalī</span> (the late king’s daughter) was immediately won over by his appearance, and willingly agreed to be his queen. He was told of the other conditions mentioned by the dead king; he solved the riddles contained in some and fulfilled them all.

In time <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalī</span> bore him a son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghāvu</span> *kumāra*, whom, in due course, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janaka</span> made viceroy. One day <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janaka</span> went into his park, and noticing how a mango tree, which bore fruit had been plundered by his courtiers while another which was barren was left in peace, he realized that possessions meant sorrow, and retiring into a room, lived the ascetic life. His life-span was ten thousand years, of which three thousand still remained to him. After living for four months in the palace, he resolved to renounce the world, and having made his preparations, secretly left the palace. The queen met him on the stairs, but did not recognise him in his ascetic garb. On discovering his absence, she ran after him and tried by many devices to persuade him to return, but in vain. She then urged his people to follow him, but he turned them back. She, however, would not obey him, and for sixty leagues she and the people followed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janaka</span>.

The sage <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span>, dwelling in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himavā</span>, saw <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janaka</span> with his divine eye and encouraged him in his resolve, as did another ascetic, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migājina</span>, who had just risen from a *jhāna*. Thus they journeyed on till they reached the village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thūṇa</span>. There the king saw a dog running away with a morsel of roasted flesh, which it dropped in its flight. The king picked it up, cleaned it, and ate it. The queen, very disgusted, felt that he was not worthy to be a king. Further on they saw a girl shaking sand in a winnowing basket; on one arm she wore a single bracelet, on the other arm, two. The two bracelets jingled, while the single one was noiseless. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janaka</span> pointed out the moral of this to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalī</span>, and she agreed to go a different way, but soon came running back to him and followed him till they came across a fletcher, straightening an arrow, looking at it with one eye only. On being questioned by the king, he answered that the wide horizon of two eyes served but to distract the view. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalī</span> still refused to leave him till, on the edge of a forest, he told her there could be no more intercourse between them, and she fell senseless. The king rushed into the forest, while the ministers revived the queen. When she recovered the king was no more to be seen, and she returned to the city. *Thūpas* were erected on various spots connected with the king’s renunciation, and the queen lived as an ascetic in the royal garden of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span>.

The story was told in reference to the Buddha’s Renunciation.

- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Maṇimekhalā</span> is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Uppalavaṇṇā</span>,
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Nārada</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sāriputta</span>,
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Migājina</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Moggallāna</span>,
- the girl with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Khemā</span>,
- the fletcher with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Ānanda</span>,
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Sīvalī</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Rāhulamātā</span>,
- <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Dīghāvu</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Rāhula</span>,
- while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mahā Janaka</span> was the Bodhisatta (Ja.iii.30-68).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> exemplifies *viriyapāramitā*. Bv­a.51.
