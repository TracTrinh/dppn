# Migāra 01

## English source

Migāra 01. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>. His son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇavaḍḍhana</span>, married <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>. He was evidently not as rich as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>’s father, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya</span>, for he drove back, on the plea that he could not afford to feed them, the large retinue who wished to follow <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>, to her new home. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> was a follower of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span>, and was angry when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> refused to wait on them and pay homage to them when they visited his house. One day, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> was eating and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> was standing by his side fanning him, a monk stopped at their door, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> stepped aside that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> might see him. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> refused to notice the monk, whom, therefore, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> asked to go away, saying that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> ate “stale food” (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">purānaṁ</span>). This greatly annoyed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span>, and he ordered her to be cast out of the house. But the servants refused to carry out his orders, and he was obliged to agree to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>’s suggestion that the matter should be submitted for arbitration to the eight householders who had accompanied her to enquire into disputes of such a nature. To them, therefore, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> recited a list of all his grievances against <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>, but she was adjudged quite innocent and threatened to return at once to her father. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> begged her to stay, and she agreed on condition that he invited the Buddha and his monks for a meal. He did so, but the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span> would not allow him to wait upon the Buddha. At the conclusion of the meal, however, out of politeness, he insisted on listening to the Buddha’s sermon, if only from behind a screen. At the conclusion of the sermon <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>, and, realizing the error of his ways, adopted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> as his mother by sucking her breast. Henceforth <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāramātā</span>. The next day, again, the Buddha was invited, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span>’s wife became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>. From that day onwards they kept open house for the Buddha and his monks. As a token of his gratitude, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> held a great festival in honour of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>, to which the Buddha and his monks were invited. She was bathed in sixteen pots of perfumed water and presented with a jeweled ornament called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ghanamaṭṭhakapasādhana</span> (Dhp­a.i.387ff.; AN­a.i.220; MN­a.i.471f). It is probably this same <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> whose grandson was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāḷha</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāranattā</span>; but see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāra</span> (2).

## Vietnamese translation

Chưa dịch.
