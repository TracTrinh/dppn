# Mahā Abhaya 02

## English source

Mahā Abhaya 02. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thera</span>. Usually called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Dīghabhāṇaka</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span>. He was evidently an eminent Thera. It is said that when news arrived that the brigand <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span> was coming to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyagiri</span> to pillage it, the patrons of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> informed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya Thera</span> of this (Vin-a.ii.474f.; referred to at Dhs-a.399). He asked them to collect various kinds of food and drink wherewith to entertain the brigand. The latter was greatly pleased, and, on learning that this hospitality was shown him at the instigation of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya Thera</span><span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">,</span> he visited the Elder and took upon himself the protection of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> from all danger. Later the monks questioned the right of the Elder to entertain a robber with what belonged to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span>, but he convened an assembly of the monks and convinced them that he was guiltless, as his act had been solely for the protection of the belongings of the Saṅgha. This story shows that the Elder lived at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyagiri</span>.

On another occasion (MN­a.i.65f) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya Thera</span> preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Ariyavaṁsa</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">paṭipadā</span>, and the whole village (? <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Gāma</span>) came to hear him. After the sermon he received great honour. An elderly monk was full of envy and abused him, saying that under guise of preaching he had filled the night with noise. The two monks had to travel along the same road for the distance of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gāvuta</span>, and all the way the old monk abused <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span>. When their roads separated, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span> worshipped the monk and took leave of him and said, when asked later, that he had heard none of the abuse because he had been all the time engaged in meditation. One day, a certain woman who had come all the way from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ullabha Kolakaṇṇikā</span>, a distance of five leagues, to hear <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span> preach the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariyavaṁsa</span>, found that he was about to arise without finishing the sermon, and reminded him that he was omitting the most important part. The monk praised her and continued to preach till dawn, when the woman became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span> (AN­a.i.386). It is said that once <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span> wanted to know if a certain <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">piṇḍapātika</span> monk were a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">puthujjana</span> or not. While this monk was bathing at the mouth of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalyāṇī</span> River, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span> told a novice to dive into the water and catch hold of his foot. The monk, thinking it was a crocodile, screamed with fear, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span> knew that he was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">puthujjana</span> (MN­a.ii.869). Thieves once bound an Elder with creepers and made him lie down. A jungle fire spread to him, but before the creepers could be cut, he established insight and died an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span>, coming along with five hundred others, saw the body, cremated it, and had a shrine built over the remains (Vsm.36).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span>’s opinion was evidently greatly respected, and he is mentioned as having been consulted by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Dhammarakkhita</span> (Vibh­a.81) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallaka</span> (Vsm.266) on problems connected with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhaya</span> is mentioned among those who could remember very early incidents in their lives. When he was only nine days old, his mother bent down to embrace him, but her hair became loose and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sumana</span>-flowers she wore on her head fell on him, hurting him. In later life he could remember this incident. DN­a.ii.530; MNid-a.234.
