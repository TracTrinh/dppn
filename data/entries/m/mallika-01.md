# Mallikā 01

## English source

Mallikā 01. Chief queen of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>. She was the daughter of the chief garland-maker of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>, and was very good and beautiful. When she was sixteen she was, one day, on her way to the garden with some companions, carrying with her three portions of sour gruel in a basket. Meeting the Buddha, she offered them to him and worshipped him. The Buddha, seeing her wrapt in joy, smiled, and, in answer to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>’s question, said she would be chief queen of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> that very day. Ja.iii.405; SN­a.i.110ff. It was to explain <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span>’s good fortune that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kummāsapiṇḍajātaka</span> (Ja 416) was preached.

It happened that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, having suffered defeat at the hands of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> that very day, was passing by and entered the flower garden, attracted by Mallikā’s voice. Mallikā, seeing him coming, and noting his weariness, seized his horse’s bridle. The king, discovering that she was unmarried, dismounted, and, having rested awhile, his head on her lap, entered the town with her and took her to her own house. In the evening he sent a chariot for her, and with great honour and pomp brought her from her own home, set her on a heap of jewels and anointed her chief queen. From that day onward she was the beloved and devoted wife of the king and an undeviating follower of the Buddha (Dhp­a.iii.121f). The king found her sagacious and practical minded and consulted her and accepted her advice when in difficulty – e.g., in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asadisadāna</span>, wherein he wished to excel his subjects, and again when he was troubled by evil dreams as narrated in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Supīnajātaka</span> (Ja 77). Dhp­a.ii.8ff. says that Mallikā called the king a simpleton for putting his faith in Brahmins and took him to the Buddha, and while the king sat trembling, asked the questions for him and had them explained.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> states how Mallikā saved many innocent lives from being sacrificed, and the Buddha declared that in a past life too, as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dinnā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), she had saved the lives of a large number of people by her wisdom (Dhp­a.ii.15f).

Both Mallikā and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>’s other queen, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsabhakhattiyā</span>, desired to learn the Dhamma, and, at their request conveyed through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>, the Buddha asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> to visit the palace regularly and teach them the Dhamma. Ānanda found in Mallikā an apt and ready pupil, conscientious in her work; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsabhakhattiyā</span> was not so devoted to her duties. Dhp­a.i.382f. For an incident connected with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>’s visit to the palace, see Vin.iv.158f.

Mallika’s knowledge of the Dhamma made her wiser than <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> would have desired, and he once, in a moment of great affection, asked if anyone were dearer to her than her own “self.” “No, Sire,” was the answer; the king was evidently greatly disappointed, for he sought the Buddha, who explained to him that Mallikā, in making that answer, had uttered a great truth (SN.i.75; Ud.v.1). Mallikā, though an exemplary wife, was not without lapses. Reference is made to the quarrels she had with her husband, once, at least, on the question of conjugal rights, as a result of which they both sulked and had to be reconciled by the Buddha. Ja.iv.437; also Ja.iii.20; in these quarrels the king was probably more to blame than Mallikā; it is said that until reconciled by the Buddha he ignored her very existence, saying that prosperity had turned her head.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapada Commentary</span> (Dhp­a.iii.119ff) relates a ridiculous story about her misbehaviour with a dog in the bath house. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> was a witness of this scene, but she was able to convince him that it was the fault of the lighting of the bath house. Nevertheless, it is said that at the moment of her death she recollected this misdeed, and, as a result, was reborn in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avīci</span>. The king was overcome by grief at Mallika’s death, and, after the funeral rites, went to the Buddha to ask where she had been reborn. The Buddha, not wishing him to know, caused the king to forget the question, every time he came to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span>, for a whole week, till Mallikā’s suffering in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avīci</span> was over; then he allowed the question to be asked, and he was able to assure <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> that she had been reborn in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tusita</span> and to console him in his grief. It is said (AN.iii.57) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> was on a visit to the Buddha when a man came with the whispered message that the queen was dead. It was a terrible shock, “his shoulders drooped, his mouth fell, and he sat brooding, unable to speak.”

Mallikā had a daughter by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span>; no mention is made of a son. Probably <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajīrī</span>, who is spoken of as the king’s only daughter (MN.ii.110). He is said to have been disappointed on hearing that the child was a girl; but the Buddha assured him that women were sometimes wiser than men (SN.i.86f).

Mallikā is mentioned (Mil. 115, 291) as one of seven persons whose acts of devotion bore fruit in this life and whose fame reached even to the gods. Only one instance is on record of Mallikā asking a question of the Buddha. She wished to know why some women are plain, others beautiful, some rich, and others poor. And the Buddha explained to her the reasons for these discrepancies (See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikāsutta</span> 1).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piyajātikasutta</span> (MN.ii.106ff) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> is said to have taunted her because “her recluse <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>” had said that dear ones bring sorrow and tribulation. “If the Lord says so, it must be so,” she replies; but secretly sends <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālijaṅgha</span> to find out from the Buddha himself if he had said so and why. Having learnt the facts, she faces <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> again, and convinced him too that the Buddha is right.

Mallikā had a garden, called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikārāma</span>, in which was a Hall among the Deispyros trees (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t18">tindukācīra</span>) set apart for religious discussions between members of various sects (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">samayappavādaka</span>). MN.ii.22; MN­a.710; DN.i.178; see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekasālā</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span> is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujātā</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujātajātaka</span> (Ja 269, Ja.iii.22), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kinnarī</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhallāṭiyajātaka</span> (Ja 504, Ja.iv.444) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambulā</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambulajātaka</span> (Ja 519, Ja.v.98). In all three births <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> was her husband. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallikā</span> is included in a list of eminent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t18">upāsikās</span> (AN.iv.348).
