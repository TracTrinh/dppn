# Mahā Phussadeva Thera

## English source

Mahā Phussadeva Thera. Generally called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ālindakavāsī</span> Mahā Phussadeva. For twenty-one years he practised meditation on his way up and down to the village for alms (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gatapaccāgatikavatta</span>). People working in the fields, seeing him constantly stop and walk back again, would wonder why he did so. But he did not heed their curiosity, and after twenty years he became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. That night the deity at the end of his walk illuminated it with the radiance of her fingers, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahma</span>, and other gods came to do him honour. His colleague, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vanavāsī Mahā Tissa</span>, asked him the next day the reason for all the light, but he evaded the question (SN­a.iii.154f.; Vibh-a.352; MN­a.i.208f; Snp­a.i.55f). It is said (MN­a.i.524) that during the period of his meditations, he wept every <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pavāraṇa</span> day to see that he was yet a “learner.”
