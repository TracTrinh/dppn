# Mahā Haṁsajātaka

## English source

Mahā Haṁsajātaka (Ja 534). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemā</span>, wife of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyama</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, had a dream, after which she longed to see a golden *haṁsa* preach the law from the royal throne. When the king came to know this, he consulted various people, and, acting on their advice, had a pond dug to the north of the city in the hope of enticing a golden *haṁsa* there, and appointed a fowler, who came to be called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemaka</span>, to look after the pond.

The plan succeeded. Five different kinds of geese came: the grass geese, the yellow geese, the scarlet geese, the white geese, and the *pāka* geese.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhataraṭṭha</span>, king of the golden geese, who lived in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittakūṭa</span>, had taken as wife a *pāka* goose, and at the repeated suggestion of his minister, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumukha</span>, arrived with his flock of ninety thousand, to see the wonderful pond at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>. Khemaka saw them and waited his opportunity. On the seventh day he found it, and set a snare in which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhataraṭṭha</span> was caught. At his cry of alarm the flock fled, with the exception of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumukha</span>, who stayed and asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemaka</span> for permission to take <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhataraṭṭha</span>’s place. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumukha</span> heard why they had been caught, he asked that both he and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhataraṭṭha</span> should be taken before <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyama</span>. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṁyama</span> heard of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumukha</span>’s devotion he was greatly touched, and showed the *haṁsas* every possible honour, after asking their forgiveness for the way they had been treated. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhataraṭṭha</span> preached to the queen and the royal household, and, having exhorted the king to rule righteously, returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittakūṭa</span>.

The story was told in reference to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>’s attempt to sacrifice his own life for that of the Buddha, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālāgiri</span> (*q.v.*) was sent to kill him.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemaka</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Channa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemā</span> was the Therī <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemā</span>, the king was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumukha</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhataraṭṭha</span> the Bodhisatta. Ja.v.354-82; cp. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Haṁsajātaka</span>.
