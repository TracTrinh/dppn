# Mandhātā

## English source

Mandhātā. A primeval king, descended from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sammata</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Roja</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vararoja</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalyāṇa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Varakaḷyāna</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uposatha</span>, the last named being his father. He was thus an ancestor of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span>. Ja.ii.311; iii.454; Mvu.ii.2; Dpv.iii.5; but see Snp­a.i.352, where the genealogy is slightly different.

He had the seven Jewels of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span> and his four Supernatural Powers. When he clenched his left hand and touched it with his right, a shower of the seven kinds of jewels fell knee-deep from the sky. For eighty-four thousand years he was a prince, for eighty-four thousand a viceroy, and for eighty-four thousand more a king. His life-span was an *asaṅkheyya*. But he grew discontented, and, at the suggestion of his ministers, visited the *Deva* world. First he went to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cātummahārājika</span> world, where he ruled; but still unsatisfied, he went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>. There <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> welcomed him and gave him half his kingdom. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mandhātā</span> ruled there during the lifetime of thirty-six Sakkas, each <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s life lasting for thirty-six million years and sixty times one hundred thousand. As time went on, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mandhātā</span>’s craving increased; he wished to kill <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> and gain the whole kingdom. Because of his greed his power waned, and he fell from heaven into his park. The gardener announced his arrival to the royal family, and they provided a resting place for him and there he lay dying. When asked for a message for his people, he wished them to know how even he, in spite of his great pomp and power, had to die (Ja.ii.311ff.; DN­a.ii.481f.; MN­a.i.182f.; cp. Divy.210ff).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mandhātā</span> is identified with the Bodhisatta (Ja.ii.314). His son was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Varamandhātā</span>, whose son was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cara</span> and grandson <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upacara</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apacara</span>, Ja.iii.454; Dpv.iii.6). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mandhātā</span> ruled at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> (Snp­a.ii.413; DN­a.i.132), and is named as one of the four persons who attained, while yet in their earthly bodies, to the glory of the gods. Mil. 115, 291; MN­a.ii.737f.; the others being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Guttila</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sādhīna</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nimi</span>.

He is considered as chief of those given up to the pleasures of the senses and as an example of one whose desires could never be satisfied. AN.ii.17; AN­a.ii.474; e.g., Vibh­a.506. Thīg.486.

When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mandhātā</span> went to the *Deva* world he was accompanied by inhabitants of all the four continents. After his return to earth, however, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkaratana</span>, stuck fast in the ground, and the others could not return to their homes. They thereupon begged for the intervention of the minister (*Parināyaka*), who was carrying on the government with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mandhātā</span>’s shoes on the throne. He gave them lands in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>. There those who came from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pubbavideha</span> called their land <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videharaṭṭha</span>; those from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparagoyāna</span> called it <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparantajanapada</span>, and those from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> dwelt in what afterwards came to be known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kururaṭṭha</span>. DN­a.ii.482; MN­a.i.183f.
