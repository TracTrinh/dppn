# Malaya 02

## English source

Malaya 02. The mountainous country of Ceylon, originally the home of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pulindā</span> (Mhv.vii.68; see Mhv.Trs.60, n.5). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> fled from his father’s wrath, it was in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malaya</span> that he hid (Mhv.xxiv.7). In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malaya</span> was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭhakolalena</span>, from which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> obtained silver for the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> (Mhv.xxviii.20). The hill country provided protection from marauders who invaded Ceylon (e.g., in the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṭṭagāmaṇī</span>; Mhv.xxxiii.62; also xxxv.26), and also from those causing danger to the rightful ruler (e.g., ibid., xxxvi.50; Cv.xli.20; l.20). When Buddhism was threatened by the activities of unbelievers who had obtained possession of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>, it was to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malaya</span> and to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohaṇa</span> that the monks fled in order to save themselves and their teaching (e.g., Mhv.xxxvii.6). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malaya</span> also afforded shelter to rebels against the government both during preparations for attack and, if necessary, during their flight (e.g., Cv.xli.10; xliv.62; xlviii.98; *li*.112f.; lvii.47, 57).

In later times <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malaya</span> was treated as a special province, and was in charge of an official called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malayarāja</span>, who was generally the king’s younger son, the elder being viceroy in charge of the Eastern Province (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pācīnadesa</span>). The district of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇadesa</span> was included in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malaya</span> (See Cv.xli.33ff.; lii.68; Cv.Trs.i.54, n.4; but see Cv.xlii.6, 10; xliv.43 li.13; liii.36), but it was later separated (Cv.li.8). The *Yuvarāja* himself was sometimes *Malayarājā*, particularly when the other provinces were in the hands of enemies (e.g., Cv.lviii.7). Mention is also made (Cv.lxix.6) of a *Malayarājā* who was in charge of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Damiḷa</span> army (probably of mercenaries). In times of war the people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malaya</span> usually gave a great deal of trouble as the country was difficult of access (e.g., Cv.lxx.30). Some of the villages in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malaya</span> were composed of only one house. Vin-a.ii.298.
