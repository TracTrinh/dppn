# Milakkhatissa Thera

## English source

Milakkhatissa Thera. He was a hunter who lived near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gāmeṇḍavāla</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohaṇa</span>. One day he caught an animal, which he killed and cooked, and then was filled with a great thirst. Looking for water, he came to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span>. There he drank ten pots of water, but his thirst was still unquenched, and while he complained bitterly about the absence of water, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Piṇḍapātikatissa</span> Thera heard him and, looking about, saw plenty of water. He then knew that the man’s evil <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kamma</span> was asserting itself. The Elder poured water on to the man’s hands, but it all dried-up. The man, realizing his wickedness, was greatly alarmed, and went and set all the captive animals free and destroyed his traps. He then returned to the monastery and asked to be ordained. His request was granted, and the Elder gave him a formula for meditation. One day, while learning the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadūtasutta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> wished to know how fierce were the fires of hell, and his teacher showed him how one spark of the fire could reduce to ashes a whole heap of wood. This induced <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> to put forth even greater effort, and he spent all his time in meditation living sometimes in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittalapabbata</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> and sometimes in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gāmeṇḍavāla</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> with a wet blanket round his head and his feet in water. Then, one day, he heard a novice recite the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aruṇavatīsutta</span> and he became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Anāgāmī</span>, attaining <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship in due course (AN­a.i.21f.; SN­a.ii.199f).

He is quoted as an example of one who strove hard to rid himself of sloth and torpor, e.g., AN­a.i.29; Snp­a.i.236.
