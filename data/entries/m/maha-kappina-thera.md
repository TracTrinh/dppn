# Mahā Kappina Thera

## English source

Mahā Kappina Thera. One of the most eminent disciples of the Buddha, considered foremost among those who taught the monks (*bhikkuovādakānaṁ*) (AN.i.25). He was older than the Buddha, and was born in a frontier kingdom three hundred *yojanas* in extent, in the city of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkuṭavatī</span>. On the death of his father he became *rājā* under the name of Mahā Kappina. His chief wife was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anojā</span>, from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgala</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span> kingdom. She had been his companion in good works in past births. Every morning Mahā Kappina would send men out of the four gates of the city to stop any scholarly or learned men who might happen to pass along the road, and then to return and tell him of them. He owned five horses: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puppha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vālavāhana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pupphavāhana</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Supatta</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Supatta</span> he alone rode, the others were used by his messengers. One day, after the Buddha’s appearance in the world, traders came from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkuṭavatī</span> and, after disposing of their goods, went to see Mahā Kappina. He received them and asked them about their country and the teaching (*Sāsana*) which they followed. “Sire,” they replied, “we cannot tell you with unwashed mouths.” A golden jug of water was brought, and with cleansed mouths and clasped hands they told the king of the appearance of the Buddha. At the word “*Buddha*” Kappina’s body was suffused with rapture. He made them utter the word three times, giving them one hundred thousand pieces. The men told him also of the Dhamma and the Saṅgha, and he trebled his gifts and forthwith renounced the world, followed by his ministers. They set forth to find the Buddha, and reached the bank of a river which they crossed by an “Act of Truth,” saying, “If this teacher be a Sammāsambuddha, let not even a hoof of these horses be wetted.” In this manner they crossed three rivers: the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aravacchā</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nīlavāhana</span> (*q.v.*), and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candabhāgā</span>. The Buddha perceived them with his divine eye, and after he had eaten at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, went through the air to the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candabhāgā</span> (one hundred and twenty *yojanas*, says Ja.iv.180; see also Snp­a.ii.440) and sat down under the great banyan tree facing the landing stage of the river, sending forth Buddha rays. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kappina</span> and his men saw him and prostrated themselves. The Buddha taught them the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span>, and they became *Arahants* and joined the Saṅgha, the formula “*Ehi bhikkhu*” being their sanction and their ordination. But see Vsm.393, where it says that at the end of the sermon <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kappina</span> became only an *Anāgāmī* and his followers *Sotāpannas*.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anojā</span> and the wives of Kappina’s ministers hearing that their husbands had renounced the world and gone to see the Buddha, determined to do likewise. They crossed the river in the same way as Kappina and his retinue, and approached the Buddha as he sat under the banyan tree on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candabhāgā</span>. The Buddha made the husbands and wives invisible to each other and preached to the latter. They became *Sotāpannas* and were ordained by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uppalavaṇṇā</span>, the Buddha taking the monks to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>. Mahā Kappina spent his days in the ecstasy of *jhāna*, and so full of happiness was he that he constantly repeated “*aho sukhaṁ, aho sukhaṁ*,” which made the monks suspect that he was longing for the pleasures of kingship which he had left behind, until the Buddha dispelled their doubts.

One day the Buddha discovered that Kappina lived inactively, enjoying his happiness, and that he never taught anybody (Vin.i.105 records that when Kappina was in the Deer Park at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maddakucchi</span> he wondered whether he need attend the *uposatha* ceremonies, since he himself was pure. The Buddha appeared before him, telling him to go). He sent for him and asked him to teach the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span> to his associates. This Kappina did, and at the end of a single sermon one thousand listening recluses became *Arahants*, hence the title conferred on him.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>, Kappina had registered a vow to become chief among admonishers of monks, having seen a similar honour conferred on a disciple of the Buddha. He was at that time an assessor (*akkhadassa*) of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsavatī</span>, and having invited the Buddha and his monks entertained them with great honour. In another birth he was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyan</span>, and waited upon five hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> and gave them robes. The story of the entertainment of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> is given at length in Dhp­a.ii.112ff., and the number given there is one thousand. They came to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, but the king, occupied with the ploughing festival, asked them to return on the third day. The wife of the senior weaver of a village near by heard this and invited the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> to her village, where there were one thousand artisans. On the invitation being accepted, she returned quickly to the village, told the people of what she had done, and they all made the necessary preparations, each family looking after one <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>, by their own wish, stayed on for three months, the same woman seeing to all their comforts. At the end of their visit, she persuaded each family to give a set of robes to its own <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>. The senior weaver was Kappina and his wife <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anojā</span>.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span>, he was the leader of a guild of one thousand men and built a great *pariveṇa* containing one thousand rooms. AN­a.i.175ff.; Thag­a.i.507ff.; SN­a.ii.172ff.; Dhp­a.ii.117ff. gives a more detailed and slightly different version; cp. Avś.ii.102f.

It is said that once <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> was preaching and that all the householders of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, with their families, went to hear him (Dhp­a.ii.115f). Scarcely had they entered the monastery when there was a heavy downpour of rain. Those who had friends among the novices and monks found shelter in their cells, the others were unprotected. The senior householder then suggested that they should build a great monastery so that all might be sheltered in future; the others agreeing, he himself gave one thousand, each of the other men five hundred, and each woman two hundred and fifty. The monastery had one thousand pinnacles, and when money ran short, each gave half as much again. At the dedication ceremony the festival lasted for seven days. The senior householder’s wife, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anojā</span>, offered the Buddha a casket of *anoja*-flowers and placed at his feet a garment of the colour of the flowers worth one thousand, and made a wish that in future births her body should be of the colour of the *anoja*-flower.

Although Kappina was famed as a teacher of monks, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span>, curiously enough, contains verses in which he admonishes the nuns (*bhikkhunīyo*) (Thag.vss. 547-556; Thag­a.i.511).

Kappina is described by the Buddha as pale (? *odāta*), thin, and having a prominent nose (*tanukaṁ tuṅganāsikaṁ*). He possessed great *iddhi*-powers and had attained every *samāpatti*, which could be attained (Ja.ii.284). (It was owing to his *iddhi* powers that he was able to follow the Buddha to the *Brahma* world, SN.i.145; see also SN.v.315, where he is described as *samādhibhāvanīya*). It has been remarked (*Brethren*, 257 n.2) that the verses attributed to him are, for the most part, more gnomic sayings of popular philosophy than genuine Dhamma, and that they would have befitted an early Greek Pagan. Mrs. Rhys Davids has an interesting theory that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kappina</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji</span>’s teacher (JRAS. 1927, ii.p.206f; also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākya</span>, p.140).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kappina</span> was quite often in the company of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, and it is said (Thag.vs.1086) that once, seeing the profound homage the gods payed to his colleague, he smiled by way of congratulation.

See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kappinasutta</span>.
