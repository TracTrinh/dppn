# Makkhali Gosāla

## English source

Makkhali Gosāla. One of the six heretical teachers contemporaneous with the Buddha. He held that there is no cause, either ultimate or remote, for the depravity of beings or for their rectitude. DN.i.53 f.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makkhali</span>, his views and his followers are also referred to at MN.i.231, 238, 483, 516f.; SN.i.66, 68; iii.211; iv.398; AN.i.33f., 286; iii.276, 384; also Ja.i.493, 509; SN.iii.69 ascribes the first portion of the account of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makkhali</span>’s views (as given in DN.i.53) that there is no cause, no reason for depravity or purity to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūraṇa Kassapa</span>. AN.i.286 apparently confounds <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makkhali</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajita Kesakambala</span>, and AN.iii.383f. represents <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūraṇa Kassapa</span> as though he were a disciple of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makkhali</span>.

The attainment of any given condition or character does not depend either on one’s own acts, nor on the acts of another, nor on human effort. There is no such thing as power or energy or human strength or human vigour. All beings (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sattā</span>), all lives (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pāṇā</span>), all existent things (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">bhūtā</span>), all living substances (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jīvā</span>), are bent this way and that by their fate, by the necessary conditions of the class to which they belong, by their individual nature; it is according to their position in one or other of the six classes (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">abhijāti</span>) that they experience ease or pain.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (DN­a.i.160 ff.) gives details of these four classes showing how they are meant to include all that has life on this earth, from men down to plants. But the explanation is very confused and makes the terms by no means mutually exclusive.

There are fourteen hundred thousands of principle genera or species (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pamukhayoniyo</span>), again six thousand others and again six hundred. There are five hundred kinds of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kamma</span> – there are sixty-two paths (or modes of conduct), sixty-two periods, six classes among men, eight stages of a prophet’s existence (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">atthapurisabhūmi</span>), forty-nine hundred kinds of occupation, forty-nine hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ājīvakas</span>, forty-nine hundred Wanderers (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Paribbājaka</span>), forty-nine hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> abodes (or species), two thousand sentient existences (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vīse indriyasate</span>), three thousand infernal states, thirty-six celestial, mundane or passionate grades (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">rajodhātuyo</span>), seven classes of animate beings (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">saññigabbhā</span>), or beings with the capacity of generating by means of separate sexes, seven of inanimate production (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">asaññigabbhā</span>), seven of production by grafting (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nigaṇṭhagabbhā</span>), seven grades of gods, men, devils, great lakes, precipices, dreams.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> gives them as babyhood, playtime, trial time, erect time, learning time, ascetic time, prophet time, and prostrate time, with (very necessary) comments on each.

There are eighty-four thousand periods during which both fools and wise alike, wandering in transmigration, shall at last make an end of pain. This cannot be done by virtue, or penance, or righteousness. Ease and pain, measured out as it were with a measure, cannot be altered in the course of transmigration (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Saṁsāra</span>); there can be neither increase nor decrease thereof both fools and wise alike, wandering in transmigration, exactly for the allotted term, shall then, and then only, make an end of pain.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makkhali</span>’s views as given in the Buddhist books are difficult to understand, the Commentators themselves finding it a hopeless task. He seems to have believed in infinite gradations of existence; in his view, each individual thing has eternal existence, if not individually, at least in type. He evidently had definite conceptions of numerous grades of beings, celestial, infernal and mundane, as also of the infinity of time and the recurrent cycles of existence. He seems to have conceived the world as a system in which everything has a place and a function assigned to it, a system in which chance has no place and which admits of no other cause whatever of the depravity or purity of beings, but that which is implied in the word Fate or Destiny (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">niyati</span>). All types of things and all species of beings, however, are individually capable of transformation that is of elevation or degradation in type. His theory of purification through transmigration (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Saṁsārasuddhi</span>) probably meant perfection through transformation (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pariṇatā</span>) – transformation which implies not only the process of constant change, but also a fixed orderly mode of progression and retrogression. All things must, in course of time, attain perfection (for a discussion on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makkhali</span> and his doctrines see Barua: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pre-Buddhistic Indian Philosophy</span>, 297ff). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makkhali</span>’s followers are known as the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ājīvakas</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>).

According to the books, the Buddha considered <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makkhali</span> as the most dangerous of the heretical teachers: “I know not of any other single person fraught with such loss to many folk, such discomfort, such sorrow to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> and men, as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makkhali</span>, the infatuate (AN.i.33). The Buddha also considered his view the meanest – just as the hair blanket is reckoned the meanest of all woven garments, even so, of all the teachings of recluses, that of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makkhali</span> is the meanest (AN.i.286). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> (DN­a.i.166f) draws particular distinction between the moral effect of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makkhali</span>’s doctrine on the one hand and that of the doctrines of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūraṇa Kassapa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajita</span> on the other. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūraṇa</span>, by his theory of the passivity of the soul, denied action; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajita</span>, by his annihilationistic theory denied retribution; whereas <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makkhali</span>, by his doctrine of fate or non-causation, denied both action and its result.

Very little is known of the name and the life of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makkhali</span>. The Buddhist records call him <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makkhali Gosāla</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> explains (DN­a.i.143f; MN­a.i.422) that he was once employed as a servant; one day, while carrying an oil-pot along a muddy road, he slipped and fell through carelessness, although warned thus by his master: “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mā khali</span>,” (stumble not) – hence his name. When he found that the oil pot was broken, he fled; his master chased him and caught him by his garment, but he left it and ran along naked. He was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gosāla</span>, because he was born in a cow shed. According to Jaina records (e.g. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Uvāsagadasāo</span>, p.1), he is called Gosāla Maṅkhaliputta; he was born at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saravana</span> near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, his father’s name being Maṅkhali and his mother’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā</span>. His father was a Maṅkha i.e., a dealer in pictures – and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gosāla</span> followed this profession until he became a monk.

The philosopher’s true name (Barua., op.cit., 298) seems to have been Maskarin, the Jaina Prakrit form of which is Maṅkhali and the Pāli form Makkhali. “Maskarin” is explained by Pāṇinī (VI.i.154) as “one who carries a bamboo staff” (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t18">maskara</span>). A Maskarin is also known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Ekadaṇḍī</span>. According to Patañjali (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Mahā Bhāsya</span> iii.96), the name indicates a School of Wanderers who were called Maskarins, not so much because they carried a bamboo staff as because they denied the freedom of the will. The Maskarins were thus fatalists or determinists.
