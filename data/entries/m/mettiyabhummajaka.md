# Mettiyabhummajakā

## English source

Mettiyabhummajakā. A group of monks, followers of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mettiya</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhummajaka</span> forming part of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chabbaggiyā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). They lived near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. Vin-a.iii.614; Ja.ii.387; Vin-a.iii.579 says they were the chief leaders of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chabbaggiyā</span>.

Twice they brought an unfounded charge of breach of morality against <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba Mallaputta</span>, who seems to have earned their special dislike. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba</span> was in charge of the distribution of alms at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ārāma</span> where they stayed, and one day it was their turn to receive alms from a certain householder who had a reputation for providing good food. When, however, the man heard from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba</span> that it was the turn of the Mettiyabhummajakā to receive his hospitality, he was much displeased, and ordered his female slave to look after them. The monks were greatly annoyed, and accused <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba</span> of having slandered them to the householder. They, therefore, persuaded a nun named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mettiyā</span> to go to the Buddha and accuse <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba</span> of having violated her chastity. The charge was investigated and proved false and the nun expelled from the Saṅgha (Vin.ii.76ff.; iii.160ff).

On another occasion, these monks persuaded a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavī</span>, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaḍḍha</span>, who was their patron and friend, to go to the Buddha and charge <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba</span> with having had relations with his wife. This, too, was proved false, and other monks refused to accept alms from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaḍḍha</span> until he had confessed his guilt (Vin.ii.124ff). One day, while descending from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span>, the Mettiyabhummajakā monks saw a heifer with a she-goat and the idea occurred to them of calling the heifer <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba</span> and the she-goat <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mettiyā</span> and then of spreading the story that they had seen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba</span> mating with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mettiyā</span>. Vin.iii.166ff.; see also iv. 37f.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammapāla</span> mentions a tradition, but contradicts it, that the persecution of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba</span> by the Mettiyabhummajakā was so persistent that in the end he committed suicide to escape from it (Ud­a.431). In any case, they managed to bring him into disfavour with the laity, and the Buddha had to take special steps to reinstate him in their esteem (Ud­a.434). The incident regarding the charge brought by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mettiyā</span> seems to have given much trouble to later commentators. Vin-a.iii.582 says that there was a great dispute about this between the monks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> and those of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhayagiri</span>. In the end, King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhātikatissa</span> intervened and had the matter settled by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīgha Kārāyaṇa</span>.
