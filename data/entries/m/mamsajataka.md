# Maṁsajātaka

## English source

Maṁsajātaka (Ja 315). The four sons of four rich merchants of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> were once sitting at the crossroads, and, seeing a deer stalker hawking venison in a cart, one of them proposed to get some flesh from him. So he went up to the man and said, “ Hi! My man! Give me some meat,” and the hunter gave him some skin and bone; the second, going up to him addressed him as “Elder Brother” and was given a joint; but the third cajoled him, calling him “Father,” and received a savoury piece of meat; while the fourth (the Bodhisatta) addressed him as “Friend,” and was given the whole of the rest of the deer, and the meat was conveyed to his house in the man’s cart.

The story was related in reference to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>. Some monks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, having taken oil as a purgative, wished for some dainty food. They sent their attendants to beg in the cooks’ quarters, but these had to come back empty-handed. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> met them, and, having heard their story, returned with them to the same street; the people gave him a full measure of dainty fare, which was distributed among the sick monks. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> is identified with the hunter of the story. Ja.iii.48-51.
