# Mahā Hatthipadopamasutta

## English source

Mahā Hatthipadopamasutta. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> addresses the monks at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> and tells them that, just as the foot of every creature will fit in the elephant’s footprint, even so are all right states of mind comprised within the Four Noble Truths. He then goes on to explain that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dukkha</span> consists of the five <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">upādānakkhandhas</span>: visible shapes, feelings, perception, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">saṅkhāras</span> and consciousness. The constituents of these attachments are the four principle elements: earth, water, fire, and air. Each element is of two kinds personal (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ajjhatta</span>) and external (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">bahiddhā</span>) – and each is transient and subject to decay. The chain of causation entails all that makes up the five attachments. Where there is eye intact, on which external shapes come to focus, and where there is developed pertinent material to sustain it, there is developed a manifestation of the pertinent section of consciousness. Thus arises the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">upādānakkhandha</span> of form; similarly with the others. MN.i.184-91.
