# Mahā Assapurasutta

## English source

Mahā Assapurasutta. Preached at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assapura</span>, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span> country. Since monks are called recluses (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">samaṇā</span>) they must train themselves to be true <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">samaṇas</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Brāhmaṇas</span>; they should beconscientious and scrupulous, pure in deed, word and thought, guarding the portals of the senses, moderate in eating, ever vigilant, mindful and self-possessed, striving to put off the five <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nivaraṇā</span> and cultivating the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">jhāna</span>. Such an almsman can truly be called a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">samaṇa</span>, a Brahmin, a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nahātaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vedagū</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">sotthiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ariya</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. He is an ideal recluse. MN.i.271-80.
