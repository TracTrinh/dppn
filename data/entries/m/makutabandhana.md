# Makuṭabandhana

## English source

Makuṭabandhana. A “shrine” of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> to the east of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusināra</span>, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>’s body was cremated (DN.ii.160-1). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> explains that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makuṭabandhana</span> was a Hall in which the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malla</span> chiefs put on their ornaments on festival days. It was called a *cetiya* because it was decorated (*cittakatthena pan’esa cetiyaṁ*). DN­a.ii.596; see also Divy.201.

Xuanzang’s description (Beal-Xuan, op.cit., ii.37) of the *stūpa* erected at what is evidently <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makuṭabandhana</span> suggests a different explanation. It was there that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span> laid aside their ornaments (*makuṭa*) and fell prostrate on the ground with grief at the Buddha’s death.
