# Mahā Pajāpatī Gotamī

## English source

Mahā Pajāpatī Gotamī. An eminent Therī. She was born at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Deva</span><span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">-daha</span> in the family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppabuddha</span> as the younger sister of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Māyā</span>. Ap.ii.538 says her father was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Añjana Sakka</span> and her mother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sulakkhaṇā</span>. Mhv.ii.18 says her father was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Añjana</span> and her mother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasodharā</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Daṇḍapāṇī</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppabuddha</span> were her brothers; cp. Dpv.xviii.7f.

At the birth of each sister, interpreters of bodily marks prophesied that their children would be <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cakkavattīs</span>. King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> married both the sisters, and when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Māyā</span> died, seven days after the birth of the Buddha, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span> looked after the Buddha and nursed him. She was the mother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span>, but it is said that she gave her own son to nurses and herself nursed the Bodhisatta. The Buddha was at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> died, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span> decided to renounce the world, and waited for an opportunity to ask the permission of the Buddha.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span> was already a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>. She attained this eminence when the Buddha first visited his father’s palace and preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Dhammapālajātaka</span> (Ja 447) (Dhp­a.i.97).

Her opportunity came when the Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> to settle the dispute between the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷiyans</span> as to the right to take water from the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rohiṇī</span>. When the dispute had been settled, the Buddha preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kalahavivādasutta</span>, and five hundred young <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> men joined the Saṅgha. Their wives, led by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span>, went to the Buddha and asked leave to be ordained as nuns. This leave the Buddha refused, and he went on to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span> and her companions, nothing daunted, had barbers cut off their hair, and donning yellow robes, followed the Buddha to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span> on foot. They arrived with wounded feet at the Buddha’s monastery and repeated their request. The Buddha again refused, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> interceded on their behalf and their request was granted, subject to eight strict conditions. For details see Vin.ii.253ff.; also AN.iv.274ff.

There was some question, which arose later as to the procedure of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span>’s ordination, which was not formal. When the nuns discovered this some of them refused to hold the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t18">uposatha</span> with her. But the Buddha declared that he himself had ordained her and that all was in order (Dhp­a.iv.149). Her <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t18">upasampadā</span> consisted in acquiescing in the eight conditions laid down for nuns (Vin-a.i.242).

After her ordination, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span> came to the Buddha and worshipped him. The Buddha preached to her and gave her a subject for meditation. With this topic she developed insight and soon after won <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship, while her five hundred companions attained to the same after listening to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandakovādasutta</span>. Later, at an assembly of monks and nuns in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span>, the Buddha declared <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span> chief of those who had experience (lit. many nights, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t18">rattaññūnaṁ</span>) (AN.i.25). Not long after, while at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, she realized that her life had come to an end. She was one hundred and twenty years old; she took leave of the Buddha, performed various miracles, and then died, her five hundred companions dying with her. It is said that the marvels which attended her cremation rites were second only to those of the Buddha.

It was in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span> made her resolve to gain eminence. She then belonged to a clansman’s family in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsavatī</span>, and, hearing the Buddha assign the foremost place in experience to a certain nun, wished for similar recognition herself, doing many good deeds to that end. After many births she was born once more at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, forewoman among five hundred slave girls. When the rains drew near, five <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> came from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandamūlaka</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> seeking lodgings. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span> saw them after the Treasurer had refused them any assistance, and, after consultation with her fellow slaves, they persuaded their several husbands to erect five huts for the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span> during the rainy season and they provided them with all requisites. At the end of the rains they gave three robes to each <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span>. After that she was born in a weaver’s village near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, and again ministered, this time to five hundred <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddhas</span>, sons of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumavatī</span> (Thīg­a.140ff.; AN­a.i.185f.; Ap.ii.529-43).

It is said that once <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span> made a robe for the Buddha of wonderful material and marvellously elaborate. But when it came to be offered to the Buddha he refused it, and suggested it should be given to the Saṅgha as a whole. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span> was greatly disappointed, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> intervened. But the Buddha explained that his suggestion was for the greater good of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span>, and also as an example to those who might wish to make similar gifts in the future. This was the occasion for the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇāvibhaṅgasutta</span> (MN.iii.253ff.; MN­a.ii.1001ff.; this incident is referred to in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milinda</span> p.240). The Buddha had a great love for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span>, and when she lay ill, as there were no monks to visit her and preach to her – that being against the rule – the Buddha amended the rule and went himself to preach to her (Vin.iv.56).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span>’s name appears several times in the Jātakas. She was the mother monkey in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Nandiyajātaka</span> (Ja 222, Ja.ii.202), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candā</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Dhammapāla</span> (Ja 358, Ja.iii.182), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhikkhudāyikā</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhikkhudāsikā</span>) daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kikī</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> (Ja.vi.481).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Pajāpatī</span> was so-called because, at her birth, augerers prophesied that she would have a large following; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotamī</span> was her <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gotta</span> name (MN­a.i.1001; cp. AN­a.ii.774).

There is a story related of a nurse employed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span> and born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadaha</span>. She renounced the world with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pajāpatī</span>, but for twenty-five years was harassed by thoughts of lust till, at last, she heard <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammadinnā</span> preach. She then practiced meditation and became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. Thīg­a.75f.
