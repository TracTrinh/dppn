# Mahā Koṭṭhita Thera

## English source

Mahā Koṭṭhita Thera (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Koṭṭhika Thera</span>). One of the foremost disciples of the Buddha, ranked foremost among masters of logical analysis (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">paṭisambhidappattānaṁ</span>) (AN.i.24; Dpv.iv.5; v. 9). He was born into a very wealthy Brahmin family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, his father being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assalāyana</span> and his mother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candavatī</span>.

He gained great proficiency in the Vedas and, after hearing the Buddha preach (to his father, says the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> account), entered the Saṅgha and, engaging in meditation, soon became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. He was extremely skilled in knowledge of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Paṭisambhidā</span>, on which were based all his questions to the Buddha and his own colleagues.

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> he was a rich householder, and, hearing the Buddha praise a monk as foremost among those skilled in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">paṭisambhidā</span>, he wished for a similar eminence for himself in the future. To this end he visited the Buddha and his monks and entertained them for seven days, giving them three robes each at the conclusion of his almsgiving. Owing to the skill showed by him in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vedallasutta</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), the Buddha declared him foremost among those skilled in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Paṭisambhidā</span> (Thag.vs.2; Thag­a.i.29ff.; AN­a.i.287; Ap.ii.479; also Avś.ii.195).

Several instances are given of discussions between Koṭṭhita and other eminent Theras – e.g., the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nalakalāpiyasutta</span> on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kamma</span> (SN.ii.112f), the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīlasutta</span> on religious discipline (SN.iii.165ff), three <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span> on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t18">samudayadhamma</span> (the nature of arising), two on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">assāda</span> (satisfaction) (SN.iii.172-7), two on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">samudaya</span> (arising) (SN.iii.173) and three on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t18">avijjā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vijjā</span> (SN.iii.17). Another similar <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> is on sense and sense objects (SN.iv.162-5), and there is a series of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span> on matters not revealed by the Buddha (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">avyākatāni</span>). SN.iv.384-91; Mrs. Rhys Davids suggests (KS.i.79i n.1) that all these <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span> were compiled rather as “lessons” to be learnt than as genuine inquiries by Koṭṭhita. The pre-eminent monks were “playing” at teacher and pupil in order to aid Koṭṭhita to win proficiency as a teacher. Another such “lesson” is given at AN.iv.382ff., as to the motives guiding those who live the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t18">brahmacariya</span> life.

All these <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span> took the form of discussions with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, in which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Koṭṭhita</span> is the questioner and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> the instructor.

One <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> (SN.iv.145-7) records a “lesson” given by the Buddha to Koṭṭhita on conceptions of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t18">anicca</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t18">dukkha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t18">anatta</span>. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (see the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāyasakkhisutta</span>, AN.i.118f) records a discussion at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saviṭṭha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṭṭhita</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, as to who is best: one who has testified to the truth with body, one who has won view, or one released by faith. Another discussion (AN.ii.161f) takes place between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and Koṭṭhita as to whether anything continues to exist after the ending of the six spheres of contact (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Nibbāna</span>). Once there was a dispute between Koṭṭhita and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta Hatthisāriputta</span>; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta</span> was constantly interrupting the elder monks who were gathered at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isipatana</span> for the discussion of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span>, and was asked by Koṭṭhita to abide his time and not interrupt. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta</span>’s friends protested that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta</span> was well qualified to take part in the discussion; but Koṭṭhita declared that, far from being wise enough, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta</span> would, not long after, renounce the Saṅgha. And so it happened (AN.iii.392ff).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> evidently had a great regard for Koṭṭhita; the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> (Thag.vss.1006-8; Thag­a.ii.117) contains three stanzas in which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> proclaims his excellence.
