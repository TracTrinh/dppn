# Maṇikaṇṭhajātaka

## English source

Maṇikaṇṭhajātaka (Ja 253). The Bodhisatta and his younger brother, after the death of their parents, lived as ascetics in leaf huts on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span>, the elder being higher up the stream than the younger. One day, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇikaṇṭha</span>, while walking along the river in the guise of a man, came to the hut of the younger ascetic and became his friend. Thereafter he called daily and their friendship grew apace. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇikaṇṭha</span> finally became so fond of the ascetic that he put off his disguise, and encircling the other in his folds, lay thus for a short while each day, until his affection was satisfied. But the ascetic was afraid of his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> shape and grew thin and pale.

The Bodhisatta noticing this, suggested that the next time <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇikaṇṭha</span> came, his brother should ask for the jewel which he wore round his throat. On the morrow, when the ascetic made this request, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇikaṇṭha</span> hurried away. Several times this happened, and then he came no more. The ascetic was much grieved by his absence, but was comforted by the Bodhisatta.

The Buddha related this story at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggāḷavacetiya</span> near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavī</span>. The monks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavī</span> became so importunate with their requests for building materials from the householders that at the mere sight of a monk the householders would hurry indoors. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span> discovered this and reported it to the Buddha, who admonished the monks against begging for things from other people. (Ja.ii.282-6; also Vin.iii.146f., where the details of the story of the past are slightly different.)

On the same occasion were preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadattajātaka</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṭṭhisenajātaka</span> (*q.v.*).
