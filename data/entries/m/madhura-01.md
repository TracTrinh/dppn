# Madhurā 01

## English source

Madhurā 01. The capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Surasena</span>, situated on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yamunā</span>. Its king, soon after the death of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avantiputta</span> (MN.ii.83), who, judging by his name, was probably related to the royal family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>. Madhurā was visited by the Buddha (AN.ii.57; iii.256), but there is no record of his having stayed there. In fact, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madhurasutta</span> (2) (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) states that he viewed the city with distinct disfavour. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span> evidently liked it, for he stayed there in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gundāvana</span>, and was visited there by the king of the city, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avantiputta</span> (MN.ii.83), and the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇḍarāyana</span> (AN.i.67). One of the most important <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span> on caste, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madhurasutta</span> 1, was preached to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avantiputta</span> by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span> at Madhurā. Perhaps it was through the agency of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span> that Buddhism gained ground in Madhurā. Already in the Buddha’s time there were, in and around Madhurā, those who accepted his teachings, for the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (AN.ii.57) mentions that once when he was journeying from Madhurā to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verañjā</span> and stopped under a tree by the wayside, a large number of householders, both men and women, came and worshipped him. Later, about 300 B.C., Madhurā became a Jain centre (CHI.i.167), but when Faxian (Giles, p. 20) and Xuanzang visited it, Buddhism was flourishing there, and there were many <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">saṅghārāmas</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">stūpas</span>. Beal-Xuan.i.179ff.; for a prophecy (attributed to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>) regarding the future greatness of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madhurā</span>, see Divy.348ff.

From <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkassa</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madhurā</span> was a distance of four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojanas</span> (thus in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāyana</span>’s Grammar, iii.1).

Madhurā is sometimes referred to as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara Madhurā</span>, to distinguish it from a city of the same name in South India. Thus, in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vimānavatthu</span> Commentary (Vv-a.118f), a woman of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara Madhurā</span> is mentioned as having been born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> as a result of having given alms to the Buddha.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ghaṭajātaka</span> (Ja.iv.79ff) speaks of Mahā Sāgara as the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara Madhurā</span>, and relates what is evidently the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṁsa</span>’s attempt to tyrannize over Madhurā by overpowering the Yādavas and his consequent death at the hands of Kṛṣṇa, a story which is found both in the Epics and in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Purāṇas</span>. This <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> confirms the Brahmanical tradition as to the association of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vasudeva</span>’s family with Madhurā (PHAL, p. 89).

There is a story (Cv.xcii.23ff) of a king called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sena</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span>, who was very generous in feeding the monks, and once thought of giving alms by cultivating a piece of land himself. He, therefore, went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara Madhurā</span> in disguise, worked as a labourer, and held an almsgiving with the gains so obtained.

Madhurā is generally identified with Maholi, five miles to the Southeast of the present town of Mathurā or Muttra. It is the Modura of Ptolemy and the Methoras of Pliny (CAGI.427f).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Milindapañha</span> (p. 331) refers to Madhurā as one of the chief cities of India. In the past, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sādhīna</span> and twenty-two of his descendants, the last of whom was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammagutta</span>, reigned in Madhurā (Dpv.iii.21).
