# Moranivāpa

## English source

Moranivāpa. A grove in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span> at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>. It contained a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Paribbājakārāma</span>, the resort of recluses of various denominations. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udumbarikāsīhanādasutta</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sakuladāyisutta</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Sakūladāyisutta</span> were preached there (DN.iii.36ff.; MN.ii.1, 29).

The place was so-called because peacocks were protected there and food was provided for them (DN­a.iii.835; MN­a.ii.694).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moranivāpa</span> was on the bank of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumāgadhā</span> and the Buddha is mentioned (DN.iii.39) as walking there. Not far away was the park of the Queen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udumbarikā</span> (DN.iii.36).

See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moranivāpasutta</span>, 1 and 2.
