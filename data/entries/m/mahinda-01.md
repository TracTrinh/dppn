# Mahinda 01

## English source

Mahinda 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Mahinda</span>. Son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> and brother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghamittā</span>. He was fourteen at the time of the coronation of his father and was ordained at the age of twenty, his preceptor being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggaliputtatissa</span>. The ordination was performed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Deva</span>, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhantika</span> recited the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kammavācā</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> on the day of his ordination (Mhv.v.204ff.; Dpv.v.24 f; Vin-a.i.51). He spent three years in study of the Dhamma under his preceptor, and, later, when the latter retired to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ahogaṅgā</span>, he left his one thousand disciples for seven years under the care of Mahinda (Mhv.v.233; Vin-a.i.52). When the Third Council was held, Mahinda had been for twelve years a monk and was charged with the mission of converting Ceylon. But he delayed for six months, until <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span> became king. He then went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇāgiri</span> and from there to his birthplace, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vedisagiri</span>, staying in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vedisagiri</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span> and visiting his mother, the queen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devī</span>. Still one more month he tarried, teaching the Dhamma to Bhaṇḍuka, and then, on the full moon day of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Jeṭṭha</span>, at the request of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, he went, in company with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Iṭṭhiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sambala</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddasāla</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sāmaṇera</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaṇḍuka</span>, to Ceylon, where he converted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span> by preaching to him the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Hatthipadopamasutta</span>. Later, on the same day, he preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samacittasutta</span>. The next day, at the request of the king, he visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>, travelling through the air and alighting on the site of the (later) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṭhamacetiya</span>. After a meal at the palace he preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Petavatthu</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vimānavatthu</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saccasaṁyutta</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anulā</span> and her five hundred companions became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpannas</span>. Later, in the elephant stables, he preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadūtasutta</span> to the assembled people, and, in the evening, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bālapaṇḍitasutta</span>, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandanavana</span>. The night he spent in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Meghavana</span>, and on the next day the king gave the park to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span>, on behalf of the Saṅgha.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> pointed out to the king various spots destined to be connected with the growth of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sāsana</span> in Ceylon, offering flowers at the same, and at the site of the (later) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span>, he described the visits of the Four Buddhas of this <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kappa</span> to Ceylon. On the fourth day he preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anamataggasutta</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandanavana</span> and helped the king in defining the boundaries of what later became the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>. On the fifth day he preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khajjanīyasutta</span>, on the sixth the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gomayapiṇḍīsutta</span>, and on the seventh the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammacakkappavattanasutta</span>.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pāsāda</span> first built for the residence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷapāsāda</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pariveṇa</span>. Other buildings associated with him were the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suṇhāta</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pariveṇa</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghacaṅka</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pariveṇa</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phalagga</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pariveṇa</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Therāpassaya</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pariveṇa</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Marugaṇa</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pariveṇa</span>, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghasandasenāpatipariveṇa</span>.

Twenty-six days <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> stayed in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Meghavana</span>, and on the thirteenth day of the bright half of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Āsāḷha</span>, after having preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāppamādasutta</span>, he went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Missakapabbata</span>, to spend the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vassa</span>. The king had sixty-eight rock cells built in the mountain and gave them to the Theras on the full moon day. On the same day <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> ordained sixty-two monks, who attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tumbarumālaka</span>. After the full moon day of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kattika</span>, at the conclusion of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pavāraṇa</span> ceremony, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> held a consultation with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span> and sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sāmaṇera</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span> to bring the relics of the Buddha from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammāsoka</span> and other relics from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>. These relics were brought and placed on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Missakapabbata</span>, which from then onwards was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyagiri</span>. The collar-bone from among the relics was deposited in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thūpārāma</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), which was built for the purpose. It was at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span>’s suggestion that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span> sent an embassy headed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Ariṭṭha</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>, with a request that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghamittā</span> should come to Ceylon with a branch of the Bodhi-tree. The request was granted, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghamittā</span> arrived in Ceylon with the branch. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span>, during the later part of his reign, acting on the advice of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span>, built numerous <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāras</span>, each one <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojana</span> from the other; among them were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Issarasamaṇaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessagiri</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> is said to have taught the Commentaries to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Tipiṭaka</span> in the Sinhalese language, after translating them from the Pāli (Cv.xxxvii.228ff).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samantapāsādikā</span> mentions a recital held by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> under the presidency of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Ariṭṭha</span> (pp. 102ff).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> continued to live for the first eight years of the reign of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttiya</span>, who succeeded <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span>. Then, at the age of sixty, he died on the eighth day of the bright half of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Assayuja</span>, in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyagiri</span>, where he was spending the rainy season. His body was brought in procession, with every splendor and honour, to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span> and placed in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañhambamālaka</span>, where homage was paid to it for a whole week. It was then burnt on a pyre of fragrant wood on the east of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Therānambandhamālaka</span>, to the left of the site of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span>. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span> was erected on that spot over half the remains, the other half being distributed in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thūpas</span> built on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiyagiri</span> and elsewhere. The place of cremation was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isibhūmaṅgaṇa</span>, and there for many centuries were cremated the remains of holy men who lived within a distance of three <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t18">yojanas</span> (for details of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="119" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> see Mhv.xiii. xx; Dpv.vii.57f., xii., xiii., iv.: xv.; Vin-a.i.61, 69ff., 79ff., 83ff., 90ff., 103, etc.)

Later, King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="120" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimeghavaṇṇa</span> had a life-size image of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="121" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> made of gold; this he took to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="122" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambatthala</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="123" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span>. For eight days a festival was held in its honour; on the ninth day the image was taken from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="124" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambatthala</span>, carried by the king himself at the head of a large and splendid procession, and placed for three days in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="125" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sotthiyākara</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="126" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vihāra</span>. On the twelfth day it was taken with all splendours to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="127" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>, to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="128" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vihāra</span>, where it was left for three months in the courtyard of the Bodhi-tree. From there it was removed to the inner city and deposited in a magnificent image house to the south-east of the palace. An endowment was set up for the annual performance of ceremonies in honour of the image, and this custom was continued for many centuries. The image was brought from the inner town to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="129" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahāvihāra</span> on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="130" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pavāraṇa</span> day, and every year an offering was made on the thirteenth day (Cv.xxxvii.66ff).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="131" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhātusena</span> had the image brought to the place where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="132" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span>’s body was cremated and there held a great festival (Cv.xxxviii.58), while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="133" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggabodhi I</span>. set up the image on the banks of the tank called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="134" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahindataṭa</span>, and ensured that the special task of carrying the image to the dyke of the tank was the task of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="135" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Taracchas</span>. Cv.xlii.30.
