# Mahā Ummaggajātaka

## English source

Mahā Ummaggajātaka (Ja 546). The Bodhisatta was once born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span> as the son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirivaḍḍhaka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanādevī</span>. The child was born with a medicinal plant in his hand, and was therefore called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span>. He talked immediately after birth, and it is said that, on the day of his conception, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span>, dreamed a dream, which presaged the birth of a sage. From early childhood <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> gave evidence of unusual ability, and one of his first acts was to build a large hall and lay out a garden with the help of his companions. The king wished to have him in the court though he was only seven years old, but was dissuaded by his wise men. But he sent a councillor to watch the boy and report of his doings from time to time. When the king was fully convinced (the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> gives an account of nineteen problems solved by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span>) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> was undoubtedly endowed with unusual wisdom, he sent for him in spite of the counsel of his ministers – <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Senaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāvinda</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devinda</span> – and appointed him as his fifth councillor. One day, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> saved the queen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udumbarā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) from the unjust wrath of the king, and ever after she was his firm and loyal friend. After his entry into the court, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> was on many occasions called upon to match his wit against that of the senior councillors, and on each occasion he emerged triumphant, e.g., in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meṇḍakapañha</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimaṇḍapañha</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>).

When aged sixteen he married <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amarādevī</span>. She was a wise woman, and frustrated many attempts of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span>’s enemies to embroil him with the king. Once they stole various things from the palace and sent them to her. She accepted them, and made assignations with each of the donors. When they arrived she had them seized, their heads shaved, and themselves thrown into the jakes, where she tormented them, and then arraigned them before the king with the stolen goods. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span>, aware of the plots against him, lay in hiding, and the deity of the king’s parasol put several questions to the king, knowing that none but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> could answer them. The king sent men to seek him, and he was discovered working for a potter. The king showed him all honour, and obtained from him the answers to the deity’s questions.

But his enemies continued to plot against him, until orders were given by the king that he should be killed the next day. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Udumbarā</span>, discovered this and warned him. But in the meantime he had discovered the guilty secrets of his enemies: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Senaka</span> had killed a courtesan, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusa</span> had a leprous spot on his thigh, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāvinda</span> was possessed by a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span> named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Naradeva</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devinda</span> had stolen the king’s most precious gem. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> posted these facts everywhere in the city, and the next day went boldly into the palace. The king professed innocence of any evil intentions against him; but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> exposed the schemes of them all, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Senaka</span> and the others were only saved from severe punishment by the intervention of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> himself. Thenceforward <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span>’s trusted councillor, and took various measures to increase his royal master’s power and glory. Spies were sent to every court, whence they brought home reports. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> also had a parrot whom he employed to ferret out the most baffling secrets. While returning from a visit to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅkhapāla</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekabala</span>, the parrot passed through <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarapañcāla</span> and there overheard a conversation between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷanī Brahmadatta</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kampilla</span>, and his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Purohita</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kevaṭṭa</span>, wherein the latter unfolded a scheme for capturing the whole of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kevaṭṭa</span> was too wise to allow <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span>, to attack <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span>, knowing of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span>’s power, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> deliberately provoked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span> by sending his men to upset a feast he had prepared, during which he had planned to poison the hundred princes whom he had brought under subjection. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span> then set out to attack <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span>. He laid siege to the city, and adopted various ways of compelling the citizens to surrender. But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> was more than a match for him, and found means of defeating all his plans. In the end <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> engaged the services of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anukevaṭṭa</span>, who, pretending to be a traitor to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span>, went over to the army of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span> and, gaining the king’s confidence, informed him that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kevaṭṭa</span> and all the other counsellors of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span> had accepted bribes from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span>. The king listened to him, and on his advice raised the siege and fled to his own city.

But <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kevaṭṭa</span> planned revenge, and, a year later, he persuaded <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span> to send poets to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span>’s city, singing songs of the peerless beauty of the daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcālacaṇḍī</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span> heard the songs and sent a proposal of marriage, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kevaṭṭa</span> came to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span> to arrange the day. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span> suggested that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kevaṭṭa</span> should meet <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> to discuss the plans, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> feigned illness, and when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kevaṭṭa</span> arrived at his house, he was grossly insulted by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span>’s men. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kevaṭṭa</span> had left, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span> consulted <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span>, but would not be dissuaded from his plan to marry <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcālacaṇḍī</span>. Finding that he could do nothing with the king, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> sent his parrot <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṭṭhara</span> to find out what he could from the maynah bird which lived in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span>’s bedchamber. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṭṭhara</span> used all his wits and won the favour of the maynah and learnt from her of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kevaṭṭa</span>’s plan, which he repeated to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span>.

With <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span>’s leave, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> went on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarapañcāla</span> to, as he said, make preparations for the wedding. But he gave orders for a village to be built on every league of ground along the road, and gave instructions to the shipwright, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānandakumāra</span>, to build and hold ready three hundred ships. At <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarapañcāla</span> he was received with great honour, and obtained the king’s permission to build in the city a palace for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span>. The king gave him a free hand, and he immediately started to threaten to pull down houses belonging to various people, from the queen mother downwards, and obtained money from them as bribes to spare their houses. Having reported to the king that no suitable spot was available within the city, he obtained his consent to erect a palace outside the city, between that and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span>. All access was forbidden to the site on penalty of a large sum, and having first erected a village called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaggali</span> for his workmen, elephants, etc., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> started to dig a tunnel, the mouth of which was in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span>. The tunnel, a marvellous place, was duly constructed, fitted with all manner of machinery, and beautifully decorated. A smaller tunnel was dug, leading into the larger, one opening, which was, however, concealed, giving access to the king’s palace. The task occupied four months, and when all preparations were complete, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> sent word to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span> arrived at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span>’s court, and a great feast was held in his honour at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upakārī</span>, the palace which had been prepared for his residence. While the feast was in progress, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> sent men by the smaller tunnel to the palace and bade them fetch <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Talatā</span> (the queen mother), the queen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandā</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcālacaṇḍī</span>, on the pretext that they had been sent for by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span> to take part in the festivities as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> had both been killed, according to plan. Meanwhile <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span> had given orders that the whole city should be surrounded. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span> was overcome with fright on discovering what was happening, but he put himself into <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span>’s hands. The latter led him into the large tunnel, and there he was brought face to face with the members of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span>’s family, who had already been conducted thither. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcālacaṇḍī</span> was placed upon a heap of treasure and married to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span>. On emerging from the tunnel, they were placed on board a waiting ship, with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Talatā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandā</span>, and sent away into safety, escorted by the other ships, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="119" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> himself remaining behind in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="120" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarapañcāla</span>.

The next day, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="121" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span> came with his army to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="122" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upakāri</span>, hoping to capture <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="123" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span>. There <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="124" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> revealed to him what had happened, and, in due course, persuaded him to forget his wrath and inspect the tunnel. While in the tunnel <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="125" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span> expressed his remorse for having listened to the evil advice of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="126" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kevaṭṭa</span>, and he and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="127" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> swore eternal friendship. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="128" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="129" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span>, taking with him <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="130" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span>’s dowry for his daughter; the members of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="131" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span>’s family returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="132" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarapañcāla</span>, and the two kings lived in great amity.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="133" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span> died ten years later, and in fulfilment of a promise made to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="134" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="135" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahosadha</span> went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="136" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarapañcāla</span>. There <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="137" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandā</span>, who had never forgiven him, tried to poison the king’s mind against him; but this plot was frustrated by a religious woman, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="138" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bherī</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="139" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="140" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmadatta</span> remained his firm friend, loving him, as he confessed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="141" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bherī</span>, more than any of his own family.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="142" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> was related to illustrate the Buddha’s great wisdom.
