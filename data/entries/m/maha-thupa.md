# Mahā Thūpa

## English source

Mahā Thūpa. The great *Thūpa* in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span>, built by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span>. The site on which it was erected was consecrated by the visit of all the four Buddhas of this *kappa* and was at the upper end of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakudha</span> *vāpi*. It was one of the spots at which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> scattered *campaka* flowers by way of homage, and the earth trembled. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> informed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devānampiyatissa</span> of the great sanctity of the spot and of its suitability for a *Thūpa*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> immediately wished to build the *Thūpa* himself, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span> bade him desist, telling him that the work would be carried out in the future by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> recorded this prophecy on a pillar of stone (Mhv.xv.51ff., 167ff). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> had won his victory over the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Damiḷas</span> and had brought peace to the country, he saw the prophecy inscribed on the stone pillar, but was unwilling to start the work as the people were too crippled with regard to money to be able to support such an immense undertaking. But the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> read his thoughts and provided him with all the necessaries for the building of the *Thūpa*. Prepared bricks were found on the banks of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gambhīranadī</span>, copper near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tambapiṭṭha</span>, silver in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambaṭṭhakolalena</span>, pearls at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelā</span>, and gems in a cave near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pelivāpigāma</span>. The building was started on the full moon day of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākha</span>. The foundation stone was laid on the fourteenth day of the bright half of the month of *Āsāḷha*. Great celebrations marked the event, arrangements for which were in the hands of the ministers <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirideva</span>. Monks were present not only from all over Ceylon but from many other places:

- eighty thousand under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Indagutta</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Rājagaha</span>,
- twelve thousand under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Dhammasena</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Isipatana</span>,
- sixty thousand under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Piyadassī</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Jetavanārāma</span>,
- eighteen thousand under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mahā Buddharakkhita</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mahā Vana</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Vesālī</span>,
- thirty thousand under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mahā Dhammarakkhita</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Ghositārāma</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kosambī</span>,
- forty thousand under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mahā Saṅgharakkhita</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Dakkhiṇāgiri</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Ujjenī</span>,
- one hundred and sixty thousand under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mittiṇṇa Asokārāma</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Pāṭaliputta</span>, two hundred and eighty thousand under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Uttiṇṇa</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kasmīra</span>,
- four hundred and sixty thousand under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mahā Deva</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Pallabhogga</span>,
- thirty thousand under Yona <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Mahā Dhammarakkhita</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Alasandā</span>,
- sixty thousand under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Uttara</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Viñjhāṭavī</span>,
- thirty thousand under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Cittagutta</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Bodhimaṇḍa</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t23">vihāra</span>,
- eighty thousand under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Candagutta</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Vanavāsa</span>,
- and ninety-six thousand under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Suriyagutta</span> from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t24" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t24">Kelāsa</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t23" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t23">vihāra</span>.

Of *Arahants* alone ninety-six crores were present. As the king stepped into the space left open for him, he expressed the desire that, if his worship were to have a happy result, Theras bearing the names of the Buddha, his Dhamma and his Saṅgha, should take their places on the east, south, and west sides respectively, and a Thera bearing the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> on the north side, each Thera to be surrounded by a group bearing the same name. The king’s wish was fulfilled; the Theras in question and their companions were called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Buddharakkhita</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Dhammarakkhita</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Saṅgharakkhita</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Ānanda</span>. As the king was about to mark the space to be covered by the *cetiya*, the Thera <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siddhattha</span>, looking into the future, told him to define only a moderate space for the *Thūpa*. This the king did; then, looking at the Theras immediately around him, he inquired their names and rejoiced to find them so auspicious, they being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Siddhattha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṅgala</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paduma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candagutta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suriyagutta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indagutta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mittasena</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayasena</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Acala</span>. He then laid the first foundation stone on the east side on sweet smelling clay prepared by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mittasena</span> and sprinkled with water by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jayasena</span>; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sumana</span> placed jasmine flowers on the stone. Immediately the earth trembled in wonder. The minister who helped the king to mark out the area of the *cetiya* was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suppatiṭṭhita</span> *brahma*, son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandisena</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanadevī</span>. At the end of the ceremony, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piyadassī</span> preached to the assembled populace, and many attained to various fruits of the Path.

The *Thūpa* was like a water bubble in shape; its architect was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirivaḍḍha</span> and his assistant <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Acala</span>. Orders were given that no unpaid work should be done in the construction of the *cetiya*. Arahants caused the three terraces of flower offerings to the *Thūpa* (*pupphādhānā*) to sink nine times into the earth, in order, as they explained, to strengthen the foundations. The *cetiya* was one hundred and twenty cubits high, and for the ten flower terraces alone ten crores of bricks were used.

The Relic Chamber was of unparalleled magnificence, and consisted of four *medavaṇṇapāsāṇā*, each eighty cubits in length and in breadth and eight inches thick. These were brought from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> by two *sāmaṇeras*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span>. In the Chamber were placed sculptural representations of the chief events connected with the Buddha’s life as well as pictures of several Jātakas, including the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span>. For a list see Mhv.xxx.71ff.; the Mhv­ṭ (549ff.) contains a long disquisition to prove that there is no reason to doubt the account given of the contents of the Relic Chamber, for in its construction the power (*iddhi*) of the king, of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>, and of *Arahants* came into play.

The work of the Relic Chamber was under the personal supervision of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indagutta</span> Thera*,* of great *iddhi* power. When the Chamber was ready for the enshrining of the Relics, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇuttara</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pūjā</span> *pariveṇa* was entrusted with the task of obtaining them. In a previous birth, as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanduttara</span>, he had vowed to have the power of doing this, and now was his opportunity. He went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mañjerika</span> *Nāgabhavana*, where the Relics, washed away from the *Thūpa* at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāmagāma</span>, were in the custody of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kāḷa</span>, and by a display of *iddhi* power obtained them from the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> against his desire. They represented one *doṇa* of the Buddha’s Relics, and the Buddha had predicted that they would ultimately be placed in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span>. These Relics were enshrined on the fifteenth *uposatha* day in the light half of the month of *Āsāḷha*, under the constellation of *Uttarāsāḷha*. Many <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> and *Brahmas* and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgas</span> were present as on the day of the Buddha’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Awakening</span>, and ninety-six crores of *Arahants* attended the ceremony.

As the king, after passing three times round the *cetiya*, ascended it on the east side, and was about to descend into the Relic Chamber, bearing on his head the Casket of Relics, the casket opened and the Relics rose out of it, and taking on the form of the Buddha, performed the Twin Miracle, as at the foot of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gaṇḍamba</span>. When the Relics were placed on the couch prepared for them they assumed, as the king had desired, the form of the Buddha as he lay on his death bed.

For a whole week the celebrations lasted, and during this period the king offered to the Relics the dominion of Ceylon, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Indagutta</span> decreed that the people of Ceylon, wherever they might be, should be able immediately to visit the *Thūpa* should they desire to do so. At the end of the seven days, the two *sāmaṇeras*, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumana</span>, closed the Chamber with the *medavaṇṇapāsāṇā* set apart for the purpose, while *Arahants* pronounced that flowers offered in the Relic Chamber should not wither, nor scents dry up; the lamps should not be extinguished nor anything whatever perish.

The building of the Māha Thūpa is described in Mhv. chaps xxviii-xxx.; Mhv­ṭ. 514-83; Dpv.xix.1ff.; also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thūpavaṁsa</span> (pp. 66ff.). The treasures enshrined in the Mahā Thūpa were worth twenty crores, the rest cost one thousand crores (Mhv.xxxii.18).

Before the parasol of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> and the plaster work could be completed, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duṭṭhagāmaṇī</span> fell ill, and his brother, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saddhātissa</span>, summoned from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dīghavāpi</span>, contrived with great skill to make the *Thūpa* look complete, that the king might see it before he died. After the king’s obsequies had been performed, in a place within sight of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> (Mhv.xxxii.58), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saddhātissa</span> finished the work yet remaining and established celebrations to be performed three times daily at the Mahā Thūpa (Mhv.xxxii.60; Mhv.xxxiii.5). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lañjatissa</span> levelled the ground between the Mahā Thūpa and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thūpārāma</span> and built three stone terraces at the cost of three hundred thousand (Mhv.xxxiii.22f). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khallāṭanāga</span> made the courtyard of sand, surrounded by a wall (Mhv.xxxiii.31). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhātika</span> constructed two *vedikā* round the courtyard (Mhv.xxxiv.39). It is said (Mhv­ṭ. 553f) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhātika</span> was taken by the *Arahants* into the Relic Chamber, and he held great celebrations in its honour (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhātikābhaya</span>). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Dāṭhika Mahā Nāga</span> converted the sand courtyard into a wide court laid out with *kiñcakkha*-stones on plaster (Mhv.xxxiv.69), while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āmaṇḍagāmaṇī</span> erected a parasol over the *cetiya* (Mhv.xxxv.2) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="119" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Iḷanāga</span> made the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="120" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lambakaṇṇas</span> construct a roadway leading up to the Mahā Thūpa (Mhv.xxxiv.17). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="121" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirināga</span> had the whole *Thūpa* gilded and crowned with a new parasol (Mhv.xxxvi.24), this work being undertaken again later by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="122" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghatissa</span> (Mhv.xxxvi.65), while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="123" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅghabodhi</span> made rain to pour down by means of prostrating himself in the courtyard (Mhv.xxxvi.75). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="124" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jeṭṭhatissa</span> offered two precious gems to the *Thūpa* (Mhv.xxxvi.126), while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="125" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggabodhi I</span>. placed on the *Thūpa* a golden umbrella (Cv.xlii.32), From this time onward the country passed through very troublous times and the Mahā Thūpa was neglected. But it was restored by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="126" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu I</span>. (Cv.lxxiv.10; lxxvi.106f; lxxviii.97) and again by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="127" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kittinissaṅka</span> (Cv.lxxx.20); it was later pillaged by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="128" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māgha</span> (Cv.lxxx.68), and remained neglected till the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="129" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Parakkamabāhu II</span>., who started the work of reconstruction (Cv.lxxxvii.66), which was completed by his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="130" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vijayabāhu IV</span>. Cv.lxxxviii.83; after this, the *cetiya* once more fell into disrepair and has so continued till recently, when an attempt is being made to rebuild it.

The Mahā Thūpa has been a place of pilgrimage for Buddhists from the time of its building down to the present day, even when the place was deserted and its courtyards overgrown with creepers (e.g., Vibh-a.446). There seems to have been a hall for pilgrims to the west of the *cetiya* (Vibh-a.446). When the Buddha’s *Sāsana* disappears, all the Relics of the Buddha deposited in various *cetiyas* all over Ceylon will gather together at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="131" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Cetiya</span>, and from there will go to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="132" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājāyatana</span> *cetiya* in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="133" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadīpa</span>, thence to the *Mahā Bodhipallaṅka*, where all the Relics, assembled from everywhere, will take the form of the Buddha seated at the foot of the Bodhi-tree. Then they will be consumed by self-generated flames (Vibh-a.433).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="134" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Thūpa</span> is known by other names: <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="135" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Cetiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="136" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratanavāluka</span> (Cv.lxxvi.106), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="137" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ratanavāli</span> (Cv.lxxx.68), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="138" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇṇamāli</span> (Mhv.xxvii.3) (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="139" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemamāli</span>), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="140" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hemavāluka</span> (Cv.li.82).
