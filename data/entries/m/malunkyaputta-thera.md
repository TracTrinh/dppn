# Māluṅkyāputta Thera

## English source

Māluṅkyāputta Thera (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">v.l.</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māluṅkyaputta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mālukyaputta</span>). Son of the assessor (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">agghāpanika</span>) of the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>, his mother being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māluṅkyā</span>. He was religious by nature, and, when he came of age, became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Paribbājaka</span>. Later, he heard the Buddha preach and joined the Saṅgha, becoming an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span> (Thag­a.i.446f). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> contains two sets of verses attributed to him: one (vv. 399-404) spoken on his visit to his home after attaining <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship when his people tried to lure him back by a great display of hospitality; the other in connection with a brief sermon preached to him by the Buddha before he became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>, vv. 794-817; the reference is probably to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māluṅkyāputtasutta</span> of AN.ii.248; see also SN.iv.72, where the verses are quoted in full.

The Thera asked the Buddha for a doctrine in brief and the Buddha gave him one. The verses contain a detailed account of the stanzas which were only outlined to him by the Buddha. There the monk is described as a broken down old man, far on in years. The Commentators (AN­a.ii.582 and SN­a.iii.20) add that he had, in his youth, neglected the detailed teaching and fallen back, through love of possessions.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhimanikāya</span> are two <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span> – the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Māluṅkyā</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Māluṅkyā</span> (this is referred to at Mil. 144) – both evidently preached before Māluṅkyāputta’s attainment of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship, because in both the Buddha speaks disparagingly of him.
