# Meṇḍaka

## English source

Meṇḍaka. A very rich householder of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nagara</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span>. He was the father of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya</span> and, therefore, the grandfather of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>. He was one of the five Treasurers of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>. When the Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span>, Meṇḍaka, with the help of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>, entertained him and the monks, and, after listening to the Buddha, he became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span>. Dhp­a.i.384ff.; he had been earlier a follower of the heretics. The heretics tried in vain to stop him from visiting the Buddha; AN­a.i.219f.

It is said (Vin.i.240f.; also Paṭis-a.509; Dhp­a.iii.372f.; Vsm.383; the accounts differ slightly) that when he went to his granaries after his ceremonial bath, as he stood at the door, showers of grain would fall from heaven and fill the stores. His wife, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candapadumā</span>, would cook one measure of rice and one curry and serve the food, ladle in hand. As long as there were people coming to receive the food, so long would the food cooked be unexhausted. Meṇḍaka’s son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhanañjaya</span>, would put one thousand pieces into a purse and give money from this purse to all who needed it, and at the end of the day the purse would remain full. His daughter-in-law, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumanadevī</span>, would sit by a basket containing four <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t18">doṇas</span> of seed paddy and distribute from this supply among the servants, enough to last for six months, but the supply of paddy would remain unexhausted. Meṇḍaka’s slave, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Puṇṇaka</span>, ploughed his fields with a golden plough. With every furrow so ploughed, six other furrows would appear, three on either side, each one <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">ammaṇa</span> wide. These five people came to be known as the five very lucky ones (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Pañcamahāpuññā</span>). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> heard of this, he sent his minister to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span> with a fourfold army and discovered that it was true.

When the Buddha left <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddiya</span> for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttarāpa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meṇḍaka</span> gave orders to his servants and followed the Buddha with abundant provisions of all sorts, entertaining the Buddha and his monks with luxurious food and fresh milk. At the end of the meal, Meṇḍaka provided the monks with ghee and butter for their journey. At first the monks were unwilling to accept the gifts, but the Buddha, at Meṇḍaka’s request, allowed them to do so (Viii.i.243ff).

Meṇḍaka was so-called (“Ram”) because, behind his house, in a yard eight <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t18">karīsa</span>s in extent, some golden rams pranced up and down, as big as elephants, horses or bulls, hoofing the earth, smiting each other back to back. Whenever Meṇḍaka needed food or garments or money, he would place balls of colored thread in the mouths of the rams, and when he pulled these out, there would follow them all that he needed (Paṭis-a.504; Bv­a.24).

All this was because of good deeds done in the past by Meṇḍaka. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span>, he was a householder named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avaroja</span>. He had an uncle of the same name, and when the latter proposed building a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhakuṭi</span> for the Buddha, his nephew wished to help with it. But the uncle refused his help. He therefore built an Elephant Hall (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t18">kuñjarasālā</span>) opposite the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhakuṭi</span>. In the middle of the hall was a jeweled pavilion with a seat for preaching, which contained a foot rest, all this supported by golden rams. At the festival of dedication, he gave alms for four mouths to sixty-eight hundred thousand monks and presented them with sets of three robes each, the robes given to the novices being worth one hundred thousand. After many births, he was born in this age as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>.

One day, when on his way to the palace, he met the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Purohita</span>, who told him that there would be a famine in three months. Profiting by this warning, the Treasurer exerted himself to collect all possible grain and store it in every available place. The famine came, and for many months the Treasurer and his retinue lived on the stored grain, but, in the end, the supplies were exhausted, and most of them, acting on his advice, went to the mountains in search of food. He, his wife, his son and daughter-in-law and a slave remained behind. One day, his wife cooked a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nāḷi</span> of rice which she had hidden away and divided it into five portions. As the family were about to eat, a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> came to the door for alms; they all gave him their portions of food and made various wishes. As a result of these earnest wishes, Meṇḍaka and the members of his family and his slave possessed the supernatural powers above mentioned. During the afternoon, after the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacceka Buddha</span> had had the food, the Treasurer felt very hungry and asked his wife if there were any lumps of rice sticking to the bottom of the pot. She went into the kitchen to the pot and found it full of fragrant food. From that time their supply of food never failed. Dhp­a.iii.363ff.; but according to Dhp­a.iv.203, Meṇḍaka’s name in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vipassī Buddha</span> was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparājita</span>. He was a nephew of that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aparājita</span> who, in this life, became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jotiya</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span> (see also Divy., pp.123ff., 131ff.). Meṇḍaka’s grandson was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uggaha</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>).
