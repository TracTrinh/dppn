# Mahā Kaccāna

## English source

Mahā Kaccāna, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāyana</span>. One of the most eminent disciples of the Buddha, considered chief among expounders in full of the brief saying of the Buddha (*saṅkhittena bhāsitassa vitthārena atthaṁ vibhajantānaṁ*) (AN.i.23). He was born at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span> in the family of the chaplain of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍappajjota</span>, and was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāna</span> both because of his golden colour and because <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāna</span> was the name of his *gotta*. He studied the Vedas, and, on the death of his father, succeeded him as chaplain. With seven others he visited the Buddha, at the request of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍappajjota</span>, to invite him to come to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāna</span> and his friends listened to the Buddha’s sermon, and having attained *Arahant*-ship, joined the order. He then conveyed the king’s invitation to the Buddha, who pointed out that it would now suffice if <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāna</span> himself returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāna</span> accordingly set out for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span> with his seven companions, accepting alms on the way at the house of a very poor girl of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Telappanāḷi</span>, who later became <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍappajjota</span>’s queen. For details see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Telappanāḷi</span>.

Arrived in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāna</span> lived in the royal park, where the king showed him all honour. He preached constantly to the people, and, attracted by his discourses, numerous persons joined the Saṅgha, so that the whole city was one blaze of orange robes. It is said that after having duly established the *Sāsana* in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>, Kaccāna returned once more to the Buddha (thus, the explanation of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madhupiṇḍikasutta</span> was given at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍappajjota</span> consulted him on various occasions, and among the verses attributed to him in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> (Thag.vss.494-501), are several addressed to the king himself.

It was in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> that Kaccāna had made his resolve to win the eminence he did, after listening to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara</span>’s praise of a monk, also named Kaccāna, for similar proficiency. Kaccāna was then a *vijjādhara*, and offered the Buddha three *kaṇikāra*-flowers. So says the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> ii.463, but Thag­a. says he was a *vijjādhara* in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumedha Buddha</span>. In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kassapa Buddha</span> he was a householder of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, and offered a golden brick, worth one hundred thousand, to the *cetiya* which was being built over the Buddha’s remains, and then made a vow that in future births his body should be golden (Thag­a.i.483f.; AN­a.i.117f).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (Ap.ii.465), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāna</span>’s father was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tirīṭivaccha</span> (or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tidivavaccha</span>), and his mother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candapadumā</span>. There is another account of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Apadāna</span> (AN.i.84f), in which it is said that in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> he built a *gandhakuṭi* named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paduma</span> in the shape of a lotus and covered with lotus flowers, and that thirty *kappas* later he became king under the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pabhassara</span>.

Three *Suttas* are mentioned (AN­a.i.118) as having obtained for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāna</span> his title of eminence – the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madhupiṇḍika</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāyana</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pārāyaṇa</span>; several instances are given of people seeking <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span>’s assistance, for a detailed explanation of something said in brief by the Buddha – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hāliddikāni</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samiddhi</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Valliya</span> (see also AN.iii.314, 321; v. 225; MN.iii.223). Among <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāna</span>’s pupils and followers and those who consulted him were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa Kuṭikaṇṇa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Isidatta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avantiputta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lohicca</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Arāmadaṇḍa</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaṇḍarāyaṇa</span>.

In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāna</span> is said to have stayed, not in the king’s park, where he lived soon after his return from the Buddha, but chiefly in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kuraraghara</span> *papātā* (e.g., SN.iii.9; AN.v.46; Ud.v.6; Vin.i.194; Dhp­a.iv.101) and in a hut in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makkarakaṭa</span> forest. SN.iv.116; see also Vv-a.259, according to which he stayed near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Potali</span>.

Mention is also made of his staying at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Varaṇā</span> on the bank of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaddamadaha</span> (AN.i.65); at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gundāvana</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madhurā</span> (AN.i.67; MN.ii.83); at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tapodā</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> (AN.iii.192), in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soreyya</span> (Dhp­a.i.325; for a curious incident connected with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kaccāna</span>’s visit see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soreyya</span>), and in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosambī</span> (Pv-a. 140). According to Divy.551, 585, 586 he also stayed in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Roruka</span>.

It is said that even when Kaccāna was living at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>, a long distance away, he went regularly to hear the Buddha preach, and when the chief Theras took their places in the assembly, they always left room for him (Dhp­a.ii.176). On one such occasion <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> showed him great honour, falling at his feet, and the Buddha explained that this was because Mahā Kaccāna kept his senses well guarded.

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhima Commentary</span> (MN­a.ii.854) records a curious story in reference to Kaccāna. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span>, minister of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, saw Kaccāna descending <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span> and said he looked like a monkey. The Buddha read <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span>’s thoughts, and warned him that after death he would be born as a monkey in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span>. He believed the Buddha, and made provision in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span> for his future comfort as monkey. And this be did indeed become, living in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span> and answering to the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vassakāra</span>!

Kaccāna is identified with the charioteer in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kurudhammajātaka</span> (Ja 276, Ja.ii.181), and with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devala</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhaṅgajātaka</span> (Ja 522, Ja.v.151).

According to tradition, Kaccāna was the author of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nettippakaraṇa</span>, the Pāli grammar bearing his name, and of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Peṭakopadesa</span>. It is probable that these works were the compilations of a school, which traced its descent to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kaccāna</span>. See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madhurasutta</span>.
