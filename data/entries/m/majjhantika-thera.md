# Majjhantika Thera

## English source

Majjhantika Thera. An *Arahant*. He recited the *kammavācā* (or ecclesiastical act) at the ordination of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahinda</span>, on whom he later conferred the *upasampadā* ordination (Mhv.v.207; Vin-a.i.51; Dpv.vii.24). Later, at the conclusion of the Third Council, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhantika</span> went as preacher to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kasmīra-Gandhāra</span>. There, by his great *iddhi* powers, he overcame the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aravāḷa</span> and converted him to the Faith, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍaka</span> and his wife <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Hāritā</span> and their five hundred sons became *Sotāpannas*. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhantika</span> preached the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āsīvisopamasutta</span> to the assembled concourse and later ordained one hundred thousand persons (Mhv.xii.3, 9ff.; Vin-a.i.64ff.; Dpv.viii.4; Mbv.113; for the Tibetan version see Rockhill, op.cit., 167ff.). The sermon preached by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhantika</span> is referred to in the Scholiast to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarabhaṅgajātaka</span> (Ja.v.142).

This same Elder is referred to elsewhere as an example of one who practised *pariyatti appicchatā* (Snp­a.ii.494; DN­a.iii.1061, but at AN­a.i.263 he is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhantika Tissa</span>). He was the leader of the assembly of monks (*saṅghathera*). On the day of the dedication of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s *vihāra*, the Thera was a *khīṇāsava* and was present, but his begging bowl and robe were hardly worth a farthing. People, seeing him there, asked him to make way; but he sank into the earth, rising to receive the alms given to the leader of the monks, knowing that he alone was fit to accept it. The story is given at AN­a.i.43; MN­a.i.350.
