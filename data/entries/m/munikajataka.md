# Muṇikajātaka

## English source

Muṇikajātaka (Ja 30). The Bodhisatta was once an ox, called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Lohita</span>, in a householder’s family, where his brother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Lohita</span> and he did all the work. When their master’s daughter was about to be married, a pig, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Muṇika</span>, was brought and fattened on all kinds of luxuries. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Lohita</span> protested to his brother, but the latter warned him of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Muṇika</span>’s fate. And soon after <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Muṇika</span> was killed and eaten.

The origin of the story is the same as that of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Nāradakassapajātaka</span>. The passion-tost monk was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Muṇika</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> the younger ox. Ja.i.196ff.
