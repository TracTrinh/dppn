# Mahā Nāma 03

## English source

Mahā Nāma 03. A <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> *rājā*, son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amitodana</span>; he was elder brother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> and cousin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>. When the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> families of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> sent their representatives to join the Saṅgha of their distinguished kinsman, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> allowed <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> to leave the household, he knowing nothing of household affairs. Vin.ii.180f.; Dhp­a.i.133; iv.124, etc.; but according to Northern sources (Rockhill, p. 13) he was son of Dronodana; according to Thag­a. (ii.123) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> was a brother (or, at least, a step-brother) of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span>, for there <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>’s father is given as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amitodana</span>. But see MN­a.i.289, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span>’s father is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sukkodana</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Amitodana</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> showed great generosity to the Saṅgha, and was proclaimed best of those who gave choice alms to the monks (AN.i.26). Once, with the Buddha’s permission, he supplied the Saṅgha with medicaments for three periods of four months each. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chabbaggiyā</span>, always intent on mischief, tried in vain to discourage him. Vin.iv.101; AN­a. (i.213) adds that this was during the period of want experienced by the Buddha and his monks at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verañjā</span>. At the end of the year, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> wished to continue the supply of good food to the Buddha and his monks, but the Buddha refused his permission.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> was a devoted follower of the Buddha and wished to understand the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t20">Dhamma</span>. The books record several conversations between him and the Buddha, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Godha</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lomasavaṅgīsa</span> (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāmasutta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lomasavaṅgīsa</span>). Once when the Buddha arrived at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> he asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> to find him lodging for the night. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> tried everywhere without success, and finally suggested that the Buddha should spend the night in the hermitage of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bharaṇḍu Kāḷāma</span> (SN.v.327f). This he did, and was joined there the next morning by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span>; as a result of the discussion between the Buddha, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bharaṇḍu</span>, the last-named left <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span> never to return. On another occasion, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> visited the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigrodhārāma</span> where the Buddha was convalescing after a severe illness, and at once <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> asked a question as to whether concentration followed or preceded knowledge. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, who was present, not wishing the Buddha to be troubled, took <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> aside and explained to him the Buddha’s teachings on the subject. See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakkasutta</span> (SN.i.219f.).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> had a daughter <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsabhakhattiyā</span>, born to him by a slave-girl named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgamuṇḍā</span>, and when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> asked the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> to give him in marriage a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyan</span> maiden they met in the Mote Hall, and, following the advice of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span>, sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsabhakhattiyā</span> to him. In order to allay any suspicions, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> sat down to a meal with her, taking one mouthful from the same dish; but before he could swallow it a messenger arrived, as secretly arranged, and summoned him away. He left, asking <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāsabhakhattiyā</span> to continue her meal (Dhp­a.i.345f.; Ja.i.133; iv. 145f).

See also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Dukkhakkhandhasutta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sekhasutta</span>, both preached to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span>.

His resolve to attain to eminence as the best distributor of pleasant food to the monks was made in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span>. He was then a householder of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Haṁsavatī</span> and heard the Buddha confer a similar rank on a monk (AN­a.i.213).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> is included in a list of exemplary lay devotees (AN.iii.451). The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Samantapāsādikā</span> (Vin-a.iv.857) adds that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Nāma</span> was one month older than the Buddha and that he was a *Sakadāgāmī*.
