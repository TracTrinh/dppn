# Mahā Maṅgalajātaka

## English source

Mahā Maṅgalajātaka (Ja 453). The Bodhisatta, called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rakkhita</span>, was born in a wealthy Brahmin family. He married, and then, having distributed all his wealth, became an ascetic with five hundred followers. During the rains, his disciples went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> and dwelt in the king’s park, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rakkhita</span> stayed in the hermitage. At that time there was a great discussion going on among men as to what constituted auspiciousness, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rakkhita</span>’s disciples, on being consulted, said that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rakkhita</span> would solve the problem. They, therefore, went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rakkhita</span>’s hermitage and asked him the question, which he answered in a series of eight verses. (The *maṅgalas* enumerated in these verses differ from those given in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṅgalasutta</span>). The disciples, having learnt the verses, returned to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, where they expounded them, thus setting all doubts at rest.

The story was related in reference to the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Maṅgalasutta</span>. It happened that in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> there was a large assembly at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Santhāgāra</span>, and a man rose and went out, saying, “This is a day of good omen.” Some one, hearing this, inquired the meaning of “good omen.” One said, “The sight of a lucky thing is a good omen.” But this was denied, and then began the discussion on omens, which, in the end, was carried to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, and referred by him to the Buddha. The senior disciple of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rakkhita</span> is identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> (Ja.iv.72-9).
