# Muṇḍa

## English source

Muṇḍa. A king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, great-grandson of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> and son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span>. He slew his father and came to the throne, but, in turn, he was slain by his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāgadāsaka</span> (Mhv.iv.2ff.; DN­a.i.153; Divy.369). It is probably this same king who is referred to in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅguttaranikāya</span> (iii.57ff). His wife <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā</span> died, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Muṇḍa</span> gave himself up to complete despair and mummified the queen’s body. The king’s Treasurer, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Piyaka</span>, consulted the Elder <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span> who lived at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkuṭārāma</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span> and persuaded him to visit the king. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span> preached to him, and his sorrow vanished.
