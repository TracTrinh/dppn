# Mejjha

## English source

Mejjha. A king and his country. The sixteen thousand Brahmins who had enjoyed the patronage of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṇḍavya</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), after they had lost caste through having eaten the leavings of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span>, went to live in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Meghiyaraṭṭha</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span> himself proceeded thither that he might humble their pride. The Brahmins saw him and reported to the king that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span> was a juggler and a mountebank. The king, therefore, sent messengers to seize him. They found him sitting on a bench eating, and, approaching him from behind, struck him dead with their swords. The gods were enraged, and pouring down hot ashes on the kingdom utterly destroyed it (Ja.iv.388f; MN­a.ii.613ff). The country became a wilderness known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mejjhārañña</span> (e.g., MN.i.378; Ja.v.114, 267; Mil.130).

The scene of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vighāsajātaka</span> (Ja 393) is said to have been in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mejjhārañña</span> (Ja.iii.310). The wilderness was also known as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅgārañña</span>, being connected with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span>. MN­a.ii.615.
