# Madda

## English source

Madda, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maddā</span>. The name of a country and its people (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Maddā</span>). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusajātaka</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusa</span>, son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Okkāka</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sammata</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malla</span> country, is mentioned as having married <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pabhāvatī</span>, daughter of the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span>, and the capital of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span> king was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgala</span> (Ja.v.283ff.; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusāvati</span> was one hundred leagues from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgala</span> (Ja.v.290), cp. Mvu.ii.441f).

In the similar story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anitthigandha</span>, a prince of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> contracts a marriage with a daughter of the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgala</span> his name being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maddava</span>; but the girl dies on the way to her husband. (Snp­a.i.68f.; cp. Dhp­a.iii.281, about the other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anitthigandha</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> of the Buddha’s days, who also married a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span> princess).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Chaddantajātaka</span> also mentions a matrimonial alliance between the royal houses of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> and Sāga<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">la</span>, while in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāliṅgabodhijātaka</span> (Ja.iv.230f) the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span> king’s daughter marries a prince of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāliṅga</span> while both are in exile.

Ja.v.39f.; so also in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mūgapakkhajātaka</span> (Ja.vi.1), the wife of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span> king was the daughter of the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candadevī</span> by name; while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Phusatī</span>, wife of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sañjaya</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetuttara</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sivi</span> kingdom and mother of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span>, was also a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span> princess (Ja.vi.480); likewise <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maddī</span>, wife of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷanī</span>, son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Talatā</span>, also married a princess of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span> (Ja.vi.471). According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsa</span> (Mhv.viii.7; this probably refers to Madras and not to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span> country, whose capital was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgala</span>), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sumitta</span>, son of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīhabāhu</span> and king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīhapura</span>, married the daughter of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span> king and had three sons by him, the youngest of whom, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Paṇḍuvāsudeva</span>, became king of Ceylon.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhaddā Kāpilānī</span> wife of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pippali</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t18">māṇava</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span>), was the daughter of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosiyagotta</span> Brahmin of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgala</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span> country. Men went there in search of a wife for him because it was famed for the beauty of its women (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Maddaraṭṭham nāma itthāgāro</span>) (Thag­a.ii.142; Thīg­a.68). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anojā</span>, wife of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kappina</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kukkuṭavatī</span>, also came from the royal household of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span> (Dhp­a.ii.116), as did <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khemā</span>, wife of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> (Thīg­a.127).

The wife of a Cakkavattī comes either from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span> or from the royal family of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span> (MN­a.ii.950; DN­a.ii.626; Khp­a.173).

For the identification of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Madda</span> see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāgala</span>.
