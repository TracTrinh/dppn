# Mātaṅga 03

## English source

Mātaṅga 03. A hermit. One day he arrived in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> and went to a potter’s hall for the night. He found the place already occupied by another hermit named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātimā</span>, and was told by the potter that he could only stay there with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātimā</span>’s permission. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātimā</span> agreed to his staying, but, on finding that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span> was a *Caṇḍāla*, he wished him to occupy a place apart. During the night <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span> wished to go out, and, not knowing where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātimā</span> was lying, trod on his chest. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span> returned he took the other way with the idea of passing near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātimā</span>’s feet, but meanwhile <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātimā</span> had changed his position, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span> again trod on his chest. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātimā</span> thereupon cursed him, saying that his head would split in seven pieces at sunrise. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅga</span> thereupon stopped the sun from rising (SN­a.ii.176f).

The rest of the story is as in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātaṅgajātaka</span>. It may be a variety of the same legend. cp. also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nārada</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devala</span>.
