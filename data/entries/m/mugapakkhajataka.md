# Mūgapakkhajātaka

## English source

Mūgapakkhajātaka, also called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Temiyajātaka</span> (Ja 538). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candādevī</span>, wife of the king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span>, had, to her great grief, no son. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s throne was heated by her piety, and he persuaded the Bodhisatta, then in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, to be born as her son. The Bodhisatta reluctantly agreed. Great were the rejoicings over his birth. He was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Temiya</span> because on the day of his birth there was a great shower throughout the kingdom and he was born wet. When he was one month old, he was brought to the king, and, as he lay in his lap, he heard grievous sentences passed on some robbers brought before the king. Later, as he lay in bed, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Temiya</span> recollected his past births and remembered how he had once reigned for twenty years as king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span>, and, as a result, had suffered in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ussada</span> *niraya* for twenty thousand years. Anguish seized him at the thought of having to be king once more, but the goddess of his parasol, who had once been his mother, consoled him by advising him to pretend to be dumb and incapable of any action. He took this advice, and for sixteen years the king and queen, in consultation with the ministers and others, tried every conceivable means of breaking his resolve, knowing him to be normal in body. But all their attempts failed, and at last he was put in a chariot and sent with the royal charioteer, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunanda</span>, to the charnel ground, where he was to be clubbed to death and buried. At the queen’s urgent request, however, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Temiya</span> was appointed to rule over <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāsi</span> for one week before being put to death, but the enjoyment of royal power did not weaken his resolve. The charioteer, under the influence of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>, took <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Temiya</span> to what he considered to be the charnel-ground and there, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunanda</span> was digging the grave, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Temiya</span> stole up behind him and confided to him his purpose and his resolve to lead the ascetic life. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunanda</span> was so impressed by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Temiya</span>’s words that he immediately wished to become an ascetic himself, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Temiya</span> desired him to inform his parents of what had happened. When the king and queen heard <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunanda</span>’s news, they went with all their retinue to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Temiya</span>’s hermitage and there, after hearing <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Temiya</span> preach, they all became ascetics. The inhabitants of the three kingdoms adjacent to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Benares</span> followed their example, and great was the number of ascetics. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vissakamma</span> provided shelter for them. The crowds who thus flocked together were called the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mūgapakkha</span> *samāgama*. With the death of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malayamahādeva</span> Thera (*q.v.*) came the end of those who participated in this great collection of ascetics.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Temiya</span>’s parents are identified with the parents of the Buddha, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunanda</span> with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and the goddess of the parasol with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uppalavaṇṇā</span>. The story was told in reference to the Buddha’s Renunciation (Ja.vi.1-30; the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Temiya</span> is also given in Temiyacariyā in Cpiii.6). It is often referred to (e.g., Bv­a.51) as giving an example of the Bodhisattva’s great determination. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammikasutta</span> (*q.v.*) mentions <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mūgapakkha</span> in a list of teachers of old.
