# Mahā Sāropamasutta

## English source

Mahā Sāropamasutta. Preached at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gijjhakūṭa</span>, soon after <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>’s secession. Some young men leave the household, being lured by the life of a monk. As monks, they receive presents, esteem, and repute. These things so please them and satisfy their aspirations that they become conceited and disparage others. Thus they grow remiss and a prey to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t20">suffering</span>. Their case resembles that of a man who, needing the best of timber, goes into a forest and is satisfied with cutting off the leafy foliage or the bark of the trees, knowing nothing of the grades of wood. On the other hand, the monk who is satisfied only when he reaches the end of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t20" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t20">suffering</span> is like a man who takes only the choicest timber, passing over the other. MN.i.192-7.
