# Manojajātaka

## English source

Manojajātaka (Ja 397). The Bodhisatta was once a lion and had a son called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manoja</span>. One day <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manoja</span>, in spite of his father’s warnings, made friends with a jackal called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Giriya</span>. Acting on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Giriya</span>’s suggestion, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manoja</span> went in search of horse flesh and attacked the king’s horses. The king engaged the services of an archer, who shot <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manoja</span> as he was making off with a horse. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manoja</span> managed to reach his den, but there he fell down dead (Ja.iii.321ff).

The circumstances in which the story was related are similar to those of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahilāmukhajātaka</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>).
