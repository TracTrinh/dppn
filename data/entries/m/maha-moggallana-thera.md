# Mahā Moggallāna Thera

## English source

Mahā Moggallāna Thera. The second of the Chief Disciples of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama Buddha</span>. He was born in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷitagāma</span> near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, on the same day as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> (they were both older than the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddha</span>), and was called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koḷita</span> after his village. His mother was a *Brāhmiṇī* called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggalī</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāni</span>), and his father was the chief householder of the village. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>’s and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>’s families had maintained an unbroken friendship for seven generations, and so the children were friends from their childhood. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> had five hundred golden palanquins and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> five hundred carriages drawn by thoroughbreds. One day the two friends went together to see a mime play (*giraggasamajjā*), and there, realizing the impermanence of things, decided to renounce the world. They first lived as disciples of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sañjaya</span>, and then wandered all over <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>, discussing with all learned men, but finding no satisfaction. Then they separated, after agreeing that whoever first succeeded in finding what they sought should inform the other.

After some time, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, wandering about in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span>, met <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji</span>, was converted by him to the faith of the Buddha, and became a *Sotāpanna*. He found <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> and repeated the stanza he had heard from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji</span> (*ye dhammā hetuppabhavā*, etc.), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> also became a *Sotāpanna*. The two then resolved to visit the Buddha at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span>, after an unsuccessful attempt to persuade <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sañjaya</span> to accompany them. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sañjaya</span>’s disciples, however, five hundred in number, agreed to go, and they all arrived at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span>. The Buddha preached to them, and ordained them by the “*ehi bhikkhu*” *pabbajjā*. All became *Arahants* except <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> went to the hamlet of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kallavāla</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, and there, on the seventh day after his ordination, drowsiness overcame him as he sat meditating (for details see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pacalasutta</span>, AN.iv.85f, where the village is called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kallavālamutta</span>). The Buddha knew this, and appearing before him, exhorted him to be zealous. That very day he attained *Arahant*-ship.

On the day that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> were ordained, the Buddha announced in the assembly of monks that he had assigned to them the place of Chief Disciples and then recited the *Pātimokkha*. The monks were offended that newcomers should be shown such great honour. But the Buddha told them how these two had for a whole *asaṅkheyya* and one hundred thousand aeons strenuously exerted themselves to win this great eminence under him. They had made the first resolve in the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anomadassī Buddha</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> had been a householder, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirivaḍḍha</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> a householder, called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarada</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarada</span> gave away his immense wealth and became an ascetic. The Buddha visited him in his hermitage, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarada</span> and his seventy-four thousand pupils showed him great honour. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anomadassī</span>’s chief disciple, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nisabha</span>, gave thanks, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarada</span> made a vow that he would become the chief disciple of some future Buddha. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anomadassī</span> saw that his wish would be fulfilled and told him so.

After the Buddha’s departure, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sarada</span> went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirivaḍḍha</span>, and, announcing the Buddha’s prophecy, advised <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirivaḍḍha</span> to wish for the place of second disciple. Acting on this advice, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirivaḍḍha</span> made elaborate preparations and entertained the Buddha and his monks for seven days. At the end of that time, he announced his wish to the Buddha, who declared that it would be fulfilled. From that time, the two friends, in that and subsequent births, engaged in good deeds. AN­a.i.84ff.; Ap.ii.31ff.; Dhp­a.i.73f.; Snp­a.i.326ff.; the story of the present is given in brief at Vin.i.39ff.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> are declared to be the ideal disciples, whose example others should try to follow (e.g., SN.ii.235; AN.i.88). In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saccavibhaṅgasutta</span> (MN.iii.248) the Buddha thus distinguishes these “twin brethren” from the others: “<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> is as she who brings forth and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> is as the nurse of what is brought forth; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> trains in the fruits of conversion, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> trains in the highest good. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> is able to teach and make plain the four Noble Truths; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>, on the other hand, teaches by his *iddhipāṭihāriya*” (Bv­a.31). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>’s pre-eminence lay in his possession of *iddhi* power (AN.i.23). He could create a living shape innumerable times and could transfer himself into any shape at will. Thag.vs.1183; he is recorded as saying that he could crush <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span> like a kidney bean, and, rolling the earth like a mat between his fingers, could make it rotate like a potter’s wheel, or could place the earth on <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span> like an umbrella on its stand (Dhp­a.iii.212). When the Buddha and his monks failed to get alms in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Verañjā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> offered to turn the earth upside down, so that the essence of the earth, which lay on the under surface, might serve as food. He also offered to open a way from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nalerupucimanda</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarakuru</span>, that the monks might easily go there for alms; but this offer was refused by the Buddha (Vin.iii.7; Vin-a.i.182f.; Dhp­a.ii.153).

Several instances are given of this special display of *iddhi*. Once, at the Buddha’s request, with his great toe he shook the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāramātupāsāda</span>, and made it rattle in order to terrify some monks who sat in the ground floor of the building, talking loosely and frivolously, regardless even of the fact that the Buddha was in the upper storey. See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasādakampanasutta</span>, SN.v.269ff.; also the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Utthāṇasutta</span>, Snp­a.i.336f.

On another occasion, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> to find out if he had profited by the Buddha’s teaching, he found him far too proud and obsessed by the thought of his own splendour. He thereupon shook <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s palace, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vejayanta</span>, till <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span>’s hair stood on end with fright and his pride was humbled (See <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cūḷa Taṇhāsaṅkhayasutta</span>, MN.i.251ff). Again, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> is mentioned as visiting the *Brahma* world in order to help the Buddha in quelling the arrogance of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bakabrahma</span>. He himself questioned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Baka</span> in solemn conclave in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudhammā</span> Hall in the *Brahma* world and made him confess his conviction that his earlier views were erroneous. Thag.vs.1198; Thag­a.ii.185; SN.i.144f.; other visits of his to the *Brahma* world are also recorded when he held converse with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span> *Brahma* (AN.iii.331ff.; iv.75ff.; cp. Mhv.i.54ff.).

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māratajjanīyasutta</span> (MN.i.332ff) we are told how <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> worried <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> by entering into his belly, but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> ordered him out and told him how he himself had once been a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span> named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dūsī</span> whose sister <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷī</span> was the mother of the present <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Māra</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dūsī</span> incited the householders against <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakusandha Buddha</span> and was, as a result, born in purgatory.

But, according to the Commentaries (e.g., Thag­a.ii.188ff), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>’s greatest exhibition of *iddhi* power was the subjugation of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandopananda</span>. No other monk could have survived the ordeal because no other was able to enter so rapidly into the fourth *jhāna*; which was the reason why the Buddha would give permission to no other monk but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> to quell the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span>’s pride. Similar, in many ways, was his subjection of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> who lived near the hermitage of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggidatta</span> (Dhp­a.iii.242, *q.v.*). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> could see, without entering into any special state of mind, *Petas* and other spirits invisible to the ordinary mortal eye (see, e.g., Dhp­a.ii.64; iii.60, 410f., 479; SN.ii.254ff.; where he saw *Petas* while in the company of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Lakkhaṇa</span>; cp. Avś.i.246ff.). He would visit various worlds and bring back to the Buddha reports of their inhabitants (see also Mhv.i.4ff. regarding his visit to the *Nirayas*), which the Buddha used in illustration of his sermons. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vimānavatthu</span> (see also Dhp­a.iii.291, *re* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nandiya</span>, and iii.314) contains a collection of stories of such visits, and we are told (SN.v.366f) that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>’s visits to the *Deva* worlds, e.g., that to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span> were very welcome to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span>.

Though <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>’s pre-eminence was in *iddhi* power, yet in wisdom, too, he was second only to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="104" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>. These two could answer questions within the range of no other disciple of the Buddha (Dhp­a.iii.227). The Buddha paid a compliment to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="105" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>’s powers of preaching, when, having preached himself to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="106" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākiyans</span> in their new Mote Hall at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="107" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, he asked <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="108" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>, after their departure, to talk to the monks, as he himself was weary. And <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="109" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> spoke to them of lusts and of the means of getting rid of them. At the end of the sermon the Buddha praised him warmly (SN.iv.183ff). Mention is made elsewhere of eloquent sermons preached by him on the *jhānas* (SN.iv.262-9), on qualities which lead to true emancipation (AN.v.155ff), and of visits paid to him by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="110" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakka</span> in company with numerous other gods in order to hear him preach. Other <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="111" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> also went to hear him – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="112" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="113" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suyāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="114" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Santusita</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="115" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunimitta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="116" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vasavatti</span> (SN.iv. 269-80). He was also consulted by those, such as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="117" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vacchagotta</span> (e.g., SN.iv.391ff), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="118" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vappa</span> (AN.ii.196ff), eager to learn from him the teachings of the Buddha. When the Buddha went to preach the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="119" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Abhidhamma</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="120" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, it was to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="121" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> that he entrusted the task of preaching to the people who were waiting for his return. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="122" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>, therefore, provided for these people spiritually, while <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="123" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anāthapiṇḍika</span> looked after their bodily needs (Dhp­a.iii.219). When the time drew near for the Buddha’s return, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="124" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>, at the request of the people, went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="125" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tāvatiṁsa</span>, diving into the earth and climbing <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="126" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sineru</span>, in full view of them all, in order to find out what the Buddha intended doing, so that the people might be kept informed (Dhp­a.224; Ja.iv.265; cp. Divy.375). No task, which he might be told by the Buddha to perform, seemed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="127" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> too insignificant. Thus we find him employed by the Buddha as messenger to the *Arahant* <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="128" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uggasena</span>, telling him that the Buddha wished to see him (ibid., iv.62). He was also sent to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="129" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sakkhara</span>, to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="130" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Macchariya Kosiya</span>, to check his miserliness and bring him to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="131" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> (ibid., i.369f.; Ja.i.347); and to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="132" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīlavā</span>, whom <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="133" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> was plotting to kill (Thag­a.i.536). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="134" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> was building the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="135" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāramātupāsāda</span> and the Buddha was away on one of his journeys, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="136" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>, because of his *iddhi* power, and five hundred monks were left to supervise the work, which was carried through without difficulty (Dhp­a.i.414f). The Buddha placed great faith in his two chief disciples and looked to them to keep the Saṅgha pure. There is one instance recorded of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="137" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> seizing a wicked monk, thrusting outside and bolting the door (AN.iv.204ff.). Once, when a monk charged <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="138" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> with having offended him as he was about to start on a journey, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="139" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="140" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> went from lodging to lodging to summon the monks that they might hear <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="141" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> vindicate himself (Vin.ii.236; AN.iv.374).

Their fame had reached even to the *Brahma* world, for we find <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="142" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tudu</span> *Brahma* singing their praises, much to the annoyance of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="143" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokālika</span> monk (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="144" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kokalika</span> had a great hatred of them – e.g., AN.v.170ff.; Snp., p. 231ff.; Snp­a.ii.473ff).

When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="145" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span> created a schism among the monks and took five hundred of them to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="146" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gayāsīsa</span>, the Buddha sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="147" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="148" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> to bring them back. They were successful in this mission (Dhp­a.i.143ff.; see also Dhp­a.ii.109f., where they were sent to admonish the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="149" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Assaji-Punabbasukā</span>). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="150" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakudha Koḷiyaputta</span>, once servant of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="151" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> and later born in a huge *manomayakāya*, had warned <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="152" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="153" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devadatta</span>’s intrigues against the Buddha, but the Buddha ignored this information carried to him by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="154" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> (Vin.ii.185; AN.iii.122ff). When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="155" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span>, the Buddha’s son, was ordained, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="156" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> was his preceptor and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="157" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> his teacher (Ja.i.161; see Snp­a.i.304f., where the account is slightly different. There <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="158" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> is spoken of as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="159" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāhula</span>’s *kammavācācariya*.). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="160" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> seems to have carried out diligently the charge laid on him by the Buddha of looking after the monks’ welfare. Among the verses, attributed to him in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="161" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span>, are several containing exhortations to his colleagues (Thag.vss.1146-9, 1165f); some of the colleagues are mentioned by name – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="162" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tissa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="163" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaḍḍhamāna</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="164" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Poṭṭhila</span> (Thag.vss.1162, 1163, 1174f). Elsewhere (SN.i.194f) mention is made of his living at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="165" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷasilā</span>, with a company of five hundred monks, watching over them and discovering that all were *Arahants*. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="166" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṅgīsa</span> witnessed this and praised <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="167" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> in verse before the Buddha.

The love existing between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="168" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="169" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> was mutual, as was the admiration. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="170" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>’s verses in praise of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="171" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> (Thag.vss.1178-81) are even more eloquent than those of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="172" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> in praise of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="173" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> (Thag.vss.1176). Their strongest bond was the love of each for the Buddha; when away from him, they would relate to each other how they had been conversing with him by means of the divine ear and the divine eye, e.g., SN.ii.275ff.; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="174" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> elsewhere alsotells the monks of a conversation he held with the Buddha by means of these divine powers (SN.ii.273f.). For another discussion between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="175" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="176" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>, see AN.ii.154f.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="177" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Gosiṅgasutta</span> (MN.i.212) we find them staying in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="178" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gosiṅgasālavana</span> in the company of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="179" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Kassapa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="180" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="181" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Revata</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="182" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span>, engaged in friendly discussion, referring their conclusions to the Buddha for his opinion. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="183" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="184" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="185" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> are again mentioned as staying in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="186" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ketakavana</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="187" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāketa</span> (SN.v.174f., 299). Among discussions between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="188" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="189" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> is recorded one in which <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="190" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anuruddha</span> speaks of the value of cultivating the four *satipaṭṭhāna*s (SN.v.294f). It seems to have been usual for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="191" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="192" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>, in their journeys, to travel together at the head of the monks, and lay disciples, who gave alms to the monks, were anxious to include them in their invitations. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="193" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷukaṇḍaki</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="194" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dakkhiṇagiri</span> (AN.iii.336; iv.63); and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="195" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cittagahapati</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="196" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Macchikāsaṇḍa</span> (Dhp­a.ii.74f.).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="197" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> died before the Buddha, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="198" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> dying before either. The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="199" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Theragāthā</span> contains several verses attributed to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="200" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> regarding <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="201" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span>’s death (vs.1158-61). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="202" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> died on the full moon day of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="203" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kattika</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="204" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> two weeks later, on the new moon day (SN­a.iii.181).

According to the Commentaries (Ja.v.125ff) his death resulted from a plot of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="205" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭhas</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="206" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> used to visit various worlds and return with his report that he had discovered that those who followed the Buddha’s teaching reached happy worlds, while the followers of the heretics were reborn in woeful conditions. These statements diminished the number of the heretics and they bribed brigands to kill <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="207" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>. They surrounded the Elder’s cell in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="208" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷasilā</span>, but he, aware of their intentions, escaped through the keyhole. On six successive days this happened; on the seventh, they caught him and beat him, crushing his bones and leaving him for dead. Having recovered consciousness, with a great effort of will, he dragged himself to the Buddha in order to take his leave, and there he died, to the sorrow of the *Deva* worlds. This sad death is said to have been the result of a sin committed by him in a previous birth. Acting on the instigation of his wife, he had taken his blind parents into a forest, where, pretending that they were attacked by thieves, he had beaten them to death. For this deed he suffered in hell for innumerable years, and in his last birth lost his life by violence.

The account in Dhp­a.iii.65ff. differs in several details. The thieves tried for two months before succeeding in their plot and, in the story of the past, when the blind parents were being beaten, they cried out to the supposed thieves to spare their son. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="209" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>, very touched by this, did not kill them. Before passing into *Nibbāna*, he preached to the Buddha, at his request, and performed many miracles, returning to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="210" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kāḷasilā</span> to die. According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="211" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jātaka</span> account his cremation was performed with much honour, and the Buddha had the relics collected and a *Thūpa* erected in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="212" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Veḷuvana</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="213" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>’s body was of the colour of the blue lotus or the rain cloud (Bv.i.58). There exists in Ceylon an oral tradition that this colour is due to his having suffered in hell in the recent past!
