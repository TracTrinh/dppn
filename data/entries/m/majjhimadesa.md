# Majjhimadesa

## English source

Majjhimadesa. The country of Central India which was the birthplace of Buddhism and the region of its early activities. It extended in the east to the town of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kajaṅgala</span>, beyond which was <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sāla</span>; on the south-east to the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Salaḷavatī</span>; on the south-west to the town of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Satakaṇṇika</span>; on the west to the Brahmin village of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Thūna</span>; on the north to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Usiraddhaja</span> Mountain.

Vin.i.197; Ja.i.49, 80; Mbv.12; Divy.21f, extends the eastern boundary to include Puṇḍavardhana, roughly identical with North Bengal. It is interesting to note that in early Brahminical literature (e.g. the *Dharmasūtra* of Baudhāyana), Āryāvarta, which is practically identical with what came to be called Madhyadesa, is described as lying to the east of the region where the Sarasvatī disappears, to the west of the *Kāḷakavana*, to the north of *Pāripātra*, and to the south of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span>. This excludes the whole of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> (Baudhāyana i. 1, 2, 9, etc.).

It is also noteworthy that in the Commentaries the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhimadesa</span> is extended to include the whole of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jambudīpa</span>, the other continents being *Paccantimajanapadā*. The term came also to be used in a generic sense. Thus, in Ceylon (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Tambapaṇṇidīpa</span>) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurādhapura</span> came to be called the Majjhimadesa (AN­a.i.165).

The Majjhimadesa was three hundred *yojanas* in length, two hundred and fifty in breadth, and nine hundred in circumference (DN­a.i.173). It contained fourteen of the sixteen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janapadas</span>, that is to say all but <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhāra</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kamboja</span>, which belonged to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarāpatha</span>.

The people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhimadesa</span> were regarded as wise and virtuous (Ja.iii.115, 116). It was the birthplace of noble men (*purisājanīyā*) including the Buddhas (Dhp­a.iii.248; AN­a.i.265), and all kinds of marvellous things happened there (Snp­a.i.197). The people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Majjhimadesa</span> considered peacocks’ flesh a luxury. Vibh­a.10.
