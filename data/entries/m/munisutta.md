# Munisutta

## English source

Munisutta. The twelfth <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suttanipāta</span> (Snp., pp. 35-8). It defines the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t18">muni</span> as one who lives the homeless life, free of encumbrances, devoid of strife and covetousness, firm, self-restrained, thoughtful, and delighting in meditation. He has overcome all obstacles and knows all things. He is as different from a householder as a peacock from a fast-flying goose. According to the Commentary (Snp­a.i.254 t) the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sutta</span> is a composite one made up of stanzas preached on various occasions; thus, the first four verses had reference to a mother and a son who joined the Saṅgha, met frequently, and, owing to their affection for each other, fell into sin (cp. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātuputtikasutta</span>). The fifth was in reference to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upaka</span>’s attainment of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t18">anāgāmiphala</span>; the sixth to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khadiravaniya Revata</span>; the seventh was preached to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suddhodana</span> to explain why the Buddha had renounced his luxuries. The ninth was in reference to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ciñcā</span>’s attempt to malign the Buddha; the tenth was preached to the daughter of a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span>. Seeing a weaver’s spindle and reflecting on it, she realized the crookedness of beings and was disgusted with the worldly life. The Buddha, reading her thoughts, appeared before her in a ray of light and preached to her. The eleventh was preached to the seven-year old daughter of a weaver of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Āḷavi</span>, who became a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sotāpanna</span> and died soon after (See Dhp­a.iii.170ff). The twelfth was preached to the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pañcaggadāyaka</span>; and the thirteenth was in reference to a treasurer of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> who joined the Saṅgha and left it three times, on the fourth time of joining he became an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>. The fourteenth was in reference to the Buddha’s cousin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span>, who was being teased by the monks even after he had attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship. The last was in defence of a monk for whom a hunter conceived a friendship, providing him with alms; the monk was a forest dweller, and men blamed him saying that he told the hunter of the animals’ haunts.
