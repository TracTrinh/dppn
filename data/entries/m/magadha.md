# Magadha

## English source

Magadha. One of the four chief kingdoms of India at the time of the Buddha, the others being <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span>, the kingdom of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vaṁsas</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Avanti</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> formed one of the sixteen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janapadas</span> and had its capital at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> or <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Giribbaja</span> where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, and after him <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, reigned. Later, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span> became the capital. By the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span>, too, formed a part of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>, and he was known as king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga-Magadha</span> (see, e.g., Vin.i.27 and Thag­a.i.544, where <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> sends for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa Koḷivisa</span>, a prominent citizen of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Campā</span>, capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span>). But prior to that, these were two separate kingdoms, often at war with each other (e.g., Ja.iv.454f). Several kings of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> are mentioned by name in the Jātakas – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Arindama</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Duyyodhana</span>. In one story the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> kingdom is said to have been under the suzerainty of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span> (Ja.vi.272). In the Buddha’s day, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> (inclusive of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span>) consisted of eighty thousand villages (Vin.i.179) and had a circumference of some three hundred leagues (DN­a.i.148).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> succeeded in annexing <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kosala</span> with the help of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>, and he succeeded also in bringing the confederation of the latter under his sway; preliminaries to this struggle are mentioned in the books (e.g., DN.ii.73f., 86).

Under <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> rose to such political eminence that for several centuries, right down to the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>, the history of Northern India was practically the history of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> (a list of the kings from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> is found in Divy.369; cp. DN­a.i.153; Mbv.96, 98).

At the time of the Buddha, the kingdom of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> was bounded on the east by the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Campā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Campā</span> flowed between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>; Ja.iv.454), on the south by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vindhyā</span> Mountains, on the west by the river <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Soṇa</span>, and on the north by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span>. The latter river formed the boundary between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> and the republican country of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>, and both the Māgadhas and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> evidently had equal rights over the river. When the Buddha visited <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> made a road five leagues long, from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> to the river, and decorated it, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> did the same on the other side. Dhp­a.iii.439 f.; the *Divyāvadāna* (1p.55) says that monks going from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rājagaha</span> could cross the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ganges</span> in boats kept either by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span> or by the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vesālī</span>.

During the early Buddhist period <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> was an important political and commercial centre, and was visited by people from all parts of Northern India in search of commerce and of learning. The kings of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> maintained friendly relations with their neighbours, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pasenadi</span> marrying each other’s sisters. Mention is made of an alliance between <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusāti</span>, king of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gandhāra</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span>. When <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Caṇḍappajjota</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ujjenī</span> was suffering from jaundice, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bimbisāra</span> sent him his own personal physician, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jīvaka</span>.

In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> was the real birth of Buddhism (see, e.g., the words put in the mouth of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sahampati</span> in Vin.i.5, *pātur-ahosi Magadhesu pubbe Dhammo*, etc.), and it was from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> that it spread after the Third Council. The Buddha’s chief disciples, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāriputta</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span>, came from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>. In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s time the income from the four gates of his capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaliputta</span> was four hundred thousand *kahāpaṇas* daily, and in the Sabhā, or Council, he would daily receive another hundred thousand *kahāpaṇas* (Vin-a.i.52). The cornfields of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> were rich and fertile (Thag.vs.208), and each <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> field was about one *gāvuta* in extent. Thus AN­a.ii.616 explains the extent of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kakudha</span>’s body, which filled two or three Māgadha village fields (AN.iii.122).

The names of several places in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> occur in the books, e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ekanāḷā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālakagāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Senāṇigāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khānumata</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Andhakavindha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Macala</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mātulā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="87" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambalaṭṭhikā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="88" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāṭaligāma</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="89" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nālandā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="90" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sālindiya</span>.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="91" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> says (Snp­a.i.135 f) that there are many fanciful explanations (*bahudhā papañcanti*) of the word <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="92" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span>. One such is that king <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="93" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cetiya</span>, when about to be swallowed up by the earth for having introduced lying into the world, was thus admonished by those standing round – “*Mā gadhaṁ pavisa*;” another that those who were digging in the earth saw the king, and that he said to them: “*Mā gadhaṁ karotha*.” The real explanation, accepted by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="94" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Buddhaghosa</span> himself, seems to have been that the country was the residence of a tribe of *Khattiyas* called Magadhā.

The *Magadhabhāsā* is regarded as the speech of the Āriyans (e.g., Vin-a.i.255). If children grow up without being taught any language, they will spontaneously use the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="95" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> language; it is spread all over *Niraya*, among lower animals, *Petas*, humans and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="96" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devas</span> (Vibh­a.387f).

The people of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="97" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅga</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="98" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> were in the habit of holding a great annual sacrifice to *Māha Brahma* in which a fire was kindled with sixty cartloads of firewood. They held the view that anything cast into the sacrificial fire would bring a thousand-fold reward. SN­a.i.269; but it is curious that in Vedic, *Brāhmaṇa* and *Sūtra* periods, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="99" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> was considered as outside the pale of Ariyan and Brahmanical culture, and was therefore looked down upon by Brahmanical writers. But it was the holy land of the Buddhists. See VT.ii.207; Thomas: *Life and Legend of the Buddha*, 13, 96.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="100" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> was famous for a special kind of garlic (Vin-a.iv.920) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="101" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> *nāla* was a standard of measure. (e.g., AN­a.i.101).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="102" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadha</span> is identified with the modern South Behar. See also <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="103" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Magadhakhetta</span>.
