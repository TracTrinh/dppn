# Moriyā

## English source

Moriyā, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moriyas</span>. A *Khattiya* clan of India. Among those claiming a share of the Buddha’s relics were the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moriyas</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pipphalivana</span>. They came rather late and had to be satisfied with a share of the ashes (DN.ii.166; Bv.xxviii.4).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candagutta</span>, grandfather of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>, was also a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moriyan</span> (Mhv.v.16; Dpv.vi.19).

The <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vaṁsaṭīkā</span> (Mhv­ṭ. 180) contains an account of the origin of the name. According to one theory they were so-called because they rejoiced in the prosperity of their city (*attānaṁ nagarasiriyā modāpīti, ettha sañjātā ti, dakārassa rakāraṁ katvā Moriyā ti laddhavohārā*). They lived in a delightful land. Another theory connects the name with *mora* (peacock). The city which they founded had buildings of blue stone, like the neck of the peacock, and the place always resounded with the cries of peacocks. It is said that the Moriyans were originally <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sākyan</span> princes of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kapilavatthu</span>, who escaped to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Himālaya</span> regions to save themselves from the attacks of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Viḍūḍabha</span>, and established a city there. Thus <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span> was a kinsman of the Buddha, for <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Candagutta</span> was the son of the chief queen of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moriyan</span> king. The king was killed by a neighbouring ruler and the city pillaged. Mhv­ṭ.183; but according to the *Mudrārākṣasa* (Act iii.) Candragupta, was a *Vṛṣala*, a person of low birth, an illegitimate son of the last <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span>, king by a *Sūdra* woman, Murā.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asoka</span>’s mother, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammā</span>, was also a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moriyan</span> princess (Mhv­ṭ.189). Mention is also made of the Moriyans as a Sinhalese clan (Cv.xxxviii.13; xli.69; see also Cv.Trs.i.29, n.2). Whether these had any connection with the Moriyans of India is not known.
