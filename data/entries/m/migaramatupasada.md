# Migāramātupāsāda

## English source

Migāramātupāsāda. The name given to the monastery erected by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Migāramātā</span> in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pubbārāma</span>, to the east of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>. It is said (Dhp­a.i.410ff.; Snp­a.ii.502; Ud­a.158; DN­a.iii.860; SN­a.i.116, etc.) that, one day, when <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> had gone to the monastery to hear the Dhamma and afterwards attend on the sick monks and novices, she left in the preaching hall her <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Latāpasādhana</span> and her servant girl forgot to remove it (this incident is referred to at Vin.iv.161f., as the cause of the institution of a Vinaya rule).

Later, on going to fetch it, she found that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> had put it away, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>, being told of this, decided not to wear it again. She had it valued by goldsmiths, who declared that it was worth nine crores and one hundred thousand. She had the ornament put in a cart and sent round for sale. But there was none in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span> rich enough to buy it, and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> herself bought it back. With the money thus obtained she built the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāramātupāsāda</span> at the Buddha’s suggestion. The site for the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t18">pāsāda</span> on the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pubbārāma</span> cost nine crores, the buildings costing another nine. While the building was being erected, the Buddha went on one of his journeys and, at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>’s request; <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> was left to supervise the work with five hundred other monks. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Moggallāna</span> made use of his <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t18">iddhi</span> powers in order to expedite and facilitate the work. The building had two floors with five hundred rooms in each, the whole structure being surmounted by a pinnacle of solid gold, capable of holding sixty water pots. The work was completed in nine months, and the celebration of its dedication was held on the Buddha’s return. These celebrations lasted for four months and cost a further nine crores. On the last day, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span> gave gifts of cloth to the monks, each novice receiving robes worth one thousand. The building was so richly equipped that one of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākhā</span>’s friends, wishing to spread a small carpet, worth one hundred thousand, wandered all over the building, but could find no place of which it was worthy. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> found her weeping in disappointment, and suggested that it should be spread between the foot of the stairs and the spot where the monks washed their feet.

During the last twenty years of his life, when the Buddha was living at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sāvatthī</span>, he divided his time between the Anāthapiṇḍikārāma at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Jetavana</span> and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāramātupāsāda</span>, spending the day in one place and the night in the other and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">vice versa</span> (Snp­a.i.336).

It is, therefore, to be expected that numerous <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Suttas</span> were preached there; chief among these were the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aggañña</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Utthāṇa</span>, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariyapariyesana</span>, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāsādakampana</span>. See also SN.i.77, 190 (= Ud.vi.2); iii.100; v. 216, 222f.; AN.i.193f.; ii.183f.; iii.344f.; (cp Thag.vss.689-704); iv. 204f., 255, 265, 269; Ud.ii.9; Dhp­a.iv.142f.; iv.176.

It was at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Migāramātupāsāda</span> that the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vighāsajātaka</span> (Ja 393) was preached, and the Buddha gave permission for the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pātimokkha</span> to be recited in his absence. Vin-a.i.187.
