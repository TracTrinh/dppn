# Mithilā

## English source

Mithilā. The capital of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span> country. The city was very ancient, and, according to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Govindasutta</span> (DN.ii.235), was founded by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Govinda</span>, steward of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Reṇu</span>. It was also the capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makhādeva</span> (MN.ii.72f; Mhv­ṭ.129; see also Dpv.iii.9, 29, 35) and eighty-four thousand of his descendants, and of various other kings mentioned in the Jātakas – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Aṅgati</span> (vi. 220), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ariṭṭhajanaka</span> (vi. 30), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nimi</span> (iii.378), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videha</span> (ii.39), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vedeha</span> (vi.330), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janaka</span> (vi. 30f.), <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sādhīna</span> (iv. 355), and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suruci</span> (ii.333).

The size of the city is frequently given (e.g., Ja.iii.365) as seven leagues in circumference, and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janakajātaka</span> (Ja 539, Ja.vi.46f) contains a description of it. There was a road leading from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Campā</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span>, a distance of sixty leagues (Ja.vi.32).

According to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Ummaggajātaka</span> (Ja 546, Ja.vi.330f) there were four market towns at the four gates of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span>, each being known by the name of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yavamajjhaka</span>.

The Buddha is mentioned as having stayed in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span> and having preached there the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makhādevasutta</span> (MN.ii.74) and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Brahmāyusutta</span> (MN.ii.133).

It was also in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span> that <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāseṭṭhī Therī</span> (Thīg. vs. 135; see also Divy., p. 60) first met the Buddha and entered the Saṅgha, after having heard him preach.

After the Buddha’s death, the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Videhas</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span> claimed a part of his relics and obtained them (Bv.xxviii.11).

In the time of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Koṇāgamana Buddha Mithilā</span> was the capital of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pabbata</span>, and the Buddha preached there on his visit to the city (Bv­a. 215). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Padumuttara Buddha</span> preached his first sermon to his cousins, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Devala</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sujāta</span>, in the park of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span> (Bv.xi.23; Bv­a.159), and later to King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> and his retinue in the same spot (Bv­a.160).

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span> is generally identified with <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janakapura</span>, a small town within the Nepal border, north of which the Mazaffarpur and Darbhanga districts meet (CAGI.718).

In the Indian Epics (e.g., *Rāmāyaṇa* i. 48) <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mithilā</span>, is chiefly famous as the residence of King <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Janaka</span>.
