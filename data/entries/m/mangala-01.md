# Maṅgala 01

## English source

Maṅgala 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṅgala Buddha</span>. The third of the twenty-four Buddhas. He was born in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttaramadhura</span> Park, in the city of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span>, his father being a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Khattiya</span> named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span> and his mother <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span>. It is said that from the day of her conception, an aura shed its rays night and day from her body, to a distance of eighty hands – hence his name. He surpassed other Buddhas in glory of body. In his last birth as a human being (corresponding to that of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessantara</span> in the case of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Gotama</span>) he lived with his family as an ascetic. A man eating <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kharadāṭhika</span>, took from him his two children and ate them in his presence, “crunching them as though they were yams,” while the blood dripped from his mouth (it is probably this incident, which is referred to at Ja.iv.13). The Bodhisatta stood firm in his resolve and repented not of his gift to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>, but registered a desire that in future births his body should emit light as bright as the blood which flowed down the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yakkha</span>’s face. In a previous birth, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṅgala</span> paid honour to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span> of a Buddha by wrapping his body in cloth drenched with oil, setting fire to it and walking round the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span> throughout the night, carrying on his head a golden bowl filled with scented oil and lighted with one thousand wicks. Not a hair on his body suffered damage.

For nine thousand years <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṅgala</span> lived in the household in three palaces, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasavā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sucimā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirimā</span>, with his wife <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Yasavatī</span>, by whom he had one son, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvala</span>. He left the world on a horse and practised austerities for eight months. Just before his Awakening he ate a meal of milk-rice given by a maiden, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttarā</span>, daughter of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t18">seṭṭhi</span> in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttaragāma</span>; an <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ājīvaka</span>, named <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span>, gave him grass for his seat. His <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bodhi</span> was a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nāga</span> tree. After his Awakening he lived for ninety thousand years, and for all that time the aura from his body spread throughout the ten thousand world systems, shutting out sun, moon and stars. People knew the times and the seasons by the cries of the birds and the blooming of the flowers.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṅgala</span>’s first sermon was preached in the Sirivaruttama Grove, near <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sirivaḍḍha</span>. His chief disciples among men were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudeva</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammasena</span>, and his chief nuns <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīvalā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Asokā</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pālita</span> was his constant attendant (Bv­a.124 calls him <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uttara</span>). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nanda</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Visākha</span> were his chief patrons among lay men and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anulā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sutanā</span> among lay women. In <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṅgala</span>’s time the Bodhisatta was the Brahmin <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Suruci</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Maṅgala</span>’s body was eighty cubits high; he held three assemblies: the first at the preaching of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammacakka</span>, the second at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Citta</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t18">nagara</span>, when he preached to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sunanda</span>, king of Surabhinagara, and his son <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anurāja</span>, and the third at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mekhala</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sudeva</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dhammasena</span> who later became his chief disciples.

He died in the park of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vessara</span>, and a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span>, thirty leagues high, was erected over his ashes (Bv.iv.1ff.; Bv­a.115ff.; Ja.i.30 ff.; the particulars found in Mvu.i.248-50, are slightly different). It is said that all Maṅgala Buddha’s personal disciples attained <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Arahant</span>-ship before their death (Bv.iv.29).
