# Mallā 01

## English source

Mallā 01. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="1" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mallas</span>. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="2" data-dppn-term-class="t18">v.l.</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="3" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mālā</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="4" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malatā</span>. The name of a people and their country. The country is included in the sixteen <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="5" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Janapadas</span> of the Buddha’s time. The kingdom, at that time, was divided into two parts, having their respective capitals in <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="6" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="7" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>. The Mallas of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="8" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span> were called Pāveyyaka Mallā, those of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="9" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusināra</span>, Kosinārakā. That these were separate kingdoms is shown by the fact that after the Buddha’s death at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="10" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusināra</span>, the Mallas of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="11" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span> sent messengers to claim their share of the Buddha’s relics (DN). Each had their Mote Hall.

In the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="12" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Saṅgītisutta</span> we are told that the Buddha, in the course of one of his journeys, came with five hundred followers to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="13" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span> and stayed in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="14" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ambavana</span> of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="15" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span> the smith. A new Mote Hall, called <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="16" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ubbhaṭaka</span>, had just been completed for the Mallas of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="17" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span>, and the Buddha was invited to be the first to occupy it that it might be consecrated thereby. The Buddha accepted the invitation, and preached in the Hall far into the night. It was also at <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="18" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span> that the Buddha took his last meal, of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="19" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sūkaramaddava</span>, at the house of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="20" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cunda</span> (DN). From there he went to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="21" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>, and there, as he lay dying, he sent <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="22" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> to the Mallas of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="23" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusināra</span>, who were assembled in their Mote Hall to announce his approaching death. The Mallas thereupon came to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="24" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Upavattana</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="25" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Sāla</span> grove where the Buddha was, in order to pay him their last respects. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="26" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> made them stand in groups according to family, and then presented them to the Buddha, announcing the name of each family. After the Buddha’s death, they met together once more in the Mote Hall, and made arrangements to pay him all the honour due to a <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="27" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Cakkavatti</span>. They cremated the Buddha’s body at the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="28" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Makuṭabandhana</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="29" data-dppn-term-class="t18">cetiya</span>, and then collected the relics, which they deposited in their Mote Hall, surrounding them with a lattice work of spears and a rampart of bows till they were distributed among the various claimants by <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="30" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Doṇa</span> (DN). The Mallas, both of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="31" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="32" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusināra</span>, erected <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="33" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Thūpas</span> over their respective shares of the relics and held feasts in their honour (DN).

The Malla capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="34" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> was, in the Buddha’s day, a place of small importance. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="35" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> contemptuously refers to it as a “little wattle and daub town in the midst of a jungle, a branch township,” quite unworthy of being the scene of the Buddha’s <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="36" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Parinibbāna</span>. But the Buddha informs <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="37" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ānanda</span> that it was once <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="38" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sammata</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="39" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>), the mighty capital of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="40" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusa</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="41" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Sudassana</span>. This shows that the Mallas had, at first, a monarchical constitution, but in the sixth century B.C. they were regarded, together with the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="42" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vajjī</span>, as a typical example of a republic (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="43" data-dppn-term-class="t18">saṅgha</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="44" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gaṇa</span>) (MN.i.231). The chief Mallas administered the state in turn. Those who were free from such duties engaged in trade, sometimes undertaking long caravan journeys (DN­a.ii.569).

Both the Buddha and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="45" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta</span> appear to have had followers among the Mallas. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="46" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span> was the scene of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="47" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Nātaputta</span>’s death, just as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="48" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span> was of the Buddha’s (see <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="49" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span>). Several followers of the Buddha among the Mallas are mentioned by name – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="50" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Dabba</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="51" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pukkusa</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="52" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Khaṇḍasumana</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="53" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhadragaka</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="54" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Rāsiya</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="55" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Roja</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="56" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Sīha</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="57" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). The Mallas seem to have lived at peace with their neighbours, though there was apparently some trouble between them and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="58" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span>, as shown by the story of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="59" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula Malla</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="60" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). Both the Mallas and the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="61" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="62" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Khattiyas</span>, belonging to the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="63" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vasiṭṭha</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="64" data-dppn-term-class="t18">gotta</span>, because in the books both tribes are repeatedly referred to as <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="65" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Vāseṭṭhā</span> (<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="66" data-dppn-term-class="t18">q.v.</span>). <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="67" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Manu</span> says that both <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="68" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> and Mallas had <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="69" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Kṣatriya</span> parents, but their fathers were <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="70" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Vrātyas</span> – i.e., had not gone through the ceremony of Vedic initiation at the proper time.

There is reason to believe that the Malla republic fell into the hands of <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="71" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Ajātasattu</span>, as did that of the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="72" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Licchavīs</span> (Bhaṇḍarkar, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="73" data-dppn-term-class="t18">Carmichael Lectures</span>, 1918, p.79).

The Mallas are generally identified with the Malloi mentioned in the Greek accounts of Alexander’s invasion of India. The Malloi were a warlike tribe who, for some time, successfully resisted Alexander’s attack. Their territory must have been situated in or near the Punjab.

Other places in the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="74" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malla</span> country, besides <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="75" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Pāvā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="76" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusinārā</span>, are mentioned where the Buddha stayed – e.g., <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="77" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bhoganagara</span>, <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="78" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Anupiyā</span> and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="79" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Uruvelakappa</span>, near which was the <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="80" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mahā Vana</span>, a wide tract of forest.

<span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="81" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Bandhula</span> went from <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="82" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Kusināra</span> to <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="83" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Takkasilā</span> for purposes of study. <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t18" data-dppn-term-pos="84" data-dppn-term-class="t18">v.l.</span> <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="85" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Mālā</span> (e.g., Ud­a.377) and <span class="dppn-term dppn-term-t14" data-dppn-term-pos="86" data-dppn-term-class="t14">Malatā</span> (e.g., AN­a.ii.814), evidently both wrong readings.
